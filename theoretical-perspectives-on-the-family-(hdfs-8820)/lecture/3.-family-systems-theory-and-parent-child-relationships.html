Themes

  • Decreased contact
    • Connection across distance
    • Distance from children
      • Parents with less time with parents tended to not be cisgender women: only one of these seven parents was a cisgender woman or only a biological parent; rather, three were trans or nonbinary, two were cisgender men, and most were a  combination of stepparents, nonbiological parents, biological parents, and adoptive parents.
      • Pandemic may have more of an impact on parent–child relationships when one or more characteristics of parents deviates from the dominant heteronorm of the primary parent (i.e., female, biological).
      • The children lived with other parents prior to the pandemic, and in others, parents made the decision for children to live with different caregivers such as grandparents or coparents during the pandemic due to risk of exposure (e.g., parent was a frontline worker) or socialization considerations (e.g., the desire to give the child more time with other family members).
      • "A relationship, shared,  a family, we decided it would be good for my kid to spend some time with my ex. Once that happened I have been able to visit my boyfriend twice (after 3 months apart). Both the kid and my ex is happier for the kid being up there.” Hsin-Yi’s account illustrates one way of resolving the desire to see their boyfriend and limit networks of risk exposure while also allowing their child to spend more time with other coparents.
  • Changing dynamics and configurations at home
    • More time at home
      • •This practice of incorporating multiple partners into a living unit could be beneﬁcial in having a “large, robust household” to combat isolation, but also introduced challenges related to sharing space and dividing time among partners. Isabella, a bisexual white polyamorous stepmother to a toddler, elaborated:
        • Fortunately, even those who discussed the stress of living in close quarters commented on the skills and strengths that they possessed as people who engaged in CNM, which enabled them to effectively communicate about their needs and boundaries.
        • But one thing polys are pretty good at is sort of working through issues as they arise.”“Several parents spoke to the strengths of their communication and conflict resolution skills.
        • Another approach to spend more time with partners while avoiding some of the complications of moving everyone into the same house was creating multi-household quarantine pods, which sometimes further challenged heteronormative notions of family through the intentional and collaborative construction of small communities.
    • Increased parenting responsibility
      • Significant stress and desire for greater social support.
      • Support for parents was generally appreciated both by children and co-parents.
  • Negotiating risk
    • For some, this involved a process of pausing and unpausing CNM relationships as participants made decisions about safety and relative risk.
    • Newly exploring CNM, the pandemic seemed to at least temporarily foreclose the possibility of opening the relationship.
    • For other participants with established relationships, the labor and thought that went into seeing existing partners appeared to take priority over the work of trying to safely meet new partners.
    • Others noted their reluctance to meet with new individuals given the politicization of responses to COVID-19.
    • Perhaps because those in CNM relationships often have significant experience communicating needs and boundaries to partners (including negotiating expectations around safer sex behaviors), many participants mentioned the conversations they have with partners about COVID risk without characterizing them as challenging or conflictual.
    • Differences in protective measured ended some relationships.

Discussion Questions

Family Processes and Children's and Adolescent's Well-Being (Buehler, 2020)

Increased attention to complexity and specificity

  • Complexity (Effects of the Family Processes via Explanatory Pathways) (Mediation)
    • Conflict affected child behavior problems through parent-to-child hostility in all family configurations except mother-child hostility in unrelated pairs (stepparents?)
    • Genes (relatedness, low serotonin)
      • Anger mediated association between parenting hostility and child health only for those with low serotonin.
    • Inclusion of Siblings
      • Child trauma symptoms is higher when siblings are treated more harshly and more parenting stress; consistent parenting for siblings; older sibling influence of delinquency (bidirectional)
    • Inclusion of dyadic and triadic processes (e.g., Triangulation)
    • Family-level constructs (e.g., boundary disturbances associated with emotional dysregulation)
  • Specificity (Identification of moderating factors that suggested for whom or under what conditions associations are salient) ( Moderation)
    • Moderation (e.g., meta-analyses; associations between chronic health condition and sibling well-being - stronger for older siblings and when illness was life-threatening)
      • Advanced the literature by examining moderators that provided important information regarding participant characteristics, sample features, and research methods.
      • The positive association between parents' marital hostility and young adolescents' internalizing problems was significant only when parents' marital cooperation was low (Zhou & Buehler, 2019)
    • Latent class analyses (e.g., association between child effects and harsh parenting strong for those behaving poorly)
    • Conceptual distinctions (e.g., everyday conflict vs. Acculturation -based conflict)
    • Multiple markers (e.g., adjustment and positive outcomes)
  • Importance of time in the study of family processes and child and adolescent well-being.
  • Future Directions
    • Examination of emotion, cognitive, physiological, and behavioral regulation within and between diverse families
    • Continue research that considers dyadic, triadic, and family-level regulation
    • Focus on studying and enhancing family strengths and reducing risks
    • Consider both risks and strengths as Predictor s, Mediation (Mediator), and Moderation (Moderator)
    • Continue investigations of gender as a moderator
  • Increased attention to family processes
    • Family Diversity
      • More diverse families (e.g., more inclusive definitions of 'family' - same-sex, between-group comparisons, rural AA families, low-income families)
    • Cultural Adaptations
      • (e.g., FSM)
      • Revisioning of these theories (Family Systems Theory to include historical time and choice with racial socialization)
  • Importance of time in the study of family processes and child and adolescent well-being
    • Diary Diary Studies
      • (e.g., self-blame associated with you feeling more anger on days with higher conflict)
    • Longitudinal Studies
      • (i.e., growth curves, cross-lagged models)
      • Identifying developmental periods (e.g., violence during toddler and early childhood associated with IPV at age 23)
      • Intergenerational association (e.g., accumulation of family conflict across generations)
      • Changes in family processes (e.g., increased marital conflict associated with children's later disruptive behavior and family conflict in adolescence associated with later youth depressive symptoms)
      • Reciprocal associations (e.g., child anxiety associated with parental hostility but reverse not true when considering other behaviors; reciprocal association between maternal sensitivity and child emotional regulation)
  • Strengths in family functioning
    • Strengths:
      • Reflected in processes that have the potential to be associated with greater child and youth positive development or fewer (or less severe) developmental difficulties or adjustment problems.
    • Resiliency
      • The capacities of families as functional systems to withstand and rebound from stressful challenges and demands.
    • Family Cohesion
      • Closeness, support, shared fun
  • Future directions
  • Main takeaways
    • Move beyond markers of child adjustment problems and focus more of positive aspects of well-being (e.g., emotion regulation, healthy sleep, positive affect, positive racial-ethnic identity)
    • Development and reformulation of conceptual models that account for diversity in families
    • The importance of studying everyday lives of families was highlighted and research methods like audio recording and daily diaries were crucial in furthering this area
    • Cross-lagged designs and growth curves were used often
    • There is a lot of variability both within and between families that needs to be studied.
