Prevention of OUD: The HOME (Housing, Opportunities, Motivation, and Engagement)

  • NIH Heal Initiative (preventing opioid use disorder in older adolescents and young adults)
  • Testing CFT with adolescents as a treatment plan (1990s)
  • Engaging the family is difficult when child has to leave
  • Leaving justice system that have no where to go.
  • Individual interventions rather than using any relational therapy
  • Most kids on the streets deal with substance use. Mostly interested in giving the children a foundation to stand upon. Stability.
  • Vulnerable populations (adolescents and emerging adults)

How much is this children not knowing the resources that exist to help them

  • Some grants require you to work with other researchers on their projects who received the grant. Everyone collects data, have to make rules to what to do with the data, can be tedious.

  • Youth experiencing (Poly-drug users) homelessness (YEH) have extremely high rates of substance use and other risks

  • 2-5 million YEH in the US (not rare), likely bearing the greatest burden from the epidemic. Replicable, evidence-based preventive interventions that comprehensively address these youth's range of needs are not established. While shelters are often the 'front door' or first contact for service provision among homeless populations, most of those under 24 years do not reach the shelter system.

  • in 2022, the overdose death rate in Ohio in 38.3 deaths out of every 100,000 residents. This is 85% higher than the national average OD death rate.

  • US has also seen a rapid increase in opioid use among youth, which has been described as the most alarming drug use trend.

  • Compared to housed youth:

    • YEH Have significantly higher rates of substance use, physical and mental health problems
    • YEH have 12 times higher mortality rate

  • Reports indicate that only 10% of YEH in need of services access available services.

  • Black persons make up 13% of the population, but 40% of the homeless population

  • Black youth have 83% higher change of reporting homelessness than their White peers.

  • Being Black is associated with a lower likelihood of finding permanent housing, with White families 1.9 times more likely to find housing than black families.

  • Youth identifying as sexual and/or gender minorities (SGM) are also overrepresented in the homeless population (40%)

  • Housing First:

    • Prevent opioids use disorder (OUD) and other risk behaviors associated with living on the streets, Housing First interventions may be necessary, as well as linkage to a range of services
    • Housing First is the name given to the philosophical approach that housing is a basic human right, and housing before or concurrent with other interventions make those interventions more effective.
    • Having autonomy to make choices and decisions for oneself
    • Do not take away housing if struggling with substance abuse/use or mental health

  • Prior prevention work:

    • The odds of recent hard drugs use among HIV positive adults experiencing homelessness were four times higher when they remained homeless.
    • Those who obtained housing were half as likely to use hard drugs and needs, to share needs, and engage in unprotected sex.

  • Engagement Challenges:

    • Services available but youth cannot or will not access them
    • Unless there's active outreach and meeting youth where they are, youth trying to live day to day will not take advantage of prevention services.
    • Youth often feel by betrayed by the system and its representatives.
    • 18-24 are told to go to adult center
    • 14-18 are sent to shelters

  • Strengths-Based Outreach Approach includes following deatures:

    • Use paraprofessional personnel
    • A focus on client strengths rather than deficits
    • A high degree of responsibility give t the client in directing and influencing the intervention that they receive from the system

  • Motivational Interviewing (MI):

    • Strong record of prevention in alcohol and drug problems.
    • Demands much less of a hard-to-reach population
    • Several studies have shown positive impact of MI on drug use among runaway and homeless youth

  • Prevention program / intervention description:

    • Using a housing first philosophy
    • 6 months of rent
    • utility assistance

  • Eligibility

    • 18-24 years
    • Youth experiences homelessness
    • Youth fails to meet DSM-5 criteria for Opioid Use Disorder (OUD)

  • Drug use (primarily marijuana use), showed a 35% Reduction in frequency of use

    • While statistical significant cannot be expected with a small sample, we found statistically significant improvement
    • no youth began opioid use during treatment

  • Qualitative Interviews:

    • 17 participants; overall participants reported noticing positive changes in their lives from the intervention had nothing about the project they disliked.
    • 5 landlords interviewed; generally positive experiences with project, willing to rent to future
      • Liked:
        • Consistent rent payments
        • Opportunity to help others
      • Disliked:
        • 6-month lease (prefer full year)
        • Problematic Tenant (one landlord)

  • Phase I Summary:

    • Lessons learned:
      • Youth very engaged, even using remote strategies
      • HIV session attentance poor
      • landlord and youth experiences very good

  • Phase I Findings:

    • First study to engage at risk youth experiencing homelessness into a holist opioid prevention

  • Phase II:

    • Baseline Assessment -> Random Assignment
      • Housing and risk prevention services

  • Conclusions:

    • Even though these youth are at high risk for OUD and overdose death and are considered difficult to engage, they CAN be engaged in m

  • Recommendations:

    • Transition planning would save money and prevent drug use and suicide if youth never go to street.
    • Need greater funding lalocations to drop-in centers AND for opiois and other prevention services - most federal funding overall given to shelters

  • Housing First:

    • If we conclude that housing is a powerful opioid prevention tool, then follows that housing for those experiencing homelessness has to be made a top priority in the drug precention world?
    • Support Housing First (not Housing Fourth)
    • Change rules on who is eligible for housing
      • Rules for obtaining identification
      • Justice record expungement to ease housing barriers
    • Address utility arrears.
