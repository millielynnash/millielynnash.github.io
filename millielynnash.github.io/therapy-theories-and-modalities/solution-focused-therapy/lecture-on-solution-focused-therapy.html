Leading Figures:

  • Steve de Shazer:
    • 1979:
      • Started the Brief Family Therapy Center (BFTC)
      • Worked in University Resources/Couple and Family Therapy/Keywords/Palo Alto (1952); influenced by MRI
      • Focuses on solutions that work rather than solutions that do not work as in the MRI model.
    • Insoo Kim Berg:
      • BFTC
      • Applied the model to alcoholism, marital therapy, and family-based services to the poor.

Theoretical Background:

  • Derived from MRI
  • Focus on cognitions rather than behavior
  • Focus on the presenting the problem
    • Find the simplest solution
  • Focus on exceptions
    • Exceptions to client's problem are the solution
  • Assumed people are capable of behaving effectively; blocked by negative mindset
  • People already have the solution(s) to their problems.
  • People constrained by narrow view of their problems.
    • Rigid patterns of false solutions
  • Future-oriented
    • Look to future where problems can be solved.
  • Changing the way people talk about their problems.
  • The language of problems tends to be different from the language of solutions.
    • Problem talk:
      • Negative
      • Focuses on the past
      • Implies permanence of problems
    • Solution talk:
      • Hopeful
      • Future oriented

Normal Family Development:

  • Avoid judgements about 'what is normal'
  • Clients as experts in their situation
  • Interest in language
    • How people describe themselves and problems
  • Focus on complaints presented
    • Should not impose values by suggesting clients address other un-presented problems.

Development of Behavior Disorders:

  • Solutions to problems are unrelated to the development of the problem.
  • Tracking the development of the problem should be avoided.
  • Problem-focused thinking prevents effective solutions from being recognized.

Mechanisms of Change:

  • Set clear goals and build on exceptions to problems.
  • Good therapy is more than implementing specific techniques, it also involves therapeutic alliance.
  • The goal of solution-focused therapy is to resolve the presenting problem as expeditiously as possible.
  • The goal is not about how families should be structured, but only what they want different in their lives.
  • The model emphasizes the importance of assisting clients in creating well-defined goals, framed in positive terms and modest enough to be achievable.

Goals of Therapy:

  • Resolve presenting complaint by helping clients do or think differently.
  • Trust clients to reach their own goals.
  • Help clients identify the problem-solving skills they have lost sight of.
  • Help clients locate their resources.

Therapy Structure:

  • Time limited
  • Sessions are made one at a time
  • One may be enough
  • Typical course includes 3-5 sessions
  • Clients can return as-needed
  • Often a week to a few weeks apart
  • Once you know more of what works, do more of it

The Role of the Therapist:

  • Consultant
  • Don't get bogged down in 'problem talk'
  • Direct clients away from the problem-saturated narrative
  • Therapists attempts to create a context for the family to develop more useful ways of responding
  • Focus on the 'here and now'
  • The future provides a blank canvas on which the family can paint a picture of their wishes and hopes
  • Assumes a position of 'not knowing'

Assessment:

  • Very little intake information
  • Avoid assessing how problems develop
  • Concentrate on assessing future goals
  • Therapy should only include people concerned about the problem
  • Hear the client's constructions of their problems without preconceptions

Assessment Questions:

  • What do you think the problem is now?
  • How will you know when the problem is solved?
  • How will you know when you don't have to come here anymore? What will the signs be?
  • What will have to be different for that to happen in terms of your behavior, thoughts, and feelings?
  • What will you notice that is different about others involved in the situation?
  • What is your wildest fantasy about what you want to happen?

Goal Development:

  1. What needs to happen as a result of coming here so that afterward you will look back and be able to say it was a good idea?
  2. What needs to happen so that this will not have been a waste of your time?
  3. Often in our experience, we have found that in between scheduling an appointment and coming in, something happens that contributes to making a problem better. Has anything happened to improve the problems that brought you here today?

Assessing / Scaling:

  • Once a description of the goal has been developed, the therapist asks the clients to assess their current level of progress toward that goal by imagining a scale of 1-10.
    • Where 1 represented the problem at its worst, and 10 is the point at which the problem is gone or the clients are coping so well that its no longer problematic

Overview of Therapy Process:

  • Solution-focused therapists help clients identify their preferred solution by:
    • Talking about the problem, exceptions, and desired outcomes.
    • Working with clients to take small active steps in this general direction each week.
  • This can take 1-10 sessions or it may take years depending on the complexity of the case.

Goal Setting:

  • How do we know when to stop meeting like this?
  • Goals should be:
    • Small
    • Salient to clients
    • Concrete, behavioral, measurable
    • Achievable
    • Involve hard work
    • Start of something
    • Involve new behaviors
  • What will be present? What will be happening?

Interventions:

Intervention: Miracle Question:

  • Activates problem-solving mindset by giving people a vision of their goal.
  • Helps clients to look beyond the problem to see what they really want
    • Might not be the elimination of the problem but to be able to do things that the problem has been obstructing
  • "Suppose one night there is a miracle and while you are sleeping the problem that brought you into therapy is solved."
    • How would you know?
    • What would be different?
    • What will you notice the next morning that will tell you there has been a miracle?
    • What will your family member notice?

Interventions: Exploring Exceptions:

  • Directs clients' attention to times when they did not have the problem and expand on exceptions.
    • Helping families see that their problems have exceptions - times when they don't occur.
  • Exceptions are solutions that families already have in their repertoire.
  • Finding exceptions in the recent past is most useful because clients can remember them in greater detail; also since these exceptions just happened, it is more plausible they could happen again.
    • When in the recent past might the problem have happened but didn't (or was less intense or more manageable)?
    • What's different about those times when the problem doesn't happen?
      • How have you let your partner know when they do something that makes a positive difference to you?

Coping Questions:

  • Punctuate with enthusiasm and support.
  • Work to keep solution talk in the forefront.
  • Listen carefully for an exception by getting more details about it and congragulating the family on it.
  • Connect the exception to the family's goals by asking how the goal would be reached if this exception were to occur more often.
    • "How did you manage that?"
    • "What keeps you going under such difficult circumstances?"
    • "How come things aren't worse?"
    • "What have you done to keep them from getting worse?"

Scaling Questions:

  • Help therapist and clients talk about vague topics (e.g., depression)
    • On a scale from 1 to 10, how sad do you feel?
  • Help clients to set goals in a clear/manageable manner.
    • What do you think you need to do to achieve a 3?

Compliments:

  • To be effective, compliments should point toward what to do more, not what to eliminate.
  • Compliments can be used to highlight successful strategies and keep clients focused on those that work.

Therapeutic Suggestions:

  • Formula first-session task
  • Do more of what works
  • Do soemthing different
  • Go slow
  • Do the opposite
  • The prediction task

Later Sessions:

  • Devote to finding, amplifying, and measuring progress.
  • Inquire about progress, seeking detailed descriptions of any movement toward the family's goal and the clients' role in attaining it.
    • What's better?
    • What happened that you liked?
    • Tell me more. Walk me through how the two of you did that.
    • How did you keep things from getting worse?
    • What's your idea about what might be helpful?

Termination:

  • How can we know when to stop meeting like this?
  • What needs to be different in your life as a result of coming here for you to say that meeting with me was worthwhile?

Critiques:

  • Toxic positivity
  • Therapist doesn't get to know client's past, which make it harder to understand and empathize with client
  • The brief, goal-oriented focus does not work with every kind of person or problem
  • It is easier to focus on the positive and solutions than to do the deep, difficult work of talking about trauma and the past
