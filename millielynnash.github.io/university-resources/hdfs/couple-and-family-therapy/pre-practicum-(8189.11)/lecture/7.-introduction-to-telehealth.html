Telehealth:

  • Ethical Concerns and Telehealth:
    1. Remember our ethical imperatives for in-person therapy.
    2. Rely on common factors
    3. Rely on our professional standards of practice
      1. AAMFT Code of Ethics
      2. Coalition for Technology in Behavioral Science (CTiBS) TBH Competencies
    4. Know and adhere to legal guidelines (federal and state)
    5. Continue education and training.
    6. Seek supervision.
  • Assessing Appropriateness for Telehealth:
    1. Conduct an initial assessment of the client to determine if telehealth is appropriate.
    2. Conduct ongoing assessment to determine appropriateness to receive services via teletherapy throughout the court of treatment.
    • Consider:
      1. Current Mental and Emotional Status
      2. Is the treatment model conducive to telehealth?
      3. Is the current delivery of the service effective?
      4. Does the client have the technological ability and resources necessary?
    • A client may be inappropriate for telehealth if:
      • Recurrently experiences, or is likley to experience, crises or emergencies.
      • Is a suicide risk, or likely to become a suicide risk.
      • Has a history of previous non-suicidal self-injury.
      • Is violent, or likely to become violent.
  • Risk Management in Telehealth:
    • Suicidality
    • Homicidality
    • Child Abuse/Neglect
    • Intimate Partner Violence
    • Animal Abuse
    • Physical Health Issues or Crises
    • Obtain your client's physical location at the start of each session.
      • Complete Address
      • Make, Model, Color of Car
      • Nearest Physical Location
    • Obtain a client's emergency contact at initial intake and review regularly
      • Physical Location
      • Distance from the Client (nearest ETA)
    • Obtain contact information for local emergency resources:
      • Local police and fire departments
      • Local Crisis Centers
      • Family Pediatrician/PCP
      • National Crisis Numbers
  • Safety Assessment:
    • Is it safe for them to return or remain home>
    • Are there weapons in the house?
    • Are the children in danger?
    • Is the violence escalating?
    • Are they unwilling to complete a safety plan?
    • Is their situation too severe and they require a higher level of care?
    • The most important intervention a therapist can use when working with a suicidal client is compassion for the pain they are in and patience in listening to their story.
    • What is a Safety Plan?
      • A safety plan is a prioritized written list of coping strategies and sources of support developed by a clinician in collaboration with clients who are at high risk for suicide.
      • A safety plan is not a 'no-suicide contract,' which is not recommended by experts in the field of suicide prevention.
        • As generally used, no-suicide contracts asks clients to promise stay alive without telling them how to stay alive.
  • Safety Planning:
    • Safety planning incorporates elements of four evidence-based suicide risk reduction strategies:
      1. Reducing Access to Lethal Means
      2. Teaching Brief Problem Solving and Coping Skills
      3. Enhancing Social Support and Identifying Emergency Contacts
      4. Using Motivational Enhancement to Increase likelihood of Engagement in Further Treatment
  • Assessing for Child Abuse on Video Session:
    • On a video call, observe how the house appears:
      • Look for ongoing sings that go beyond messiness.
      • Is perishable good sitting out after multiple calls?
      • Is there an absence of food or do family members appear to be losing weight?
      • Are there signs of pests or rodents?
      • Do children appear significantly unkempt or not bathed?
    • Look for signs of increased frustration.
    • This is a time of great stress for parents, ask questions to see how well a parent is coping.
      • "How are things going with the kids?"
      • "What are you getting to do during the day?"
    • Speak with the Children:
      • Ask for time to speak with the children along.
      • Observe their reactions to who and what is around them.
      • What is their level of focus?
      • Do they seem particularly stressed or emotional?
      • Is their behavior atypical?
    • Be Understanding:
      • If a parent is expressing desperation, be empathetic.
      • It is normal for parents to express frustration with statements such as 'being over it' or 'giving up.'
      • Red flags for a listener include:
        • Over rigidity
        • Persistent expression of negative emotions
        • Inability to see beyond their immediate concern
        • Extreme frustration that suggest possible escalation to physical aggression
  • Telepresence and Virtual Professionalism:
    • Telebehavioral health (TBH) professionals can demonstrate how to apply appropriate techniques to maximize therapeutic atmosphere in both physical and virtual environments as well as minimize distraction and interruptions.
    • Professionals can show how to approximate an in-person relationship and foster spontaneity through TBH.
  • Creating a Virtual Office:
    • When setting up your virtual office, we must consider the following:
      • Physical Location
      • Privacy
      • Lighting
      • Comfort
    • Where is your home based or online office located physically?
      • Is it near a power source?
      • Is there a way to 'close the door' to your office? (i.e., is there at least a door between your office space and other common areas in your location?)
      • Is it a high traffic area?
    • How can you create a distraction free environment?
      • Turn off anything that makes noise.
      • A sign outside the door can help remind others not to make noise during the session.
      • Avoid a space with lots of clutter on the desk.
      • Be mindful of what is behind you in the camera's view.
    • How can you create a quality video image?
      • Make sure you are well lit.
      • Are there shadows being cast on my face?
      • Am I lit well enough to see me clearly?
      • Find the best source of lighting.
        • Overhead light (may not be the best)
        • Desk Lamp
        • Single Floor Lamp (Often Good: 4-5 ft, bulbs point up to the ceiling)
      • Make sure your camera is well placed.
        • Eye level with the camera straight at you (ideal).
      • Practice good posture (use the picture-in-picture)
    • How can you create a therapeutic environment?
      • Find an office space with minimal distractions or a solid color background.
      • Be mindful of clothing to limit distractions.
        • Solid colors are best
        • Avoid busy patterns
        • Avoid large jewelry and hats
  • Before the First Session:
    • Provide clear instructions to your clients about how to join the session.
    • Become aware of common technical problems that could arise and how to trouble shoot for these problems.
    • If possible, host a test call.
    • Both therapist and client obtain contact information in case of technology failure.
  • During the First Session:
    • Ask for initial feedback for sound and video quality
    • Allow for more time for interviews
    • Remain attentive
    • Keep eyes on camera
    • Avoid distracting behavior and excessive movement
    • Turn off anything that makes noise
    • Remain in view of camera at all times
    • Be mindful of possible delays and lags in video
    • Speak slowly and deliberately
  • Understanding Telebehavioral Health Competencies:
    1. Clinical Evaluation and Care
    2. Virtual Environment and Telepresence
    3. Technology
    4. Legal & Regulatory Issues
    5. Evidence-Based & Ethical Practice
    6. Mobile Health Technologies Including Applications (Apps)
    7. Telepractice Development
  • Telebehavioral Health Competency Levels:
    • Novice:
      • Identify and describe issues
      • Conduct basic screening
      • List pros and cons of using various technologies
      • Protect security, privacy, and confidentiality
      • Educate clients about fundamental TBH, laws, rules, regulations, ethical requirements, policies, procedures, assessments, interventions, standards and communication styles.
    • Proficient
    • Authority:
      • Demonstrate the basics of using computers, remote patient monitoring, social media and mobile health and the issues related to recommending the use of apps.
      • Discuss how to legally and ethically market their own services online.
