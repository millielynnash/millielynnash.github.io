Satir's Growth Model (Human Validation Process Movement)

Reiter (2023):

Satir Growth Model (Human Validation Process Movement)

  • Virginia Satir (1916-1988)
    • In the late 1950 s, she joined Don Jackson, Jay Haley, and others in developing the Mental Research Institute (MRI).
    • Satir wrote one of the first books about family therapy, Conjoint Family Therapy, in which she provided transcripts of how she worked.

Why Families Have Problems

  • Every person has resources available to them, but when troubled, they do not usually utilize these resources to their utmost abilities.
  • Self-Mandala:
    • The individual is at the center, and surrounding them are the eight universal human resources: spiritual, contextual, nutritional, interactional, sensual, emotional, intellectual, and physical.
    • Each part is interconnected. The less these 8 areas are given energy and time, the lower a person's self-esteem.
      • When people experience low self-esteem, they attempt to cope by using Survival Stances.
        • These stances help people temporarily deal with presumed threats and protect their self-worth. The stances help to protect us, but do not help us or other people to grow and thrive. The four survival stances are:
          • Placating (Placator):
            • Occurs when people discount themselves and privilege the other person and the context. Individual tends to apologize a lot, feels worthless, and may become depressed or suicidal. They tend to be quite sensitive and caring.
            • The placating stance discounts self and privileges the other person and the context.
          • Blaming (Blamer):
            • Privileges self and context while discounting the other.
            • To cope with low self-esteem, the person attacks another individual.
            • On the outside the individual seems assertive, but on the inside feel isolated and alone.
            • Their experience leads them to sometimes feel paranoid and even potentially homicidal.
          • Super-Reasonable (Computer):
            • Privileges the context and discounts the self and others.
            • Focuses on the rules rather than on emotions and relationships.
            • Very data driven and people appreciate them for their intellect.
            • To offset their internal vulnerability, the super-reasonable person doesn't allow themselves to connect with any emotions. This can lead to a rigidness that may be displayed via obsessive-compulsive behaviors.
          • Irrelevant (Distractor):
            • Discounts self, other, and context.
            • Engage in distraction to avoid having to deal with their low self-esteem.
            • People may describe them as fun and spontaneous. However, you cannot have a serious or straightforward conversation with them. This leads to a psychological orientation of confusion and possible psychosis.
  • The goal of Satir's therapy is to help people enhance their own and others' self-esteem. When we do so, we function from a Congruent (Congruence) communication stance. This is the healthiest position we can operate from.

How Problems Get Solved

  • The Satir Growth Model is a strength-based approach, focusing on the existing resources that family members bring with them.
  • Faulty coping strategies maintain problematic interactions.
  • Therapy provides a safe space for people to choose alternative ways of coping. Therapy increases people's self-esteem, leading them to be able to lean into their uniqueness and provide a space for their loved ones to feel comfortable in their own uniqueness as well.
  • Whatever techniques the therapists uses, they are designed to help raise awareness within and between people in hopes of increasing self-esteem and self-worth so that people feel freer to be authentic. (Congruence)
    • Self-Esteem Maintenance Kit:
      • Detective Hat:
        • Used when there is something new that the person wants to understand better. It helped them shift away from judging an experience to exploring it.
      • Medallion:
        • Two-sided and says 'yes' on one side and 'no' on the other. It is intended to help the person recognize that they can say either word, depending on what they truly want, so they can maintain their integrity.
      • Wand (Empowering wand, courage stick, wishing wand):
        • Helps people move from inaction based on fear to action based on desire.
      • Golden Key:
        • Opens any door - helping the person ask questions they might not have previously, so the unsayable, and do the undoable.
      • Wisdom Box:
        • Helps connect the person to the wider world and all that has come before them.
  • Experiential therapists are interactive in the therapy room. They try to use their whole self for contact. This includes adopting an I-Thou Perspective, where they try to promote human-to-human interaction.
    • Since one of the main goals of therapy is to help the client become more congruent, it is important for the therapist to engage the client congruently. The therapist must be a real person who helps model a way of being for the client.
  • Experiential therapists conceptualize a few main processes:
    • Status Quo:
      • The family is currently in this process, and is leading to difficulties bringing them to therapy.
    • Foreign Element:
      • The family's expression of the desire to change.
    • Chaos:
      • The therapist's introduction of novelty into the system.
    • Integration:
      • Family members put what they learn from therapy into practice.
    • Practice:
      • Involves trying out these learnings.
    • New Status Quo:
      - New homeostasis takes hold.
  • The therapist raises the intensity of interactions so that people encounter their own experiences and can thus become more authentic. Therapy then helps people to allow others to be more authentic.
  • Sculpting:
    • Sculpting helps people get in touch with their inner experience and express this experience to others in a way that bypasses the typical spoken word.
    • Sculpting can be done in one of two primary ways:
      • The client is asked to sculpt their family based on their experience of what it is like to be part of this family now. Then the therapist can sculpt the family based on their own inner picture of the family's experience.
        • Family members are asked to place other people in physical poses spatially located around the room and then to position themselves somewhere within. This provides the family with a new way of engaging with self and other. This allows for new meanings to come forth, where people can become more authentically themselves.
        • The therapist will begin with one member of the family. The choice of person will depend on the family organization, the presenting problem, and the therapeutic process. Once that person has presented their image of the family, the therapist acts as a guide for each family member, bringing forth their perceptions of this picture and what it represents about the various relationships in the family. Therapist then shifts to another family member to explore their experience, as every family member unique has their own way of seeing and being in the family.
      • Therapist may also end the sculpt by having family members sculpt their desired picture of the family. This sculpt shifts the energy in the room from a focus on past problems and dysfunction to a future functional and supportive arrangement of relationships.
  • Satir viewed people as housing energy and as able to transfer this energy through physical connections with one another.
  • Family Reconstruction:
    • Designed to help an individual connect better to the historical and psychological interconnections of their family. By seeing self and family of origin with a new perspective, the individual might then feel more freedom to engage in new possibilities for the future.
    • Star:
      • The person whose relationship with the family is being explored and worked on.
    • The therapist prepares the reconstruction by talking with the star about their family history. This involves developing a Family Map, or the primary family that can include the grandparents, a Family Life Chronology, highlighting three generations of the star's family, including important dates and historical events (similar to the genogram), and the Wheel of Influence, a chart including all the people who influenced the star when they were growing up.
    • The therapist ('guide') begins the family reconstruction by telling the star's story; then sculpts the family of origin, the star's parents' families of origin, and the star's parents' relationship; and then resculpts the star's family of origin.
    • The overall goal is to increase the star's self-esteem so they are empowered to engage with themselves and others in new and more productive ways. This should engender more hope in them for their future.
  • Parts Party:
    • Based on the notion that people have many faces (or aspects of self), a parts party helps people to accept that they also have many possibilities. Some of our parts may be lying dormant, and when we access them, we increase our resources.
    • The parts party helps people to acknolwedge, accept, transform, and integrate their parts.
    • Other participants play the role of the various parts to help the person move from internal processes to more interpersonal processes.

Introduction

  • "The Growth Model," where the focus was on helping people access their inner resources as opposed to, "The Medical Model," where the emphasis was on pathology and symptom reduction.
    • Satir likened the role of a family therapist to that of a gardener in that people are like seeds, they carry all the resources they need for growth if nurtured and protected from blocks to their growth. She believed that the key resource for each person was their Unique Spirit from which emanated other resources such as: self-awareness, self-acceptance, change, choice, compassion, curiosity, determination, hope, love, and wisdom.
      • "The question for me was never whether (people) had spirits, but how I could contact them... It was as though I saw through to the inner core of each being, seeing through to the inner core of each being, seeing the shining light of the spirit trapped in a thick black cylinder of limitation and self-rejection. My effort was to enable the person to see what I saw; then, together, we could turn the dark cylinder into a large, lighted screen and build new possibilities." (Satir, 1988, p. 340-341)
    • Her goals for family therapy included helping the individual members increase their sense of worth and value by accessing their inner resources and by decreasing any emotional blocks. And for the family as a whole, she saw the goal as being to increase congruent communication and heart connections.
    • She began to observe that there were some universal unspoken rules that blocked the individual members from functioning at full capacity and also hindered the connections between members. These were:
      • It is not okay to see what is going on in the family.
      • It is not okay to feel what you feel.
      • It is not okay to talk about what you see and feel.
      • It is not okay to ask for what you want.
      • It is not okay to take risks.
    • She would observe the family members and their interaction and if she detected any of these rules in the family, she would help them break them by asking questions such as:
      • "What do you see going on between your and your ?"
      • "What are you aware of feeling at this moment?"
      • "Can you tell your ___ what you are feeling?"
      • "Can you ask your ___ for what you want?"
      • "I invite you to use your courage and say what you see happening in your family."
    • She also helped them to make better connections with each other by decreasing their defensiveness. Satir identified the primary defensive stances as Blaming (Blamer), Placating (Placator), Distracting, and being Super-Reasonable (Computer).
      • Forms of Fight:
        • Blaming (Blamer) (This is all your fault.)
        • Generalizing (You always think you are right.)
        • Labeling (You are so stupid.)
        • Threatening (You better shape up or else.)
        • Physically Attacking the Other.
      • Forms of Flight:
        • Placating (Placator) (Do what you wish, my needs don't matter.)
        • Being Super-Reasonable (Computer) (I don't care what you feel, just give me the facts.)
        • Emotionally Withdrawing (Physically present but not emotionally present.)
        • Physically Withdrawing (Leaving the room or hanging up on someone.)
        • Cutting-Off (Refusing to talk to the other for days or longer.)
      • Within a family system, these defenses can be played out in interactional patterns such as:
        • Blame - Blame
        • Blame - Withdraw
        • Placate - Placate
        • Blame - Placate
        • Blame - Distract
        • Withdraw - Withdraw

The Satir Process

  • Satir developed a process for helping people change and become more connected. Her process is described as having six phases:
    • Phase 1 - Making Contact
      • Satir would reach out using her body position, eye contact, and facial expression to make contact with each member. She would also consciously work to connect from her spirit to theirs.
    • Phase 2 - Validating
      • Satir worked to help people feel validated in many ways such as reflecting their feelings, affirming their values and highlighting their inner resources. Examples include:
        • "You feel scared because your son got suspended from school."
        • "You value education."
        • "You are courageous to seek help."
    • Phase 3 - Facilitating Awareness
      • Satir had many different ways that she would attempt to bring awareness to a family about their defensive patterns in a non-blaming way.
      • Sculpting:
        • Satir placed the members in body positions to give them a picture of their defensive behavior.
          • For the blaming stance, she would position the person leaning toward another and pointing at them and for the placating stance, she would position the person down on one knee with their head down and their hand up in a pleading position.
      • Satir would also bring awareness to members by exploring their inner experiences. She used the metaphor of an iceberg to help the family therapist to conceptualize the inner level so experience driving defensive behaviors.
        • Above the water line were the inner levels of experience such as feelings, perceptions, expectations, beliefs, and yearnings. (What meaning are you giving to what your ___ said that is causing you to feel hurt? Is that a familiar place for you to go to when someone is disappointed in you? What is it you yearn for?)
    • Phase 4 - Promoting Acceptance
      • One of the ways that satir promoted acceptance was through 'normalizing,' that is, reassuring the family member that what they were experiencing was normal. ("It is normal to...")
      • Another way that she promoted acceptance was by 'bridging' between conflicting parties. ("I am hearing that is is not easy for _, I would be interested to know how life was for you when you were their age?")
    • Phase 5 - Eliciting Positive Choices and Changes
      • Once Satir observed that the family members were aware of and accepting of their unhelpful patterns, she would lead them into seeing new possibilities for their interactions.
      • After sculpting the family, she would ask them to each to stand in a balanced way and look at each other. She would then guide them in a dialogue helping them to express themselves in non-defensives ways. Other times she would simply have them sit face to face and she would guide the dialogue between them.
      • "What happens for you when your says you choose over your ? You feel... Did you know that your felt __? What happened for you when you heard them express their feelings? You long to be more connected with them. You miss them. How do you feel to hear how much they miss you?"
    • Phase 6 - Reinforcing Change
      • Satir would often combine verbal responses with some supportive touch to reinforce a positive shift.
  • The therapist also needs to work on their own personal growth. This is true for working with individuals but it is even more true when working with families as the chances of being triggered by your own family experience are even greater.
    • As Satir used to say, "You are the most important instrument in the room!"

  • Under threat and reward, change is painful: change is viewed as unnatural, the unknown is feared, the status quo is maintained, life's natural 'flow' is blocked, and the familiar is valued despite discomfort.
  • The seed model has people viewing change more positively; as naturally occurring at a variety of levels (from cellular to cosmic) and as scary, but offering new opportunities. Discomfort is seen as a signal for change.
    • Therapists using the seed model keep in mind that families entering therapy are problem-focused and will maintain the status quo as a necessary survival behavior.
    • Therapist should make contact with each person to communicate a climate of acceptance (e.g., people do the best they can) and hear them out to discover 'what is.'
    • Understanding the status quo stage is essential to clarify how the change process gets started or is blocked. This stage is explored further by examining resistance and readiness concepts in detail.
  • The coin of change has resistance and readiness as its head and tail sides. Experientially, we may want to change in one moment and fear or sabotage it in the next. Then status quo is considered a trance, accounting for change resistance as well as a medium in which change may be fostered.

Resistance and Readiness to Change

  • Changing circumstances elicit shifts in perception, which in turn stimulate changes in thinking, feeling, and acting. But when circumstances shift back, behavior also reverts.
  • Perception can appear to create change of behavior when the meaning of an event is reframed, but the underlying pattern is not altered. Altering the underlying pattern requires dealing with resistance to change.
    • Resistance is a coping behavior intended to preserve the organism rather than obstruct change. Reframing resistance in terms of its positive intention.
    • The way we are socialized has us, "Gradually acquiring a variety of commitments which constrain the person into following a consistent pattern of behavior."
      • The effect is to develop a lifestyle that stabilizes the events in our lives, our interdependence of meaning of these events, and the way we define ourselves, as well as predisposing us to seek consistent situations.
  • Experientially, change involves invoking the unfamiliar. Intellectualizing, avoiding feelings, compulsive activity, impatience about change, and dwelling in the past are all ways of dealing with the confusion by maintaining the status quo.
    • The tasks at this stage are to cope with the threat to past security and sense of competence and self-esteem; deal with feelings of loss and longing for the past.
    • Conservative impulse, the will to change has to overcome an impulse to restore the past, to resist the change. Survival depends on this impulse. By stabilizing perceptions, the conservative impulse enhances the ability to learn from experience. Only when it does not fit this construct is its 'newness' experienced.
  • Readiness refers to what Satir considered the unstable balance of elements in the Status Quo.
    • Persons considering change are more open to consciousness-raising interventions, more aware of themselves, more conscious of their psychic distress, and more likely to seek out information about their problem.
    • Variation in readiness for change (even in one person) depends on social circumstances, timing, opportunity, location, change goal, the degree of social pressure or support for the goal, alternations in environments circumstances, developmental crises, and physical or emotional pain.
  • Four patterns emerge reflecting the relative influence of these readiness factors. These patters are based on the degree of difficulty involved in making the change and the degree of responsibility the person is wiling to take for making the change.
    • Preemptive Change:
      • This pattern describes 'overnight' change. The inordinate strength of the motivating influences lowers the degree of difficulty in making the change. The degree of responsibility for the change is also low.
      • The change in circumstances preempts issues of loss (leaving family and friends), self-efficacy ("Can I do it?"), and self-esteem ("Am I worth the effort?") that are ordinarily addressed in making changes. Problems maintaining the change may require attention at a later point in the change process. Change literally 'happens' to us in this pattern, but we have little sense of ownership of it.
    • Self-Managed Change:
      • In this pattern, the influences motivating the change are less extreme or immediate than those noted in the preemptive patter. The person is influenced to take a conscious and self-responsible approach to change and is required to deal with one or more of the issues of loss, self-efficacy, and self-esteem in order to resolve the resistance to change.

The Juice: Significant Contributions

Communications Stances
  • Five Communication Stances:
    • Congruent (Congruence)
      • Able to acknowledge what you are thinking and feeling, what the other person may be thinking or feeling
    • Placating (Placator)
      • Ignore their own thoughts and feelings and focus on the thoughts and feelings of others. Quick to put aside their own emotions for the sake of others. They often are so quick to deny their own feelings. Your job as a therapist is to help them learn to acknowledge their own thoughts and feelings but also the thoughts and feelings of others.
      • They do not want to increase tension when needs are not met and risk having tension in the relationship.
      • Minimizes self
      • People-pleasing tendencies
      • Needs less directive therapy methods
    • Blamer
      • Assert their needs and desires while minimizing those of others.
      • You will find placator and blamer married in therapy because their stances are complimentary.
      • Your job as a therapist is to help blamer to learn how to acknowledge and develop empathy for the thoughts and needs of others.
      • Minimizes other
      • Needs to increase awareness of others' thoughts and feelings
      • Direct confrontation often strengthens therapeutic relationship (they will find placators as weak)
    • Super-Reasonable (Computer)
      • When they are feeling threated, they revert to the context. Some logical external system. They are typically not emotional.
      • Minimizes self and others
      • Logic and rules reign supreme
      • Therapists must refer to context to gain validity
      • Therapist should understand the logic and/or rules they use to organize their world. Therapists may need to refer to this context in order to gain validity. It is important to understand the logic of the system, but at the same time bringing in recognition and value to their own needs AND the needs and desires of others are valid. Learning how to teach them how to acknowledge all three areas of functioning.
    • Irrelevant (Distractor)
      • Under distress, they distract. The class clown. When tension rises in a relationship, they distract with humor or something off-topic. They may distract. They are more likely to see this in schizophrenia.
      • Your job as a therapist is to get the client reengaged with the difficult topic that they would prefer to just avoid in one way or another.
      • Minimizes self, others, and context.
      • No consistent grounding in self, other, or context
      • Must increase ability to recognize thoughts and feelings of self and others.
      • The focus for the therapist is on creating safety. Help them learn to recognize, and not be afraid of their thoughts and the thoughts of others.
  • Stances acknowledge or minimize three realities:
    • Self
    • Other
    • Context
  • When we feel unsafe, we revert back to one of these communication stances. These are the stances used when someone is frightened. When we don't feel safe as children, we evolve communication stances to survive when we do not feel safe when we are young.
  • You can use the communication stances to help better understand how to intervene, or how to communicate with your client.
    • When you are working with a placator, be cautious if you are going to be making a suggestion it may help to give options. "Which do you think is the best fit or might work for you?" Ask them to give voice to their thoughts and opinions because they will be very quick even to please the therapist. Their task us to learn to assert themselves in a healthy way, how can they assert their needs so that they don't become resentful, depressed (needs do not get taken care of because they are not advocating for themselves)

Making Connection: The Therapeutic Relationship

Assumptions of Satir's Growth Model
  • People naturally tend toward positive growth
  • All people possess the resources for positive growth
  • Everything impacts and is impacted by everything else.
  • Therapy is a process involving interaction between therapist and client; each person is responsible for themselves.
The Therapeutic Relationship
  • Therapeutic presence (warmth and humanity)
  • Making contact
    • Making emotional contact. About real contact with the person at an intimate level. Contacting them in a very intimate, authentic, engaged way.
  • Empathy
  • Conveying hope
  • Establishing credibility
    • You must instill the hope to clients you can be helpful for them.

The Viewing: Case Conceptualization and Assessment

Role of the Symptom
  • Emotional Role of Symptom
    • Symptom has a role in the family system
      • Example:
        • Child's exaggerated acting out may serve to reduce tension in marriage by getting parents on the same page.
    • If the therapist can understand how symptom makes emotional sense, it will be easier to help find ways to interact successfully.
Family Dyanmics
  • Problematic family dynamics typically involve:
    • Power struggles
    • Parental conflicts
    • Lack of validation
    • Lack of intimacy
Family Roles
  • Each person's role in family is assessed to understand the function of the problem.
  • Possible roles:
    • Martyr
    • Victim or helpless one
    • Rescuer
    • Good child/parent
    • Bad child/parent
Family Life Chronology
  • Timeline that includes major events in individual's or family's life.
    • Births and deaths
    • Important family events:
      • Marriages, moves, tragedies, major illnesses, job loss
    • Important historical events:
      • Wars, natural disasters, economic downturns
  • Gives both the therapist and client insight into the context of the problem as well as strengths and resources might exist.
Survival Triad
  • Child, mother, and father and quality of relationship between them.
  • In this primary triad a child learns how to be human.
  • The triad should serve as nurturing system for child.
    • When the child is experiencing difficulty, the therapist considers how the nurturing function of these relationships can be improved.
Six Levels of Experience: The Iceberg
  • Layers of the iceberg:
    • Behavior (Top Layer)
    • Coping (Above)
    • Feelings (Below) that are related to coping
    • Perceptions, how is the person perceiving their life or how do they make meaning?
    • Expectations, what are the expectations that are fueling all of this
    • Yearnings (what are the primal yearnings)
  • Helps clients transform their feelings about feelings to make change.
  • Helps clients observe and evaluate themselves.
Self-Worth and Self-Esteem
  • Considers aspects of the self that the client values and aspects of which they are ashamed
  • Self-Compassion:
    • Better indicator of happiness than self-esteem
  • As self-compassion and self-worth rise,
    • Become more realistic and tolerant of their own and others' weaknesses.
  • What extent does the client value themselves?
Mind-Body Connection
  • How emotional issues may be manifesting in the body, either symbolically or functionally
  • The way the body is used indicates a person's communication stance:
    • Congruent (Congruence) Communication:
      • Open and relaxed body postures
    • Placating (Placator)
      • Timid and reserved postures
    • Super-Reasonable (Computer):
      • Cold and distant postures
    • Irrelevant (Distractor):
      • Hyper and distracted

The Doing: Interventions

Interventions
  • Therapist's self
    • The way the therapist is in the room.
  • Ingredients of an interaction
    • Whenever the therapist is talking with clients about difficult situations and interactions with others, they ask a series of questions. Go back to the iceberg. What do I hear and see? What is happening at the behavioral level without any interpretation. What are the meanings I make of this? What are they saying? What feelings do I have about these meanings I am making? What feelings do I have about those feelings? What defenses do I use/am I using? How does this trigger me and what kind of defensive behaviors might I bring, that makes them . ?
    • What are the rules within the system about how to comment about what's going on? What is allowed and what is appropriate? How am I responding and what part do I play in the system?
  • Facilitating emotional expression
    • What is allowed and what is appropriate? What is my response and what am I doing in this situation?
    • Congruent expression, without the communication stances, turn chairs to each other directly, hold hands, make eye contact
  • Softening family rules
    • When families have rigid rules about who can say what, when, where, and how, she will soften some of these to create more space for everyone's particular way of communicating in a comfortable way to hopefully reduce the use of communication stances.
  • Communication enhancement
    • Helping each member of the family learn to communicate congruently in stressful circumstances, even when they do feel threatened by their partner or their child,
    • Rather than reverting to a survival stance, to help them learn how to be fully congruent and present and to have more effective communications.
  • Sculpting or spatial metaphor
    • She would position each person in the family to metaphorically represent their role. (Example: A placator may be sculpted as being on their knees and begging, the blamer may be there pointing their finger angrily at someone, the irrelevant type might be in a strange position far off from everybody else, the super-reasonable might be sitting there in a very cold, distant, "I am citing the rulebook")
    • Because it is more visual than verbal - you can add a verbal element to it by having each person say a motto of theirs (eg., "Please be nice to me," "Do what I say," "Follow the rules,") - that represents their position in the family. It can push past the survival stances, intellectual defenses, the family could sculpt themselves.
    • It can be a powerful intervention.
  • Touch
    • Touch clients, help clients learn to hold and touch each other in nurturing ways,

  • One can use more than one stance, but usually use one style predominantly.
Placating Stance
  • Acknowledge Other/Context
    • Minimizes self (sometimes context)
    • People-pleasing tendencies
  • Therapeutic Implications:
    • Acknowledge own thoughts, feelings, needs
    • Assertiveness/clear communication of needs
    • Less directive therapy methods
    • Multiple choice options to practice sense of agency
Blaming Stance
  • Acknowledge Self/Context
    • Minimize other
    • Best connect with those who blame by addressing their expectations
  • Therapeutic Implications:
    • Generally prefer more upfront and direct communication
    • Increase blamers' awareness of others' feelings
    • Learn how to communicate in ways that are respectful of others
    • Direct confrontation often strengthens the therapeutic relationship
Super Reasonable Stance
  • Acknowledge context
    • Minimizes feelings/needs of self and others
    • Prioritize a system of 'logic' (could be rational logic, religion doctrine, etc.)
  • Therapeutic Implications:
    • Reference and respect system of logic
    • Help clients to also consider internal, subjective realities of self and others
    • Might engage by starting with their bodily reactions, expectations, practical realities
Irrelevant Stance
  • Avoidance
    • No consistent grounding in self, other, or context
    • Associated with most severe pathology
  • Therapeutic Implications:
    • Creates significant challenge for therapist
    • Identify the unique 'anchors' of the client's reality
    • Make the therapeutic relationship a place of upmost safety; less need for distracting communication
    • Increase client's ability to recognize the thoughts and feelings of self and others
    • Acknowledge the demands of context

    • Model emphasizes that the therapist perturbs the system.
  • 6 stages:
    • Status Quo

Making a Connection: The Therapeutic Relationship

  • Satir's way of being in the world
    • Unshakable hope and deep-felt respect for her clients
  • Satir's presence in the room
    • Put people at ease and allowed them to feel secure enough to relate to one another non-defensively
  • This quality of being, or therapeutic presence, is difficult to define
    • Few methods for systematically developing it
  • More that therapists are congruent, the better they can create therapeutic warmth and humanity

Making Contact

  • Satir et al., (1991) describe establishing a therapeutic relationship as 'making contact'
  • Making Contact:
    • Begins with the therapist being in contact with themselves, including all resources of the self in the self-mandala.
  • Therapist works to make contact with each client in the room, engaging them on 'all channels':
    • Mind
    • Body
    • Spirit
  • Once the therapist has made contact with each person, the therapist works to help family members make contact with each other.

Empathy

  • Convey their understanding of clients' subjective, inner realities by expressing empathy
  • Having empathy does NOT mean that a therapist:
    • Takes the client's side
    • Avoids confronting inconsistencies
    • Ignores the client's responsibility
  • Honoring the client's unique experience without validating it
    • Makes it easier to move the client toward seeing that the other person has their unique experience, which is 'true' for that person

The Iceberg
  • Levels are likened to an iceberg
    • Behavior is the only visible layer
    • Other 5 layers unseen beneath the surface
  • The Iceberg Model:
    • Used to help individuals understand their intrapsychic experience
    • Used for couples to understand how the levels of each person's experience resonate with the other
6 Levels of Experience
  • Behavior
    • External manifestation of the person's inner world
  • Coping
    • Defenses and survival stances (Placating (Placator); Blaming (Blamer); Super-Reasonable (Computer); Irrelevant (Distractor))
  • Feelings
    • Present feelings that are strongly past based
    • Using past events to interpret the present
  • Perceptions
    • Beliefs, attitudes, and values that inform one's sense of self
  • Expectations
    • Strong belief about how life should go, how people should behave, and how one should perform
  • Yearnings
    • Universal longings to be loved, accepted, validated, and confirmed
Self-Worth and Self-Esteem
  • Satir always assessed a person's level of self-esteem
  • More useful to consider:
    • Aspects of the self that a client values
    • Aspects of which client are ashamed
  • Research indicates that self-compassion, or acceptance of one's strengths and weaknesses, is a better indicator of happiness than self-esteem.
  • As self-compassion and self-worth rise, people become more realistic and tolerant of their own and others' weaknesses
Mind-Body Connection
  • Mind-Body Connection
    • How emotional issues may manifest in the body, either symbolically or functionally
  • Also assess the role of nutrition and exercise
    • Example:
      • If a person is feeling burdened, this emotional feeling may manifest symbolically as stooped shoulders.
  • Satir maintained that the way the body is used indicates the person's communication stance
    • Congruent (Congruence) Communication:
      • Open and relaxed body postures
    • Placating (Placator)
      • Timid and reserved postures
    • Super-Reasonable (Computer):
      • Cold and distant postures
    • Irrelevant (Distractor):
      • Hyper and distracted

    • Systemic/family goals
    • Individual goals

Relationally Focused Goals: Congruent Communication

  • Relational Goal:
    • Congruent communication
      • Communicating authentically while responding to the needs of self and others
  • Goal to help the family develop ways for members to communicate so the system's homeostasis does not need the symptoms

Individually Focused Goals: Self-Actualization

  • Overarching individual goal is consistent with other humanistic approaches:
    • To promote self-actualization
  • Self-Actualization:
    • Fulfilling one's potential and living an authentic and meaningful life.
  • Specific Goal Example:
    • Increase sense of self-worth and self-compassion
    • Reduce defensiveness and use of survival stances

The Doing: Interventions

The Use of Self

  • Therapists' use of self
    • Being authentically who they are is one of the most essential interventions in Satir's approach
    • Creates a safe relationship in which clients can practice communicating congruently

Ingredients of an Interaction

  • Ingredients of an interaction:
    • Details the internal communication process
    • Teaches clients about internal and relational processes
  • Ingredient Questions:
    • Used to help people better understand their interactions with others
    • Can be used with individuals, couples, or families
  • Walk the client through the ingredients of an interaction:
    • What do I hear and see?
    • What meanings do I make of what I hear and see?
    • What feelings do I have about the meanings I make?
    • What feelings do I have about these feelings?
    • What defenses do I use?
    • What rules for commenting do I use?
    • What is my response to the situation?

Communication Enhancement: Coaching, Role Play, and Enactment

  • Facilitating Emotional Expression
    • Help put difficult emotions into words
    • Naming emotions
    • Locating in body
    • Metaphor
  • Coaching clients in how to have congruent communication in session
    • May involve the 'ingredients of an interaction' with specific coaching strategies

Family Sculpting

  • Satir's most distinctive intervention
  • Done either with the family or in a group setting
  • Puts family members in physical positions that represent how the 'sculptor' sees each person's role in the family
  • Bypassing Denial/Resistance:
    • Sculpting is a highly effective nonverbal confrontation that bypasses cognitive defenses

Touch

  • Satir used touch in therapy to:
    • Connect with clients
    • Encourage and reassure clients when they were practicing new ways of communicating
  • In today's environment:
    • Touching less common because it can easily be misinterpreted as harassment
  • Carefully consider trauma history, culture, gender, legal/ethical issues of using touch
  • Coaching clients to touch each other

Lights, Camera, Action: Telehealth Applications

  • Experiential therapies can be challenging online.
    • Require attending to emotions and affect, which are communicated non-verbally
  • Requires
    • Using more verbal communication to make up for the loss of non-verbal communication
    • Adapting non-verbal communication
    • Sculpting:
      • With drawings
      • View whole

Tapestry Weaving

  • Experiential approaches value:
    • Clear, congruent, emotional expression
    • Willingness to be vulnerable
  • Cautions
    • Cultural
    • Gender
    • Social Class/Educational
    • Norms for emotional expression vary tremendously
  • May initially be too threatening for clients hwo have been mandated to receive treatment.

  • Cross-Theoretical Comparison

Lay of the Land

  • Integrating systemic theory with humanism, experiential family therapists are noted for their warmth and direct emotional engagement with clients. These approaches include:
    • The Satir Model
    • Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT)
      • EFT integrates experiential, systemic, and attachment theories to help couples and families create emotional safety to foster wellness interaction patterns.
    • Symbolic-Experiential Family Therapy
      • Carl Whitaker developed symbolic-experiential therapy, which focuses on symbolic meanings and emotional exchanges within the family using a balance of warmth and confrontation to promote change.

Shared Assumptions and Practices in Experiential Approaches

Targeting Emotional Transactions

  • Whereas systemic, strategic, structural, and cognitive-behavioral family therapists primarily track behavior interaction sequences, experiential family therapists focus more on the affective or emotional layer of those same interactions - while still attending to behavior and cognitions.
  • Assessment and interventions target the emotional exchanges between family members and significant others in relation to the presenting problem.

Warmth, Empathy, and the Therapist's Use of Self

  • Experiential family therapists use warmth and empathy in building relationships with clients.
  • Therapists use themselves - their personhood - to make this strong affective connection with clients.
  • This approach creates a sense of safety that allows clients to explore areas of emotional vulnerability.

Individual and Family Focus

  • Experiential family therapies affress individual and family concerns as distinct sets of problems.
  • In contrast, systemic, structural, and integenerational therapies conceptualize individual systems as part of the family system. They assume that individual symptoms will resolve if the family system is treated.

The Satir Model

In a Nutshell: The Least You Need to Know

Virginia Satir
  • She began her career in family therapy at the Mental Research Institute (MRI) working alongside Jay Haley, Paul Watzlawick, Richard Fisch, and the other leading family therapists in Palo Alto.
  • Her therapy focused on fostering individual growth as well as improving family interactions.

The Juice: Significant Contributions to the Field

Communication Stances
  • The Communication Stances in the Satir model offer a clinician an efficient and effect means of conceptualizing how best to communicate and interact with the client.
  • She describes 4 communication survival or coping stances:
    • Blaming (Blamer)
    • Placating (Placator)
    • Super-Reasonable (Computer)
    • Irrelevant (Distractor)
  • These stances develop in childhoodand reveal how a person communicates under stress, especially when they do not feel emotionally safe in intimate relationships.
  • A person can use more than one, but typically a person uses one style predominantly.
  • Each stance acknowledges or minimizes three basic realities:
    • Self
    • Other
    • Context
  • The goal is to move people toward Congruent (Congruence) communication in which they respectfully balance the needs of self and others while responding appropriately within and acknowledging the context .
Survival Stances
  • Each survival stance minimizes one or more essential part of the total picture.
Placating (Placator) Stance
  • Because placatory have people-pleasing tendencies, therapists use less directive therapy methods, such as multiple choice questions and open-ended reflections, to require them to voice their opinion and take a stand.
  • Therapists should carefully avoid giving opinions, making it seem that they have an opinion, or offering too much personal information.
    • Clients will use this type of information to know what parts of themselves to hide and what parts to foreground to gain therapist approval.
  • Research indicates that some clients make up things to give the impression that therapy is progressing.
  • Not until the placator regularly and openly disagrees with the therapist has rapport been established.
Blaming (Blamer)
  • Therapists can best connect with those who blame through addressing their expectations. The goal often is to increase blamers' awareness of others' thoughts and feelings and help them learn how to communicate their personal perspectives in ways that are respectful of others.
  • With these clients, direct confrontation often strengthens the therapeutic relationship.
  • Blamers generally prefer more upfront and direct communication than is generally tolerated in polite society.
Super-Reasonable (Computer)
  • When working with those who use a super-reasonable stance, logic and rules reign supreme.
  • Since these clients avoid all emotions, therapists might best engage them by starting with their bodily reactions and expectations before moving onto feelings.
  • The goal with this stance is to help clients value the internal, subjective realities of themselves and others.
Irrelevant (Distractor)
  • A client with the irrelevant stance creates a unique challenge for therapists because there is no consistent grounding in self, other, or context for the therapist to use in understanding and communicating with the client.
  • The therapist must spend time 'floating' along with the client's distractions to identify the unique 'anchors' of the client's reality that the therapist can tap into.
  • Bodily sensations, touch, and physical activities often work best with this client stance.
  • As treatment progresses, the therapist works with these clients to increase their ability to recognize the thoughts and feelings of self and others and to acknowledge the demands of context.
  • Progress is typically slower with those who use this stance frequently.

Rumor Has It: The People and Their Stories

  • Virginia Satir
    • She began her private practice in 1951, and by 1955, she was training therapists at the Illinois Psychiatric Institute to work with families. She then joined MRI in Palo Alto.
    • 1962 was the year she received a grant from the NIMH that provided funding to establish the first family therapy training program.

The Big Picture: Overview of Treatment

  • Satir uses a six-stage model of change that is based on research on Cybernetics systems conducted by Satir while at the MRI.
    • Her six-stage model describes how the therapy process helps families move toward a Second-Order Change in the family structure.
  • The model emphasizes that the therapist perturbs the system (Shaking it up and respecting its ability to naturally reorganize itself in a more useful way) rather than attempting to direct and control the system from the outside.
  • Even though Satir uses education and coaches clients on how to better communicate, the aim is not for clients to follow her instructions literally but rather to adapt and respond to the instructions in a way that works for their system.
  • The six-stages:
    • Status Quo
      • This is a state of homeostasis that includes at least one symptomatic member.
    • Foreign Element
      • May be a life crisis, tragedy, or therapeutic intervention, gets the system off balance.
    • Chaos
      • The new perspective creates a Positive Feedback loop that throws the system into a state of chaos. At this point, the 'natural' response is to feel uncomfortable, and in almost all cases the family tries to regain the Status Quo, which may or may not be possible.
    • Integration of New Possibilities
      • Eventually, the family system interprets the new information in a meaningful way; the therapist needs to be respectful of how the system uses the information and responds to therapist-client interactions, honoring and trusting the system's autonomy.
    • Practice
      • The system develops a new set of interaction patterns based on the new information.
      • This may or may not look like what the therapist expects, but the therapist asks two key evaluative questions:
        • Are the symptoms improving?
        • Is each person able to self-actualize and grow?
    • New Status Quo
      • This is a state of new homeostasis that does not include a symptomatic member and that allows all members to grow and flourish.
  • Therapy involves going through these 6 stages several times, with the discomfort and relative sense of chaos diminishing each time the client cycles through, becoming increasingly comfortable with change.

Making Connections: The Therapeutic Relationship

Humanistic and Systemic Foundations
  • Four primary assumptions about people and therapy:
    • People naturally tend toward positive growth (humanistic principle).
    • All people possess the resources for positive growth (humanistic principle).
    • Every person and every thing or situation impacts and is impacted by everyone and everything else (systemic principle)
    • Therapy is a process that involves interaction between a therapist and a client. In this relationship, each person is responsible for themselves (systemic and humanistic principle).
  • These assumptions clarify the therapist's role in the process (the therapist is 'responsible for themself'), but also how the therapist believes the therapeutic process works:
    • Clients already possess the natural inclination toward growth and the resources for it, and it is the therapist's job to activate these tendencies.
  • The role of the therapist is that of a guide to the process of becoming more fully human.
Therapeutic Presence: Warmth and Humanity
  • Carl Rogers, a leader in humanistic, experiential therapies, based his client-centered approach on three therapist qualities:
    • Congruent (Congruence) or Genuineness
    • Accurate Empathy
    • Unconditional Positive Regard
  • These conditions are the theoretical foundations of warmth and humanity.
    • This quality of being, or therapeutic presnce, is difficult to define, and there are methods for systematically developing it.
    • The more therapists are Congruent (Congruence) - that is being able to communicate authentically while responding to the needs of both self and others - the better they can create the therapeutic warmth and humanity.
Making Contact
  • Satir describes establishing a therapeutic relationship as 'making contact,' which refers to a series of connection both within the therapist and between the therapist and client.
  • Making contact begins with the therapist being in contact with themselves, including all resources of the self as defined in the Self-Mandala: physical, intellectual, emotional, sensual, interactional, nutritional, contextual, and spiritual.
  • The therapist also prepares to meet another 'miracle,' a fellow human being.
  • The therapist then works to make contact with each client in the room, engaging them on 'all channels':
    • Mind
    • Body
    • Spirit
    • Open body position and congruent communication are critical.
  • Once the therapist has made contact with each person, the therapist works to help family members make contact with each other and ultimately with others in their broader social system.
  • The therapy process cannot move forward until the therapist has made contact with the client.
    • When contact is made, clients feel valued for who they are, regardless of their problem, and they feel free to make mistakes in the therapist's presence.
  • Making contact involves:
    • Making direct eye contact
    • Touching clients (e.g., shaking hands)
    • Sitting or standing at the same physical level so that eye contact is easy (i.e., leaning down to talk with children)
    • Asking each person's name and how he or she prefers to be called
Empathy
  • Satir practitioners convey their understanding of clients' subjective, inner realities by expressing Empathy, an accurate understanding of another's emotional reality.
  • Clients like therapists who take their side, don't confront them, and ignore their responsibility in the problem situation. This type of validation is detrimental to therapeutic progress.
  • Comments that imply a client has a right to feel a certain way can also shut down the process of reflecting on the client's responsibility for their half of an interaction.
  • Expressing empathy emphasizes that is not wrong or right, not normal or abnormal, to feel a certain way - it is simply how the client feels and means that this is their 'truth,' at least for the moment (e.g., "It sounds as though you felt betrayed")
    • Honoring the client's unique experience without validating it as right or normal makes it much easier to move the client toward seeing that the other person has their unique experience that is also 'true' for that person, creating a context from which to understand how these two (or more) realities may collide and create problematic interactions.
Conveying Hope
  • In joining with clients, therapists instill hope that they can change and that the situation can be better, even if it seems hopeless at the moment.
  • Satir et al., (1991) emphasize that clients must have faith that change is possible before they can move forward with treatment.
Establishing Credibility
  • Clients need to believe that therapy and their therapist can help them resolve their problems.
  • Therapists establish credibility by making a personal connection (making contact) and by being confident and competent (modeling high self-esteem).

The Viewing: Case Conceptualization and Assessment

  • Both family functioning and individual functioning are assessed.
Assessment of Family Functioning
  • Role of the Symptom in the System
    • Describe how the symptom maintains the family's homeostasis at an emotional level, often regulating closeness and distance (Distance Regulation; Differentiation of Self)
  • Family Dynamics
    • Identify salient dynamics
      • Power struggles
      • Parental conflict
      • Lack of validation
      • Lack of intimacy
  • Family Roles
    • Identify possible family roles.
      • Martyr
      • Victim or Helpless One
      • Rescuer
      • Good Child or Parent
      • Bad Child or Parent
  • Family Life Chronology
    • Describe key historical events, deaths, births, divorces, major life changes.
  • Survival Triad
    • Describe the emotional and nurturing relationships between each child and parents.
