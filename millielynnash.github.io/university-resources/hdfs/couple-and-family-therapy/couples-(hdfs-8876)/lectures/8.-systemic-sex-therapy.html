8. Systemic Sex Therapy

  • Weeks et al., (Chapters 1, 6-13, and 16)
  • These techniques are not for the usual clients with sexual discrepancies (i.e., one client has a higher libido than the other). It is for individuals that struggle with sexual disorders/dysfunctions.

Sex Therapy Homework and Other Techniques

At-Home Assignments

  • Therapist directed or structured.
    • You can collaborate, but the therapist is not coming up with their own examples.
  • Related to practicing activities learned in sessions and other behaviors that are carefully crafted and explained during the session.
  • Expected to facilitate a therapeutic effect.
  • Need to be practical.
  • Practiced in small increments toward a goal (typically not erection, penetration, or orgasm).
  • Major goal is to reduce anxiety and reinforce new behavior.
  • Need to be relevant to the issues, and within the ability of the client or couple.
  • Make the assignment (perhaps in writing as well) and then work with the couple and what they think about the assignment, ay questions they have and any fears ,, they have about starting the assignment.
  • Collaborate so that the clients are invested and the assignment is tailored to them.
  • Negotiate the schedule, who initiates, and make sure they clearly understand what they are supposed to do.
  • Process the assignment the next session, what worked, what didn't, etc. Access effort.

Sensate Focus and Mindfulness

  • Incorporates psychoeducation about the sexual response and a series of incremental physical exercises performed outside of the therapeutic setting.
  • Viewed as a form of desensitization intended to promote relaxation, communication and attention to physical sensations of touch, and the physical emotional connections between partners.
  • Combined with mindfulness (being present in the moment, not thinking about past or future; making no judgements, not spectating). Authors use a more systemic approach to sensate focus then originally designed. They include both partners, and create smaller increments of behaviors moving toward genital touch then Masters and Johnson.

Sensate Focus

  • 9 functions of sensate focus:
    1. Become more aware of sensations
    2. Focus solely on one's own needs for pleasure
    3. Communicate sensual and sexual needs, wishes and desires
    4. Increase awareness of partner's sensual and sexual needs
    5. Expand the repertoire of intimate touch, sensual and sexual behaviors
    6. Learn to appreciate foreplay or touch as an end in itself rather than a means to an end
    7. Create positive relationship experiences
    8. Build sexual desire
    9. Enhance the level of love, caring, commitment, intimacy cooperation, and sexual interest in the relationship

Readiness

  • Describe what is involved, noting each partner's reaction (see questions on page 183)
    • What is 'good enough' sex?
  • Go slowly if one or both partners express hesitation or ambivalence or simple refusal.

Structure of the Exercises

  • Tailored to the specific needs of the couple.
  • The 'goal' of the exercise is the touch and the experience of the sensations, not erection, orgasm, penetration, etc.
  • Specific assignments should occur weekly, so assignments can be reviewed and revised. Remind couples that this is a chance to rebuild physical and emotional intimacy and there is no hurry.
  • Couple is instructed to avoid intercourse and any activity that is sexual. The idea is to create a non-demand atmosphere. (reducing performance anxiety, increasing pleasurable encounters).
  • Create a sensual environment.
  • Partners are both givers and receivers (regardless of the Identified Patient (IP))
  • Gradually build, first touch in general, no genital touch, with and without clothing to focus on sensations from touch, coach them to talk about what they like and what they want/need. During each session the giver listens to the receiver. The receiver progresses to being present (mindful) during the exercise only.
  • Review the assignment at the next session (questions on page 187)
  • Once the couple is comfortable with nonsexual touch, move to erotic and genital touch, again in small increments. The goal is for the couple to experience sexual stimulation as a goal in itself.
  • If desired, then the couple can move to intercourse (no pressure). This can also be done in gradual steps.
    • Use exercises listed as examples for specific disorders.

Other notes

  • Using the Miracle Question to distill down the preferred narrative.
  • CBCT is a good example to help couples. Couples need to focus on emotions rather than logical thoughts that keep them stuck in their cycle.

Factors that can Complicate Sex Therapy

  • Sexual Ignorance:
    • Performance-based, goal-oriented model of normal is damaging to sexual intimacy.
  • Sexual Mythology:
    • Misguided beliefs, misconceptions, and distortions that promote unrealistic standards and rigid expectations.
  • Faculty Cognitions:
    • These can be engrained and automatic. They then can interfere with sexual desire, and limit a range of sexual behaviors (i.e., masturbation).
  • Sexual guilt, anxiety, and shame

Intervening

  • Counter Faulty Beliefs
    • Psychoeducation
    • CBT Techniques
  • Dealing with Intimacy Fears
    • Underlying and unconscious fears of intimacy can complicate sex therapy (do couple work first)
    • Distance regulation (fears of rejection/abandonment, dependency, anger, emotion, losing control).

Physical/Medical Issues

  • Sex therapists need to be collaborators with medical practitioners in the treatment of medically related sexual issues and cognitive disabilities
    • Physical Illness:
      • Sexual functioning involves many body systems. Thus, illness can impact sexual functioning. It is best to research the illness and what body systems are impacted by it (it is systemic) before deciding on a course of treatment.
      • Neurological Ailments (affect physical)
