1. Philosophy of Change Framework

Families as Systems

Systems

  • Parts in interaction such that the whole is greater than the sum of its parts.
  • University Resources/Couple and Family Therapy/Keywords/Open System (i.e. human systems) exchange matter/energy with the environment (for Family Systems and our purposes this in information)
  • University Resources/Couple and Family Therapy/Keywords/Open System s have to have monitoring functions that allow them to pay attention to what is important in their environments (i.e. feedback)
  • If they have monitoring functions then there needs to be increasing complexity of rules about how to handle the feedback, and when to change in order to continue to grow and adapt to both internal and external sources of information (Rule about interaction, rules about changing the rule of interaction, and rules about change rules about changing the rules of interaction, etc.)
  • All interaction is fed into the system and becomes both input and output, with a constant flow of information to these monitoring functions, with decisions made to either assimilate or accommodate.
  • Systems are goal directed, and purposeful using both negative (deviation dampening) and positive (deviation amplifying) feedback.

Concepts:

  • System Complexity:
    • An increase in the autonomy of the component parts and an increase in the extent to which the system or its parts determines system behavior (parts of not simply reactive to input from the environment).
  • Requisite Variety:
    • A wide range and quantity of varied information, experience and input into the system that is somehow stored for future use.
  • Morphostasis:
    • As the system assesses information from internal and external interactions it may 'choose' to maintain itself in the same way, however because of the new information it has to change in some way to stay the same (assimilation).
  • University Resources/Couple and Family Therapy/Keywords/Morphogenesis:
    • As the system assesses information from internal and external interactions it may 'choose' to change in order to accommodate to the new information.
  • These processes are simultaneous and constant as the system adapts and also maintains its integrity.

Adaptation:

  • When continuity and the need to accommodate to change become integrated in the system then the system is flexible and stable at the same time.
  • When that is happening we would say the system is 'effectively' functioning and capable of growth and change.

Propositions:

  • Behaviors and interaction patterns will not be repeated unless they serve the system in some way.
  • If a pattern of interaction is repeated it is serving the system in some way (symptoms serve a function).

Theory Critique Framework:

  • How do families develop?

Symptoms

  • Symptoms:

  • What is the focus of the intervention?
