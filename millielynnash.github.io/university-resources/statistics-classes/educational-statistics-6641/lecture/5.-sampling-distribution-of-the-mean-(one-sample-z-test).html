Inference of the Mean

Inferential Statistics

  • Here we begin to move from descriptive statistics to inferential statistics.
  • After collecting sample data and generating sample statistics, we may also want to know to what extend we can generalize from these sample statistics to their corresponding population parameters (e.g., What does x tell us about ¬µ?)
  • We already understand how probability and distribution(s) work. Now we want to know (a) how much uncertainty exists in our sample statistics, and (b) how much confidence can we place in our sample statistics.
  • Inferential statistics allow us to make an inference or generalization from a sample to the population.

Sampling Distribution of the Mean

  • In our Gardyn pricing experiment, we took random samples of size n = 4 students and recorded each sample's mean x1, x2, x3,‚Ä¶ and many other samples.
  • Each sample mean is a point estimate of the population mean.
  • We can construct a frequency distribution of sample means, known as the sampling distribution of the mean. It is the distribution of x for all possible samples of size n.

Standard Error of the Mean

  • The mean of the sampling distribution of the mean (¬µx) is the estimate of population mean (i.e., ¬µx = ¬µ).
  • Sampling error (x - ¬µx): the difference (deviation) between a particular sample mean and the population mean.
  • Variance error of the mean (ùúé 2/x): the variance of the sampling distribution of the mean.
  • Standard error of the mean (ùúéx): the standard deviation of the sampling distribution of the mean.

Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

  • As sample size n increases (say 30), the sampling distribution of the mean from a random sample more closely approximates a normal distribution.
    • If the population distribution is normal, then the sampling distribution of the mean is also normal in shape.
    • If the population distribution is not normal, then the sampling distribution of the mean becomes more nearly normal as sample size increases ('approximately normal')
  • In reality, we'd only draw sample once and observe one sample mean x (i.e., ùúéx is not calculated from various samples). According to CLT, we can use x to estimate ¬µ with the standard error of the mean ùúéx = (ùúé √∑ ‚àön)

CLT: Sample Means Approximately Normally Distributed

Why is it Important?

  • CLT tells us the theoretical distribution of the sample mean x so we don't have to take hundreds of samples, find their mean and variance or create a histogram in order to use this information!
  • We can create a confidence interval (CI) for the mean:
    • Rather than a point estimate for a population parameter, we can construct an interval estimate (i.e., the likely range capturing the population parameter).
  • Hypothesis tests about the mean. So we can make a decision about a parameter based on sample data.
  • Allow us to move from descriptive statistics to inferential statistics.

Example: Physical Performance Test (Ohio Sample)

  • Scores on a physical performance test for middle school students have been normed nationally for all students with a mean ¬µ = 175 and a standard deviation ùúé = 12. We took a sample of n = 120 students in Ohio found that the sample mean x = 162.
    • Ohio population might be different from national norm so x = 162 deviates from ¬µ = 175.
  • Suppose we'd like to estimate the population mean for Ohio based on this sample, what is the 95% confidence interval (CI)? Report the lower confidence limit (LCL) and the upper confidence limit (UCL).
  • Population ¬µ = 175, ùúé = 12, Ohio Sample x = 162, n = 120

Hypothesis Testing

  • Hypothesis testing is a decision-making process where two possible decisions are weighed in a statistical fashion. Hypotheses are written for each decision and then sample data are used to help make the decision.
  • Here we examine a statistical test to make inferences about a single mean with a known population standard deviation (ùúé):
    • One-sample t-test (6. One-Sample T-Test, Power)
    • Two samples, paired data: Paired (7. Paired-Sample T-Test)
    • Two samples, independent groups: Independent t-test (8. Independent T-Test, Homogeneity of Variance)

Types of Hypotheses

  • Null hypothesis (H0):
    • A statement about the value of an unknown population parameter. There is no difference or a 'null' difference between the population and hypothesized means (statistical hypothesis, chance model).
  • Alternative Hypothesis (H1 or Ha):
    • The opposite of H0. The scientific hypothesis that the researcher believes the outcome of the study will be, based on previous research and theory (research hypothesis, your theory).
  • Hypothesis testing in a nutshell:
    • Compare sample statistics to hypothesized distribution. If the data evidence is extreme, we can reject the null hypothesis and conclude that the event did not happen by chance. Instead, our theory is supported by data.
  1. Set up hypotheses based on the research question.
  2. Where does the sample/observed mean lie on the sampling distribution?
  3. Given the confidence level 100 (1 - ùõº)%, what is considered extreme evidence?
  4. Compared to the critical value is the sample mean extreme enough to be considered different from ùúá0?

Level of Significance (ùõº)

  • Probability of Type I Error (what we want to avoid in decision making).
  • Determine how far the sample mean needs to be from the hypothesized mean in order to reject H0.
  • Set by the researcher or discipline, but the most commonly used values in the behavioral sciences and education are:
    • ùõº = .05 (wrong 5% of the time, 95% confidence level)
    • ùõº = .01 (wrong 1% of the time, 99% confidence interval)
  • A statistician is a person whose lifetime ambition is to be wrong 5% of the time.

Nondirectional or Directional

  • Nondirectional (two-tailed): to test whether the population mean (ùúá) from which we drew sample equals a specific hypothesized value (ùúá0).
    • Critical region can be either right (upper) or left (lower) tail.
    • H0: ùúá = ùúá0
    • H1: ùúá ‚â† ùúá0
  • Directional (one-tailed): To test whether the population mean (ùúá) from which we drew sample is significantly greater or less than a specific hypothesized value (ùúá0).
    • Right (upper) tail (test if greater):
      • H0: ùúá ‚â§ ùúá0 vs. H1: ùúá > ùúá0
    • Left (lower) tail (test if less):
      • H0: ùúá ‚â• ùúá0 vs. H1: ùúá < ùúá0

One-Sample z-Test

  • Used when population ùúé is known, to test if sample is drawn from the same population (ùúá0: hypothesized mean).
  • Consider the research question and decide nondirectional or directional.
  • Set up null and alternative hypotheses.
  • Calculate observed z-score from sample statistics. Recall:
    • zobs = (x bar - ùúá0) √∑ (ùúé / ‚àöùëõ)
  • Given confidence level and direction, find the critical z-score and region from table.
  • Compare zobs to zcrit and reject H0 if zobs is in the critical region.

Example: Physical Performance Test (Ohio Sample)

  • Population ùúá = 175, ùúé = 12, Ohio sample x bar = 162, n = 120
  • Nondirectional (two-tailed) so the hypotheses are:
    • H0: ùúá = 175
    • H1: ùúá ‚â† 175
  • Calculate observed z-score:
    • zobs = (x bar - ùúá0) √∑ (ùúé / ‚àöùëõ)
    • = (162-175) √∑ (12 / ‚àö120) ‚âà -11.87
  • Since ùõº = .05 and two-tailed (each tail is 0.25), look for z with area .0250:
    • zobs = -1.96
  • Critical (rejection) region:
    • Reject H0 if zobs < -1.96 (or zobs > 1.96)
  • Since zobs < -1.96, reject H0, indicating Ohio mean (162) is significantly different from national mean (175).

p-value (P-Value)

  • Probability of obtaining a test statistic at least as extreme as the one observed. Visually, it is the area more extreme (same direction of critical region) than observed statistic.
  • Therefore, comparing p-value to ùõº will reach the same conclusion as comparing observed and critical statistics. Reject H0 if p-value < ùõº (i.e., sample stats in rejection region).
    • ùõº is usually .05 (95% confidence level).
  • Notice p-values remain the same for tests with different ùõº levels, but p-value from a non-directional (two-tailed) test is 2 times larger than pvalue from a directional (one-tailed) test.
  • For extreme cases p-values can be very close to 0, but theoretically p-values cannot equal 0. Therefore, report p < .001 (NEVER report p = 0)
  • Physical performance in Ohio: Look up ùëßùëúùëèùë† (-11.87) on z-table, p-value is very small!
    • Since ùëù < .001, reject H0 at 95% confidence level. (NEVER report ùëù = 0.)
  • Physical performance in Colorado: Look up ùëßùëúùëèùë† (1.49) on z-table, p-value = 2 √ó (1 ‚àí .9319) = .1362
    • Since ùëù = .1362 (ùõº > .05), cannot reject H0 at 95% confidence level.
  • Vocational program: Look up ùëßùëúùëèùë† (1.70) on z-table, p-value = 1 ‚àí .9554 = .0446

    • Notice this test would have been non-significant if it was non-directional (ùëù = 2 √ó .0446 = .0892, which would have been greater than .05).

Recap: Three Ways to Reject H0

  • The idea is to compare sample statistics (say x bar) to the theorized distribution (say z). Reject H0 if data (zobs) shows extreme evidence (zcrit determined by ùõº level, usually .05). Three ways to reject H0 (one-sample z-test):
    • The confidence interval constructed based on x bar does not cover ùúá0.
    • zobs > zcrit (or negative values, zobs < zcrit)
    • p < ùõº (usually .05)

Effect Size

  • Hypothesis testing confirms if there is a significant effect (H0 rejected), but it does not tell us how large the effect is.
  • The effect size tells us how large the effect is (i.e., how meaningful/practical the effect).
  • Different statistics have been developed to report effect size for different statistical analysis.

  • For one-sample z-test (population ùúé known), we use Cohen's d to report effect size.

  • Size of Cohen's d (by convention):

    • d = ¬± 0.5: Medium effect
