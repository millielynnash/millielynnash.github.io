9. Sexuality and LGBTQ+ Persons in Therapy

Gender in Family Therapy

The Early Years:

  • Gender was invisible, implicitly binary, sexist, racist, and White cis-men were the standard.

Late 1970 s

  • White feminist lens (i.e., White binary, heterosexual perspective, Patriarchy, and sexism)

21 st Century

  • Postmodern approach to gender (still binary and biologically determined)

Hare-Mustin (1987): The problem of gender in family therapy theory

  • Fmaily therapy theory rests on normative concepts of the traditional family and idealized conceptions of family relationships. Inequalities in the family associated with gender have been regarded as of little importance to the development of macro theory in the field. The two most influential therapeutic models, psychodynamic and systemic approaches, are each marked by gender bias.

Feminist Family Therapy

  • Feminist therapy is an integrative approach to psychotherapy that focused on the challenges that women face as a result of bias, stereotyping, oppression, discrimination, and other factors, and how those stressors can negatively affect their mental health.
  • 'Feminist Family Therapy is the application of feminist theory and values to family therapy.'

7 General Principles

  1. Acknowledge and examine the roles of values in therapy for both the therapist and the client (s).
  2. Intentionally introduce a discussion of gender into the therapy process using clinical language and techinques that acknowledge experiences across genders.
  3. Assess power inequalities in relationships rather than assuming equity in relationships.
  4. Include discussion of individual choice and responsibility in conceptualizations of family dynamics.
  5. Honor and appreciate the diversity inherent to families.
  6. Consider the clinical implications of interventions on both the family and on individual members of the family.
  7. Work actively to reduce power inequalities between therapist and client. (Leslie & Southard 2009)

Intersectional Feminism and Family Therapy

  • According to intersectional feminists, there is not enough emphasis on the intersection of patriarchy with other factors, such as race, religion, and class.
  • Intersectional feminists argue that other types of feminism assume that all women are exploited in the same way in the family.

Guidelines for Exploring Gender in Clinical

  • Self of the therapist
  • Locating the self
  • Locating the client
  • Unpacking gender stories in context
  • Examining the use of power
  • Seeking affirmation
  • Reclaiming self and relationships

Working with LGBTQIA Persons in Therapy

AAMFT Guidelines for LGBTQIA Affirming C/MFT

  • Pillar 1: Intersectional
  • Pillar 2: Systemic
  • Pillar 3: Relational
  • Pillar 4: Liberatory
  • Pillar 5: Transformative

Affirming Intake Process

  • Introduce yourself and pronouns and asking for your client/patient's name and pronouns
  • Gather client/patient information using inclusive language
  • Avoid assumptions around identity, body parts, and sexual practices
  • Gather biopsychosocial/sexual history information
  • End the session by thanking the patient

Practitioner Skills when Working with TGDNB Young People

  • Keeley (2022). Integrative Family Therapy with Transgender, Gender Diverse, and Non-binary (TGDNB) Young People

Actions that Promote a Therapeutic Relationship

  • Read peer-reviewed published literature to provide best support and understand terminology.
  • Recognize the family as a key protective factor in mental health outcomes of TGNDB children and work with the whole system.
  • Use the pronouns and name the young person requests you use. This may include checking in at each session and using a different name and pronoun when their family is present if this is more comfortable for them.
  • Explore the power in the system and the political climate in which you are seeing a TGNDB person.
    • Example: Be aware of news articles or the area the person lives in to check about transphobic experiences and/or discrimination
  • Connect TGDNB young people to community and peers to build their sense of connection and reduce stigma and isolation
    • Therapist will need to each out to local services and be aware of local networks
    • This can include online community connection

Lesbian, Gay, and Bisexual Identity Scale (LGBIS)

  • The lesbian, gay, and bisexual identity scale (LGBIS) is a 27-item measure designed to assess eight dimensions of lesbian, gay, and bisexual (LGB) identity that have been discussed in clinical and theoretical literature.
  • The development of the LGBIS is fully described by Mohr and Kendra (2011)

LGBIS Negative Identity Subscales

  • Accepting Concerns:
    • Worry relating to potential rejection of one's sexual identity
  • Concealment Motivation:
    • Being concerned with protecting one's sexual identity
  • Identity Uncertainty:
    • How uncertain one is concerning their sexual identity.
  • Internalized Homonegativity:
    • The degree to which to which one rejects their sexual identity.
  • Difficult Process:
    • How difficult one's sexual identity develop was

LGBIS Positive Identity Subscales

  • Identity Superiority:
    • How favorable one views sexual minorities compared to heterosexual people
  • Identity Affirmation:
    • The association of positive thoughts with one's sexual identity
  • Identity Centrality:
    • How central one's sexual identity is to one's overall identity
