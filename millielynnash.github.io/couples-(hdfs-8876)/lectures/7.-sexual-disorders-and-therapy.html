Diagnoses and Assessment for Sexual Disorders

DSM-5 Sexual Disorders

  • A heterogeneous group of disorders that are characterized by:
    • A clinically significant disturbance in a person's ability to respond sexually or experience sexual pleasure
  • Three female sexual disorders:
    • Female Sexual Interest/Arousal Disorder
    • Female Orgasmic Disorder
    • Genito-Pelvic Pain/Penetration Disorder (GPPPD)
  • Four Male Sexual Disorders:
    • Male Hypoactive Sexual Desire Disorder
    • Erectile Disorder
    • Delayed Ejaculation
    • Premature Ejaculation
  • Other categories:
    • Substance/Medication-Induced Sexual Dysfunction
    • Other Specified Sexual Dysfunction (includes distressing sexual symptoms but does not meet the criteria for a specific disorder with reasons why)
    • Unspecified Sexual Dysfunction (same as above with no reasons why)
  • Specifiers
    • Lifelong vs. Acquired
    • Generalized vs. Situational
    • Severity of Distress

Etiology

  • Individual/Biological
  • Individual/Psychological
  • The Dyadic Relationship
  • Family of Origin Influences
  • Contextual Factors (Society/Culture/History/Religion etc.)

Diagnoses in DSM-5 (Female)

  • Female Sexual Interest (Arousal Disorder):
    • Absent/reduced interest in sexual activity
    • Absent/reduced sexual/erotic thoughts or fantasies
    • No/reduced initiation of sexual activity
    • Typically unreceptive to a partner's attempt to initiate
    • Absent/reduced sexual excitement/pleasure during sexual activity in all or almost all (75%-100%) sexual encounters
    • Absent/reduced sexual interest/arousal in response to any internal or external sexual/erotic cues
    • Absent/reduced genital or non-genital sensations during sexual activity in all or almost all (75%-100%) sexual encounters
    • The changes to this definition (from previous DSM version) was in recognition that sexual desire is less spontaneous for women than men, and more related to contextual and relational variables. Authors believed that this helped to depathologize women for the lack of so-called spontaneous desire, especially because these symptoms need to be present for at least 6 months to render this diagnosis.
  • Female Orgasmic Disorder:
    • Marked delay in, marked infrequency of or absence of orgasm or markedly reduced intensity or orgasmic sensations (essential to assess for levels of distress around this).
  • Genito-Pelvic Pain (Penetration Disorder) (GPPPD):
    • Vaginal penetration during intercourse
    • Marked vulvovaginal or pelvic pain during vaginal intercourse or penetration attempts marked fear or anxiety about vulvovaginal or pelvic pain in anticipation of, during, or as a result of vaginal penetration.
    • Marked tensing or tightening of the pelvic floor muscles during attempted vaginal penetration.
    • Important to explore all the factors that may be at play here (physiological, cognitive, emotional, behavioral and interpersonal, including past trauma).

Diagnoses in DSM-5 (Male)

  • Male Hypoactive Sexual Desire Disorder:
    • Male must present with persistently or recurrent deficient (or absent) sexual/erotic thoughts or fantasies and desire for sexual activity.
    • Not much known about this, and it rarely the primary presenting concern.
  • Erectile Disorder (at least one of 3)
    • Marked difficulty in obtaining an erection during sexual activity.
    • Marked difficulty in maintainig an erection until completion of sexual activity.
    • Marked decrease in erectil rigidity.
    • Prevalence increases with age and other disease states. Medical screenings are essential when working with clients with this condition.
  • Premature Ejaculation
    • A persistent or recurrent pattern of ejaculation occurring during partnered sexual activity within approximately 1-minute following vaginal penetration and before the individual wishes it.
    • Symptoms of erectile disfunction and premature ejaculation are often co-occurring. Performance anxiety is often found with either disorder.
  • Delayed Ejaculation:
    • Marked delay in ejaculation or marked infrequency or absence of ejaculation and the individual must desire ejaculation.

Disorder vs. Problem

  • It is probably more common to see couples who do not have diagnosable disorders but have complaints about the sexual aspects of their relationships.
  • The most common problem is desire discrepancy.
  • Some conditions are currently being considered for inclusion in the DSM:
    • Sexual Compulsivity, Addition, or Hypersexuality
    • Gender Non-Conformity (DSM-5 less restrictive, some things that would automatically mean a disorder are now only a disorder if there is distress or impairment around the activity)
    • Persistent Genital Arousal Disorder (for females, unrelenting genital arousal that does not diminish with orgasm)
    • Sexual Aversion Disorder (no longer listed as a sexual disorder, but as a phobia)
    • Male Dyspareunia (men who had persistent pain with sexual activity; could be as simple as an infection, or more complex floor abnormalities).

Intersystem Assessment

  • More comprehensive than talking about symptoms alone. Include the following:
    • The couple's perception of the problem (s)
    • Current baseline of individual and relational functioning
    • Physical status of each partner
    • The couple's expectations or goals for the treatment
    • Realistic treatment goals
    • Level of sexual desire of each partner
    • Perceived degree of sexual satisfaction
    • Level of distress experienced because of the sexual problem
    • Impact on the intimate relationship
    • The functions of sex in the relationship
  • Get a sexual history from each partner (can be questionnaires or through interviewing, authors suggest conjoint interviewing to develop alliance and watch the process, they recommend flexible use of conjoint and individual sessions).
  • Authors recommend that therapists understand their own sexual so that they can avoid inadvertently or overtly shaming. Use more open-ended questions and be careful about the words you use (i.e., have you been unfaithful? Vs. Do you have intimate relations with others in addition to your primary partner?)
  • Adopt a stance of both never assuming and always assuming:
    • Never assume a client's sexual orientation
    • Always assume that everyone does everything
    • Never assume that clients are educated about all aspects of sexuality
  • Book provides lots of suggestions for how to ask questions to get at specific diagnoses and questions to ask when taking a sexual history.

The Sexual Genogram

  • Focus on gender, romantic love, attachment, intimacy, communication, gender orientation, and other themes associated with sexuality (provides questions to ask to complete the genogram)
    • Beliefs about being a man or a woman
    • Family patterns of relationships
    • Couple relationship patterns (affairs, divorces, abandonment, loyalty and sacrifice)
    • Family impact on current gender behavior
    • Peer expectations
    • Cultural expectations

Measures of Sexual Function and Dysfunction

  • Assessment in sex therapy is essential for determining a treatment plan (like all therapy)
  • There are formal assessment instruments but they were mostly designed for previous DSM versions, not the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM) -5, they can still be used but may not be as useful for diagnosis.
  • Behavioral assessment (i.e., similar to BCT)
  • Physiological measures (scores for respondent's perceptions of satisfaction with, or pain during phases of the sexual response cycle).
