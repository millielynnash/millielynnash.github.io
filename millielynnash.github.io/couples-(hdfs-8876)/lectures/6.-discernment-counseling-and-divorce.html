Discernment

Different than Couple Therapy

  • Goals for Discernment Counseling:
    • Clarity and confidence about a direction for the marriage based on a deeper understanding of what has happened to the marriage, and each person's contributions to the problems.
  • Discernment counseling ends with a decision to take one of three paths:
    • Couple therapy
    • Divorce
    • Status Quo

Mixed-Agenda Couples:

  • Could be up to 30% of couples who come to therapy, but our models and techniques do not prepare us for these types of couples (one is leaning in the other leaning out).
  • These types of couples are difficult to treat because of their conflicting goals for therapy. One to say it is worth investing in the relationship and we can fix the problems, the other perhaps to prove that we have done all we can it is time to split.
  • Doherty believed that before trying to do any sort of couple work, these couples needed to decide if that is what they wanted.

Overview

  • Short term processes: 1-5 sessions
  • Goal is to get a deeper understanding of what has happened in the relationship and each partner's contributions to the problems.
  • Three paths:
    • Status quo
    • Divorce
    • Therapy

Flow of Sessions

  1. Couple together
  2. One spouse separately
  3. Summary to the other spouse
  4. One spouse separately
  5. Summary to the other spouse
  6. Couple together

Research

  • Not much research on Mixed-Agenda Couples couples and how to work with them. Most work suggests that Mixed-Agenda Couples couples are common (about 30%). Outcome research is sparse but suggest that these couples when treated with couple therapy are more likely to separate.
  • Also evidence that people going through the divorce process believe the marriage could be saved (about 26%) and are interested in reconciliation services (about 33%).
  • Lots of evidence of ambivalence about the divorce and the relationship.
  • About 66.2% of folks in the divorce process were sure they wanted the divorce, 16.5% had mixed feelings, and 9.8% would consider reconciliation.
  • Conclusion:
    • We need some way of providing services to folks considering divorce to help them with their mixed feelings and decide on a path.

Evaluation of Discernment Counseling

  • 100 cases evaluated
    • 97 heterosexual couples, 3 same-sex
    • Married an average of 15.5 years
    • Highly distressed based on the DAS short form
    • 51% had consulted a divorce attorney
    • Well educated
    • 48% came based on self-referral, 34% from therapists or social service agencies, 11% from attorneys or judges.
  • Findings:
    • Average number of sessions 3.61
    • 47% decided on path 3 (therapy), 41% separation/divorce, and 12% status quo.
    • Contact with a divorce attorney associated with a lower likelihood of path 3, and higher likelihood of path 2.
    • At follow-up, those who chose path 2:
      • 90% were divorced or in the process, one couple reconciled, and 3 were on hold.
    • Those who chose path 3:
      • 36% reconciled, 6% in process, 28% had divorced, 17% were in process of divorce, and 13% were on hold.

Introduction the Process

  • Ask what they understand about discernment counseling.
  • For the leaning-out partner:
    • Make sure they feel heard and that they understand the process of discernment and that it is different than couple therapy then ask if they are still seeking some sort of clarity on the process of divorce, and how confident they are in their decision.
  • For the leaning-in partner:
    • They recommend they read 'The Divorce Remedy by Michele Weiner-Davis', which provides advice for folks who have just found out their partner wants a divorce.
  • Recommended that you contact both partners separately for these intake conversations.

Session 1

  • Block out 2 hours for the first session.
  • Meet together to ask questions, meet with each partner separately, provide summaries to the partners after each individual session, then come back together to summarize the set of sessions, therapist's impressions of the couple, then if both partners agree, schedule the second session.
  • Questions for both:
    • What happened to your marriage that's gotten you to the point where divorce is a possibility? Can you tell me what you have done to try to fix the problems in the marriage, whether on your own or with help?
    • What role, if any, do your children play in your decision making about the future of your relationship?
    • Can you tell me about a time in your relationship, since the time you met, when you felt the most connection and joy?
  • Questions for Leaning-Out partner (see this person first):
    • Tell me more about your pain and reasons for considering divorce.
    • (Within no more than 10 minutes) switch to a conversation about the paths.
    • Find out how far they have gone in formalizing divorce plans (contacted attorney, papers drawn up, etc.)
    • Ask them their perspective on their contributions to the problems in the marriage.
    • Prepare a summary statement for the other partner about something they have taken away from the individual time, then invite partner 2 in the room to hear the summary statement.
  • Now work with the leaning-in partner:
    • Help them articulate that they understand their partner's concerns and can understand why their partner might be interested in divorce.
    • Ask about their perspective on their own contributions to the problems in the relationship.
    • Help them figure out how to respond without being too anxious or withdrawing completely.
    • Summary statement:
      • Must convey the sense that the leaning-in partner understands why their partner is contemplating divorce, and some empathy for this position.
    • Once both people are in the room again, and the leaning-in partner has shared their summary statement, then the therapist shares their observations.
      • What themes have you noticed, and your perception of how they got to this place.
      • Couple does not have to agree on a path at the end of this session, but they may, they recommend that the couple return for at least a 2 nd discernment session before deciding, especially Path 3.

Middle Sessions

  • Goals:
    • Clarity
    • Confidence
    • More understanding
  • 90-minute sessions:
    • Time together
    • Time separately with summaries
    • Time together wrap-up
  • Check-in:
    • 3 goals:
      • Learning where each partner is in their discernment process
      • Reminding partners of the goals of discernment
      • Acoiding an open-ended invitation to talk about the relationship problems.
  • Leaning-out partner:
    • Move to help them see their contributions
  • Leaning-In partner:
    • Move to help them develop a plan for change
  • Discernment is not the same for both partners, leaning in partner carries a bigger burden of leadership about what they may need to change in order for the marriage to be more viable.
  • Summaries should become more self-revealing and intense, therapist feedback can also be more in-depth in terms of positives and negatives.

Choosing Path 3

  • Need to prepare partners for the work that will need to be done in therapy.
  • If you are going to be the couple therapist have a transition session possibly using the Agreement to Pursue Reconciliation Form (pg. 103).
  • Generate personal agendas for change (pg. 105) and have them share with each other.

Choosing Path 2

  • Explore the divorce decision:
    • Freedom
    • Relief
    • Painful letting go
  • Deal with the emotional fall-out of the decision
  • Prepare couple for a constructive divorce process in their interactions with each other, in their dealings with their children, and in choosing professional assistance.
  • Offer more sessions about a constructive divorce process.
  • Monitor the emotions and although emphasizing with each partner interrupt intense emotional outpourings from leaning-in partner, keep things as calm as possible.
  • In your summary, state any commonalities you have heard about their divorce hopes and dreams for their children, co-parenting, and their interest in mediation and collaborative divorce processes.

Choosing Path 1

  • Why couples may choose this path:
    • Leaning-out partner still can't decide and leaning-in partner is still leaning-in.
    • The couple may have gotten enough from the session (relief, able to talk more calmly about the issues, feel heard for the first time, etc.) so they feel ready to continue the relationship without more therapy.
    • It may also be it doesn't seem like the right time for a big change in their lives (considering their children or other situations).
    • Some believe that situations that are impinging on their relationship might change so they decide to wait it out.
