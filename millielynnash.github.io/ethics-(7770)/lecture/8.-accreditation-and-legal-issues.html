  • Commission on Accreditation for Marriage and Family Therapy Education

    • Purpose of accreditation:
      • Ensure the quality of marriage and family therapy programs.
      • Accredited programs meet or exceed all standards for accreditation.
    • Standards for Accreditation: Version 12.5
      • Outcome Based Education (You have to show that your people are meeting target): Vs input based (You offer these courses, your people are confident).
      • 360 Quality and Improvement Review Process
      • Start with Program goals, then student learning outcomes, and then targets for those outcomes.
      • Review and gather data to show that you are meeting the targets or not and what you are doing to change the program to allow students to meet the SLO's

  • Accreditation:

    • If programs are accredited, licensure boards typically accept that they meet the educational requirements and client contact contact hour requirements for licensure (again, typically, always check with the state board)
      • State of Ohio -> 500 hour requirement
      • Board -> 300 hour requirement
    • Set standards for what adequate education and training is in the field. Protect students in those programs, knowing that they are able to be licensed in their state with that education, and protects the public in general, knowing that a therapist who graduated from an accredited program has the education and training needed to provide the service.

  • Family Law in Ohio:

    • Legal Age for Marriage in Ohio:
      • 16
        • What are the Marriage Age Requirement Laws in Ohio?
          • While females in Ohio may get married as young as 16 with the consent of both parents, only a juvenile court (in limiting cases) may consent to a marriage involving a male under 18.
    • Same-sex marriage has been 'legal' and recognized in Ohio since 2015.
    • Parentage:
      • A man is presumed to be the natural father of a child in certain circumstances, including:
        • The man and the child's mother are or have been married to each other, and the child is born during the marriage or is born within 300 days after the marriage is terminated.
        • An acknowledgement of paternity has been filed pursuant to Sec. 3111.23
        • The term 'Punative Father' means a man, including one under age 18, who may be a child's father and to whom all of the following apply.
          • He is not married to the child's mother at the time of the child's conception or birth.
          • He has not adopted the child.
          • He has not been determined, prior to the date a petition to adopt the child is filed, to have a parent and child relationship with the child by a court proceeding or an administrative agency proceeding.
          • He has not acknowledged paternity of the child pursuant to Sec. 3111.21 to 3111.35.

  • Family Law and the Therapist:

    • Understanding Divorce and Custody Issues:
      • Dissolution vs. Divorce:
        • Dissolution is when both parties can agree on all matters pertaining to separation (i.e., no fault divorce).
        • Divorce is when both parties can't come to an agreement.
    • To get a no-fault divorce, you need to state in the Complaint for Divorce that 'that the parties are incompatible,' or 'the parties have been living separate and apart without cohabitation for 1 year.' The grounds of being incompatible can be defeated if your spouse denies incompatibility.
    • The fault-based grounds for divorce are bigamy, willful absence of the other party for 1 year, adultery, extreme cruelty, fraudulent contract, gross neglect of duty, habitual drunkness, imprisonment in a state or federal prison at the time of filing, and when the divorce decree of another state does not use any of these, since they had complexity to the process by requiring proof.

  • Custody:

    • If parties don't agree about custody agreements, the judge will decide based on child's best interest:
      • The wishes of the parties,
      • The wishes of the child, if the judge interviewed the child in private,
      • The child's adjustment to home, school, and community,
      • The mental and physical health of all persons involved,
        • You may get subpoenaed, good idea to contact attorney and ask. If private practice, have attorney on retainer,
      • The party more likely to honor and facilitate parenting time rights,
      • Whether either party failed to make all child support payments, including arrearages,
      • Whether either party or a household member was a perpetrator of child abuse or neglect, or a violent or sexually oriented offense involving a member of the family,
      • Whether either party has continuously and willfully, denied the other party's rights to parenting time in accordance with an an order of the court,
      • Whether either party has, or has plans to, establish a residence outside of Ohio.
    • Other Considerations:
      • The ability of the parties to cooperate and make decisions jointly,
      • Each party's ability to encourage contact between the child and the other party,
      • Any history of, or potential for, child abuse, domestic violence, or parental kidnapping,
      • The geographic proximity of the parties to each other,
      • The recommendation of any guardian ad litem of the child

  • Child Support:

    • Child support is determined by reference to the Ohio Child Support Guidelines and includes a chart to calculate the child's needs based upon the combined income of the parties. For example, if the combined income of you and your spouse is $96,000
      • Recommend learn the laws around divorce and custody in the state you are located in.

  • Testifying in Custody Determinations

    • Don't. (I am not unbiased) (There are training programs for this)
    • If you decide you want to do that work, then you are not also the therapist for the parties involved.
    • Sometimes you may be asked to testify based on the work you have done with one of the parents, do your best not to. You can put that into your consent form, although might not be considered a legal document.

  • Custody and Working with Children:

    • Whenever there is a divorce with custody agreements about children, then you need to review those custody agreements and get the consent of parents who share custody with their children. If a parent has sole custody then their consent is enough. If there is shared custody, then both parents must provide consent to treatment, unless there is a stipulation in the custody agreement that says otherwise.
      • You can work with child and parent of up to 6 session without consent.
