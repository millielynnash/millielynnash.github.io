Abstract

Following recent interest in this journal in building bridges between a psychoanalytic and systemic approach to family therapy (Vol. 19, 1997), the authors take up the debate. A clinical vignette from a recent article is used for discussion and, in order to describe further work from a psychoanalytic perspective, two case vignettes are presented. The comparisons are discussed with particular reference to the concepts of projective identification, transference/countertransference and internal object relations.

“two different models to thinking about families and their interactions: (1) an inside-out model which takes account of the family’s internal phantasy world and how this impinges upon external relationships, and (2) an outside-in model which considers the manner in which family members relate to one another as well as to the external world and how this impinges upon individual family members’ thoughts, awareness and beliefs about each other” (Brodie and Wright, 2002, p. 2)

“Gregory Bateson’s (1972) observation that ‘mind is immanent’ in the larger system. In this sense mental processes are brought about by a total system, or series of interconnections between external relationships. In more recent years this has been developed further by the influence of feminism and postmodernist thinking, which question assumptions of power and authority in authorship inherent in the system” (Brodie and Wright, 2002, p. 2)

“Gergen’s idea of the ‘distributed self” (Brodie and Wright, 2002, p. 2)

“there is no true core self because self is a product of relationships, settings and contexts. This is important for systemic family therapists, since an individual’s sense of self is maintained by ongoing relationships with other members of the family which are non-linear.” (Brodie and Wright, 2002, p. 2)

“This conceptualization is enshrined in circularity in relationships and” (Brodie and Wright, 2002, p. 2)

“the interviewing technique known as ‘circular questioning” (Brodie and Wright, 2002, p. 3)

“Milan school emphasized an attitude of neutrality” (Brodie and Wright, 2002, p. 3)

“being necessary in order to really engage with and understand the system and that this might be considered as similar to Freud’s idea of evenly suspended attention” (Brodie and Wright, 2002, p. 3)

“a psychoanalytic approach emphasizes that whatever the pressures of the external circumstances, the way in which these are engaged with is also significantly influenced by how individual family members function internally (e.g. splitting or integrating).” (Brodie and Wright, 2002, p. 3)

“the family’s tendency to organize itself around the intolerance of pain and adversity, and on the other, to organize itself around the enjoyment and promotion of hope and well-being, whatever the external circumstances” (Brodie and Wright, 2002, p. 3)

“certain modes of thinking and learning can become dominant within families.” (Brodie and Wright, 2002, p. 3)

“These patterns are based on identificatory processes (introjective, projective, adhesive) operating within an internal (psychic) reality.
Consequently, the understanding of family life and emotional nurturance may be seen as broadly organized under three general learning types: (1) those which learn from experience, (2) those relying on more narcissistic mechanisms, and (3) those functioning at a parasitic level. As a consequence of these fundamental differences between kinds of learning, the growth of the personality is either facilitated or opposed, thereby blocking development.” (Brodie and Wright, 2002, p. 3)

“what we observe within the therapeutic encounter is that transference and countertransference are hereand-now processes which actively use the therapist as an object in which to put these unwanted states of mind. The repetition, in this sense, is the re-enactment, in the lived moment of the therapy, of the way the family has related to objects in the past” (Brodie and Wright, 2002, p. 4)

“patterns of relating are not repeated for the sake of it but because the family is unable or unwilling to relate emotionally in a more developmentally complex way.” (Brodie and Wright, 2002, p. 4)
