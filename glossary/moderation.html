Overview

Definition

  • Moderation occurs when the strength or direction of the relationship between an Independent Variable and a Dependent Variable changes depending on the level of a third variable, known as a Moderator. In other words, the moderator influences how, or under what conditions, the IV affects the DV.

Purpose

  • Helps determine under what conditions or for whom an effect holds true.

Example

  • Imagine you are studying the impact of a training program (IV) on job performance (DV). However, you hypothesize that this relationship differs based on work experience (moderator). For individuals with high work experience, the training might have a stronger (or weaker) effect on performance compared to those with low work experience. In this case, work experience moderates the effect of training on job performance.

Assumptions of Moderation Analysis

  • When conducting moderation analysis (typically through Multiple Regression with an interaction term). Several assumptions should be met:

Linearity

  • The relationship between predictors (including the interaction term) and the DV should be linear.

How to Check in SPSS

Scatterplots / Partial Regression Plots
  • Click Graphs > Legacy Dialogs > Scatter/Dot
  • Choose 'Simple Scatter' and click Define
  • Plot the DV on the Y-axis and each predictor (and, if possible, the interaction term) on the X-axis.
  • Look for a straight-line relationship between each predictor and the DV.
Residuals vs. Predicted Values Plot
  • Run your Regression Analysis
    • Analyze > Regression > Linear
    • In the Linear Regression dialog, click Plots.
    • Move the standardized predicted values on the X box and standardized residuals to the Y box.
    • Click Continue and then OK.
    • Check the plot for a random scatter (no obvious patterns or curves) which supports the linearity assumption.

Homoscedasticity

  • The variance of residuals should be consistent across levels of the predictors.

How to Check in SPSS

Residuals vs. Predicted Values Plot
  • Use the same plot created for checking linearity.
  • Look for a consistent spread of residuals across all predicted values (no funnel shape or systematic change in spread).
Statistical Tests (Optional)
  • You can also use tests like the Breusch-Pagan test in SPSS (via additional syntax or plugins) if a more formal assessment is needed.

Normality of Residuals

  • Residuals from the regression should be approximately normally distributed.

How to Check in SPSS

Histogram
  • After running your regression, save the standardized residuals.
  • Go to Graphs > Legacy Dialogs > Histogram and plot the residuals.
  • The histogram should show a bell-shaped curve.
Q-Q Plot
  • In the regression dialog (Analyze > Regression > Linear), Click Plots.
  • Add a Normal Q-Q plot by moving the standardized residuals to the Y box.
  • A Q-Q plot where the points closely follow the diagonal line indicates that the residuals are normally distributed.

Independence of Observations

  • The observations should be independent of each other (i.e., no Autocorrelation)

How to Check in SPSS

Study Design
  • Ensure that your data collection method supports the assumption of independence (e.g., participants are sampled independently)
Durbin-Watson Statistics
  • When running the regression (Analyze > Regression > Linear), SPSS outputs the Durbin-Watson statistic.
  • A value around 2 suggests that the residuals are independent. Values deviating substantially from 2 indicate potential issues.

No Multicollinearity

  • Predictor variables (including the interaction term) should not be too highly correlated.

Performing Moderation Analysis in SPSS

Data Preparation

  • Ensure that variables are centered (or standardized) to reduce Multicollinearity when creating interaction terms.

Creating Interaction Term

  • Multiply the centered IV and Moderator.

Regression Analysis

  • Click Analyze > Regression > PROCESS v
  • Enter the IV, moderator, and the interaction term as Predictor s.
  • For moderation analysis, use Model 1.
  • PROCESS automatically creates the interaction term (Training x Work Experience)
  • If your variables are continuous, it is often recommended to center them first (or use PROCESS options that center them automatically)

Interpreting the Output

Main Effects

  • IV and Moderator Coefficients:
    • These represent the effect of the IV and moderator on the DV when the other variable is at its mean (if centered).

Interaction Effect

  • Interaction Coefficient (IV × Moderator):
    • Significance: A statistically significant interaction term (typically p < .05) indicates that the effect of the IV on the DV changes depending on the level of the moderator.
    • Interpretation: If the coefficient is positive, as the moderator increases, the effect of the IV on the DV becomes stronger (or more positive); if negative, the effect weakens (or becomes more negative).

Simple Slopes Analysis

  • Purpose: To determine how the IV-DV relationship varies at specific values of the moderator (e.g., at the mean, one standard deviation above, and one standard deviation below the mean).
  • Output: SPSS (via PROCESS) often provides these slopes along with their confidence intervals and p-values.

Example Output Interpretation

  • Suppose you get the following (hypothetical) output:
  • IV (Training): b = 0.30, SE = 0.10, p = .003
  • Moderator (Work Experience): b = 0.20, SE = 0.08, p =.012
  • Interaction (Training × Work Experience): b = 0.15, SE = 0.05, p = .002

Interpretation

  • The significant interaction (p = .002) suggests that the effect of training on job performance depends on work experience.
  • A simple slopes analysis might reveal that for individuals with high work experience (e.g., +1 SD), the effect of training on performance is stronger (say, b=0.45b = 0.45b=0.45) compared to those with low work experience (e.g., -1 SD, b=0.15b = 0.15b=0.15).

APA-Style Write-Up Example

  • Below is an example of how you might report your findings in APA style:
  • A moderation analysis was conducted using the PROCESS macro (Model 1) to examine whether work experience moderated the relationship between training and job performance. The results indicated a significant interaction between training and work experience, b = 0.15, SE = 0.05, p = .002, suggesting that the effect of training on job performance varied as a function of work experience. Simple slopes analysis revealed that the relationship between training and job performance was stronger for participants with high work experience (b = 0.45, p < .001) compared to those with low work experience (b = 0.15, p = .045). These findings indicate that work experience significantly moderates the relationship between training and job performance.
