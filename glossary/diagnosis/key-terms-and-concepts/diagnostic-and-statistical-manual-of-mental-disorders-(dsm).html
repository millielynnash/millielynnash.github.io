Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM)

  • The handbook used by health care professionals in the US and much of the world as the authoritative guide to the diagnosis of mental disorders
  • DSM contains descriptions, symptoms, and other criteria for diagnosing mental disorders.
  • It also provides a common language for researchers to study the criteria for potential future revisions and to aid in the development of medications and other interventions.

History of DSM

  • 1840:
    • Category 'insanity' first attempt to quantify and name psychiatric diagnoses
  • 1844:
    • APA published a predecessor of the DSM:
      • A classification of institutionalized mental patients
  • Early 1900's
    • First description of schizophrenia by Emil Kraeplin
  • 1917:
    • Statistical Manual for the Use of Institutions for the insane, including 22 diagnoses.
  • 1952:
    • First DSM, 130 pages long, 106 disorders
  • 1994:
    • DSM-IV, 886 pages long, 297 disorders
  • 2000:
    • DSM-IV-TR, 943 pages long, 365 disorders
  • 2013:
    • DSM-5, 991 pages long, 157 disorders
  • 2022:
    • DSM-5-TR, 1050 pages long

Advantages:

  • Standardizes billing and coding
  • Standardizes diagnoses and treatment
  • Guides research
  • Guides treatment

Disadvantages:

  • Oversimplifies human behavior
  • Increases risk of misdiagnosis or over-diagnosis
  • Provides labels, which can be stigmatizing

Assessment Measures:

  • Growing scientific evidence favors dimensional diagnosis of mental disorders
  • Takes into account individual subjective report of symptom experience and clinician interpretation
  • Integrates both objective and subjective data to enhance the accuracy

Categorical vs. Dimensional:

  • Categorical:
    • A categorical approach to assessment relied on diagnostic criteria to determine the presence or absence of disruptive or other abnormal behaviors, whereas a dimensional approach places such behaviors on a continuum of frequency and/or severity.
    • Presence or absence
    • Yes or no
  • Dimensional:
    • Frequency:
      • None of the time to all of the time
      • Hourly, daily, weekly, monthly
    • Intensity/Severity:
      • Absent 0, mild 1, moderate 2, severe 3
    • Duration:
      • How long does it last?
  • First (Categorical):
    • Yes / no
  • Second (Frequency):
    • How often?
  • Third (Intensity/Severity):
    • Rating (Mild, moderate, severe)
  • Fourth (Duration):
    • How long does it last?
  • Growing scientific evidence favors dimensional diagnosis of mental disorders.
  • Takes into account individual subjective report of symptom experience and clinician interpretation.
  • Integrates both objective and subjective data to enhance the accuracy of the diagnostic process.
  • Integrates measures administered at initial interview and over time to track symptom status and response to treatment.

Severity Measures:

  • Disorder-specific and correspond closely to the diagnostic criteria.
  • Some are self-completed by the individual, couple, or family.
    • Interpreted by the clinician
    • Shared back with the individual, couple, or family
  • Others require a clinician to complete.

WHODAS:

  • A generic assessment instrument for health and disability.
  • Used across all diseases, including mental, neurological, and addictive disorders.
  • Short, simple and easy to administer (5 to 20 minutes)
  • Applicable in both clinical and general population settings.
  • A tool to produce standardized disability levels and profiles.
  • Applicable across cultures, in all adult populations.
  • Directly linked at the level of concepts to the International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health (ICF)

Reason for Assessment:

  • The reason for the assessment, including the member's:
    • Perception of the condition
    • Description of symptoms (including reason for referral)
    • History of mental health treatment (including review of member's records)
    • Important developmental incidents
    • Maltreatment, trauma or abuse issues
    • History of alcohol and drug usage and treatment
    • Health history and family health history, including physical, chemical and mental health history
    • Cultural influences and their impact on the member

What is a Diagnostic Assessment?

  • An assessment of the member's needs based on:
    • Baseline measurements
    • Symptoms
    • Behavior
    • Skills
    • Abilities
    • Resources
    • Vulnerabilities
    • Safety
  • Assessment methods and use of standardized assessment tools
  • Clinical summary, including recommendations and prioritization of needed mental health or other services
  • Involvement of the member and member's family in the assessment and his or her preferences or referrals to services.
  • Sufficient data to support findings on all axes of the current Diagnostic and Statistic Manual (DSM), and any differential diagnosis.
