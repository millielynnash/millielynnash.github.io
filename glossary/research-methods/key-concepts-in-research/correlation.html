• The statistical association between two variables measured at ordinal, interval or ratio level. Many different 'correlation co-efficients' are used as measures of association.

Bivariate Measures of Association

  • We already know many ways to represent the association between two variables, both measured on at least an interval scale.
  • Scatterplot
  • Covariance sxy = (∑i = 1n (x1 - x-bar)(yi - y-bar)) ÷ n - 1 (𝜎xy for population)
  • Pearson correlation coefficient rxy = (sxy) ÷ (sxsy) (𝜌xy for population)
    • The correlation reflects the noisiness and direction of a linear relationship, but not the slope of that relationship, nor many aspects of non-linear relationships.

Inferences about the Pearson r: One Single Sample

  • To test whether the sample correlation between x and y is significantly different from zero (non-directional):
    • H0: 𝜌 = 0 vs. H1: 𝜌 ≠ 0
      • Directional alternatives are also possible.
  • Assumptions:
    • Linearity:
      • The Pearson correlation assumes that the relationship among x and y is a linear relationship.
    • Independence:
      • Units or cases are randomly sampled from the population.

Observed t and Critical t

  • tobs = r√(n - 2) ÷ (1 - r2)
  • tcrit: Given the 𝛼 level, t distribution with degrees of freedom (df = n - 2)
  • Reject H0 if tobs > tcrit (or for negative values: tobs < tcrit)
  • Alternatively, reject H0 if p < 𝛼

    • A Pearson correlation coefficient was computed to assess the linear relationship between x and y. There was a (weak / moderate / strong negative / positive) correlation between x and y, r (df) = (p-value).
    • Notice:
      • No need to report t.

Caution 1: Restriction of Range

  • Restricting the range of scores on one or both variables will reduce the strength of a correlation.
  • If the size of the standard deviation for one variable is reduced, everything else being equal, then the size of correlations with other variables will also be reduced. In other words, we need sufficient variation for a relationship to be evidenced through the correlation coefficient value.
  • HS GPA and ACT example:
    • The strength of correlation between GPA and ACT will be lessened if we only focus on the graduates with summa cum laude (weighted GPA 4.2 or above).

Caution 2: Correlation does not Imply Causation!

  • Correlation means the variables are related in some way, but correlations alone should not be used to infer cause. A high correlation between x and y might be a result of:
    • x causes y
    • y causes x
    • A third variable z is causing both x and y (or even more variables are involved).
  • Causality is not easy to prove. Application of complex statistical methods is not evidence for causality!
  • Causation (x -> y) can only be inferred (formalized by the 19 th-century philosopher John Stuart Mill) if:
    • x proceeded y
    • x was related to y
    • We can find no other plausible alternative explanation for the effect on y other than x.

Effect Size

  • 'Ballpark' estimates for Pearson correlation coefficient r:
    • r = ± .2 (weak)
    • r = ± .4 (moderate)
    • r = ± .8 (strong)
    • It also depends on the nature of variables, studies, and fields (literature review).
  • Cohen's (1988) standards for Pearson correlation coefficient r:
    • r = ± .1 (weak)
    • r = ± .3 (moderate)
    • r = ± .5 (strong)

Simple Linear Regression (SLR)

  • Correlation does not imply causation. With proper literature review and research design, however we can investigate how one variable (x or IV) explains the variability (variance) in the other variable (y or DV). If x is indeed a significant predictor of y, we can use x to predict y.
  • Still a bivariate analysis, but with a different goal in mind. For example, the school district might want to use GPA to explain the variable in ACT scores and use GPA to predict ACT.
  • Here we have only one predictor (IV) in the model: Simple Linear Regression (SLR).
    • You will learn regression models with multiple predictors, multiple linear regression (MLR), in more advanced stats classes.
  • Regression means you are trying to find a pattern between x and y. You are using x to predict y. We are only using one.

Correlation and Proportion of Variance Explained

  • If we square the Pearson correlation coefficient (r) between x and y, r2 indicates the proportion of the Variance in y that can be explained by x.
  • HS GPA and ACT example: r = .698
  • Square it:
    • r2 = (. 698)2 ≈ .487
    • This indicates 48.7% of the variance in ACT scores can be explained (or accounted for) by GPA.
  • Recall r ranges from -1 to 1. What does it mean for the range of r2?
  • Recall 'ballpark' effect size:
    • Weak (|r| ≥ .2)
      • Weak (r2 ≥ .04 or 4%)
    • Moderate (|r| ≥ .4)
      • Moderate (r2 ≥ .16 or 16%)
    • Strong (|r| ≥ .8)
      • Strong (r2 ≥ .64 or 64%)

Finding Equation for the Regression Line

  • xi: IV data point for subject i
  • yi: DV data point for subject i

    • 𝛽0: Intercept (Constant)
    • 𝛽1: Slope (How much DV increases as IV increases by 1 unit)

SPSS

  • Analyze -> Regression -> Linear
  • Define DV and IV (one IV for SLR)
  • Usually you'd already review descriptive statistics and correlations before running a regression, but you can also ask SPSS to report Descriptive in the Statistics option.
  • Many other options are for MLR only.
  • The default SPSS output has 3 sections:
    • Model Summary (How much variance in DV is explained by the model)
    • ANOVA Table (Model significance)
    • Regression Coefficients (Equation for the regression line)

Model Summary: Variance in DV Explained

  • Total variability (variance) in DV is partitioned into two parts:
    • What we can explain by the model (regression)
    • What we still cannot explain (error)
  • Regression sum of squares (SSR)
  • Error Sum of Squares (SSE)
  • Total Sum of Squares (SSTO)
  • SSTO = SSR + SSE
  • SSR, SSE, and SSTO are available in the ANOVA table

Coefficient of Determination R2 and Multiple R

  • R2 indicates the proportion of variance in DV explained (accounted for) by the model, which can be considered the overall model fit.
  • R2 = (SSR) ÷ (SSTO) = 1 - (SSE ÷ SSTO) = (Multiple R)2
    • In SLR, R2 equals the square of the Pearson correlation between x and y
  • Multiple R is the correlation between yi and y-hati (always positive)
    • In SLR, Multiple R = | rxy |

ANOVA Table and the Omnibus Test

  • Omnibus:
    • Comprising several items
    • "Congress passed an omnibus anticrime package."
  • The omnibus test is used to evaluate the significance of overall regression model. Statistics used to conduct the omnibus test are available in the ANOVA table.
  • Many different (equivalent) ways to represent the omnibus test, but they are asking the same question:
    • Is the regression model good enough to be used to explain/predict DV?
  • Since we are comparing variances (SSR and SSE), the sample statistics is compared to a critical F statistics, given the significance level (usually α=. 05). General idea:
    • Large observed F means much variance in DV can be explained by the model (desired)!

Ommibus Test Hypotheses (SLR)

  • H0: β1 = 0
  • H1: β1 ≠ 0
  • H0: y-hat = y-bar
  • H1: y-hat = b0 +b1x (DV cannot be predicted by model)
  • H0: ρ = 0 (ρ is the population correlation between model and data, estimated by Multiple R)
  • H1: ρ > 0
  • H0: ρ2 = 0
  • H1: ρ2 > 0

Omnibus Test Significance (SLR)

  • Compare Fobs to the critical F(1, n -2)
    • Reject H0 if Fobs > F(1, n -2)
  • Alternatively, reject H0 if p < α
  • For APA style writing, make sure you report the F statistic along with both degrees of freedom and the p-value.
