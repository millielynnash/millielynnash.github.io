Paired T-Test

Paired-Sample t-test

  • AKA dependent t-test:
  • Dependent variable (DV):
    • Scores being compared
      • Measured on at least an interval level scale
      • Often used to compare post-score to pre-score
  • Independent variable (IV):
    • The treatment or intervention
  • Typical research question:
    • Is there a mean difference between the post-score and pre-score?
    • Also used to compare twins, couples, eyeballs, etc.

Research Ideas (IV -> DV, Comparing Pre- and Post-Scores)

  • 20 weeks of cognitive behavioral therapy -> Amount and severity of depressive symptoms
  • Intervention -> Students' math proficiency
  • Constraint induced movement therapy -> hand function

Quasi-Experimental Designs without Control Groups

  • One-group pretest-posttest design:
    • O1 (pre) X O2 (post)
  • One-group pretest-posttest design using a double pretest:
    • O1 O2 X O3
  • One-group pretest-posttest design using a nonequivalent dependent variable:
    • (O1 A, O1 B) x (O2 B, O2 B)
  • The removed-treatment design:
    • O1 x O2 (O3) x O4
  • The repeated-treatment design:
    • O1 x O2 x O3 x O4

Quasi-Experimental Designs with Control Groups

  • Untreated control group design with dependent pretest and posttest samples
    • NR (Not Random) O1 X O2
    • NR (Not Random) O1 O2
  • Untreated control group design with dependent pretest and posttest samples using a double pretest
    • NR O1 O2 X O3
    • NR O1 O2 O3
  • Untreated control group design with dependent pretest and posttest samples using switching replications
    • NR O1 x O2 (O3) x O4
    • NR O1 O2 (O3) x O4
  • Untreated control group design dependent pretest and posttest samples using reversed-treatment control group
    • NR O1 (pre) X O2 (post)
    • NR O1 (pre) X O2 (post)

Hypotheses (Paired-Sample t-Test)

  • Nondirectional:
    • Test if post-score (¬µ2) is significantly different from pre-score (¬µ1)
      • H0: ¬µ2 = ¬µ1 vs. H1: ¬µ2 ‚â† ¬µ1
    • Alternatively, in terms of mean difference:
      • H0: ¬µ2 - ¬µ1 ‚â§ 0 vs. H1: ¬µ2 - ¬µ1 > 0
    • The mean difference is the difference in pre- and post-scores, so a paired-sample t-test is identical to a one-sample t-test, testing the difference in scores against the hypothesized (test) value!
  • Directional:
    • Test if post-score (¬µ2) is significantly greater from pre-score (¬µ1).
      • H0: ¬µ2 - ¬µ1 ‚â§ 0 vs. H1: ¬µ2 - ¬µ1 > 0
  • Directional:
    • Test if post-score (¬µ2) is significantly smaller than pre-scores (¬µ1):
      • H0: ¬µ2 - ¬µ1 ‚â• 0 vs. H1: ¬µ2 - ¬µ1 < 0

  • Recall rejection region is outside of critical t('s) toward the tail (s).

    • ùõº level is usually .05 (95% confidence level)

Confidence Interval

  • Nondirectional:
    • 100 (1 - ùõº)% CI:
      • ¬± tdf, ùõº/2 x sd-bar
      • ¬± tdf, ùõº/2 x (sd-bar √∑ ‚àön)
    • To test greater (significant increase in post-scores):
      • 100 (1 - ùõº)% LCL: - tdf, ùõº/2 x sd-bar
    • To test smaller (significant decrease in post-scores):
      • 100 (1 - ùõº)% UCL: + tdf, ùõº/2 x sd-bar

SPSS

  • DV data recorded as 2 variables (e.g., pre-score and post-score) in SPSS
  • IV (treatment, intervention) is usually not recorded as a variable in SPSS
  • Analyze -> Compare Means -> Paired-Samples t-test
  • Move the two variables of interest (e.g., pre-score and post-score) to the paired variable 1 and variable 2.
  • Notice the mean difference SPSS computes is variable 1 minus variable 2, so sometimes the sign of observed t can be confusing. However, you can easily figure out if the effect is positive or negative based on the descriptive statistics. I like to enter post-score as variable 1 and pre-score as variable 2 so I can see the change (positive or negative) from pre-score to post-score.

Effect Size (Paired-Sample t-test)

  • Cohen's d = d-bar √∑ sd-bar
    • Recall Cohen's d for one-sample t-test:
      • d = ( - ¬µ0) √∑ s
  • Size of Cohen's d (by convention):
    • d = ¬±0.2: Small effect
    • d = ¬±0.5: Medium effect
    • d = ¬±0.8: Large effect

APA Writing Template (Negative Emotion)

  • The pre-video and post-video negative emotion scores were collected from a sample of 34 participants, with a pre-score of 4.80 (SD = 0.926) and a post-score mean of 4.60 (SD = 1.028). Thus, negative emotion changed by -0.20 (decrease) after the video. The paired-sample t-test was conducted to determine if this change was significantly smaller than zero (significant decrease after the video), and the results indicate that the post-score was significantly lower than the pre-score, t(33) = -2.393, p = 0.011. The null hypothesis was rejected at the .05 level of significance.
  • The effect size Cohen's d was -0.410. Using Cohen's (1988) guidelines, this is interpreted as a small to medium effect.
  • The results provide evidence to support the conclusion that the negative emotion significantly decreased after watching cat videos. The effect size suggests about 0.4 standard deviation unit difference between pre-score and post-score.

APA Writing Template (Positive Emotion)

  • The pre-video and post-video positive emotion scores were collected from a sample of 34 participants, with a pre-score of 3.76 (SD = 0.998) and a post-score mean of 4.82 (SD = 1.014). Thus, positive emotion increased by 1.06 after the video. The paired-sample t-test was conducted to determine if this change was significantly greater than zero (significant increase after the video), and the results indicate that the post-score was significantly higher than the pre-score, t(33) = 6.448, p < .001. The null hypothesis was rejected at the .05 level of significance.
  • The effect size Cohen's d was 1.106. Using Cohen's (1988) guidelines, this is interpreted as a large effect.
  • The results provide evidence to support the conclusion that the positive emotion significantly increased after watching cat videos. The effect size suggests more than one standard deviation unit difference between pre-score and post-score.

Some Reminders About APA Style Writing

  • If you're hypothesis testing, restate your hypothesis. If your t-test is one-tailed, you need to say so.
  • It's preferred to report the exact p-value unless it's smaller than .001. You need to state your alpha level (usually .05) early in the results section. If your p-value is smaller than .001, it's conventional simply to state p < .001 (NEVER report p = 0)
  • Remember to drop the leading 0 from the p-value.
  • No need to provide a formula for observed t in your paper.

Assumptions (Paired-Sample t-test)

  • Observations are from a random sample, and the scores are independent of each other.
  • Dependent variable of interest is measured on at least an interval scale.
  • Difference scores (d's) are normally distributed.
    • Typically considered robust to violations of non-normality, especially for big sample (n > 30)
    • If one of the samples is highly skewed (particularly for a one-tailed test), a lot of error will tend to be built into the difference scores. Other non-parametric analyses would be better alternatives.
