6. Studying Diverse Populations

Random Sampling

  • Cases (individuals, families, etc.) are only selected on the basis of chance
  • Keep in mind:
    • You must know the population (your sample frame)
    • Poor response rates makes your sample nonrandom and thus nonrepresentative

Probability Sampling Methods

  • Cluster Sampling:
    • Random sample of clusters is drawn
    • Random sample of elements within each selected cluster is draw
    • At least to stages of sampling
    • Really helpful for studying populations with large geographic area OR when it is easier to sample from groups (e.g., within classes instead of randomly at OSU)
    • When cluster sampling is used, sampling weights are also used to make sure the descriptive information of the sample will match that of the population.
  • Stratified Random Sampling:
    • To ensure that various groups are included in your sample
      • Proportionate:
        • Random sampling in direct proportion to groups in population
      • Disproportionate:
        • Proportion of each group does not match population
        • Pros of Disproportionate Sampling
          • Facilitate group comparisons
          • Enable the study of within group processes
          • They still allow for population-level generalizability

Importance of a Population Perspective

  • Why should we strive for population representative sampling
    • Importance for policy or practice (We don't want to recommend changes that aren't based on research that is representative of the full population those changes would impact.)
  • How?
    • Narrow our populations of interest (e.g., Columbus instead of the US)
    • Work on large teams
    • Use secondary data

Getting a Sampling Frame

  • How do you get a full list of your population
    • Phone numbers
    • Addresses
  • What is your population is subjective? (e.g., likely voterts, experienced trauma) - need to operationalize wel
  • Really hard to do for large populations with individual-level units
  • Easier with something like hospitals or schools
  • Easier to do with more targeted populations (e.g., OSU) but still confidentiality issues

Sampling Bias

  • How do you actually reach the individuals that were randomly selected to be sampled?
  • Who actually is willing to participate?
    • If participation rates are incredibly low, are our samples still representative?

Selective Attrition

  • Who stays in our sample?
  • Another way that our sample can end up biased in longitudinal work
    • Generalizability concerns (e.g., what if families experiencing poverty dropout at much higher rates)
    • Causal validity concerns (e.g., what is the individuals experiencing the most acute depressive symptoms are more likely to leave our study of a new therapy model?)
  • Are design and statistical ways to reduce the impact of this problem but still needs to be avoided as much as possible.

Realistic Sampling

  • You will likely work a lot with quota or convenience sampling in your quantitative work. That's okay but:
    • Recognize the limits of your works generalizability
    • Think about the balance across types of validity
    • See if there are representative samples available to use to answer your questions
      • Pair multiple datasets (e.g., a representative secondary dataset with less great measurement with a datasetr you collect with great measurement)
        What do we learn across studies?
  • Again, the goal is to do valid, high quality research and to be transparent about how the strengths and limitations of our work shape its contributions to our knowledge and understanding of the world.
  • Aim for the best sampling plan, but recognize it may not happen immediately.
  • Putting your sampling plan into a pre-registration that details your target population (even if not random sampling), planned sample size, and strategies for recruitment helps you establish a solid plan.
    • Talk to others who do similar research. How do they find participants?

Studying Diverse Populations

  • WEIRD
  • Diversifying our samples is not enough; we need to think critically about how our work (and our methods) acknowledge/incorporate/ignore systems of power and oppression in the world

Deficit Framing

  • Placing onus on individuals or groups rather than structures and environment
  • Moving beyond disparities to what creates these disparities
  • Thinking about strengths-based approaches

Including Demographic Covariates

  • Should we control for things like race, gender identity, immigration status?
    • Within-group analyses ideal
    • Using moderation to understand key processes also ideal
    • When not available, controlling for these things is another option, but need to think through and justify why or why not

Acknowledging and Examining Structural Factors

  • What is structural racism?
    • Moving beyond the individual
    • Policies, lived environments, etc.

Intersectionality

  • Individuals hold multiple identities
  • Commonly examined in qualitative work, but important for quantitative research as well
  • Key principle:
    • Cannot simply add up the experiences of multiple marginalized identities - need to understand the complex ways in which multiple identities shape experiences

Within Group vs. Between Group

  • Between Group
    • Default comparison group
    • Inability to look at how processes vary across groups
    • Assume homogeneity
  • Within Group
    • Highlight unique processes within groups
    • Allow for examination of heterogeneity within a population

Measurement Issues

  • Measurement invariance
    • Do measures operate in the same way for different groups of people?
  • Easy to test statistically in a latent framework
    • Do items hang together the same way? (reliability)
    • Does the measure have the same construct and discriminant validity?
  • If we don't ensure our measures are appropriate for the participants we are studying, we are not going to get closer to truths about the world.

Importance of Framing

  • Understand that your own bias shapes the question
  • How would answering that question enhance children's lives?
  • The potential solution (s)

Deficit vs. Strengths Perspective

Deficit

  • Problem focused
  • What is missing that we must go find?
  • Leads to depression, dysfunction, and learned helplessness

Strengths

  • Opportunity focused
  • What is present that can be leveraged and build on?
  • Leads to innovation and meeting the needs of the community.

Sampling

  • Acknowledign your positionality
    • Researcher
    • Other identities
  • Building relationships with organizations, community laders, and community members
  • Identifying your sample
    • Based on the literature about that sample
    • With deedback from community members
    • Attending to any comparisons within your sample and how you will provide context for them
  • Ensuring sample is not confounded by race/income
    • If it is, generalize your findings specifically to the sample study is drawn from
  • Ensuring sample is representative of the communities being studies
  • Ensuring that pan-ethic and racial groups effect the heterogeneity
    • If they do not, address this when discussing your work.

Measures

  • Research measures should accurately and truthfully capture your data of interest
  • Use of inappropriate measures can lead to biased findings
    • Leading to deficit framing of your population
  • Do not be race 'mute' or 'blind' when choosing measures
    • Interrogate race when identifying what to measure and how to measure it
  • Measures should yield equitable data for BIPOC participants
  • Do your measures allow for your sample's BIPOC, strengths to be highlighted?
    • If no, you need to re-assess your measurement choice
  • Reliable and valid for your population
    • Culturally relevant
  • What do you do when the measures for your variables of interest do not have reliability or validity estimates for your population?
    • Identify multiple forms of measure
