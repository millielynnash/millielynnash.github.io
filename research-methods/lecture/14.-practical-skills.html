The Basics of Data Management

Data Management and Data Management Plans

Why Share Data?

  • Three reasons you should care about data management:
    1. Federal funders are moving to requiring a data management plan
    2. You need to share your data when the grant is over
      • You'll need a plan that will have your data ready to share publicly by the time you're done.
    3. Science is more interesting when it's true:
      • Data management makes your data and your conclusions more accurate.
  • Some researchers are very uncomfortable with data sharing.
    • They make 3 basic arguments:
      • It was my idea to collect these data.
      • I did all the work
      • It's against the ethos of science
  • This will be required for federal grants:
    • Data should be owned by the public that funded it
    • Allows data to have a wider reach
    • Makes sharing with other authors easier
    • Keeps data archived and accessible long-term

Before You Start

Know Where You Are Going

  • Where you're going is dependent on collecting good information.
  • Important to have analyses in mind when choosing what to collect
  • Be thoughtful of how you present and word questions to your participants.
    • Make sure your instructions are clear.
  • One of the first things you have to do is select measures.
  • You can't answer a question you haven't measured on your participants.
  • It is important to select ones that reliably and validly measure what you want to know.
  • Document everything.
    • It is hard to remember what you measured, when you measured it, or why you measured it. Your best defense is well documented protocols, a good codebook, documentation of what all of your variable names mean.

The Protocol

  • Key to success with any project is writing things down.
  • Future you won't remember what current you decides to do today, or why you decided to do it.
  • Protocols are written documentation of the decisions you make when designing your data collection.
  • They are living documents that should be continuously updated.
  • They have two important points:
    • The decision made
      • Document who made the decision and when
      • The decision is a rule to be implemented across the project
    • A rationale for why the decision was made.
Deciding What to Document
  • Think about someone else using your data later, and what they will need to know.
  • Every project has at least two investigators:
    • You
    • Future you
Examples of Protocol Topics
  • Inclusionary criteria for participants
  • Recruitment procedures
  • Assessment administration
  • Training of project staff
  • Communication with participants
  • ID schemes
  • Protocol Example:
RE:Survey Administration
Authored Date:11/30/2017
Investigators of Consensus:Jessica Logan
Decision:Participants were randomly divided into 3 groups, and surveys will be emailed to participants in 3 waves corresponding to these groups.
Rationale:Follow-up phone calls will need to be made to participants one week post survey distribution. This staggering of survey distribution disperses the workload of calls over several weeks instead of concentrated in one block.

ID Schemes

  • Can convey lots of information (but doesn't have to)
  • Most important thing is that you make a rule and don't change
  • Once a participant has an ID don't change
  • Don't change it
  • We always have a protocol for:
    • What an ID looks like
    • When an ID gets assigned

Variable Naming

  • The shortened digital representation of each variable in your dataset
    • Column names
  • Important that all variable names are unique
  • Variable names follow a rule:
    • Develop a naming convention for yourself
    • Be consistent across datasets (and projects if possible)
    • For item level data, always put the number of the item LAST.
    • Global variable naming protocol:
      • XXXX#YYY or PPVT1001
        • X: Short Survey / Scale
        • #: Timepoint
        • Y: Unique Identifier
    • You can make your own rule, but make sure you document them.
    • Don't change them.

Codebook

  • A codebook is a key translating what you give participants into the data you store.
  • Necessary to link between the items given and the variables in your database.
  • Makes interpretation and analysis easier later.

Variable Values

  • Variable values follow similar rules
  • Keep them consistent across your project
  • Document them in the codebook
  • Think about someone else using your data later, and what they will need to know.
  • Hack:
    • If you have a dichotomous variables, don't name it after the construct.
    • Name it after a category, such that 1 = yes or has that attribute.
      • For the question: Is your house green or red? (Bad: 'HouseColor'; Good: 'HouseGreen')
    • With multiple response questions, make sure the order of options makes sense. (Bad: '-20k, 10k, <10k, >20k'; Better: -<10k, 10k, 20k, >20k)

Who is in Your Study?

  • Tracking your participants (master list, consort, change form)
  • Tracking data collection (on the fly messiness, scheduled and completing data)
Master List
  • A master list is a list of every person the project touches.
  • You may have a sub-master-list, which contains only those participants who you have assigned an ID.
    • How do you determine who gets an ID?
    • You make a decision and you write it down and you don't change it.
  • The master list is a living document from which nothing should ever be deleted
  • Contains basic demographic and contact information
  • This is the lifeblood of the project - how you determine who needs to be assessed and when
  • All information in the master file needs to be stored as letters or numbers

Tracking Participant Movement

  • Participants in studies are transient.
  • Keeping track of movement of participants is important.
  • You can keep track of this in multiple ways:
    • The Master List
    • A separate excel log
    • A form or digital form

Data is Collected

Backup your Data

  • Backing up your data is one of the most essential steps in data processing.
    • This may mean scanning or copying paper data or a more sophisticated digital system.
  • Try to make sure that your department/university has a file server.
    • Shared drive
  • Allow for multiple computers to access the same datafiles.
  • Easier to set back-ups.

Data Cleaning (Range, Validity, Total Scores)

  • Every data set has some problems. If you don't think you have data problem, then you haven't looked at it yet.
  • Data needs to be cleaned to be used meaningfully. Be flexible because data cleaning is hard.
  • Avoid manually-entered questions.
Principles of Data Cleaning
  1. Create an organizational system for the data files.
  2. Create a system for tracking issues and resolutions (codebook/protocol)
  3. Ensure variables and values match those in the codebook
  4. Run descriptives on key variables/calculated scores
  5. Do the means make sense across ages/grades/timepoints?
  6. Eyeball the data
  7. Compare actual data capture to data in database

Getting to Final Data Sets SSRS Codebook



  • Final inclusion of SSRS in Released Publishable Database:
    - Total scores and standards scores
    - For each of the two subscales
    - At each of the three time-points

Final Dataset
  • After final dataset is made, never touch it again.
  • Make a copy of the final, clean dataset. Store it somewhere separate as backup.
