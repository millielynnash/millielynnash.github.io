  1. Normal Distribution (Z-Scores)

Brief Introduction to Probability

  • The area of probability began in the Middle Ages (17th and 18th centuries).
  • Royal and affluent gamblers consulted with mathematicians for advice on games of chance and gambling.
  • Probability was also used for practical purposes to help determine life expectancy for life insurance policies.

Definition of Probability

  • P(A) = S / T
    • P(A) is probability of event A
    • S is # of times event A can occur
    • T is the total # of events possible
  • Example with a fair six-sided die:
    • P (4) = S/T = 1/6
    • P (x >/= 5) = 2/6 = 33.3%

Properties of Probability

  • The sum of the probabilities for all possible outcomes is exactly 1 (i.e., 100% chance)
  • The probability of any given event (a) cannot exceed 1 and (b) cannot be less than 0.
  • An event having a probability of 1 means that no other event can possibly occur.
    • "In this world nothing can be said to be certain, except death and taxes." (Benjamin Franklin, 1789)
  • Related to 0, although you cannot speak of an event occurring less than never, you can have an event with 0 probability.
    • "You can't win the lottery if you don't buy a ticket."

Where did the Normal Distribution Come From?

  • Long ago many disciplines found that a particular bell-shaed or normal curve occurred often with many different variables (recall histograms).
  • Later in the 1700 s, mathematicians were called on to find an equation to approximate the normal distribution. If an equation could be developed then the probability associated with any point on the curve could be determined.
  • Until 1920, the development of the equation was attributed to Karl Friedrich Gauss, and it was known as the Gaussian curve.
  • Then Pearson, however, found this equation written earlier than Gauss's time in an article by Abraham DeMoivre in 1733 and renamed the curve as the normal distribution.
  • F (x): probability density function
  • 𝜇: 'Location' parameter (center), can be estimated by sample mean 𝑥.
  • 𝜎: 'Scale' parameter (spread), can be estimated by sample standard deviation s.
  • 𝜋 ≈ 3.14159
  • 𝑒 ≈ 2.71828

Normal Distribution

A Standard Curve

  • Normal distribution follows a very specific pattern:
    • Symmetric around the mean (mean = median = mode)
    • Bell-shaped
    • Unimodal
    • Aymptotic (the curve goes on forever on both sides of 𝜇)

A Family of Normal Curves

  • There are an infinite number of normal curves, one for every distinct pair of values of the mean and variance.
    • Every member of the family of normal curves has the same characteristics except that the scale, mean, and variance can differ across different variables and populations.
  • General Notation: 𝑥i~𝑁(𝜇, 𝜎2)
    • Xi follow a normal distribution with mean 𝜇 and variance (𝜎2).
    • Notice this notation reports variance (𝜎2), not standard deviation (𝜎).
    • From a sample: 𝑥i~𝑁(𝜇, 𝜎2)

A Continuous Curve

  • For any random variable x, there are an infinite number of possibilities even within a small range.
  • Theoretically, probability of x being a specific value is zero. P (x=xobs)=0
  • For continuous data, we find probability always within a range or interval, but we can make that interval as small as we like.
  • Probability:
    • Area under the curve between two values.

Probability: Area Under the Curve

  • Normal Curve (68-95-99.7 rule):
    • 68.3% of scores are expected to fall within 1 standard deviation of the mean. (𝑥 ± 1s)
    • 95.4% of scores are expected to fall within 2 standard deviations of the mean (𝑥 ± 2s)
    • 99.7% of scores are expected to fall within 3 standard deviations of the mean (𝑥 ± 3s)

Standard Normal Distribution (z-score)

  • We can convert any normal distribution to a standard normal distribution (aka unit normal distribution or standard unit normal distribution).
    • Z-score:
      • The standardized scores.
  • This allows us to evaluate an individual's performance relative to the scores in the distribution.
  • Make comparisons between two different individuals, or across two different variables.
  • Identify extreme values (outliers)
  • Notation: 𝑥i~𝑁(0,1)
    • Mean is 0.
    • Variance (and standard deviation) is 1.
  • To standardize, center mean to 0 and transform (stretch or compress) standard deviation to 1.
  • Equation: zi = (xi-𝜇)/𝜎
    • For sample data:
      • zi = (xi-𝜇)/s
    • Useful equation:
      • Xi=(𝜇+zi)(𝜎)
      • Xi=(x+zi)(s) for sample data

Z-Table and Area Under the Curve

  • A typical z-table reports cumulative probability given a z score (i.e., area under the curve to the left of z, think PRz=%).
  • We can determine any area under the curve (below or above a give z, or between any two z values).
  • We can also find a specific z value given the cumulative probability (think P%=z).

Use Z-Table to Confirm the 68-95-99.7 Rule

  • 68.3% of data within 1 standard deviation from the mean?
    • P (z ≤ 1) - P (z ≤-1)= 0.8413 - 0.1587 = 0.6826
  • 95.4% data within 2 standard deviations from the mean?
    • P (z ≤ 2) - P (z ≤ -2) = 0.9772 - 0.0228 = 0.9544
  • 99.7% data within 3 standard deviations from the mean?
    • P (z ≤ 1) - P (z ≤ -1) = 0.9987 - 0.0013 = 0.9974

Example: Household Income

  • In a recent census, the average income for several large rural counties was 𝜇 = $35, 285 and the standard deviation was 𝜎 = $7,200.
    • What proportion of families have incomes below $35,000?
      • Want to find P (x<35000)
      • Convert x ($) to z, given mean and standard deviation:
        • Z = (x-𝜇)/𝜎 = (35000-35285)/7200 = -0.04
        • So we are looking for P (z ≤ -0.04) = 0.4840
      • About 48.4% of families had household incomes below $35,000.
    • What was the 80th percentile household income?
      • Use z-table to find the z score with cumulative probability 80% (area is 0.8000).
      • Z = 0.845 (between 0.84 and 0.85 from table)
      • Convert to $ (recall z = (x-𝜇)/𝜎):
        • X = (𝜇 + z)(𝜎) = (35,285 + 0.845)(7200) ≈ 41369
      • The 80th percentile household income was $41,369.

Example: Practice

  • Learning a new instrument is exciting, but it takes consistent practicing to truly make music. A study shows aspiring musicians usually spend about 30 minutes practicing every day, with a standard deviation of 12.5 minutes. Assuming the practice time follows a normal distribution, what is the probability of a student practicing more than 70 minutes a day?
    • We want to find P (x > 70)
    • Convert x to z, given mean and standard deviation:
      • Z = (x-𝜇)/𝜎) = (70-30)/12.5 = 3.2
    • So we are looking for P (z > 3.2)
      • From z-table, P (z ≤ 3.2) = 0.9993 (table only gives area to the left. )
      • So P (z > 3.2) = 1-0.9993 = 0.0007
  • There is only a slight chance (0.07%) that a student would practice more than 70 minutes a day.

Example: Anxiety

  • The GAD-7 (Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item assessment) is a short questionnaire used to screen for anxiety. GAD-7 scores range from 0 to 21 and indicate the severity of generalized anxiety. In a clinical study based on 90 participants, the distribution of GAD-7 scores followed a normal distribution with a mean of 6.3 and a standard deviation of 2.8.
  • Given the descriptive statistics, how many participants were considered to have moderate anxiety?
    • Probability between 10 and 15 is P (x ≤ 15) - P (x ≤ 10).
      • I used range 10-15 to include the score of 14.
    • P (z ≤ (15-6.3)/2.8) - P (z ≤ 3.11) - P (z ≤ 1.32)
    • From z-table: 0.991 - 0.9066 = 0.0925 ≈ 9.3%
    • About 9.3% of the 90 participants had a GAD-7 score between 10 to 14. That is about 8 individuals (90 x 9.3% ≈ 8) who were considered to have moderate anxiety.

Example: Ohio's Weather in June

  • The average June daily high temperature in Ohio is 80˚F, with a standard deviation of 3 degrees. Normality assumed, what are the expected range (high and low) of daily high temperatures 95% of the time?
    • Find the middle 95% range from z-table: each tail is (1-0.95)/2 = (0.05/2) = 0.025
    • Z for upper limit (can be notated as z0.025): 1.96 (area to the left is 0.9750)
    • Z for lower limit: -1.96 (area to the left is 0.0250)
      • Notice the two z scores have the same value but different signs (symmetric).
    • Middle 95% range for z: 0 ± 1.96 or (-1.96, 1.96)
    • Convert to temperature (𝜇 ± 1.96 x 𝜎): 80 ± 1.96 x 3 = 80 ± 5.88 = (74.86)
    • 95% of the time, June daily high temperature in Ohio fluctuates between 74˚F and 86˚F.

Use SPSS to Convert a Variable to Z Scores (optional)

  • Analyze -> Descriptive Statistics -> Descriptives
    • Check option Save standardized values as variables.

Other Types of Standardized Scores (Optional)

  • Negative and decimal values occur in a standard normal distribution, thus other types of standardized scores have been developed, such as:
    • SAT (College Board): µ = 500, 𝜎 = 100
    • IQ: µ = 100, 𝜎 = 15
    • Normal Curve Equivalent (NCE):
      • µ = 50, 𝜎 = 21.06

Is the Data Normally Distributed?

  • Many analyses require the normality assumption (i.e., variables should be normally distributed in the populaiton). Most tests are robust against such violations if sample size is large (30).
  • However, non-normality is often associated with biased standard deviations, which affects decision making in hypothesis testing. If non-normality is severe, we might need to transform the data.
  • For non-normal and small samples, other alternative nonparametric tests are available.
  • Recall boxplot:
    • Is the median in the middle of the box?
  • Recall Central Tendency:
    • Is median close to the mean?
  • Compare Histogram (data) to normal curve (theory):
    • SPSS: In Histogram, check option Display normal curve.
  • Skewness and kurtosis ratios within ±2 or ±3.
  • Forthcoming:
    • Q-Q plot (quantile-quantile plot)
    • Tests for normality:
      • Kolmogorov-Smirnov test and Shapiro-Wilk test

Skewness

  • "Whenever the smallest or the largest observation is less than one standard deviation from the mean, this is evidence of severe skew." This suggests that the distribution of scores is very compressed on one side of the mean.
  • SPSS measures skewness by the 3 rd moment about the mean: ∑(z3/n)
    • If normal, this measure should be 0.
    • Negative (Skew to the left)
    • Positive (Skew to the right)
  • Skewed if standardized skewness |skew÷se (skew)| ≥ 2 (large sample) or ≥ 3 (small sample)
    • Se(skew) is the standard error of the skewness measure.

Kurtosis

  • Access "whether or not there are more or fewer extreme scores than expected in a normal distribution." Generally, kurtosis is less concerning than skewness.
  • SPSS measures kurtosis by the 4th moment about the mean: ∑(z4÷n) - 3
    • If normal (mesokurtic), this measure should be 0.
    • Negative kurtosis (platykurtic): flat or broad
    • Positive kurtosis (leptokurtic): slender or narrow
  • Non-normal if standardized kurtosis |kurtosis÷se (kurtosis)| ≥ 2 (large sample) or ≥ 3 (small sample)
    • is the standard error of the kurtosis measure.
