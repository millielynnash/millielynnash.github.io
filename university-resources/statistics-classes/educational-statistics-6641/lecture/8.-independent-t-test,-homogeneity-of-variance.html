  1. Independent T-Test, Homogeneity of Variance

Independent t-test

  • AKA two-sample t-test, independent-samples t-test or student's t-test.
  • Dependent (DV):
    • Scores being compared
  • Measured on at least an interval level scale
  • Often used to compare treatment and control groups.
  • Independent variance (IV): The group characteristics (dichotomous)
    • Intervention vs. No intervention
    • Medicine vs. Placebo
  • Typical research question:
    • Is there a mean difference between two groups?

Research Ideas (IV -> DV, Comparing Two Groups)

  • Socioeconomic status (2 levels)
  • Type of therapeutic intervention (2 types) -> Results of GAD-7 questionnaire over time
  • Genre-based approach (yes/no) -> Writing skills
  • Exposure to community violence (yes/no) -> Feelings of safety
  • Teaching method (2 kinds) -> Students scores on the exam (TOEFL)
  • Type of social emotional curriculum implemented in a school (2 types) -> classroom behavior, academic achievement scores
  • Teaching style (2 types) -> reading comprehension skills of EFL middle school students
  • Participant or non-participant in school sport -> student's cumulative grade point average.

Quasi-Experimental Designs with Control Groups

  • Untreated control group design with dependent pretest and posttest samples
    • NR (Not Random) O1 X O2
    • NR (Not Random) O1 O2
  • Untreated control group design with dependent pretest and posttest samples using a double pretest
    • NR O1 O2 X O3
    • NR O1 O2 O3
  • Untreated control group design with dependent pretest and posttest samples using switching replications
    • NR O1 x O2 (O3) x O4
    • NR O1 O2 (O3) x O4
  • Untreated control group design dependent pretest and posttest samples using reversed-treatment control group
    • NR O1 (pre) X O2 (post)
    • NR O1 (pre) X O2 (post)

Hypotheses (Independent t-test)

  • Nondirectional:
    • Test if group 1 (µ1) is significantly different from group 2 (µ2)
      • H0: µ2 = µ1 vs. H1: µ2 ≠ µ1
      • Alternatively, H0: µ2 - µ1 = 0 vs. H1: µ2 - µ1 ≠ 0
      • For experiment and control, usually group 1 is the experiment group.
  • Directional:
    • Test if group 1 (µ1) is significantly greater than group 2 (µ2):
      • H0: µ2 - µ1 ≤ 0 vs. H1: µ2 - µ1 > 0
    • Test if group 1 (µ1) is significantly smaller than group 2 (µ2):
      • H0: µ2 - µ1 ≥ 0 vs. H1: µ2 - µ1 < 0

Observed t

  • tobs = (1 - 2) ÷ (sx-bar 1 - sx-bar 2)
  • 1 and 2 are the means for group 1 and group 2, respectively.
  • sx-bar 1 - sx-bar 2 is the standard error of the difference between two means, which is the standard deviation of the sampling distribution of the difference between two means:
    • sx-bar 1 - sx-bar 2 = spooled √(1 ÷ n1) + (1 ÷ n2)
    • Where spooled is the pooled standard deviation computed as spooled = √ ((n1 - 1) s12 + (n2 - 1) s22) ÷ (n1 + n2 - 2) where n1 is group 1 sample size, and n2 is group 2 sample size.
      • Recall for paired-sample t-test:
        • sd-bar = sd ÷ √n

Critical t

  • The observed t is then compared against the critical value from the t-table to determine a statistical decision.
  • For independent t-test, tcrit has the degrees of freedom v = n1 + n2 - 2, at a particular level of significant 𝛼 (usually .05), for a one- or two-tailed test.
    • Recall rejection region is outside of tcrit (s) toward the tail(s): sample data more extreme!
  • Reject H0 if a positive tobs > tcrit (for negative values: tobs < tcrit)
  • Alternatively, reject H0 if p < 𝛼 (SPSS output)
    • 𝛼 level is usually .05 (95% confidence level)
  • Make sure to report t, df, and p-value to support your conclusion.

Confidence Interval

  • Nondirectional:
    • 100 (1 - 𝛼)% CI:
      • (1 - 2) ± tdf, 𝛼/2 x (sx-bar 1 - sx-bar 2)
    • Recall for paired-sample t-test:
      • 100 (1 - 𝛼)% CI:
        • ± tdf, 𝛼/2 x sd-bar
  • To test greater (treatment group is greater):
    • 100 (1 - 𝛼)% LCL:
      • (1 - 2) - tdf, 𝛼/2 x (sx-bar 1 - sx-bar 2)
  • To test smaller (treatment group is smaller):
    • 100 (1 - 𝛼)% UCL:
      • (1 - 2) + tdf, 𝛼/2 x (sx-bar 1 - sx-bar 2)

SPSS

  • DV is usually recorded as one variable.
  • IV is usually recorded as another variable to identify groups (dichotomous, or pick 2 groups at a time to compare).
  • Analyze -> Compare Means -> Independent-Samples t-test
  • Move DV to Test Variable(s)
  • Move IV to Grouping Variable and define groups (Group 1 and Group 2)
    • If there are treatment and control groups, group 1 is usually treatment and group 2 is control.
  • The mean difference is group 1 minus group 2. You can refer to the descriptive statistics to confirm which group is higher.

Homogeneity of Variance (HOV)

  • AKA homoscedasticity
  • When comparing means, we need the dispersion to be similar in both groups. The distortion in spread can interfere with our investigation of difference between means.
  • HOV assumes population variances for the two groups are equal. If design is balanced (n1 = n2), no need to worry about departure from HOV (independent t-test is robust in this case).
  • HOV tests are sensitive to violations of other assumptions about the data, especially normality. So there are many versions of this test:
    • An easy way (by hand) is the F-max test (aka Hartley's F-max test).
    • SPSS uses Levene's HOV test for independent t-test.

F-Max Test

  • This is the first time we do not reject the null hypothesis.
  • F has two degrees of freedom
  • H0: 𝜎12 = 𝜎22 vs. H0: 𝜎12 ≠ 𝜎22
    • Or alternatively,
      • H0: 𝜎12 ÷ 𝜎22 = 1 vs. H0: 𝜎12 ÷ 𝜎22 ≠ 1 (whether the ratio of 2 variances equals 1)
  • For convenience we generally put the larger variance in the numerator and smaller variance in the denominator, and the ratio follows an F-distribution:
    • Fmax = (s2) (larger) ÷ (s2) (smaller)
  • Fmax is compared to a critical F value. For this test, we hope to retain (cannot reject) H0 so HOV is tenable (satisfactory)!
    • Small Fmax or big p-value are good (i.e., equal variances)!
F Distribution
  • The F distribution is usually used to compare variances. Since variances are always a squared value and always non-negative, the F statistic does not go below 0, and it is not symmetric.
  • The F-statistics has two sets of degrees of freedom:
    • Numerator (v1) and denominator (v2). The pair of degrees of freedom determine the shape of the F distribution. When comparing two groups (sample sizes n1 and n2), v1 = n1 - 1 and ), v2 = n2 -1.
  • If F-max test, v1 (dfnum) is the degree of freedom for group with larger variance, and v2 (dfdenom) is the degree of freedom for the group with smaller variance.
  • Reject H0 if Fmax ≥ F (α, dfnum, dfdenom) but we don't want to reject it!
F-Max Test Example: July 4th
  • Fmax = 375.225 ÷ 297.065 ≈ 1.263
  • Critical F (. 05, 25, 21) = 2.045
  • Since 1.263 < 2.045, cannot reject H0
  • Fireworks spending for people living in the city has a variance comparable to that of in the country (HOV satisfactory)!

Levene's HOV Test

  • H0: 𝜎12 = 𝜎22 = ... = 𝜎k2 vs. H1: At least 2 group variances are different (k = 2 for our purpose)
  • [Optional] Test statistics Fobs
    • Total sample size n divided into k groups (j = 1, 2, ..., k); We are just comparing 2 groups (k = 2)
    • Sample size for each group is n1, n2, ..., nj, ..., nk
    • i denotes the ith subject (data point) in group j
    • zij = | xij - xj | is the absolute deviation from a data point to jth group mean
      • Brown and Forsythe extended this to median or 10% trimmed mean
    • Numerator:
      • Between group variation (in terms of absolute deviation)
    • Denominator:
      • Within group variation (in terms of absolute deviation)
  • We would reject H0 if Fobs > Fk - 1, n - k (here k = 2)
    • Alternatively, reject H0 if p < .05
  • Since we want to find no statistically significant difference in variances between the two groups, a p ≥ .05 (big p-value) is desired here!
  • We will let SPSS do Levene's HOV test for us! Just remember if we see a big p-value (p-value ≥ .05), HOV is tenable (satisfactory). If p-value is small (p < .05), HOV is violated.

Welch's t-test (When HOV is Violated)

  • The Welch's t-test (aka separate variances t-test, unequal variances t-test) is usually appropriate when the population variances are unequal and the sample sizes are unequal.
  • [Optional]
    • tobs = unstandardized b ÷ standard error
    • Variance errors of the mean are computed for each group where s12 are sample variances for groups.
  • The criteria to reject H0 are the same as independent t-test (extreme t or small p).
  • We'll let SPSS do Welch's t-test for us! Just remember if we see a small p-value (p < .05), we can reject H0 and claim difference between the two group means is significantly different from 0 (or one-sided p-value to test greater/smaller).
  • Make sure to report t, df, and p-value to support your conclusion.

Effect Size (Independent t-test)

  • Cohen's d = (1 - 2) ÷ spooled
  • Size of Cohen's d (by convention):
    • d = ±0.2: Small effect
    • d = ±0.5: Medium effect
    • d = ±0.8: Large effect
  • Use Cohen's d when HOV is satisfactory and sample size is large (total n at least 40 or 50)

More Effect Sizes (Independent t-test)

  • Hedges' g ≈ (1 - 2) ÷ spooled (1 - 3 ÷ 4n - 9)
    • Total sample size n = n1 + n2
    • Provides a bias correction to Cohen's d for small sample sizes
    • Uses Hedges' g when HOV is satisfactory and sample size is small (n < 40 or 50)
  • Glass's Delta (∆) = (1 - 2) ÷ spooled
    • Uses only the control group's (group 2 in SPSS) standard deviation
    • Use Glass's delta when HOV is violated (variances significantly different between groups)
  • Use the same convention as Cohen's d to interpret Hedge's g or Glass's delta.

Assumptions (Independent t-test)

  • Measure or characteristic is normally distributed in each of the two populations (i.e., the two groups).
  • Observations are from a random sample, and the scores in each sample (people) are independent of each other.
  • DV is measured on at least an interval level scale.
  • HOV (homogeneity of variance):
    • The variances in the two populations are comparable. If violated (and design is not balanced), use Welch's t-test (separate variances t-test) instead.

What Test Should I Use to Compare Means?

  • Is vitamin C effective to treat colds? A health-researcher recruited a sample of 27 adults and followed them for a year to assess the number of colds they had in year 1. She then had them take weekly doses of vitamin C and followed them in year 2.
    • Paired because it is a pre to post.
  • A social studies teacher wanted to determine if the students in his school in the US have similar perceptions of world leaders compared to other countries. Where leaders are rated from 0 (worthless) to 10 (effective), the global norm on the 'World Leader Scale' is 6 with a standard deviation of 1.8. The teacher took a sample of 35 students from the school and had them fill out the same WLS.
    • One sample z-test
  • A counseling psychologist wanted to determine if prisoners who are depressed eat less of their meals than prisoners who are not depressed. There were 130 prisoners who volunteered for the study, and 42 of them were classified as depressed. The psychologist monitored the amount of each meal in ounces eaten by both groups for a week.
    • Independent t-test
  • A random sample of 39 museum visitors were questioned on how much they spend per month on cultural or arts-related events. The survey found that, on average, these visitors spent $40 per month with a standard deviation of $12. The researcher wanted to test whether the monthly expenditure for museum visitors is below $45.
    • One sample t-test
