Lecture

Frequency Table, Bar Chart, and Pie Chart

  • Useful for nominal and ordinal variables.
  • Report counts and/or percentages

Representation of Interval/Ratio Variables

  • A frequency table, bar chart, or pie chart might not be meaningful to depict interval/ratio variables.
  • More graphs and statistics are available to represent interval/ratio variables.

Stem-and-Leaf Plots

  • Similar to a frequency table, but data is categorized into groups.
  • Show both raw data and shape of distribution (like a histogram).
  • First find range of data (Max - Min) then break into multiple groups with equal intervals.
  • Stem:
    • Categories
    • Groups
  • Leaf:
    • Data points
  • Key:
    • An example of the stem and leaf to show what number it represents. This allows readers to determine the placement of each digit in the stem and leaf. (SPSS reports the stem width)
      • Example: 5|2 = 52

Frequency and Relative Frequency

  • Frequency (Absolute Frequency):
    • The number of times the observation occurred/recorded in an experiment or study.
  • Relative Frequency (between 0 and 1):
    • The ratio of the frequency of a particular event in a statistical experiment to the total frequency.
  • Can be reported with intervals or real limits (the lower or upper value for a continuous variable measured on a ratio scale). For example:
    • Interval: 50-59
    • Real Limit: (49.5-59.5)

Histogram

  • Visualize frequency of data.
    • X-axis label can be midpoints of interval, or real limits,
    • Y-axis can be frequency or relative frequency
  • The bars are equal intervals and continuous data (no gaps).
  • Show the shape of the distribution.
  • SPSS:
    • Can modify the bin width in Chart Editor -> select columns -> Binning

Cumulative Relative Frequency

  • Proportion of scores from this category and lower.
  • Related to percentile rank:
    • Percentage of scores in a distribution that are equal to or less than that score.

  • Y-axis: Cumulative Relative Frequency (or Cumulative Frequency)

Find Percentile Ranks from an Ogive: PR data = %

  • The percentile rank of a given score is the percent of observations falling at or below that score.
  • Start at the bottom (x-axis) to locate the score of interest, then move up to the ogive and over to the Y-axis.
  • Example:
    • Percentile rank for $50,000?
      • PR $50,000 = 61%
      • 61% of global customer support managers earn salary at or below $50,000.
  • Every data point in a distribution has an associated percentile.
  • Start on the Y-axis to locate the percent of interest, then move horizontally to the ogive and then down to the X-axis.
  • Example: Find 80th percentile (P 80)
    • P 80= $66,000 (approximately)
    • About 80% of global customer support managers earn salary at or below $66,000.

Use SPSS to Create an Ogive

  • First make sure the measurement level has been set properly for the variable.
  • Go to Graphs -> Chart Builder
  • In Gallery, select Line and drag Simple Line to the preview section
    • Mat gave to hit Reset from previous charts.
  • Under Element Properties, Statistic, select Cumulative Percentage
    • The preview does not make much sense, but it is okay.
  • Just submit to see the actual graph.

Boxplot (Box and Whisker): General Form

  • Minimum (or 10th percentile, P10)
  • Q1: First Quartile (or 25th percentile, P25)
  • Median (or 10 th percentile, P50)
  • Q3: Third Quartile (or 75th percentile, P75)
  • Maximum (or 90th percentile, P90)
  • Each sections 25% of data.
  • The box contains 50% of data:
    • Interquartile range IQR = Q3 - Q1

Boxplot: SPSS Modifications

  • Minimum: Q 1 = 1.5 X IQR (the data point above it)
  • Maximum: Q 3 + 1.5 X IQR (the data point below it)
  • Outliers (Circles): More than 1.5 IQR from the upper or lower of the box
  • Extreme values (asterisks): More than 3 times the IQR!

What Information does a Boxplot Show?

  • Box plots are useful as they provide a visual summary of the data enabling resarchers to quickly identify:
    • Median (center) of data
    • Dispersion (spread) of data
    • Skewness of data
    • Potential outliers/extreme values
  • Box Plot Explained: Interpretation, Examples, & Comparison

Scatterplot

  • Depict the relationship between two variables by plotting the pair of scores on x and y for each record on a two-dimensional figure (coordinate).
    • X-axis represents the values for variable x (usually IV).
    • Y-axis represents the values for variable y (usually DV).
  • The scatterplot is useful for providing a quick indication of the nature of the relationship among variables x and y (bivariate data).

How to Read a Scatterplot?

  • Direction of the relationship (positive or negative):
    • A positive relationship occurs when both variables are increasing.
    • A negative (inverse) relation occurs when one variable is increasing as the other is decreasing.
    • Can draw a straight line (aka regression line) through the data points that cuts the bivariate distribution in hald. The slope of the regression line shows the direction of relationship.
    • Alternatively, split the chart into 4 equal sections (quadrants) and examine how data points are spread across the 4 quadrants.
  • Strength of the relationship:
    • Strong relationships: Data points tend to fall close to the regression line.
    • Weak relationship: Data points are widely scattered around the regression line.

Frequency Tables

  • Also referred to as Demographics Table
  • In research, typically used to describe your sample
    • Sex/Gender, Race/Ethnicity, Level of Education, etc.
  • Only used to display discrete data.
  • In SPSS:
    • Analyze -> Descriptive Statistics -> Frequencies -> drag variable (s) of interest (VOI) into 'Variables' box -> select 'Display Frequency Tables' box in bottom left-hand corner of the menu.
  • Unfortunately, SPSS does not provide us with output that is 'APA-Ready'
  • In order to create an APA-style frequency (demographics) table, you need to use Excel (or similar program)

Bar Graphs

  • When do we use them?
    • Nominal or ordinal data (discrete data)
  • There are two ways to produce Bar Graphs, each produces the same graph.
  • SPSS:
    • Analyze -> Descriptive Statistics -> Frequencies -> Charts -> 'Bar Charts'
    • Graphs -> Chart Builder -> drag 'Bar' to the previewer, place variable of interest on x-axis.
  • Note that the bars are separated by some space and do not touch one another, reflecting the nature of nominal data being discrete.

Pie Chart

  • When do we use them?
    • Nominal or ordinal data (discrete data)
  • In SPSS:
    • Analyze -> Descriptive Statistics -> Frequencies -> Charts -> 'Pie Charts'
    • Graphs -> Chart Builder -> drag 'Pie/Polar' to the previewer, place variable of interest on x-axis.

Histograms

  • When do we use them?
    • Displaying interval and ratio data (continuous data)
    • Checking for the shape of the distribution
      • Skewness, normality
  • There are three different ways to produce histograms using SPSS, but each produces the same graph.
    • Typically making them while producing other descriptive statistics, so that will guide which menu you use.
  • In SPSS:
    • Analyze -> Descriptive Statistics -> Frequencies -> Charts -> 'Histogram'
    • Analyze -> Descriptive Statistics -> Explore -> Plots -> 'Histogram'
    • Graphs -> Chart Builder -> drag 'Histograms' to the previewer -> drag the variable of interest to the x-axis.

Shape of the Distribution

  • Normal Distribution:
    • Mode = Median = Mean
  • Negatively Skewed (Skewed to the Left, tail toward the left):
    • Mode > Median > Mean
  • Positively Skewed (Skewed to the right, tail toward the right):
    • Mode < Median < Mean

Stem & Leaf Plots

  • Used to display interval and ratio data.
  • Not used often in publications, but can be useful for visualizing your data.
  • In SPSS:
    • Analyze -> Descriptive Statistics -> Explore -> Plots -> 'Stem-and-Leaf'

Box Plots

  • Not used as much in academic publications, but still valuable in visualizing your data.
  • In SPSS:
    • Analyze -> Descriptive Statistics -> Explore -> Plots -> it is clicked by default
    • Graphs -> Chart Builder -> drag 'Boxplot' to the previewer -> drag the variable of interest to the x-axis and drag the scale variable to the y-axis.

Shape of the Distribution

  • The box in the center displays the middle 50% of the distribution.
  • The bottom edge of the box represents the 25 th percentile (or Q 1)
  • The top edge of the box represents the 75 th percentile (or Q 3)
  • The middle thick vertical line in the box represents the 50 th percentile (also known as Q 2 or the median)
  • The lines extending from the box are known as the whiskers. The purpose of whiskers is to display data outside of the middle 50%.
  • Range:
    • Min - Max
  • Shape of the Distribution:

Polygons/OGIVE/Line Graph

  • Used to display interval and ratio data
  • Not used that often (histograms are the preferred display)
  • In SPSS:
    • Graphs -> Chart Builder -> drag 'Line' to the previewer -> drag the VOI to the x-axis
      • On the right, change statistics to 'Cumulative Percentage'
        1. On the output window, double click on the graph and click 'Show Grid Lines'
        2. Select 'Both major and minor ticks' then click 'Apply'

Percentile and Percentile Ranks

  • Percentile
    • Score below which a certain percentage of the distribution lies
    • P% = score
  • Percentile Rank
    • Percentage of a distribution of scores that falls below (or is less than a certain score)
    • PRscore = %

Finding the Percentiles and Quartiles

  • Analyze -> Descriptive Statistics -> Frequencies -> drag VOI into the box -> Statistics -> select Quartiles and Percentiles -> type the number into the box to the right of percentiles and click 'add.'
    • This gives you the percentile of the number and the first quartile (25 th percentile), second quartile (median / 50 th percentile) and third quartile (75 th percentile).

Scatter Plot

  • A plot of two scale (interval or ratio) variables
  • Helps to visually identify possible relationship between two variables.
  • Graphs -> Chart Builder -> drag 'Scatter/Dot' to the previewer -> drag one of the VOI to the x-axis and the other to y-axis.

SPSS Toolbar Shortcuts

Computing New Variables from Existing Ones

  • In SPSS, you can create new variables from old variables.
    1. In the menu bar, go to transform.
    2. Select Compute Variable.
    3. In the pop-up window that shows up, give a name for the new target variable.
    4. Define your new variable in the Numeric Expression Box
    5. Click OK

Recoding Variables

  • Recoding variables in SPSS involves altering variable values to create a new modified variable, typically done during data preparation and analysis to better suit analysis needs or simplify the data.
    1. In the menu bar, go to Transform.
    2. Select Recode to Different Variables.
  • It is not recommended to Recode into Same Variables as this overwrites existing variables.
