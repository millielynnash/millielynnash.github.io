No... Kind Of?... Normal-ish?

  • A new theoretical distribution for obtaining the critical values was developed:
    • T-distribution (student's t-distribution)
  • Developed by English statistician William Sealy Gosset under the pseudonym 'Student.'
  • Symmetric and bell-shaped like the normal distribution, but t-distribution has heavier tails (i.e., more prone to producing values that fall far from its mean).
  • The shape of t-distribution depends on degrees of freedom (df or v).
  • Shape of t-distribution depends on df (or sample size)

How to Trick SPSS to Report LCL or UCL for Directional Tests?

  • SPSS reports both one-sided and two-sided p-values, but only two-sided CI is reported.
  • To obtain one-sided upper or lower confidence limits, run a test doubling the ùõº (alpha) level
    • This doe snot affect the p-values reported in the output.
  • Say if you are conducting a directional test a 95% confidence level and want to see LCL or UCL, you can change the confidence interval percentage from 95% to 90% in Options.
  • The lower end of reported 90% CI would be 95% LCL (Lower Control Limit), and the upper end of reported 90% CI would be 95% (Upper Control Limit).
    • Any variation detected inside the control limits probably occurred by chance. On the other hand, variation outside of the control limits likely occurred due to special causes. The upper control limit (UCL) and the lower control limit (LCL) serve as boundaries for expected deviation in data.

Statistical Power

  • Definition:
    • The probability that the test correctly rejects the null hypothesis when it's false (i.e., a specific alternative hypothesis is true).
  • Power is determined by the following factors:
    • Factors that can be controlled by the researcher:
      • Level of significance (Œ±): As Œ± increases, power increases.
      • Sample size (n): As n increases, power increases.
      • Non-directional or directional test: Power is greater for a directional (one-tailed) test.
    • Factors that cannot be controlled by the researcher:
      • Size of population standard deviation (œÉ): As œÉ increases, power decreases.
      • ‚Ä¢Difference between true population mean (Œº) and hypothesized mean (Œº0): As difference increases, power increases.

Use Bonferroni Correction to Control Type I Error

  • When multiple hypotheses are tested (say m), the probability of observing a rare event increases, and the likelihood of incorrectly rejecting H0 (i.e., Type I error) increases.
  • Bonferroni correction, named after Carlo Bonferroni, counteracts this problem by testing each individual hypothesis at a significance level of Œ± √∑ m, controlling desired overall (familywise) Œ± level.
  • The simplest and most conservative method to control Type I error in multiple hypothesis testing.

Example: Senior Exit Survey

  • 6 t-tests were conducted (m = 6).
  • To control the overall (familywise) Œ± level at .05, with Bonferroni correction, each item‚Äôs p-value is now compared to the critical value Œ± √∑ m = .05 √∑ 6 = .008.
  • The 2nd question (p = .021) is no longer significant after Bonferroni correction. The 6th question (p = .003) remain significant.
  • Can also create more conservative CI‚Äôs: 100 (1 - Œ± / m)%: 99.2% CI‚Äôs are wider than 95% CI‚Äôs, making them more likely to cover the test value (Œº0).

Problem of Non-Normality

  • One-sample z-test and one-sample t-test are considered robust for violations of normal distribution (i.e., assumption can be violated without serious errors). CLT assures that, for a large sample, the sampling distribution of the mean will be approximately normal regardless of the shape of population.
  • However, when sample size is very small (e.g., n < 5), the test is not robust to non-normality. (Some studies also found the test is not as robust for one-tailed tests even with large samples). Non-normality can inflate the standard error of the mean, reduce power, and affect control of Type I error. Other non-parametric alternatives are available. They do not make assumptions on distribution.
