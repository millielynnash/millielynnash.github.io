  1. Descriptive Statistics

Why Do We Need Descriptive Statistics?

  • What do we do when we have research questions about a large sample of data? Carrying around the entire collection of data would be very difficult.
  • Descriptive statistics summarize the data in an abbreviated fashion, but they provide sufficient information to depict the data.

Math Symbols

  • 𝑥i:
    • The score for ith person in the study or data set on variable x.
  • Taking the sum of a set of scores is utilized quite often in statistics so we need a common system of notation for communicating about summation.
  • En=1 xi:
    • The sum of all data points of variable x.
      • i=1 is the lower limit or beginning of the summation.
      • i=n refers to the last person or the end of a data set.

Summation Example

  • Ages from a sample of 10 coworkers: 24, 26, 28, 28, 31, 31, 31, 35, 38, 46
  • n= 10 observations (subjects)
    • Use N to notate size of the entire population.
    • E10=1 xi = 24+26+28+28+31+31+31+35+38+47 = 318
      • Excel: =SUM (data range)
      • All excel formulas in this class are optional (but useful)

Measures of Central Tendency

  • A single index or value summarizing the entire collection of scores. Three most popular measures of central tendency are mean, median, and mode.
    • Mode:
      • Most common or frequent value
    • Median (Mdn):
      • 50th percentile (or Q 2) or the middlemost value of an ordered set of scores.
    • Mean:
      • Arithmetic average

Mode

  • The value in a distribution of scores that occurs most frequently. Sometimes there is no mode, and sometimes there are multiple modes.
  • When there are two modes (bimodal distributions), some people report both modes. If they are adjacent, some people average the two modes. Goal is to faithfully describe the distribution of your data.
  • Coworker example: 24, 26, 28, 28, 31, 31, 31, 35, 38, 46
  • A new guy just joined the team:
    • 24, 26, 28, 28, 28, 31, 31, 31, 35, 38, 46
  • Can be used with any type of measurement scales.
  • Pros: simple to obtain, the only appropriate measure for nominal data.
  • Cons: does not always have a unique value, not a function of all scores, difficult to deal with mathematically.

Median

  • We already know a lot about median (P50 or Q 2):
    • Middle most value in an ordered set of data.
  • After ordering the data, locate the ((n+1)/2) th observation.
  • Coworker example (odd number):
    • 24, 26, 28, 28, 28, 31, 31, 31, 35, 38, 46
  • New guy just quit (even number):
    • 24, 26, 28, 28, 31, 31, 31, 35, 38, 46
  • Can be used with ordinal, interval, and ratio scales.
  • Pros:
    • Not influenced by extreme scores (outliers)
    • Always has a unique value
  • Cons:
    • Not a function of all scores
    • Difficult to deal with mathematically

Mean

  • Sample Mean = (sum of xi)/n

  • Coworker example:

    • 318/10 = 31.8
    • Excel: =Average (data range)

  • Used to estimate population mean

    • The larger our sample, the better our estimate of the true population mean.

  • Only appropriate for interval and ratio measurement scales.

  • Pros:

    • Function of every score.

    • Always has a unique value.

    • Easy to deal with mathematically.

    • Most stable of the measures of central tendency.

  • Cons:

    • Influenced by extreme scores.

Extreme Scores Influencing the Mean

  • Very unusual or 'extreme'values tend to draw the mean towards these values and contribute to skew in the data.
  • Coworker example:
    • 24, 26, 28, 28, 28, 31, 31, 31, 35, 38, 46
      • Mean: 31.8
      • Median: 31
      • Mode: 31
  • A retired guy just rejoined the team: 24, 26, 28, 28, 28, 31, 31, 31, 35, 38, 46, 75
    • Mean: 393/11 = 35.7
    • Median: 31
    • Mode: 31

Central Tendency and Symmetry

  • For samples from a symmetric distribution (e.g., normal), mean, median, and mode should be very close to each other.
  • When mean, median, and mode are far from each other, the data is skewed.
    • Skewed to the right (long right tail, positive skew): Extreme large values
    • Skewed to the left (long left tail, negative skew): Extreme small values

Averaging Groups vs. Individuals

  • Sometimes we may have the means of several groups of people, and we still want to find the average. Two ways to do this:
    • Weighted Average (average all the original scores):
      • Takes into account of each group's sample size. Average is appropriate 'weighted' by number of observations in each group.
    • Unweighted Average (simply take the mean of the averages):
      • Does not 'weight' or take account of the different sample sizes.
        • AKA: mean of means, grand mean, usually used for group comparison.
  • Example (Weight Average): 8150/100=81.5
  • Unweighted average: (85+80+75)/3=80

Measures of Dispersion

  • The mean alone is not enough to tell us about the shape of a distribution.
  • Measures of dispersion describe the amount of variability among the scores (i.e., do the scores fall close to the central tendency measure or are the scores spread out?)
  • There are no measures of dispersion appropriate for nominal data.
  • We already know two measures of dispersion: Range and Interquartile range (IQR)
  • Both are measures of dispersion, but not a function of all scores.
  • They are informative measures, but don't have very strong statistical properties.

Review Range and Interquartile Range (IQR)

Range

  • Difference between the largest and smallest scores.
  • Pros:
    • Simple to obtain.
  • Cons:
    • Influenced by extreme scores, only a function of two of the scores, unstable from sample to sample.

Interquartile Range (IQR)

  • Difference between the third quartile (Q 3) and the first quartiles (Q 1)
  • AKA H spread (stands for hinge, middle 50% of the distribution), developed by Tukey.
  • Pros:
    • Unaffected by extreme scores.
  • Cons:
    • Not a function of all scores, not very stable from sample to sample.

Variance

  • The 'average' squared deviation from the mean for a set of scores.
  • Variance is a better measure of dispersion than range and IQR, because it takes into account all the values for a set of scores (but might be affected by extreme values). Conceptually, the variance is a measure of the area of the distribution (i.e., the more spread out the scores, the more area or space the distribution takes up).

Why is the Denominator (N-1) for Sample Variance?

  • The sample mean might not be the same as the population mean (usually unknown), and the deviations would be affected. Therefore, sample variance obtained would be systematically too small.
  • In order to get an unbiased sample estimate of the population variance, a slight change in the computational formula was made.
    • AKA Degree of Freedom (df)
Degree of Freedom (df)
  • When we calculate a simple statistic based in part on another statistic, we have to consider the 'degrees of freedom' in our calculation.
  • Given the sample mean, we do not need raw data from all n observations. We only need (n-1) observations, because the last missing score can be found from the mean (i.e., there is no 'freedom' in what this score might be).
  • The denominator (n-1) in sample variance is called the degrees of freedom (df), which is related to sample size.

Why So Many Formulas?

  • You may see many different forms of formulas out there to compute variances and forthcoming descriptive statistics.
  • They all come from the same 'definitional' formula, but after being modified/simplified given the mathematical properties, some 'computational' formulas are easier to deal with by hand.
  • But excel or SPSS will compute them for us anyway.

Standard Deviation (SD)

  • Since variance is a squared value, it is difficult to interpret.
  • The standard deviation (SD) is the square root of the variance, and it is the most common measure of dispersion.
  • For population: 𝜎 = square root of 𝜎2
  • For sample: 𝜎 = square root of 𝜎2
  • Measured on the same scale as original data.
  • Interpretation:
    • On average, the deviations from the mean, but on average the distance from the mean is about 2.92.
  • The 68-95-99.7 rule (more next time):
    • About 68% of data points fall within +/- 1 standard deviation from the mean.
    • About 95% of data points fall within +/- 2 standard deviations from the mean.
    • About 99.7% of data points fall within +/- 3 standard deviations from the mean.

Covariance

  • Measure the shared variance (or co-variance) between variables x and y.
  • Difficult to interpret, because the value of covariance depends on the scale of the variables. Other measures are needed.

Pearson Product-Moment Correlation Coefficient

  • Correlation coefficients are measures of relationship that are easier to interpret. The first correlation coefficient we consider is the Pearson correlation coefficient (usually notated as r), which measures the relationship between two interval or ratio variables.

Properties of Pearson Correlation Coefficient

  • Pearson r ranges from -1 to +/- 1
  • The sign denotes the direction of the relationship (positive or negative).
  • The value denotes the strength:
    • 0 is the weakest correlation.
    • +1 and -1 are the strongest correlation.
  • Correlation does not imply causation.
  • Don't need the leading zero in APA style writing, because r does not go beyond +/-1.
