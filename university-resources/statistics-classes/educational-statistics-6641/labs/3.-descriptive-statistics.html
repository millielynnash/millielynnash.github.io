Measures of Central Tendency

  • Advantages and Disadvantages of Measures of Central Tendency
Measure of Central TendencyAdvantagesDisadvantages
Mode- Quick and easy method for reporting central tendency
- Can be used with any measurement scale of variable		- Does not always have a unique value
- Not a function of all scores in the distribution
- Difficult to deal with mathematically due to its instability
Median- Not influenced by extreme scores has a unique value
- Can be used with ordinal, interval, and ratio measurement scales of variables		- Not a function of all scores in the distribution
- Difficult to deal with mathematically due to its instability
- Cannot be used with nominal data
Mean- Function of all scores in the distribution
- Has a unique value
- Easy to deal with mathematically		- Influenced by extreme scores
- Cannot be used with nominal or ordinal variables
Range- Simple to comput
- Can be used with ordinal, interval, and ratio measurement scales of variables		- Influenced by extreme scores
- Function of only two scores
- Unstable from sample to sample
- Cannot be used with nominal data
H spread- Unaffected by extreme scores
- Can be used with ordinal, interval, and ratio measurement scales of variables		- Not a function of all scores in the distribution
- Difficult to deal with mathematically due to its instability
- Cannot be used with nominal data
Variance and Standard Deviation (SD)- Function of all scores in the distribution
- Useful for deriving other statistics
- Can be used with interval and ratio measurement scales of variables		- Influenced by extreme scores
- Cannot be used with nominal or ordinal variables

Descriptive Statistics: Frequencies Menu

  • Analyze -> Descriptive Statistics -> Frequencies -> drag VOI(s) into the 'Variable(s)' screen
    • Under statistics, you can choose from a wide variety of statistics.
    • You can also create the frequency distribution figures we discussed previous weeks under the Charts menu

Descriptives Menu

  • Analyze -> Descriptive Statistics -> Descriptives -> drag VOI(s) into the 'Variable(s)' screen.
  • Under Options, you have the ability to choose from a slightly less wide variety of statistics.

Explore Menu

  • Analyze -> Descriptive Statistics -> Explore -> drag VOI(s) into the 'Dependent List' screen
  • Under Statistics, choose 'Descriptives'
    • You don't have the ability to choose what descriptive statistics you want, but this menu gives you almost all 'Descriptives'
  • Under Plots, you can also produce box-plot, histogram, and stem-and-leaf plot

Descriptive Statistics of Groups

  • You can get descriptive information based on different groups in your dataset.
    • Drag any categorical variables you are interested in into the Factor List box.
  • This will produce separate charts and figures for each of the values in the variable you place in Factor List

Compare Means Menu

  • Means
    • Analyze -> Compare Means and Proportions -> Means and Proportions -> Means -> drag dependent variable into the 'Dependent List' box
      • Under Options, select the descriptive statistics you're interested in.
    • You can also use this menu without comparing groups

Pearson Correlation

  • Both variables x and y are interval and/or ratio level variables (scale variables)
  • Population correlation is denoted by p (rho)
  • Sample correlation is denoted by r:
    • -1 < r < 1
      • -1.00:
        • a perfect negative relationship
      • 0:
        • No linear relationship
      • +1.00:
        • A perfect positive relationship
  • Characteristics of Correlations:
    • Direction:
      • Positive
      • Negative
    • Strength:
      • The actual number calculated
        • Can be any number from -1 to +1
    • A high correlation does not imply that one variable is causing the other; it simply means that these two variables are related in some fashion.
      • Correlation does not imply causation.

Visualizing Correlations

Scatterplot // SPSS

  • Graphs -> Chart Builder -> drag 'Simple Scatterplot' into the preview box -> drag one VOI to the x-axis and one VOI to the y-axis.
  • Graphs -> Chart Builder -> drag 'Scatterplot Matrix' into the preview box -> drag all VOI to Scatter matrix box

Correlation // SPSS

  • Analyze -> Correlate -> Bivariate -> drag VOIs into 'Variables' box
    • Under 'Correlation Coefficients' select which type of correlation you want (Pearson)
    • Under 'Test of Significance' select which type of test you want, based on your hypothesis.
  • Steps:
    1. Double click your table
    2. Click 'Pivot Controls'
    3. Drag 'Statistics' into the 'layer' box
    4. Select Pearson Correlation

Calculating Correlation from Covariance

  • Covariance
    • Shared variance among x and y.
  • Correlation is simply the standardized covariance.

Correlations and Covariances (SPSS)

  • Options -> Statistics -> Click Means and Standard Deviations AND Cross-Product Deviations and Covariances
