1. Independent Samples t-test

Independent Samples t-tests

  • Used when we are comparing two separate groups of people in our sample
    • Example:
      • Boys vs. Girls (Quasi-experimental design); Treatment vs. Control (Experimental Design)
  1. State the H0 and H1
  2. Define alpha level
  3. Compute t statistic
  4. Interpret the output
  5. Make decision
  6. Write conclusion

Example

  • Fred wishes to determine whether children in regular education are hurt or helped academically by the inclusion of special education students in their classes. He has gained the cooperation of the seventh-grade teachers in two similar junior high schools to participate in his study. One school recently integrated special education students into their regular classroom, while the other one has not. The teachers in both school supply Fred the students’ raw scores on a standardized achievement test at the beginning of the year and at the end of the year.
    • SPSS file contains 40 cases and 3 variables:
      • Integ (1: regular education students who are not in integrated classroom; 2. Regular education students who are in the integrated classroom)
      • Pretest (achievement test score of regular education students at the beginning of the year)
      • Posttest (achievement test score of regular education students at the end of the year)
    • Research Questions:
      • Is there a mean difference in pretest achievement scores between regular education students who are not in integrated classroom and in the integrated classroom?
  1. State the H0 and H1
    • H0: 𝜇pretest not integrated = 𝜇pretest integrated
    • H1: 𝜇pretest not integrated ≠ 𝜇pretest integrated
  2. Define alpha level
    • 𝛼 = .05
  3. Compute t statistic
    • In SPSS:
      • Analyze -> Compare Means -> Independent-Samples t-test -> drag dependent variable (s) into the 'Test Variable (s)' box > drag the categorical variable into the 'Grouping Variable' box -> Define Groups -> enter the values of your grouping variables into 'Group 1' and 'Group 2' respectively
      • t = (M1 - M2) / √(SD12/ n1) + (SD22/ n2)
  • Before you assess the t-test results:
    • You need to check the assumption of homogeneity of variance (equal variance) by checking the Levene's Test results.
      • H0: The variances are the same.
      • H1: The variances are different.
    • If significance level is ≥ .05, we have met the assumption of homogeneity of variance. Assess the t-test with the top row, 'Equal variances assumed.'
    • If significance level is < .05, we have failed to met the assumptions of homogeneity of variance. Assess the t-test with the bottom row, 'Equal variances not assumed.'

  1. Make decision
    • Fail to reject the null hypothesis because our obtained significance value was greater than our predetermined alpha level (p = .250)
  2. Write conclusion
    • An independent samples t-test was conducted to determine if the mean pretest achievement scores of regular education students who were not in an integrated classroom differed from regular education studetns who were in the integrated classroom. The test results indicate that, on average, the pretest means were not statistically significantly different for students who were not in the integrated classroom (M = 92.93, SD = 14.56) and students who were in the integrated classroom (M = 98.56, SD = 14.88); (t (38) = -1.167, p = .250, d = -. 381). The null hypothesis that the pretest means were the same for students in the integrated and not in the integrated classroom was retained at the .05 level of significance. The effect size d was -. 381. Using Cohen's (1988) guidelines, this is interpreted as a small effect.
  • Is there a mean difference in posttest achievement scores between regular education students who are not in integrated classroom and in the integrated classroom?

    • H1: 𝜇posttest not integrated ≠ 𝜇posttest integrated
