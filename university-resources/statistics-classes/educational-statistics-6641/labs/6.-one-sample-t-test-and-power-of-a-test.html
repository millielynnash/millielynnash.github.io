One Sample t-Tests

  • A one sample t-test evaluates whether the mean on a test variable is significantly different from a constant.
  • Assumptions:
    • The test variable is normally distributed in the population.
    • The cases represent a random sample from the population.
    • Scores on the test variables are independent of each other.

t-Test

  1. State the H0 and H0
  2. Define ⍺ level
  3. Compute t statistic
  4. Look up critical value in t-table/interpret output
  5. Make decision
    • Reject or fail to reject the H0.
  6. Write conclusion

Exercise

  • Janet is interested in determining if depression scores of male adolescents who do not engage in sports are significantly different from the average male adolescent. To test this hypothesis, she obtains Kansas University Depression Inventory (KUDI) scores from 30 adolescent boys who have indicated on a questionnaire that they do not engage in sports outside of school gym classes. Past research has shown that the mean depression score for male adolescents in the population in the population is 50. Janet wants to determine if the mean KUDI score for the 30 adolescents is different from the value of 50.
  1. State the H0 and H1
    • H0: 𝜇 = 50
    • H1: 𝜇 ≠ 50
  2. Define 𝛼 level
    • 𝛼 = .05
  3. Compute t-statistic
  4. Look up critical value in t-table and interpret output
  5. Make decision
    • Reject or fail to reject the H0
  6. Write conclusion

We Found Significance, but...

  • Take a look at the actual difference between the sample mean and the population mean:
    • tobs = 2.457 vs. tcrit = 2.045
    • Are you comfortable calling that difference significant?
  • Alternative (or Supplement) to significance value:
    • Cohen's d
  • Cohen's d is an effect size measure used to indicate the difference between two means.
  • For one-sample t-test, d indicates how many standard deviations the sample mean is away from the hypothesized mean.
    • If d = 1.0, the sample mean is one standard deviation away from the hypothesized mean.
  • d = (x-bar - 𝜇0) / s
  • d = t / √n
  • Effect size measures strength of relationship:
Small+/- .20
Moderate+/- .50
Large+/- .80

Power

  • A lot of times before conducting your study you need to submit a power analysis. This lets you know what sample size you need to find an effect.
  • Power is the probability that a test of significance will detect deviation from the null hypothesis if one truly exists.

G Power

  • A Priori Power
    • Determine how many cases you will need to have a good chance of detecting an effect of a specified size with the desired amount of power.
    • Imagine you are designing a new study to see if depression scores are higher in a new group of students. Using alpha = .01, what sample size would you suggest to detect a 1/2 standard deviation difference from the population mean, with 90% power?
      • In this example, we have one-tailed test. Effect size is 0.5, alpha level is 0.01 and the desired power is 0.90.
      • Based on these criteria, the required sample size for our one-sample t-test is 55.
  • A Post Hoc Power
    • Determine how much power you would have if you had a specified number of cases.
    • In SPSS example (Kudi), we have a two-tailed test. The observed effect size was 0.449. The alpha level we tested was 0.05, and the actual sample size was 30.
      • Based on these criteria, the power hoc power is 0.662

Example APA Write-Up (One-Sample t-test for Lab HW 5)

  • A one sample t-test was conducted on the KUDI scores to evaluate whether their mean was significantly different from 50, the mean for male adolescent in general. The sample mean of 54.63 (SD = 10.33) was significantly different from 50, (t (29) = 2.46, p = .02). The 95% confidence interval for the KUDI mean ranged from 50.78 to 58.49. The effect size d of .45 indicates a medium effect (Cohen, 1988). The post hoc power of the test, given the sample size, two-tailed, alpha level, and observed effect size, was .662 (M = 54.63, SD = 10.33).
  • Note for future reference:
    • When SPSS reports a p-value of .000, we indicate it in the results section as 'p-value < .001.'
