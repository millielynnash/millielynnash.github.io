7. Repeated Measures Designs

Lecture

Basic Concepts of Repeated Measures Designs

What Are Repeated Measures Designs?

  • Repeated measures designs are an extension of the dependent group (paired samples) t-test.
  • Paired samples t-tests are used to compare means between paired, dependent, or matched groups. Collection of data on the same persons over time means responses are related to each other, across time. They are Dependent Observations
    • Differences from pre to post (e.g., time 1 to time 2)
    • Often the same respondents, repeatedly measured (not always, but not our focus)
  • In comparison, independent-groups designs look at:
    • Differences in means across groups (often manipulated in experimental designs)
    • Mean differences across existing groups
      • Gender, school level, generation status (e.g., quasi-experimental designs)

A Note on Data Structure

  • With repeated measures designs, the structure of the data becomes relevant.
  • Data types
    • Long
      • Respondents have multiple rows of data corresponding to multiple measurement occasions
    • Wide
      • Respondents have one row of data
      • Multiple variables are used to represent multiple measurement occasions
  • GLM for repeated measures (aka repeated measures ANOVA/MANOVA)

Between vs. Within-Subject Factors

  • Combination of both between-and within-subjects factors are referred to as 'mixed designs'
Between-Subject Factors
  • Different people are assigned to different levels of the factor
  • Each case (person) is exposed/included to only one condition or level
Within-Subject Factors
  • The same person tests all conditions (i.e., is exposed to all levels of the factor)
  • Repeated-measures designs are 'within-subjects' designs, where each subject forms a 'group' or factor

Partitioning Variance

  • Repeated measures ANOVA
    • SST = SS (Explained) + SS (unexplained)
  • We are trying to explain differences in teh repeated measures due to time (or whatever the within factor is)
  • We are removing/controlling variability due specifically to subjects.
  • We need to characterize unexplained/residual variability carefully for repeated measures designs (i.e., through the subject x time interaction)
  • One-Way Repeated Measures ANOVA
    • One-Way Repeated measures ANOVA with A treatments/times and B persons includes three 'sum of squares' terms.

Assumptions of Repeated Measures Designs

  • Independence of Observations (e.g., people)
  • Normality (minimal effect if balanced design)
  • Homogeneity of Variance
  • Sphericity / Circularity
    • Events measured closer in time tend to be more highly correlated than events measured farther apart in time.
    • A form of compount symmetry, but less restrictive.
    • Correlations include Greenhouse-Geisser, Huynh-Feldt
    • Lack of Sphericity leads to increased Type I Errors (finding false significance)
Sphericity
  • Homogeneity of Variance for the difference score, meaning variances across conditions/time are equal.
  • Relatedly, homogeneity of covariance is equal variances and covariacnes across time.
  • Sphericity is a tough assumption to meet, as we don't expect correlations between times 1, 3, and 5 to be necessarily the same.
  • Mauchly's sphericity test in SPSS tests this assumption.
    • We do NOT want this to be statistically significant.
Consequences of Sphericity Violation
  • The distribution of the data will not be in line with an F-distribution
  • Loss of statistical power
  • Post-hoc tests become uninterpretable
  • If violated, what should we do?
    • Use a more conservative f-test. SPSS gives you three options increasing in conservatism
    • Consider using MANOVA
    • Consider using multilevel modeling
Correcting for Sphericity Violation
  • Based on estimates of sphericity (Epsilon; ε)
    • Ranges from 0 - 1 with higher scores meaning greater homogeneity
  • Huynh-Feldt Correction (least conservative) ε > .75
  • Greenhouse-Geisser (more conservative) ε < .75
  • Lower-bound (most conservative) ε <. 75
  • If the results change depending on which correction used, it's a safer bet to use Greenhouse-Geisser regardless of Epsilon value (e.g., the cutoff somehow made a difference) or report both and acknowledge the difference.

MANOVA as an Alternative

  • Instead of time as treatment effect (univariate), MANOVA treats time as separate Dependent Variable s (multivariate)
  • Repeated measures analyses automatically include both, but if sphericity is violated, it might be better to just use MANOVA.
  • Repeated Measures ANOVA
    • Univariate DV
    • More rigid assumptions of sphericity
    • When sphericity holds, use RM ANOVA
    • You can look at contrasts and other post-hoc tests
  • MANOVA
    • Multivariate DVs
    • Less rigid assumptions of sphericity
    • A large violation of sphericity (ε < .70) and sample sizes of 10 people larger than the number of groups (j + 1), use MANOVA.
    • The power of MANOVA varies as a function of the correlation between the DVs.

Multilevel Modeling as an Alternative

  • Multilevel modeling approach (aka Hierarchical Linear Models; General Linear Mixed-Effects Models)
  • Growth curve models
    • Predicts individual differences in change over time
  • Time is nested within each subject
  • Very flexible
    • Allows for random effects and variability in measurement occasion.

Contrasts

Choice of Contrasts

  • If the overall F for differences among treatments is statistically significant, further analysis is needed to evaluate where the significant differences are.
  • The SPSS procedure contrasts that can be used to evaluate differences.
  • If the repeated measure has k levels, a set of contrasts include (k - 1) comparisons
  • Options
    • Polynomial contrasts (the default in SPSS, but rarely used in social sciences)
    • Repeated contrasts
    • Simple contrasts
Simple Contrasts
  • For a study that features a control group and one or more comparison groups, the usual question is whether each of the comparison groups has a mean that differs significantly from the control group mean.
  • For repeated measures, the control group is Time 1 (baseline). So, comparisons are made based on differences between Time 1 and all other time points.
    • Time 1 > Time 2, Time 1 > Time 3
  • This set of contrasts does not provide information about all possible comparisons, as the baseline must be included in all of them.
    • Very similar to dummy coding in Multiple Regression, where we only know differences between the reference category and all other categories (not each other)
Repeated Contrasts
  • Compare the mean of each level (except the last) with the mean of the subsequent level
    • Time 1 > Time 2, Time 2 > Time 3
  • Useful if the responses are in a meaningful order (like time)
  • Equivalence (or justifiable inequivalence) of measurement is crucial
Polynomial Contrasts
  • Makes more sense when the levels of the repeated-measures factor correspond to different amounts of treatment (rather than different types)
  • If there are k levels for the repeated-measures factor, then (k - 1) polynomials will be tested.
  • Example
    - Patients' responses to an anxiety drug administered over time

Dependent Samples t-test

  • Repeated measure design in which two (and only two) paired variables are compared to each other. Typically, Time 1 and Time 2 are compared.
  • Dependent samples t-test assesses differences, not changes.
    • Change/growth requires three timepoints and often longitudinal modeling is used.
  • Analyze > Compare Means and Proportions > Paired Samples t-test
    • Always put the second timepoint first in the list so that you are getting authentic difference score.

Example. Fear of Snakes

  • A child psychologist assessed 8 children on their fear of snakes, on a scale of 1 - 10. The children were then treated for their fear and retested at four more time points, one month apart.
  • What is our factor? Number of levels? Number of subjects?
  • H0: µtreat 1 = µtreat 2 = µtreat 3 = µtreat 4
    • There is no significant difference in the mean fear of snakes between times of treatment.
  • H1: at least one of the µ is different (not all the µ are equal)
    • There is a significant difference in mean fear of snakes between times of treatment.

One-Way Repeated Measures ANOVA

  • Extension to the dependent t-test
  • Same individuals measured two or more times
  • Sometimes called a Within-Subjects Design
  • In a one-way repeated measures model, each subject responds to each level of the factor
  • In a balanced design
    • Each subject responds to all J levels of the factor
    • Each of the n subjects has J scores do N = n x J
    • Unequal cells can only happen when subjects miss the administration of one or more levels of factor A
  • A researcher tested whether 1-hour, biweekly yoga classes increased the fitness level of eight undergraduate studnets over a 4-month period (4 levels of within-subjects factors). Students were measured at the end of each month using a 10-point fitness measure (10 being most fit).

Assumptions

  • Independence
  • Homogeneity of Variances
  • Testing for Normality
  • Testing the Independence of Error
  • Sphericity
    • This assumes that the variance of the difference scores for each pair of factor levels is the same

  • The violation causes the 'Usual' F-test to become too liberal (i.e., an increase in the Type I Errors rate). Therefore, determining whether sphericity has been violated is very important.
  • Luckily, if violations of sphericity do occur, corrections have been developed to produce a more valid critical F-value (i.e., reduce the increase in Type I Errors rate)
Mauchly's Test of Sphericity
  • Like in Levene's HOV test, the null hypothesis is the variances of difference scores are equal.
  • Test NOT Significant (p > .05) > Assumption MET! Variances are the same.
  • Test Significant (p < .05) > Assumption NOT met! Variances are different.
