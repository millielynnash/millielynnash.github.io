Least Squares Method

  • Method of minimizing overall error in a particular model by producing estimates that have the least ' Sum of Squares of Error (SSE) ' (SSE = the sum of squared residual effects).
    • Residual effects
      • Unexplained variation in the outcome variable
  • Error can be positive or negative, but negative residual is illogical, so we square the residuals.
  • Best estimates for the b's are the one's that provide minimal error over any other collection of b's.
  • The model with the smallest SSE will be the best fitting model.


  • The model with the smallest Sum of Squares of Error (SSE) will be the best fitting model
  • Least squares regression line
  • These values will always give us the smallest overall error, compared to any other line draw through the set of points.

The F-Tests

  • Partitioning sum of squares into regression (explained) and error (unexplained)

ANOVA Table

  • Partitioning sum of squares into between (explained) and within/error (unexplained)

T-Test

  • Testing regression coefficient
    - T-test is based on the F-Tests (t-squared). We are testing if the beta weight of the coefficient is big enough to account for variation in Y compared to error.
    - Error in estimate = Standard Error

Model Fit

  • Omnibus test
    • How well does the model fit the actual data?
      • Good fit
        • Minimized error and strong correspondence between observed and predicted values (e.g., a large R-squared)
      • F
        • MSR / MSE provides overall statistical test of model fit
  • H0 (Null Hypothesis) (x variable (s) do not predict y)
    • With no better predictions than population mean of y
  • H1 (Alternative Hypothesis) (x variable (s) do predict y) with better predictions than population mean of y.

Statistical Control and Unique Correlations

Statistical Control

  • Mathematically accounting for the influence of another parameter on the relation between x and y.
  • When predictors are correlated, they potentially have shared or overlapping explained variance in Y. We need to consider the unique contribution of each.
  • Pearson’s correlation coefficient (r) does NOT do this. Squaring an r statistic is only useful when there is only one predictor (R-squared)
  • Unique correlations are needed to account for multiple predictors:
    • Partial (first-order) Correlation
    • Semi-partial (part) Correlations

Partitioning of Variance with Multiple Predictors

Correlations with Multiple Predictors

  • Partial (First-Order) Correlations
    • The correlation between x and y while controlling for x2 (on both x and y)
  • Semi-Partial (Part) Correlation
    • The correlation between x and y while controlling for x2 (on x1 only)
    • The relative strength of the association between x and y.
  • Squaring the partial correlation yields the proportion of the variance in Y remaining after removing the effects of all other predictors
    • (Not very useful)
  • Squaring the semi-partial correlation yields the proportion of the variance in y uniquely explained by x while controlling for all other predictors.
    • Useful as a measure of Effect Size

Consequences of Controlling for x2

  • x and y are unrelated before and after
    • No variables are related to each other
  • The relation between x and y remains stable
    • x2 is irrelevant
  • The relation between x and y drops to zero
    • x2 completely accounts for the variation between x and y
    • Signs of confounded predictors, spurious correlation, 'complete' mediation, statistical suppression
  • The relation between x and y becomes stronger or flips signs
    • x2 and x are so strongly related that findings become masked/suppressed
  • The relation between x and y becomes smaller but still significant
    • x only partly accounts for the variation in y
    • Most common!

The Fundamentals of Multiple Regression

Regression

  • Predicting variation in an outcome variable (y) based on changes in predictor variables (x1, x2, etc.)
  • Can be simple (one y, one x), or multiple (one y, many x's)
  • Can be simultaneous (all x's predicting y at the same time) or hierarchical (x's predicting y in pre-determined blocks)
  • Regular Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression assumes that x and y are linearly related and are normally distributed.
  • Special cases require special analyses.
  • The sample regression coefficients are statistics
    • They are estimates of the population values of variable effects on y (how much variation in y is predicted by changes in x)
  • Different samples will yield different values for the regression coefficients
    • Known as Sampling Error
  • Coefficients are estimates, and therefore always include some error
    • We want to minimize this error

Unstandardized vs. Standardized

  • Parameters can be unstandardized (useful for raw interpretation) or standardized (useful for comparing parameters to each other)
  • Unstandardized (b) coefficients are in the original scale and must be interpreted as a function of the original units. It is in the units you started with.
  • Standardized (𝛽) coefficients are based on z-scores (distances in Standard Deviation (SD) units from the mean) and can be compared across parameters.

Regression Coefficients

  • The values of all parameters are influenced by the other parameters in the model.
  • b0 (the intercept)
    • Predicted score on y when all predictor variables are at zero.
  • b1 (the slope of x1)
    • The change in predicted y values for every 1 unit increase in x1 (holding x2 constant)
  • b2 (the slope of x2)
    • The change in predicted y values for every 1 unit increase in x1 (holding x1 constant)

Multiple Regression

  • When more than one predictor variable is included (either continuous or categorical)
  • Often one of three types
    • Simultaneous Multiple Regression (E.g., all predictors at once)
    • Hierarchical Multiple Regression (e.g., variables added by research in theory-driven blocks)
    • Stepwise Multiple Regression (e.g., significant variables added in first or last; data driven)
  • Regression coefficients for each predictor are 'partial,' in that they control for any other predictors present in the model (they 'control' for other parameters)
Simultaneous Multiple Regression
  • All predictors are added at once
  • All parameters control for all other parameters
  • R-squared is one static value that represents the variance explained in the outcome variable by the collective set of predictors
  • Continuous Variable added as is
  • Nominal Variable must be dummy coded
  • Variables with non-zero values should be 'centered'
Hierarchical Multiple Regression
  • Predictors are added in theory-driven blocks (e.g., social-ecological models)
  • A series of multiple-regression are performed, with each block being added to the next until all variables are included
    • The final block of hierarchical multiple regression = simultaneous multiple regression
  • Very useful to look at change in R-squared for each variable or blocks of variables
  • Squared semi-partial correlation provides the unique variance explained by a given variable.
Stepwise Multiple Regression
  • Predictors are added in data-driven blocks
    • Forward Stepwise Regression
      • Most predictive variables are added first
    • Backward Stepwise Regression
      • Least predictive variables are trimmed
  • Mathematically derives the highest R-squared value with the least number of predictors.
  • NEVER USE THIS METHOD UNLESS STUDY IS ABSOLUTELY EXPLORATORY
Effect Size in Multiple Regression
  • Squared Semi-Partial (Part) Correlation
  • Adjusted R-squared values
  • Standardized regression coefficients
Other Considerations
  • Standardized coefficients are harder to interpret but more meaningful with multiple predictors (and there almost always are).
  • Change in R-squared represents the amount of unique variance each block of variables explains above and beyond the other variables already included.
  • Random Sampling is preferable, but also less feasible (biased estimates)
  • Reliable measurement of constructs is crucial, and checking psychometric properties of scales is important!

Multiple Regression on SPSS

Initial Data Screening
  • Check for assumptions
    • Linear Associations
    • Normality (Testing for Normality)
    • Multicollinearity (Multicollinearity)
  • Check descriptive statistics and correlations between all included variables
  • Confirm missing value codes
  • Three core pieces of information for every data set you open:
    • Number of cases
    • Number of variables
    • Date of last manipulation
Procedure for Multiple Regression (Menus)

  • Dependent
    • Y variable
  • Block
    • Use next and previous buttons for blocks of variables
  • Independent(s)
    • x-variables
  • Method
    • Allows you to use stepwise, never use this
    • Always use paste to send the analysis to the syntax editor and save your work.
Procedure for Multiple Regression (Statistics)

  • Include Model Fit, R-squared change, Descriptives, Part and Partial Correlations, and Collinearity Diagnostics
  • Confidence intervals are not necessary but can be helpful.
Procedure for Multiple Regression (Save)

  • You can save residuals as their own variables to detect multivariate outliers.
  • Predicted values tell you what SPSS thinks the y value should be based on the x-values of the case.
  • Residual values are how far off SPSS was
  • Mahalanobis distance tells you how far away a case is from the 'cloud' of other values.
Procedure for Multiple Regression (Output)

  • Correlation tables are clunky in regression output. Generate them separately.
  • Variables entered/removed only relevant when doing stepwise (ignore otherwise)
  • R-square tells you the proportion of variance explained in y by all of your x variables.
  • Adjusted R-square takes sample size into account (weighting). Often not used.
  • R-squared changed useful for Hierarchical Multiple Regression. Shows how much more variance in y was explained by additional x variables (in the next block)
  • Sig. F change signifies if the block significantly improved variance predictions.
  • Standard f-table that signifies if the omnibus test is significant (useful for parenthetical stats in manuscripts)
  • Where the action is. Each row conveys information about the regression equation that SPSS produced as the 'best fitting' model.
  • Unstandardized coefficients are easier to interpret, but standardized coefficients are more useful for including in manuscripts (apples to apples instead of apples to oranges)
  • Significance level of .05 is standard, though more stringent p-values can be employed to address weaknesses in design.
  • Zero-order correlations represent the standard correlation between each x and y
  • Partial and part correlations should be squared manually prior to interpretation.
  • Squared partial correlation represents the variance remaining in y after factoring out the x variable.
  • Squared part (semi-partial) correlation represents the unique variance explained in y by the x variable.
  • Tolerance reflects the proportion of the variance in x that is not predictable from other x predictor variables. 0-1 with zero meaning no new contribution and one meaning completely uncorrelated with the other predictors.
  • VIF indicates the degree of similarity between an x variable and at least one other x variable in the model. Scores of higher than 5 are seen as evidence of Multicollinearity and action must be taken (e.g., variable removal or combining).

Dummy Coding

  • For Predictor (x) variables that are categorical, the standard procedure cannot be followed. The variable must be 'dummy coded.'
  • Think of dummy coding as light switches. 1 ('on') means group membership and 0 ('off') means no group membership.
  • Dummy coding means giving every case k-1 variables that indicate group membership.
  • For the school variable
    • So, someone from Cline elementary would have the following values for group membership:
      • AEC = 0, Campbell County High = 0, Campbell County MS = 0, Campbell County Elementary = 0, Cline = 1, Crossroads = 0, Grants Lick = 0, Reiley = 0
  • k-1 groups are used to fully capture group membership.
  • A 'reference' category is selected to represent group membership when all values are zero.
    • Typically, the reference category is the most often selected category.
      • For example, Campbell County High School has roughly 49% of the data in our sample dataset, meaning that category should be selected as the referent.
  • Coefficients are interpreted as the mean differences between the reference category and the category in question.

Dummy Coding Output

  • Students in Campbell County Middle School have -.177 less global satisfaction than students at Campbell County High School, controlling for hope scores.

Regression Equations

  • The hat over y says we are looking at the predicted values.
  • We are predicting y from x.
    • y = b0 + b1X1 + e (Simple Linear Regression (SLR))
    • y = b0 + b1X1 + b2X2 + ... + bnXn + e (Multiple Linear Regression (MLR))
  • y hat is our outcome variable (Dependent Variable)
  • x is our Predictor (Independent Variable)
  • b is our slope (Regression Coefficient s)
  • b0 is our y-intercept

Mean Centering

  • Do we have an IQ of zero?
  • Sometime, interpreting the intercept becomes difficult or meaningless, especially when we are dealing with variables like IQ, SAT scores, etc.
  • We have to use what is termed as 'mean-centering'
    • That is creating a new variable by simply subtracting the mean from each individual observation
    • E.g., Centered IQi = IQi - IQ-bar (mean of IQ scores)

Simple Syntax for Centering and Running a SLR Using the Centered Variable

  • ** Centering mom_iq.
  • ** Note that the mean for mom's IQ is 100
  • COMPUTE iq_c = mom_iq - 100
  • ** Running a simple linear regression using the centered variable
  • REGRESSION
    • /DEPENDENT kid_score
    • /method=ENTER iq_c
Regression Output
  • Slope does not change (b1 = .610)
Interpretation of the Constant
  • The predicted kid's score when mom's IQ score is at the mean (i.e., 100) is 86.797.
    • The interpretation of the intercept now makes more sense.

Checking Multicollinearity

  • Multicollinearity can be checked using
    • Variance Inflation Factor (VIF)
    • Using variance proportion and condition index

Variance Inflation Factor (VIF)

  • Mostly reported in articles
  • Smaller VIF values indicate no meaningful collinearity (< 4 or < 10 if being conservative)
  • VIF = 1/tolerance
    • 1 / .884 = 1.131 in the example below
  • Our model can be said to be free from multicollinearity since the VIF value for all Independent Variable s are below cutoff value of 4.

Using Variance Proportion and Condition Index (Collinearity Diagnostics Table)

  • Condition Index
    • A way to summarize the size of each eigenvalues relative to largest eigenvalue
    • If condition index is greater than 30 AND more than one Predictor loads (or correlates) (> .4) on a single variance proportion, these together signal a potential multicollinearity problem.
    • When assessing multicollinearity with the collinearity diagnostics, report on both the variance proportions and the condition indexes.
