4. Diagnostics and Psychometrics

Lecture

Regression Assumptions and Diagnostics

Assumptions of Regression

  • Linearity
    • Linear relationships between Predictor and Outcome
  • Normality of Errors
    • Residuals in the model are random and normally distributed
  • Homoscedasticity
    • The Variance of the residuals for all variables should be the same or similar
  • Independence of Errors
    • The residuals for all variables should be independent (uncorrelated)

Consequences of Assumption Violation

  • Lack of generalizability
  • Potentially biased or inaccurate findings
  • Delays or rejections of manuscripts with faulty analyses included
    • But assumptions are easily (and often) violated. Correct when you can and be transparent when you cannot. Do not let assumption violations alone preclude your use of a statistical technique.

Linearity

  • Variables must be linearly related to each other
  • Can be visually inspected via bivariate scatterplots (but be aware of limitations of mean averages based on Likert-type scales)
  • Relations may be quadratic or cubic

Impacts of Non-Linearity

  • Biased estimates that lead to faulty interpretations
  • Nonsignificant p-values, which may be hiding interaction effects
  • What to do:
    • Inspect each pair of variables and consider potential interaction effects
    • Consider mathematical transformations to improve linear relations
    • Revise the model with variables that are linearly related (model trimming)

Normality

  • Histogram of Errors
    • Should be symmetrical and bell-shaped
  • Normality Probability Plot
    • Dots should be on the diagonal
    • Expected vs. Actual cumulative probability
  • Plots of Standardized predicted values and residuals
    • Points should be randomly and even dispersed (flat)

Impacts of Non-Normality

  • Estimates of variable effects are unbiased, but tests are flawed.
  • Outliers may go unidentified
  • Validity of interpretations may be questionable
What To Do
  • Transformations
  • If outliers or outlier-groups (special education students mainstreamed), may want to include group membership as an additional Predictor.

Testing Homoscedasticity

  • Equal residual variances for all Predictor variables.
  • Plot of predicted values and residuals can shed light on this.

Impacts of Heteroscedasticity

  • The standard errors and the statistical significance of variables are potentially invalid.
  • If severe, or if samples are small, there could be a large degree of bias.
What To Do
  • Consider interaction terms
  • WLS = Weighted Least Squares
    • Weighted observations by precision. Precision is related to square of the residual for each case.

Independence of Errors

  • Variables (and their residuals) should be uncorrelated and independent.
  • If dependent, other approaches are necessary (e.g., dependent samples t-test, repeated measures, etc.)
  • Dependence may be caused by clustered/nested data (students within schools, clients within clinics) or lack of Autocorrelation (time-related or longitudinal data).

Impact of Non-Independence of Errors

  • Invalid Standard Error s, Confidence Intervals, and significance tests.
What To Do
  • Durbin-Watson test
    • Test for serial correlations between errors (should be close to 2).

Multicollinearity

  • An inflated (too high) correlation between Predictor variables.
  • Ideally, predictors would be independent and uncorrelated, but this is not feasible.
  • Can be detected by examining correlations table for high correlations, tolerance, VIF, and other collinearity diagnostics.

Impacts of Multicollinearity

  • Standard Error s for variable effects (slopes) become quite large
  • T-Test s and F-Tests become unreliable and confidence intervals likely to contain 0 (Type II Errors inflation)
What To Do
  • Centering (in polynomial or interaction models)
  • Deleting or combining variables (easiest, but last resort)

Outliers / Extreme Values

  • Can be detected using residual diagnostics
    • Useful for detecting bivariate outliers and multivariate outliers.
  • Why is an observation extreme?
    • Extreme responses may be an error or an honest response.
    • Care should be take in what is done with extreme outliers (have a rationale)

Detection of Outliers

  • Univariate outliers
    • Boxplots, Frequency Distribution, Z-Score, Stem-and-Leaf plots, etc.
  • Multivariate outliers
    • Mahalonobis' distance (Chi-Square Test), standardized residuals, leverages, cook's distance
Three Characteristics to Consider
Distance
  • How far a case is from some point of reference
  • Mahalonobis' Distance
    • Identifies the distance of cases from the Mean (s) of Predictor (s). Extreme cases in multivariate space are found via a cut-off point (critical value of chi-square).
  • Standardized Residuals
    • Measure the distance between an observed value and its predicted value that have all been converted to z-scores. Typically flag cases if they are 2 or 3 Standard Deviation (SD) s away from the mean.
Leverage
  • How far a point deviates from the Mean of that variable
  • Gauges the influence of the observed values over the predicted values.
    • Ranges from zero to (N - 1) / N
    • The average value is (k + 1) / n
    • No greater than 2 (k + 1) / n or 3 (k + 1) / n
Influence
  • The degree to which a variable can 'pull' the regression towards itself
  • Cook's Distance
    • Measures the overall influence of a case on the model.
    • Typically flagged if value is over 1.

Summary

  • Know your data well
  • Know your model well
  • Ensure enough time to verify assumptions so you are not trying to interpret a flawed analysis
  • Delete, trim, or combine cases with extreme care
  • Understand the implications of model revisions
  • Be honest and transparent when writing up your findings

Overview of Psychometrics

What Does Psychometric Mean?

  • The theory and technique of tests and measurements
  • An often overlooked (and yet frequently asked for) element of research methods is the psychometric properties of the scales / measures used
  • Any self-reported measure of any construct has psychometric properties that can be good, fair, or poor
  • A basic understanding of psychometric statistics and how to generate them is crucial for inferential statistics

Aspects of Psychometrics

  • Statistical Reliability
    • Test-Retest Reliability
      • This is how well the measure provides the same or similar scores across time
    • Internal Consistency
      • This is the consistency of responses within items on a multiple-item measure
  • For our purposes, internal consistency is the most relevant
  • Statistical Validity
    • Glossary/Face Validity
      • When the measure appears to measure what it is supposed to measure 'on its face'
    • Content Validity
      • When the measure covers the construct of interest
    • Criterion Validity
      • Scores on one measure correlate with scores on other measures that are logically (or theoretically) related to each other
      • Known as concurrent validity when cross-sectional and predictive validity when longitudinal or panel design
    • Discriminant Validity
      • Scores on one measure are distinct from scores on another measure that should (logically and theoretically) be independent
  • We can analytically test ALL of these components.

Cronbach's Alpha / McDonald's Omega

  • Measures the internal consistency of a construct based on the items entered
  • The higher the value, the more 'tightly' the items fit together
  • Cut-off values are debatable and depend on goals of the analysis, but generally accepted that > .80 is good reliability

Exploratory Factor Analysis (EFA)

  • Mathematically derives the dimensionality or 'structure' of a set of items
  • Helps to determine the underlying constructs represented by the data
  • Ideally, the factor structure derived from the items will match theoretical expectations
  • Importantly, EFA is data-driven
    • We are asking the software to tell us where the data falls
    • Confirmatory Factor Analysis is used when we already known the factor structure and want to confirm its fit to the data
  • For the Scree plot, all other factors not included after extraction would be considered 'suppressed.'

Examining Outliers in Regression

Cook's Distance

  • This is a measure of influence
  • Cook's D greater than 1 is a cause for concern
  • Cases with Cook's D greater than 1 have to be examined again because such cases are very likely to affect the regression model
  • Cook's Distance
    - .036
    - If the maximum value is greater than 1, check the 'COO_1' for the case with the Cook's D greater than 1

Mahal Distance

  • Mahal Distance helps identify high leverage cases
  • A High Leverage case is a case that is more extreme with respect to the independent variables
  • Although High Leverages cases are less likely to affect the regression model, it is worth examining
  • You can test for the significance of Mahal Distance by using Chi-Square Test
  • The degrees of freedom in this case is the number of predictors.
  • Mahal. Distance = 8.347

  • 1 - CDF.CHSQ(MAH_1,2)
    • Degrees of freedom is 2 because we have two predictors
  • According to Tabachnick & Fidell (2013), the recommended threshold p-value is .001. So, cases with p-values less than .001 indicates significant outliers on the predictors.

  • The minimum p-value in this example is .02 which is greater than the threshold value (i.e., .001), so we can conclude that there is no significant outlier on the predictors.
  • The scatterplot shows an inverse relationship between Mahal Distance and the associated p-values. So, if the p-value of the maximum Mahal distance (Check the 'Residual Statistics Table') is greater than the threshold of .001, you can conclude that there is no significant outlier on the predictor(s).
  • You do not even have to check the Mahal distance for individual cases.

Standardized DfFITS

  • The absolute values of DfFITS (i.e., |SDF_1|) tells us the magnitude of the effect an individual case have on the predicted values.
  • This indicates that, the SDF_1, similar to the Cook's distance, help examine the influence of some cases on the predicted values.
  • Cases with absolute values of DfFITS greater than 2 (i.e., |SDF_1| > 2) should be examined.
  • Since the maximum absolute value is .33205, which is not greater than 2, we can conclude that there is no issue with an influential outlier(s).

Standardized DfBeta

  • Helps examine whether a case is influential on the intercept or the slopes.
  • The case should first be considered influential by looking at the D and/or the DfFITS.
  • A case with an DfBeta greater than 2 can be said to have greater influence on the regression parameter in question (Pituch & Stevens, 2016).
  • This case then has to be investigated.
  • In our example
    - SDB0_1 represents the DFBETA for the intercept
    - SDB 1_1 represents the DFBETA for the mom's IQ
    - SDB 2_1 represents the DFBETA for the mom age
  • The maximum absolute DFBETA (i.e., |DFBETA|) value for all the regression parameters (i.e., the intercept and the slopes) are less than 2, hence we can conclude that none of the cases have relatively high influence on our regression parameters.
