6. Analysis of Covariance (ANCOVA)

Lecture

Overview of Analysis of Covariance (ANCOVA)

Understanding ANOVA via Regression

  • ANOVA tests the mean difference across at least 3 groups
    • Compares the ratio of explained/between and unexplained/within Variance
  • In regression, ANOVA is a model in which there is one Categorical Predictor (kind of)

Characteristics of ANCOVA

  • ANOVA, plus covariate (e.g., Covariance), traditionally a Continuous Variable
  • By including a covariate, we can reduce error variability and account for confounds.
  • It indicates differences in means are 'adjusted' for differences on the covariate.
  • Covariates can be anything relevant to your study, but should be correlate with you Dependent Variable.

Why Bother with Covariates?

  • Example
    • A study on how to build a laptop
    • Suppose we have an experiment with 3 instructional groups and one covariate
      1. Instruction from a Mentor
      2. Instruction from a YouTube video
      3. No instruction at all
    • Dependent Variable: skill level on how to build a laptop before training (pe-test)
  • By adding a covariate, differences in pre-test are 'removed' (controlled for) from the error term and means are adjusted to the predicted post-test level if all cases had the same skill level going into the study.

Why Does ANCOVA Actually Test?

  • ANOVA is performed on the adjusted Dependent Variable scores
  • Research Question
    • Do groups differ on the Dependent Variable after adjusting for pre-existing differences on the covariate?
  • In the test of means, we use the F-test
  • If the covariate explains some of the between-group differences in the Dependent Variable, the MSE (error) will be smaller.
  • As the error term becomes smaller, the F-test for the effect (e.g., group differences) will become larger (in comparison), and thus more likely to become statistically significant.
  • Covariates do not guarantee statistical significance, and may make the primary Predictor less significant if they eat enough explanatory variance from the predictor variable (more on this in the assumptions section)

ANCOVA vs. Multiple Regression

  • Regression
    • What is the nature of the relationship between x2 and y?
  • ANOVA
    • Are there differences in means on y based on x1 (when Categorical)?
  • ANCOVA (Regression x ANOVA)
    • After adjusting for differences due to x2, are there differences in y based on x1.

ANCOVA Assumptions

  • Same assumptions as Multiple Regression
    • Testing Linearity
    • Testing for Normality
    • Testing Homoscedasticity
    • Testing the Independence of Error
  • Independence between covariate and the Categorical Predictor (often violated)
  • Homogeneity of Regression (HOR)
    • No interaction between the covariate and the categorical predictor/grouping variable
  • Covariate and Dependent Variable should be linearity related.
    • Can be assessed by scatterplots within groups and overall.

Independence between Covariate and the Categorical Predictor

  • a
    • A basic model that compares group means
  • b
    • The ideal scenario in which a covariate only accounts for unexplained variance in y.
  • c
    • A more realistic scenario in which the covariate accounts for both explained variance from the predictor and unexplained variance.
    • Scenario C is most common, and explains why the predictor variable becomes less significant when the covariate is present.

Homogeneity of Regression (HOR)

  • When slopes are 'homogeneous,' the covariate adjusted means on the Dependent Variable provide an adequate and appropriate description of effects of 'treatment effects.'
    • Treatment effects = difference between adjusted mean for a group and the overall mean of the DV
    • Treatment effects should be the same regardless of covariate values (parallel lines)
  • What slopes are heterogenous, HOR is violated, and treatment effects vary.
    • This implies an interaction between the covariate and the predictor.
    • The regression lines are no longer parallel, and alternatives to ANVOCA should be considered.
    • This means the focus shifts to interaction/moderation rather than statistical control.
  • If the lines are not parallel, then the relationship between x and y depend on the value of the covariate (aka a Moderator!)
HOR Not Violated

HOR Violated

Linearity between DV and Covariate

  • Related to linearity addumption of Multiple Regression, but with Dependent Variable and Covariate.
  • Can be easily assessed via bivariate scatterplots.
  • If violated, consider transformations of the data (e.g., centering)

SPSS Practice with ANCOVA

ANCOVA Procedure in SPSS

  • Analyze > General Linear Model > Univariate
  • Move your model variables into the appropriate positions (Fixed factor = Independent Variable)
  • Select appropriate diagnostic information
    • EM Means > Display means for independent Variable (allows for pairwise comparison with covariate)

Checking Assumptions for ANCOVA

  • Run an ANOVA with the Independent Variable (x) predicting the Covariate (y) (Should NOT be significant)
  • Run the ANCOVA, but build custom terms to view the interaction between the Independent Variable and the Covariate (interaction should NOT be significant)

Lab

Analysis of Covariance (ANCOVA)

  • More about ANCOVA, see Field, 2009.
  • Simply, an ANOVA with the inclusion of a covariate.
  • Reasons for including covariates
    • To reduce within-group error variance
      • This helps to more accurately assess the impact of the Independent Variable (SS explained)
    • Elimination of confounds
      • That is, variables (other than the experimental manipulation) that are known to influence the dependent variable can be partialed out.

Some Key Assumptions of ANCOVA

  • The assumption of Homogeneity of Regression Slopes
    • The relationship between the covariate and the outcome variable should be the same across groups.
    • A simple scatterplot (with a regression line) showing the relationship between the covariate and the outcome variable can help verify this assumption.
  • You can run the ANCOVA model by including an interaction term (groups x covariate).
    • The interaction term should not be statistically significant.
    • If it is statistically significant, then it means the slopes are different across groups, hence the violation of this assumption.

Syntax for a Simple ANCOVA

UNIANOVA Dependent Variable BY Factor WITH Covar
/METHOD=SSTYPE(3)
/INTERCEPT=INCLUDE
/EMMEANS=TABLES (Factor) WITH(COVAR=MEAN) COMPARE ADJ(Bonferroni)
/PRINT ETASQ DESCRIPTIVE PARAMETER HOMOGENEITY
/CRITERIA=ALPHA(.05)
/DESIGN=itbsmc 2 treat.

  • NB: you can also use the point-and-click method to generate the syntax.

Using the Point-and-Click Approach in SPSS

  • Click Analyze > General Linear Model > Univariate
  • Drag the Dependent Variable into the 'Dependent Variable' box.
  • Drag the factor (grouping variable) into the 'fixed-factor' box.
  • Drag the covariate into the 'Covariate' box.
  • Note. This is for a simple model. There are other options available for a more complete analysis.

Example

  • We want to find out whether students WISC score differ across treatment groups after controlling for their score on itbsmc 2 (IOWA test of Basic Skills-Math concept).
    UNIANOVA WISC BY Treat WITH itbsmc2
    /METHOD=SSTYPE(3)
    /INTERCEPT=INCLUDE
    /EMMEANS=TABLES (Treat) WITH (itbsmc2=MEAN) COMPARE ADJ (Bonferroni)
    /PRINT ETASQ DESCRIPTIVE PARAMETER HOMOGENEITY
    /CRITERIA=ALPHA(.05)
    /DESIGN=itbsmc2 treat.

Results

  • The mean for the three groups are very close to each other (102.23; 101.96; 101.95; and 102.05)
  • The error of variance for the dependent variable (WISC) is equal across groups (p > .05) (Sig. = .167)
  • Hence, the assumption of Homogeneity of Variances has been met.
  • This is similar to the Testing Homoscedasticity assumption in MLR

Checking the Assumption of Homogeneity of Regression Slopes

  • Syntax: just add the interaction term (itbsmc2) to the model.
    UNIANOVA WISC BY Treat WITH itbsmc 2
    /METHOD=SSTYPE (3)
    /INTERCEPT=INCLUDE
    /EMMEANS=TABLES (Treat) WITH (itbsmc 2=MEAN) COMPARE ADJ (Bonferroni)
    /PRINT ETASQ DESCRIPTIVE PARAMETER HOMOGENEITY
    /CRITERIA=ALPHA (. 05)
    /DESIGN=itbsmc 2 treat itbsmc 2 * treat.
  • We do not want the interaction term to be statistically significant.
    • If it is significant, then it implies that the relationship between the covariate and the outcome differs among the groups.
    • In other words, the regression slopes are not homogenous.
  • You can go to Graph > Scatterplot to create a simple scatter plot to visualize the relationship across groups before the analysis itself.
    • Create the scatterplot using the point-and-click method.
    • The syntax generated by clicking the paste button:
      • GRAPH
      • /SCATTERPLOT (BIVAR)=itbsmc 2 WITH wisc BY treat
      • /MISSING=LISTWISE.
      • Click on 'Add Fit Lines to Subgroups' to add the fit lines.
      • The regression slopes (lines) appear similar (homogenous) across groups.

Results of the Second Model (with Interaction Term)

  • The interaction term is NOT statistically significant (p = .425).
  • Hence, the assumption of HOR has been met.

SS Types

Type I

  • AKA Sequential Sum of Squares
  • The order matters! Each effects partials out the one before it.
  • So, if you are looking at a, b, and a x b
    • The first value is for a
    • The value for b is after controlling for a
    • a x b's value is after controlling for a and b.
  • Best for specific theory where order matters
    • I.e., partial out certain variables first.

Type II

  • a accounts for b and vice versa.
  • This is best when you don't have an interaction effect.

Type III

  • Every value accounts for every other effect (main or interaction)

Summary

  • If order matters, Type I.
  • If order does not matter, and there is no interaction, Type II.
  • If order does not matter, but there is an interaction, Type III.
