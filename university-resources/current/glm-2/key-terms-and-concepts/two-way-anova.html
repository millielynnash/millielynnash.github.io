Two-Way ANOVA

Summary Table for Two-Way ANOVA

AspectDetails
PurposeCompare the effects of two independent factors and their interaction on a Continuous DV.
Key Assumptions- Independence of Observations: Each observation is independent
- Normality of Residuals : Residuals should be normally distributed
- Homogeneity of Variance: Variances across all groups should be similar
SPSS StepsNormality: Use Explore to check Shapiro-Wilk Test for each's cell's residuals.
Homogeneity: Use Levene's Test (p > 0.05 for equal variances)
Criteria for AssumptionsNormality: Shapiro-Wilk Test (p > 0.05 per cell)
Homogeneity: Levene's Test (p > 0.05 indicates assumption met)
Independence: Ensured by study design
- Main Effects (F, df, p for each factor)
- Interaction Effect (F, df, p)
InterpretationSignificant main effects indicate differences in means across levels of each factor.

A significant interaction indicates that the effect of one factor depends on the level of the other.
APA ReportingA two-way ANOVA was conducted to examine the effects of teaching method (Traditional, Experimental) and gender (Male, Female) on exam scores. Results revealed a significant main effect of teaching method, F(1, 92) = 5.25, p = .025, and a significant main effect of gender, F(1, 92) = 4.15, p = .045. Additionally, a significant interaction was found between teaching method and gender, F(1, 92) = 6.35, p = .014, indicating that the effect of teaching method varied by gender. Levene’s test confirmed homogeneity of variances across groups, p = .135.

Purpose

  • To examine the main effects of two Independent factors and their interaction effect on a Continuous dependent variable.
  • A two-way (or factorial) ANOVA is used to examine the main effects of two Categorical Independent Variable (factors) on a Continuous Dependent Variable as well as their interaction effect.

Example

  • Investigating the effects of 'Teaching Method' (Traditional vs. Experimental) and 'Gender' (Male vs. Female) on exam scores.
