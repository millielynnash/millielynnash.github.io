What is the Definition of a Moderation and a Mediation?

  • A moderator is a variable that affects the strength or direction of the relationship between an independent variable (x) and a dependent variable (y).
  • A mediator is a variable that explains the mechanisms or process through which an independent variable (x) affects a dependent variable (y).

What is the Purpose of the Option 'Seed' in a Line of PROCESS Syntax?

  • The purpose of the ‚Äòseed‚Äô option in a line of PROCESS syntax is to lock the random number generator. This ensures that the findings remain consistent across multiple runs of the analysis. Without specifying a ‚Äòseed‚Äô value, the random number generator would draw different values each time, leading to slightly different results.

With regard to ANCOVA, What is a Potential Consequence of having a Covariate Account for both Explained Variance from the Predictor and Unexplained Variance due to Error?

  • When a covariable absorbs variable from both the predictor and the error term, it can lead to:
ConsequenceExplanation
Over-Control Or Suppression Of The Predictor Effect- If the covariate is highly correlated with the predictor variable, it may remove variance that belongs to the predictor.
- This can reduce the effect size of the predictor making it seem less important than it actually is.
Multicollinearity Issues- If a covariate is highly correlated with the predictor, it can lead to multicollinearity, making it difficult to estimate the unique contribution of each variable.
- This can cause inflated SE_s and _unstable estimates in ANCOVA.
Reduction In Statistical Power- If the covariate explains a large portion of the variance in the outcome, the residual variance available for detecting the predictor‚Äôs influence may be too small, reducing the overall model‚Äôs power.
- This increases the likelihood of Type II Errors.
Distortion Of Error Variance (Homogeneity Of Regression Slopes Violation)- With an ANCOVA, we make the assumption that the relationship between the covariate and the dependent variable is the same across all levels of the predictor.
- If the covariate accounts for variance in both the predictor and the error term, the assumption may be violated, leading to biased results (Type I and Type II error).

What is the Definition of the Sphericity Assumption, and What are the Potential Consequences for Violating it?

  • Sphericity is an assumption in repeated measures ANOVA that states the variance of the differences between all pairs of within-subject conditions (levels of the independent variable) should be equal. This means that if we have three time points (A, B, C), then the variance of (A ‚Äì B) ‚âà variance of (A ‚Äì C) ‚âà variance of (B ‚Äì C).
  • Sphericity is a special case of homogeneity of variance assumption and is specific to repeated measures designs.
  • Potential Consequences of Violating Sphericity
    • Inflated Type I Errors Rate (False Positives)
      • Standard F-tests assume sphericity, so violating it increases the risk of finding a significant effect when none actually exists.
      • The p-values become too small, leading to incorrect conclusions.
    • Reduced Statistical Power (False Negative)
      • If sphericity is violated and not corrected, the test might be too conservative, making it harder to detect real effects (Type II Error: Failing to detect a true effect).
    • Distorted F-ratios
      • The denominator degrees of freedom in the F-test get inflated which makes the ANOVA test statistics biased. This impacts the accuracy of hypothesis testing.
