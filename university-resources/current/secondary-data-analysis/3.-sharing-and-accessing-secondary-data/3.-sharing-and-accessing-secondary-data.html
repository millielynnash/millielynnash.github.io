3. Sharing and Accessing Secondary Data

Accessing Secondary Data

Data Sharing

Benefits of Data Sharing

  • Reinforces open science = science works most effectively
  • Encourages diversity of analysis and opinions
  • Promotes new research and/or new methods
  • Improves methods of data collection and measurement
  • Reduces costs by avoiding duplicate data collection efforts and enhance the impact of the project

The FAIR Guiding Principles for scientific data management and stewardship

  • How will people find your data? (repository; meta data; etc.)
  • How will people access your data (restricted/public? When will it be made available? Etc.)
  • Are there barriers to using your data? (formats, methods, documentation?)
  • Is it easy for people to re-use your data? (assure high quality, how will it be stored long-term?)

Data Sharing is a Spectrum

Not Open/Closed Binary for Access
  • Publicly available (i.e., de-identified)
  • Restricted access (i.e., request to use)
  • Unshareable (i.e., no sharing possible)
Not All or Nothing for Data Sharing
  • All verifiable/reusable data possibly through anonymization
  • Aggregated/summarized/redacted version of the data
  • Publicly share some parts and restrict access to others
  • Restrict access, but share details of data held
  • Share only details about study, no sharing possible

Types of Secondary Data

  • Many datasets have both public and restricted files.
  • Restricted data contains more data and a wider range of data values than are included in public use data.
  • For most, the public-use files provide all the data they will need for most analyses, though some users may find that only the restricted files have the specific data they need.
    • If you are not sure, first examine the public-use data.
  • Outliers (unusually high or unusually low values) are top- or bottom-coded to prevent identification of unique schools, teachers, parents, and children without affecting overall data quality.
  • Some continuous variables are converted into categorical variables, and some categorical variables have their categories collapsed.
    • Category value labels are provided for continuous variables that are converted into categorical variables.
  • Variables with too few cases and/or a sparse distribution are suppressed. The values for these variables are set to -2 and labelled 'suppressed' in the Electronic Codebook (ECB). The value -2 means that the data for this variable are suppressed to protect the respondent's confidentiality.
  • Variables that provide a particularly identifying characteristic, such as a specific disability, or information that could be matched against external data sources to obtain a specific identifying characteristic, such as exact date of marriage or divorce, are also suppressed. The values for these variables are set to -2.

To Access Restricted Data

  • Will vary for different data, but for all, you will need to complete an application.
    • Submit signed license document
      • Primary researcher
      • Senior official at institution
  • Submit signed and notarized affidavits of nondisclosure for all proposed users.
  • Submit a signed security plan (must specify the exact locked location) where the data will be secured. You cannot transmit restricted-use data.
  • Maintain a data license file and ensure that all authorized users follow the agreed upon terms.
  • Participate in unannounced security inspections to ensure compliance.
  • Adhere to established publication rules.
  • Submit all materials for disclosure review.
  • Complete annual trainings online.
  • Complete yearly reports of what work you have done.

How to Find Data

Where to Start Looking?

  • Start with a broad question.
    • Think about who might collect that data (e.g., governments, other researchers, etc.)
  • Want to stick close to a specific subfield or type of data?
    • Look for recognized repositories in your field, large research projects, or publications that have data access statements.
  • Likely need to combine several search strategies.
  • Remember
    • Some disciplines have more of a tradition of sharing
    • Non-sensitive data are easier to obtain than sensitive data
  • Also check: https://osf.io and ldbase.org

Navigating Data Archives

  • Archives are easily searchable
    • Searching by study topic is helpful when you are looking for studies focused on a specific subject.
    • Searching by variable name is helpful when you are interested in a particular variable (e.g., spanking).
  • Often, studies are organized into collections in each archive so a researcher can quickly identify groups of studies focused on a particular subarea.
  • The organization of each archive varies, but most share several major features.
  • Most archives allow you to perform basic descriptive analyses before the downloading a dataset.
    • This is great because you can determine whether a variable of interest has a large enough sample size, enough variance in repsonses, for your purposes.
  • Such online analyses are also useful for testing a new hypothesis before collecting new data on the topic or performing preliminary analyses for grant applications.
  • Most archives offer FAQs, tips for effective searching, and access to staff members via email.
  • ICPSR tutorial
    • How to search for variables and filter
    • How to search for publications
    • How to look at descriptive within and across studies
    • How to look for studies and variables within studies
    • Study usage reports

Things to Consider

  • Subject Matter
    • Consider whether the subject is consistent with your problem definition and whether the data apply to the time/population of interest
  • Methods
    • Consider whether the data can be used with your preferred method of analysis or if you'll need ot learn a new skill.
  • Measurement
    • Determine whether the data capture the measures and constructs you are interested in with sufficient/adequate detail.
  • Time
    • You will save time on data collection and get access to larger scale data, but may spend more time on cleaning, modifying, getting documentation, etc.
  • License/Copyright
    • Is the data licensed? Does it restrict usage?
  • Terms of Use
    • Are the terms you need to meet to gain access to the data or other restrictions?
  • Citations
    • You will want to formally cite the data you use; data sets should have a persistent identifier to help cite the data, such as DOI.
  • Documentation
    • Look for good documentation on how the data was collected, for what purpose, how it was transformed.
  • Credibility
    • Is the data from a recognized lab, researcher, institution? Do the publications based on the data hold?

Evaluating Your Secondary Data Sources

Overall Suitability

  • Does the dataset contain the information you require to answer your research question(s) and meet your objectives?
  • Do the measures used match those you require?
  • Is the data set a proxy for the data you really need?
  • Does the dataset cover the population that is the subject of your research?
  • Does the dataset cover the geographical area that is the subject of your research?
  • Can data about the population that is the subject of your research be separated from unwanted data?
  • Are the data for the right time period or sufficiently up to date?
  • Are data available for all the variables you require to answer your research question(s) and meet your objectives?
  • Are the variables defined clearly?

Precise Suitability

  • How reliable is the dataset?
  • How credible is the data source?
  • Is it clear what the source of the data is?
  • Do the credentials of the source of the data (author, institution, or organization sponsoring the data) suggest it is likely to be reliable?
  • Do the data have an associated copyright statement?
  • Do associated published documents exist?
  • Does the source contain contact details for obtaining further information about the data?
  • Is the method described clearly?
  • If sampling was used, what was the procedure and what were the associated sampling errors and response rates?
  • Who was responsible for collecting or recording the data?
  • (For surveys) Is a copy of the questionnaire or interview checklist included?
  • (For compiled data) Are you clear how the data were analyzed and compiled?
  • Are the data likely to contain measurement bias?
  • What was the original purpose for which the data were collected?
  • Who was the target

Codebooks and User's Guides

  • The most important step is to read ALL the data documentation.

Making Sense of Your Data

  • Before assessing data, review codebooks and questionnaires
    • What information was collected
    • For whom and when and where
    • What changes were made before archiving
  • Codebooks also include other information (e.g., topics addressed, questions and measurements used, frequency of responses or values, how to use the data, etc.)
  • Like a regular book, some codebooks are better than others.
  • If people code the variable in a certain way, is there potential for error? Would two researchers get the same outcomes if they chose to code the variable differently?
