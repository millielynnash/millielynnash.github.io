7. Sampling & Complex Designs

Weights and Complex Samples

What is and Why Should We Strive for Population Representative Sampling

Importance of a Population Perspective

  • Why should we strive for population representative sampling?
    • Important for policy or practice. We do not want to recommend changes that are not based on research that is representative of the full population those changes would impact.
  • How?
    • Narrow our populations of interest (e.g., Columbus instead of the US)
    • Work on large teams
    • Use secondary data

Design / Sample Weight

  • Normally used to compensate for over- and under-sampling of individuals
  • Example
    • It is common to over-sample minority groups... If we doubled the size of our minority sample, then each minority case would get a design weight of 1/2 or .5.
  • We use the design weight when we want the statistics to be representative of the population.

Non-Response and Attrition Weights

  • Used to compensate for the fact that persons with certain characteristics are not as likely to respond to the survey.
  • Example
    • Most population surveys have substantially more female than male respondents; because surveys over-represents females and under-represents males in the population, a weight is used to compensate for this bias.
  • There are many responsent characteristics that are likely to be related to the propensity to respond such as age, education, race/ethnicity, place of residence.

What is the Difference between Weighted and Unweighted Data?

  • With unweighted data, each case is counted equally
  • With weighted data, each case is counted relative to its representation in the population
  • Unweighted data represent only those in the sample who provide data
  • Weights allow analyses that represent the target population

STATA Command Syntax (weight)

  • Most STATA commands can deal with weighted data, where the weight is a variable in the dataset.
  • You need to specify the type of weight and the weight variable, using brackets at the end of your command, as in:
    summarize x [weight=weightvarname]
    tab x [weight=weightvarname]
    regress y x [weight=weightvarname]
  • There are 4 types of weights in STATA, but for most data analyses you will use one of the following:
    • Probability pweights, for observations sampled with unequal probability of selection. Use this for inferential analyses.
    • Analytic aweights, for data containing averages where the average is weighted by the # obs used in calculating the average…. Use this for your descriptive statistics.

Illustration of Weights

Data will Almost Always have Many Different Types of Weights

  • Different studies create different types of weights... Some data create weights depending on:
    • Level of analysis (child, teacher, or school)
    • Type of analysis (cross-sectional vs. Longitudinal)
    • Source of data (child assessment, parent interview, teacher questionnaires, etc.)
    • For example, the ECLS-K has ~ 105 different options

Attrition Weights

  • Many suggest you use combination weights when estimating longitudinal analyses because they assume that these users will not adjust for attrition on their own.
  • I suggest avoiding the use of attrition weights.
    • Instead, use modern missing data procedures for attrition bias, and use the appropriate sample weight to adjust for sampling bias
  • I make this recommendation because attrition weights have two key disadvantages
    • Attrition weights often reduce the analytical sample size, and thereby, statistical power.
    • If many non-model variables related to missing data are incorporated, missing data techniques adjust for attrition bias more effectively than attrition weights

When to Use Unweighted Data?

  • Use weights whenever possible, but some methods do not allow weights
  • Steps to follow to avoid vias in unweighted analyses
    • Include as Independent Variable that might account for the sample design or non-response
    • If a weight is available, it can also be included as an IV
    • If the weight is associated with the outcom ein a model including the design variables, then the weight is likely to have been constructed in a way related to your outcome; bias is possile.
    • E.g., some multilevel models, some structural equation programs, etc.

Primary Sampling Unites

  • National samples are first divided into primary sampling units... What these units are depends on the study.
    - E.g., in many data of the US the PSUs are geographic areas consisting of counties or groups of counties

Strata

  • After identifying PSUs, then PSUs are stratified into groups.
  • These groups are formed to keep similar units together... How many and what types of strata are determined by study goals.

    Then within each strata, and within PSUs, you sample

Standard Error Adjustment

  • Software can make the adjustment... A few different approaches with the easiest being the Taylor Series approach
    • Use primary sampling unit (PSU) and strata variables in the data file.
    • The strata and PSU identifiers are often associated with a specific sampling weight variable.
    • For example, in the ECLS-K the weight C1CW0 has the following strata and PSU: C1TVWSTR and C1TCWPSU

When Accounting for Weight, PSUs and Stratification Variables in STATA

svyset PSU [pweight = WEIGHT], strata(STRATA)

  • Then use svy: before your subsequent commands (e.g., svy: tab or svy: mean or svy: regress)
  • svy: will NOT work with all commands
  • svy: mean and mean [weight = weight] yield the same mean estimates… it is the SEs that are different)

Summary

  • Weights should be used when analyzing data from national samples; otherwise, you cannot make claims about representativeness
  • The appropriate weight should be selected based on:
    • Level of analysis
    • Round (s) of data
    • Source (s) of data
  • There may not be a 'perfect' weight for many analyses
  • All statistical tests should be based on standard errors that account for complex sample design
