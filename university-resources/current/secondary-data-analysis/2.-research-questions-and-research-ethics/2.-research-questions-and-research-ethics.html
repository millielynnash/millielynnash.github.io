Developing Research Questions and Ethics

  • Before you put pen to paper (or analyze data), formulate a research question.
    • A research question is a statement that identifies the phenomenon to be studied.
    • For example, "Does preschool influence children's language skills?"
  • Why are good research questions important and what makes them 'good?'
    • Purpose
    • Relevance
    • Feasibility

How do you Develop a Research Question?

Literature Review and Research Questions

  • To produce a research question, you need to know the literature first.
  • Find out what has been done and how you can contribute to the existing literature.

Identifying a Research Question

  1. I have a research question.
    • If you do not have a research question, narrow down your topic.
  2. Review the literature
  3. Is the question viable, original. feasible?
  4. You have a research question!
No Research Question? Where Do You Begin?
  • Narrow down on an area of interest
  • Within that area, try to answer a question that:
    • Has either not been addressed before
    • Has been addressed before but that you could extend (i.e., new data-set, different approach, etc.)
    • Has been addressed but requires replication
    • Starts a new research area (NOT recommended)
Literature Review When You Do Not Have a Research Question
  • Read as much as you can on the topic
  • Read recent studies first, then read backwards
  • Read all relevant papers (or at least the influential ones)
  • Ideas on the research topic often come from reading
  • The more you read, the easier it will be to come up with research questions
Literature Review When You Think You Have a Research Question
  • Reviewing the literature will allow you to understand whether your question has been answered/is valid.
  • By reading previous studies, you might get ideas on how to tweak your original research question into a new one.
  • Whether you have or do not have a research question, it is crucial that you review the literature in the area first.
  • Many leave the review as the last step, but this is dangerous.

Tips and Common Errors

Common Mistakes When Identifying New Research Questions
  • Not original
  • Not feasible
    • Time constraint (e.g., not enough time)
    • Resources (e.g., lack of data)
    • Knowledge (e.g., do I know method X?)
  • Too broadly defined research question
  • Not well justified
  • Not motivated by literature
Tips
  • Start searching for a topic as early as possible
  • Once you narrow down a topic/area, look at the relevant literature (i.e., a literature review)
  • Once you have done the literature review (has it already been done? No, why not? Is it interesting/relevant?), then ask yourself:
    • Is the question specific (not too broad)?
    • Is it feasible in the allotted time?
    • Do I have enough resources (data) to answer this question?
    • How about knowledge (methods)?
    • Is the research question falling within my area of research?
    • If the answer is yes to all the previous question, congratulations, you have a research question.
  • Your time is your most important resource.
  • Do not take a project that you will lead if it only leads to one paper (except for in very rare circumstances)
    • Try to commit to doing 3 papers on a topic/data set
    • More efficient time use (e.g., read similar lit)
    • Looks good when others are evaluating your CV
  • A good research question will
    • Allow outsiders to see your intentions
    • Help you develop hypotheses (not always)
    • Contributes to existing knowledge
      • Contribution does not have to be huge
      • Replication is a contribution
      • Filling a gap is not enough justification, why does this gap matter?
    • Guide the design of your methods
    • Allow the target population to be identified
    • Identify the outcome variables and key predictors of those variables
    • Determine what type of study is needed (e.g., descriptive, correlational, experimental)
    • Identify background characteristics that might influence predictors and outcomes
    • Raise questions about how to best collect data
    • Influence the number of participants in the study
  • Writing a good question is an iterative process
    1. What do you want to learn about?
    2. Draft and redraft with increasing specificity using the guidelines listed above.
    3. Ask for feedback from colleagues (science is a collective effort).
    4. Build on the work of others by conducting a systematic literature review.

Justifying Your Questions

  • This is a key writing skill that you will need to do for every paper you write.
    • Hook the reader in.
    • Practice!

Paragraph 1

  • Explain the larger context that this study will inform.
  • Start big and then work down to the end of the paragraph to set up something more specific that is important to know.

Paragraph 2

  • Flows directly from the earlier points in paragraph 1. If studying X is important, then here is what I am doing.
  • State the basic aims, hypotheses, questions, and give the briefest overview of the data and methods (focusing on strengths)

Paragraph 3

  • Explain the potential impact of the specific things you lay out in the prior paragraph - could be theoretically, in terms of policy, filling holes, etc.
  • Can be combined with paragraph 2 in some cases.

Research Ethics and Being Scooped

  • "The scientific literature is our institutional memory. It is important that this literature accurately reflect what happened, who did it, and to what extend one observation is independent of another." - Dennis McInerney

Duplicate and Piecemeal Publication

  • Double Publication involves publishing the same data more than once.
  • Piecemeal Publication involves dividing the report of a research project into multiple articles.
    • The articles cannot be integrated into one
    • Each article has a distinct purpose
  • Duplicate and piecemeal publications are misleading and distort the scientific literature.
  • Publication ethics disallow duplicate publication (Publication Ethics: Plagiarism, Self-Plagiarism, Duplicate Publication, and Piecemeal Publication - Julia Muennich Cowell, 2017)

Building on Prior Studies

  • Replicate the study with a different sample, setting, or methodology.
    • Do this if most of the past research you have read examines one type of sample, setting, or method.
  • Examine the topic using a different design. If the study was a quasi- or non-experimental, conduct an experiment.
    • Do this if causation has not been consistently established by past research, or it has not been consistently established with the population you are interested in.
  • Examine how another variable might impact results
    • Do this if you have research evidence to suggest that results may depend on another variable.
  • Conduct a similar study with a different outcome or dependent variable.
    • Do this if you find research evidence supporting the new associations you plan to examine.

We All Get Scooped At Some Point

  • Being first is not always a sign of being a good scientist
    • Many discoveries are wrong or over claimed.

Being Scooped

  • You do not show you are a great scientist by being the first to claim something.
  • A large part of getting over being scooped is getting over yourself and realizing that science is bigger than any one of us.
  • It also means you are doing something other people care about.
  • If you are scooped, do not be discouraged. It is unlikely that any two researchers would address a question in the exact same way.
