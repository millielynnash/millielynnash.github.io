Registered Reports

Ethics in Publishing

  • Why do researchers HARK and/or use their flexibility in their publications?
    • Significant findings get published
    • Desire to tell a good story
    • Hindsight bias
    • Need for speed
  • How do we as a field reduce HARKing and problematic use of flexible decisions?
    • Demand transparency
    • Change incentive structures
    • Use of pre-registration to:
      • Force less flexibility after the data is collected
      • Allow reviewers to see a priori decisions
    • Encourage replication
    • Encourage/mandate data sharing
  • Ensuring appropriate use of your findings
    • How can researchers ensure that there is appropriate use of their findings?
      • Discussion sections
      • Engaging with media
      • Not overstating relevance of findings to practice and policy - especially if exploratory work (e.g., not pre-registered, no replications)

Advocacy and Ethics

  • Our desire to advocate can cause ethical challenges.
    • Harm to particpants (e.g., zone of privacy)
    • Ways that we present findings and interpret findings
  • Something to always be aware of and thinking about when making decisions.

Ethics in Authorship

  • Discuss early and often
  • Follow professional guidelines (and journal guidelines)
    • OSU Authorship Guidelines
    • ICMJE Authorship Guidelines
    • Authorship and contributorship | COPE: Committee on Publication Ethics

  • Want valid results

    • Measurement validity=closeness to the truth; are we capturing the concepts we seek to capture?

  • How do you decide what measures to capture in a study?
    • Start with your research question.
  • Conceptualization:
    • What are the concepts we are interested in?
    • How do we define them?
    • How do we define them in ways that are measurable?
      • Specific
      • Able to be captured
    • Need to do for both objective (e.g., income) and abstract (e.g. happiness)

Measurement Operationalization

  • Operationalization:
    • The process of developing valid measurement of the variables in a study
    • E.g., choosing measures for survey developing observation protocols, developing experimental manipulations
  • Independent Variable
    • Predictor
  • Dependent Variable
    • Outcome
  • Control Variable (Covariate)
    • We think they matter for both the predictor and outcome. We want to make sure the associations we are looking at are not just
  • Moderating Variable (Changes the association you are looking at)
  • Mediating Variable (Process)
  • Need to conceptualize and operationalize all of these variables in advance.

Reliability and Validity:

  • Goal is to use measures that are reliable and valid
    • Look to theory for help with conceptualization
    • Look to prior empirical research for help with measure selection
  • When possible, choose measures for which reliability and validity has already been established.
    • However, you will still need to demonstrate this within the sample you collect/use.

Reliability

  • Measures cannot be valid if they are not reliable.
  • Reliability:
    • Degree to which a measure produces a consistent answer when the phenomenon being studied is not changing
  • Test-Retest
  • Internal Consistency
  • Interrater Reliability
  • You should be prepared to report on internal consistency and interrater reliability within your own research.
  • Reliability lets you know if you are capturing something meaningful, but not if it is what you are intending to capture.

Validity

  • Are we capturing what we want to be capturing?
    • Glossary/Face Validity
    • Content Validity
    • Criterion Validity
    • Discriminant Validity
  • Capturing psychological constructs is really hard.
    • Harder than a lot of constructs in physical sciences.
    • How can we capture constructs better?
      • Use multiple measures (Kagan)
        • Natural Observations
        • Self-Reports
        • Physiological
        • Other Reporters
        • Lab Tasks
  • Challenges of multiple measures
  • Lack of correlation
    • Multiple reasons:
      • Role of context
      • Individual perceptions
    • Brings complications, but can brings us closer to truth
    • Lorenz and the multitrait model
    • Think about Venn dyagrams and where shared and unique variance comes from

1. What is your construct?Define the construct. Describe theories and research supporting the construct.
2. Why and how did you select your measure?Justify the measure selection. Report existing validity evidence.
3. What measure did you use to operationalize the construct?Describe the measure and administration procedure. Match the measure to the construct.
4. How did you quantify your measure?Describe the measure and administration procedure. Report the items or stimuli included in each score. Describe the calculation of scores. Describe all conducted (e.g., psychometric) analyses.
5. Did you modify the scale? And if so, how and why?Describe any modifications. Indicate if modifications occurred before or after data collection. Provide justification for modifications.
6. Did you create a measure on the fly?Justify why you did not use an existing measure. Report all measurement details for the new measure. Describe all available validity evidence; if there is no evidence, report that

Measurement Invariance

  • Just because a measure works for one group of people does not mean it will work for another.
    • Huge problem in our field.
  • Choose measures that have been shown to be valid in populations you are studying.
  • Use measurement invariance testing to make sure the measures are working for subgroups in your population.
