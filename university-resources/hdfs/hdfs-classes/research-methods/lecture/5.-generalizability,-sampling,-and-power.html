Reliability

  • Measures cannot be valid if they are not reliable
  • Reliability
    • Degree to which a measure produces a consistent answer when the phenomenon being studied is not changing
  • Test-Retest
  • Internal Consistency
  • Interrater Reliability
  • You should be prepared to report on internal consistency and interrater reliability within your own research.
  • Reliability lets you know if you are capturing something meaningful, but not if it is what you are intending to capture.

Validity

  • Face Validity
    • Does a test measure what it's supposed to measure?
  • Content Validity
    • Assess whether a test is representative of all aspects of the construct
  • Criterion Validity
    • Evaluates how accurately a test measures the outcome it was designed to measure
  • Discriminant Validity
    • Measures whether constructs that theoretically should not be related to each other are, in fact, unrelated.

Measures to Variables

  • The measures we choose will eventually become the variables in our statistical analyses
  • Continuous
    • Variable can assume an infinite number of real values within a given interval (e.g., income)
  • Count
    • Individual piece of count data (e.g., number of kids)
  • Ordinal
    • Categorical variables with ordered possible values; ratings about opinions or perceptions, or demographic factors that are categorized into levels or brackets
  • Categorical
    • Dichotomous - only 2 categories (e.g., employed or not)
    • Characteristic that can't be quantifiable
  • Always measure things with as much precision as possible
  • If possible, measure things continuously
    • You can always collapse things into categories later. (e.g., age [use birthday and day of assessment], hours worked per week [can make part-time/full-time later])
  • Descriptive Statistics
    • Want to understand your data
    • Check for variability
    • Check for normality
    • Check for unusual data (e.g., bots)
    • Check for outliers
      • Have plan ready in advance
    • Look at reliability statistics
    • Look at associations among independent variables
      • Is a latent variable warranted? (unmeasured variables that is believes to influence responses to a number of survey items)
    • Do not look at associations (even correlations) among independent variables and dependent variables (HARKing)

Validity in Qualitative

  • Qualitative research also needs to demonstrate rigor.
    • Looks very different
    • Validity is about ensuring appropriate conclusions are drawn - based on what informants provide and theoretical lens being used
    • Rigor occurs throughout the research process, not just at the beginning or the end (also true for quantitative work)
  • Role of verification

Validity in Qualitative Work

  • Role of verification
    • Iterative process between the data and the literature
  • Responsiveness
    • Requires knowing your data, knowing how to listen, and knowing how to synthesize
    • Flexible in way of processing data
  • Sampling
    • Saturation
    • Seeking negative cases
  • Audit trails
  • Member checks

Thinking about your Registered Report

  • You need to demonstrate how your data or data collection plan is valid.
  • Quantitative studies: reliability and validity; prior studies; how does this measure capture the constructs you are talking about in your literature review and research questions?
  • Qualitative studies: What steps are you taking to ensure rigor?

How to Write a Literature Review

Overview

  • The literature review/introduction will direction follow your opening 2-3 paragraphs.
  • Goal is to explain the rationale for the work you are doing.
    • Not to comprehensively summarize every related study ever
    • Show your work builds off of prior theory and empirical work
    • Highlight the unique contributions of your work

Getting Started

  • Although you may not review every related study, you need to do a thorough literature review to understand how your study relates to others and how your study fits.
    • Focus on peer-reviewed journal articles
    • Try to get a sense of paper by looking at abstract and skimming before committing to fully reading
  • To do this more effectively, you should plan out the sections of your literature review in advance

Developing Sequential Logic for your Paper

  • Based on your questions
  • Provide an organization/headings for your literature review
  • Your questions may be a series of questions (each will become a header in your literature review/introduction)
  • Or your questions may build upon each other

Using Your Sequential Logic

  • Your sequential logic becomes the headers for your introduction
  • And it ensures that you know the 'argument' for each section (because you have the arrows drawn and connections made already)
    • Note: your argument does not mean you ignore findings that don't support your hypothesis - you use them as arguments as to why your research is needed.
  • Ultimately, should match your first 2/3 paragraphs, methods, results, and discussion.

More Advice

  • Keep your headings general and big picture
  • Consider ending with a, 'The present study...' section
  • May have to include a conceptual or theoretical paragraph somewhere
    • Often easier to discuss theory within your aims-based headings
  • Try not to use jargon, simple words instead
  • Do not repeat yourself even if it is written in a different way (e.g., put another way)
  • Do not provide laundry list of extant literature; instead, synthesize

Generalizability

  • What is it? Is it attainable?
  • Think about research you have read recently? Do you think they have adequately discussed generalizability?
  • Big takeaways:
    • Being as thoughtful as possible about our sampling plan before collecting data is going to help us create higher quality research.
    • Be transparent about the ways in which your research is or is not generalizable.
    • Getting good samples is really hard. Your research doesn't have to be perfect (remember no study is).
  • Sample Generalizability:
    • Can findings based on a sample be applied to the larger population from which the sample was drawn?
    • Need for 'thick description'
  • Cross-Population Generalizability
    • Can findings from a study of one population be applied ot other populations?
    • Usually infers in universality (although doesn't have to)

Why Worry about Sampling?

  • Sampling is unnecessary if all the units in the population are identical (e.g., blood)
  • But what about people?
    • Are fundamental psychological processes the same across all people (universal)?

Nonprobability Sampling Methods

  • When random sampling is not possible
  • Availability (Convenience) Sampling:
    • Take who you can get
    • Exploratory or qualitative research
    • Intro to psych!
  • It is okay to use convenience sampling but you need to be aware of and acknowledging your limitations.
  • Quota Sampling:
    • Make sure sample represents certain characteristics in the population (but can't know all characteristics, and not random)
    • This is not as good as random/probability sampling but is still a much improved strategy over convenience sampling.
  • Purposive Sampling:
    • Studying a subset of limited group
    • Studying 'key informants'
    • High on internal validity

Representative Sampling

  • Population:
    • The entire set of individuals or other entities (e.g., families, schools) to which study findings are to be generalized
    • Census = study the whole population
  • Sampling Frame
    • A list of all the elements in a population
  • Sample
    • A subset of a population used to study the population as a whole
  • Two factors that make it easier to get a representative sample:
    • Larger samples are more likely to be representative
      • Sample size does matter in quantitative research
      • Both for generalizability and power
      • However, unthoughtful sampling doesn't improve much with larger samples.
    • It is easier to derive a representative sample from a homogenous population (i.e., can have a smaller sample)

Probability Sampling

  • Allows us to know in advance how likely it is that any element of a population will be selected.
