Family Demography and Theory

Meta-Theorizing in Family Theory

Components of Theory

Assumptions
  • Every theory we discuss is limited in some way because it presents a particular way of view the world or a particular phenomenon. 
  • Assumptions of the theory help us understand the limitations or boundaries of a particular theory.
    • Examples:
      • Economic and exchange theories
        • Assume that all behavior is reducible to rewards/costs of behavioral exchanges.
      • Attachment theory (Attachment Theory)
        • Assumes that our template for socializing with people can be primarily explained by the relationship with have with a primary attachment figure.
      • Family Systems Theory (Family Systems Theory)
        • Assumes all behaviors are linked to the larger social unit of analysis. While each of these are partly true, not every interaction we have in a relationship is rationale (e.g., Exchange theory: sometimes we make costly decisions in a relationship even when we know another behavior my help the relationship are we more motivated for ourselves or the relationship?
      • Attachment theory
        • Although our relationship with primary caregivers are important in childhood, attachment theory doesn’t necessarily deal with genetic, personality, or environmental constraints/affordances that can shape these relationships; Family systems: although family dynamics are powerful, where is the role of autonomy and self-efficacy that can explain shifts away from embedded family norms and patterns of behaviors?)
Concepts
  • Concepts are the key features of a theory.
  • These are the ideas/concepts (often variables) that we measure and focus on when we think of theory.
    • For example, if you were interested in conducting a study around (social exchange, attachment, or family systems theory) what would you potentially be interested in measuring in your study? – give an example if none come up).
    • Exactly, concepts are the ideas/constructs that allow us to think/apply the theory to something we are interested in studying.
Propositions/Principles
  • These are the hypotheses based on the assumptions and constructs in a given theory.
    • For example, one proposition we might pull from social exchange theory is that in a relationship, when potential alternatives (e.g., other partners) seem more rewarding than our current relationships, we could expect someone might choose to end the relationship or cheat.
    • For attachment theory, we could hypothesize that when someone is high in the anxiety dimension of attachment, they might seem more reassurance from their partner than someone lower in anxiety. For family systems, we could hypothesize that when families have rigid boundaries, it may be harder to intervene and change a child’s problematic behavior than if the family had less rigid boundaries.

Ontology vs. Epistemology

  • Ontology = ways of being
  • Realism
    • There is a real world our theories can describe, and we should have and use theories that describe the real world.
    • How do observations and experiences determine to understand the real world?
  • Critical Realism
    • The world is knowable, but the focus is on the mechanism that governs people within social structures.
  • Skepticism
    • We cannot truly know if our theories apply to the real world.
    • How do we consider something understudied?
  • Epistemology = ways of knowing
  • Realism
  • Throughout the semester, we will talk about theories that are grand/abstract/critical—they present a big picture way of viewing the world. To mid-range or lower-range theories that attempt to explain and provide specific hypotheses about families or specific family behaviors/outcomes.
    • This idea is helpful to keep in mind as not all theories are well-suited for every level of analysis.
    • Some theories may be better suited for certain methodologies than others.

The Importance of Organizing Theories

Abstract Theory vs. Contextual Theory
  • We primarily use contextual theory.
  • Abstract Theory
    • Implicit Abstract Theory describes connections / links / causes.
    • Explicit Abstract Theory adds an explanation about the connection / links.
  • Contextual Theory
    • Connects abstract theory to the real world. (This is the world we primarily work in as family scientists).
    • These are more testable theories.
    • A good comparison might be Socioecological Theory is more of an abstract theory and the ABC-X model is more of a contextual theory.
      • The book discusses an example about family = abstract while stepfamily = contextual.
  • Descriptive Statements
    • A theoretical concept like family cohesion in a contextual theory might appear in a hypothesis as a set of survey questions about how close people in a family feel to one another, in a narrative description as an informant’s story about family celebrations, or in an empirical generalization as the frequency of family meals.
    • Descriptive statements are more about observable/measurable concepts.
  • Deduction
    • We can identify and measure concepts that allow us to test a hypothesis. We believe its true if the observations agree, and false if they do not.
  • Induction
    • Moving from observations to develop theory (a qualitative approach), he argues there is no real logic or repeatable pattern that can always help us move from observations to “true” theory.
    • Uniformity Principle
      • What will happen in the future will be the same as in the past.
  • Abduction
    • Theory is a creative process of moving from observation to theory.
    1. Use imagination, experience, literature, or intuition to generate theory
    2. Use justification to provide logical account connecting observation to theory
    • Interplay between all three—starts with abduction to generate and develop theory, then deduction to develop/test theory – induction helps make sense of testing theory.

Empiricism vs. Postmodernism

Empiricism
  • Standpoint
    • Reality is subjective and a function of our unique experiences/standpoints.
    • We can only know/theorize about things we have experienced.
    • Marginalized groups have a unique perspective.
  • Postmodern Skepticism
    • There is no one, knowable reality.
    • Abstract theory is too general, stems from theorists' own social positions, and ignores power.
  • Positivism
    • Attempts to generate general rules that could be used to develop theory to produce unbiased research/hypotheses (we now know there is no such thing).
      • Language of observations = language to turn theory into a descriptive statement
  • Empiricism
    • Focus on predicting observations based on theory (not on explanation, though)
  • Falsification
    • Theory could be deductive or inductive by to research to falsify the theory. The theory is only true because it has not been falsified. The more it holds up, the more we can be confident it’s a reliable theory.

Social Science Operates through Multiple Theoretical Frameworks

  • Multiple frameworks operate simultaneously, but also within each framework, there is a ”normal science” paradigm for each theory within sub-disciplines.
  • Challenge with multiple frameworks is the different meanings available based on different theories.
  • Conflicts between different paradigms/theories are often a function of politics, which my also reflect societal changes in the U.S.
  • Incommensurability can occur between researchers based on their own positionalities, and we now understand and see that theories were inattentive to other people’s experiences, ways of thinking, and doing science/theorizing.
    • This also helps us understand how certain fields have similar concepts with different names.
    • Give examples—spanking research by Liz Gershoff, minority vs minoritized

Family Science

  • Using contextual theory, fam scientists have integrated multiple philosophies (e.g., the uncertainty of measurement conceptualized through alphas and p-values to inform falsification/hypothesis testing).
    • Also, family scientists are skeptics that many concepts are made up and can never know if they fully match Reality.
    • Also use operationalization (logical empiricists) to measure concepts while acknowledging that they may imprecisely match theory and observation.
    • “When it comes to theory testing, most family theorists are aware of the limits of falsification, but within those limits are comfortable employing its procedures. “
    • “Most family scientists believe in verification, sometimes implicitly using Bayesian concepts of probability, so that our confidence in an abstract or contextual theory increases with additional empirical support.”
    • “However, family scientists believe that an accumulation of differently flawed observations can begin to approximate the boundaries of Reality. “
  • “Family scientists are mostly reasonable realists who anticipate that there is probably a “Real World” out there; we really do believe there are families. However, we also recognize that there are many distinct but overlapping perspectives (frameworks) from which family life can be multiple and simultaneously understood. “
  • “Family scientists are reasonable skeptics who recognize that many, if not most, of our concepts are made up from our imaginations, and that we will never know how much—or if—they match Reality.”

Future Challenges

  • “Mini revolutions, ”e.g., Family scientists integrate multiple theories to make sense of the world
  • Challenge of Meaning
    • “Supernatural explanations for disease, divine spheres for planetary motion, falling objects seeking their proper place, and the “invisible hand” of economics were replaced by germs, gravity, and individual preference schedules do . Why people think they are ill, where a falling object “wants” to be: these sorts of meaning-based explanations are no longer proposed. In these historical cases, meaning explanations have been replaced by causal explanations.
  • “In the end, both realists and skeptics agree that there are limits to what we can know, so whether improved explanations are closer to Truth as realists claim or more useful as practical understanding as skeptics claim, it doesn’t actually make a difference in what we do with our explanations. “

Demography of Families

Demography

  • The Institute for Population Research & Census are examples of what demographers do.
  • May sound familiar as you often need to include “demographics” in your research projects.
  • Traditionally defined as studying three population processes, fertility, mortality, and migrations.
    • More modern conceptualizations are social demography that seek to study population processes that may not fully align with these big three (e.g., Family Demography is a big field).
  • Family Demography typically two types of studies:
    • Careful tracking and measurement of families at either a given time point or trends over time.
    • Focus on the social, cultural, and economic correlates, precursors, and consequences of family structure and transitions.

Diverging Destinies

  • “Diverging Destinies” framework, articulated by McLanahan (2004), indicates that families are increasingly dissimilar across social class lines, often proxied by educational attainment.
  • A theme you will notice about a lot of demographic and sociological explanations is that they take an economic or structural approach to understanding families, marriage, child-birth, and development.

Demographic Transition

Marriage and Remarriage


  • Most young people still aspire to marriage, yet current trends suggest a retreat from marriage.
  • Declining in married adults partly explained by older age at first marriage (delay in marriage) and fewer people getting married (retreat from marriage).
    • Lower marriage rates for Black Americans (i.e., growing black-white differences in marriage rates). But also true for Hispanic and AINA than White and API women) – Why though?
  • Those with higher SES more likely be married (true for White and Black women).
    • May be explained by economic “readiness” the people link to marriage (it has become a luxury good), which is not as available to those without college degrees.
    • May be explained by marriage market availability and mass incarceration.
  • Second Demographic Transition and the “singles are coming”
  • Remarriage and divorce remain common.
    • Longer time to remarriage for black and Latina women compared to white women.
    • Highest remarriage rates for higher educational levels
  • Post-divorce cohabitation may be replacing marriage given the high divorce rate.
  • Remarriage = more stepfamilies.

Divorce

  • Divorce rates increased steadily between 1960 and rates remain high since 1970s, but divorce rates have declined since 1980s (though still historically high).
    • May continue to decline as younger adults have lower divorce rates. Paired with older ages of marriage, young adults who marry may also be a select group of more committed people.
  • Greater divorces rates among older Americans (i.e., 50+) has been called the “Grey Divorce”
    • Largely driven by baby boomers who had high rates of dissolution to begin with.
  • Divorce is dramatically stratified by education with up to 60% of those without HS or GED getting divorced (isn’t it expensive? And might this contribute to income inequality given that divorce and relationship dissolution is typically costly?).
    • Smaller educational difference for Black women compared to White women, but overall the differences between White and Black women, Black women’s marriages were more likely to dissolve.
  • “Since the early 2000s, the relationship between divorce rates and unemployment rates has been negative; that is, when unemployment went down, the divorce rate went up, and when unemployment went up (as during the Great Recession), divorce rates went down”
    • Small effects
    • Recessions delayed rather than forgoing divorce.
    • When men become unemployed when separated, tend to delays divorce.
    • When in economic straights, may be likely to forgo divorce unless remarriage is on the horizon.

Cohabitation

  • Marriage may be delayed but people are still not delaying living with intimate partners.
  • Rates of cohabitation with different gender partner sped up since the 1970s.
  • Most women cohabitate by there mid-late twenties.
    • Cohabitation also common among those 50+.
    • Very common to live with a spouse beforehand.
    • Marriage or dissolutions following cohabitation within 2-3 years.
  • Lower likelihood of cohabitation if at least a bachelor’s degree. College-educated women were more likely to transition into marriage from cohabitation than less educated women.
  • Cohabitation is more of a steppingstone to marriage, especially for the more economically disadvantaged
  • Decoupling of marriage and childbearing, as evidenced by increase in cohabitation and retreat from marriage.
    • Increase in non-marital births largely attributed to cohabitating couples.
  • Cohabitation used to be associated with marital instability, but more recent studies do not show this trend.
    • Explanations were selection (cohabitators had more positive attitudes towards divorce).
    • Experiences while cohabiting deterred people from marriage (trial marriage hypothesis).
    • White women who cohabited were more likely to divorce compared to Black and Mexican American women.
    • Cohabitation may stabilize remarriage.
    • Explanation for disappearing cohabitation may be the normative nature of it. Research from other countries supports this idea.

Fertility

  • Fertility rates of in US have largely declined since 1970s, with a slight increase between 1990s and 2007, after which they continued to fall – this is the case across racial/ethnic groups.
    • Decline since 2007 largely due to declines in birth among young women.
  • Teen births are at an all-time low.
  • US has higher TFR than most European countries, but still below replacement level fertility.
  • Great recession reduced fertility somewhat among younger women, but its unclear if this is a termporary or a longer-term effect.
  • Big decline in fertility for Hispanic immigrants, likely because the recession reduced immigration from Mexico.
  • Not clear why “Longer term structural changes, especially the decline of manufacturing and construction, also depress fertility; thus fertility rates may be unlikely to rebound soon (Seltzer, 2019).”
  • Educational differences in children are relatively small, but more educated women tend to have fewer children.
    • However, recent evidence suggests that fertility rates are increasing among women with advanced degrees.
      • May be explained by these women's ability to outsource childcare, availability of childcare has increased, and spouses participate more in childcare.\
      • Women college graduates also delay childbirth.
  • 40% of all births are to unmarried women – also, something like 30% - 50% of pregnancies are unintended
    • This tends to be more common for women with less than HS degree (also a group that is less likely to get married and more likely to get divorced.)
    • Non-marital births are increasing for white women, which has contributed to smaller differences in non-marital child birth differences between white/black women.
  • Multi-partner fertility has increased
    • 14%-25% in general — higher in unmarried families and cohabitating families.
    • Less associated with martial fertility in stepfamilies and non-marital fertility.
  • Covid led to a baby “bust.”
  • Same-gender unions
    • Somewhere between 500-800k married same gender couples.
  • Married and cohabitating same-gender couples tend to have higher SES.
    • More schooling
    • Both partners employed
    • Slightly more white
    • Higher median household income
  • Male same-gender couples tend to have higher SES (because of patriarchy).
  • Same-gender couples are less likely to have children (there are many barriers to having children if they aren’t from a previous relationship).
    • Higher rates of childrearing among female same-gender couples.
  • Education gradient in child rearing is more dramatic at higher than lower levels of education for same-gender couples (they have fewer children).
  • “Same-gender couples with children in the home tend to have less education, lower median incomes, and higher poverty rates than different-gender parents despite the fact that same-gender couples as a whole have higher incomes and education than different-gender couples.”
    • When controlling for relevant factors, children in female same-sex couple households have similar poverty rates as those in different gender households.
  • "Controlling for education, demographic, marital status, and health measures, lesbian women and gay men are not more likely to be poor than heterosexuals, but bisexual women and men are more likely to be poor (Badgett, 2018).”
    • We don’t really understand why bisexual people have poorer outcomes.
  • Adoption rates are higher among same-gender unions
    • Still lots of challenges in accessing pathways to parenthood and adoption.
  • Overall decline in parenthood among same-gender couples.
  • Greater dissolution among female same-gender couples.

Immigrant Families

  • “Since the Great Recession, legal immigration has leveled off, and the size of the undocumented immigrant population has remained relatively constant or declined somewhat, depending on the estimate.”
  • Foreign born population has stayed around 15% between 1890 and 2017

•Most immigrants are from the Americas, particularly, Mexico

•“As of 2017, the largest groups of immigrants are from South and East Asia (27%), Mexico (25%), other countries in Latin America (25%), and Europe or Canada (13%; Radford & Noe-Bustamante, 2019).”

•Marriage is common 61%, may be due to them being younger/typical age for marriage

•24% of Hispanic women will separate or get divorced – this rate has been increasing slowly

•There is quite a bit of heterogeneity in divorce rates based on specific ethnic groups (important point to acknowledge when talking about race/ethnicity that’s not covered here.)

•“immigration enforcement between 2005 and 2015 raised the likelihood that undocumented mothers lived without their spouses by 20% and the likelihood that Hispanic U.S.-born children lived without their parents by 19%”

•Separations are associated with increased poverty, fear, stress, anxiety, and depression

•Births to foreign-born women are a large proportion of all births

•In 2014, 23% of U.S.-born babies had foreign-born mothers. Most of these births were to women born in Latin America (54%), but the share of births to women born in Latin America has declined since 2010, while the share of births to Asian mothers has increased alongside migration from Asia

•These births are compensating for low birth rates from U.S. women and have been important for offsetting population decline in many areas.

Children’s Living Arrangements

•A lot of changes in childrens living arrangements since the 1970s

•12-27% of children lived in single-parent families This stat surprised me

•Most single parent households are female-headed

•“In 2016, the proportion of all children living in a two-biological parent married household stood at 60%. Roughly 3% lived in a two-parent biological cohabiting family, 9% in a stepparent family, 24% in a single-parent family, and the remainder in other arrangements.”

•Stepfamilies are a prominent feature of American families when we consider that many of these families have ties that contain adults rather than children

•Many children (about 8% ) live with grand parents.

•30% of children have co-resided with a grand parent at some point

•Most of these families were multi-generational households (parents and grand parents)

•Yet there were also 25% that were skipped generation households (these tend to be more economically vunerable)

•“economics and family structure play out in a way that disadvantages children with less-privileged backgrounds and underrepresented minorities.”

•“White and Asian families are more likely to be in family structures associated with low poverty rates.”

•Family instability has plateaued for education mothers, and generally family complexity had plateaued

•“Her work showed that studies on only family instability miss roughly 20% of children’s experiences of instability”

•Household instability is negatively associated with childrend educational attainment
