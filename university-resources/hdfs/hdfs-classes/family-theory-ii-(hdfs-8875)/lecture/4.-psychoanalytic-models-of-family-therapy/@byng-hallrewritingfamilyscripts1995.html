  • "We may feel secure enough to explore if we can predict that support will be readily available if and when it is needed, especially if new ventures run into trouble or if potentially dangerous situations arise. Difficulties that lead to consultations with a family therapist often arise out of conflict within the family's own relationships."
  • "The family script can provide routines for family life which can be followed almost automatically."
  • "Repetition becomes the background against which change is the foreground, that which is new becomes the focus of attention. In contrast, chaos obscures potentially significant change, unless the new behavior facilitates consistency, in which case that new patter becomes the foreground, thus becoming the new center of attention."
  • "Rewriting family scripts follows the sequence of a scripted pattern giving way to some improvisation followed by the establishment of a new pattern."
  • "Family scripts can be defined as the family's shared expectations of how family roles are to be performed within various contexts."
    • The term 'expectations' implies anticipation of what is to be said and done within family relationships, as well as family pressures to perform the roles as expected. If one member fails to perform in the prescribed way, another member of the family may be recruited to take up that role instead.
      • The plot is 'scripted' while the cast can change.
  • Family scripts are revealed when repeating patterns of family interaction are either observed or described.
  • Family Scenario:
    • An encounter of which it can be said that something significant happens within family relationships.
    • Basic elements of family scenario:
      • Context
        • Families engaging with each other around a particular task or situation
      • Plot
        • Family members 'motives' become clear as the scenario unfolds
      • Outcome
        • The consequences of the encounter are revealed
    • The expected scenarios for each particular family situation thread together to create an overall script for how all family relationships are expected to be conducted in general.
    • "Problem Scenarios":
      • Instead called 'Attempted Solution Scenarios'
        • This helps us to focus attention on how each person tries to solve the problem.
        • The behavior that is experienced now as problematic may have started out as an attempted solution to an earlier problem.
        • The co-constructed story may then become part of the family beliefs about what to expect of itself.

Improvising a New Solution to the Problem

  • Improvising implies abandoning the set script and extemporizing instead. This may happen when the old script does not fit the current situation, or when those involved are in sear of something different.
  • The therapist can help to create a new co-constructed scenario by suggesting a goal which the family then achieves in their own way.
  • Suggest a scenario in which it becomes necessary for the family to improvise. (in two parent families, the parents should devise a strategy together)
  • Try to block each move that has not worked in the past.
  • Unraveling some of the origins of the scripted scenarios frees families to explore new pathways. Work on the present and the past can reinforce each other.

Transgenerational Continuity and Discontinuity: Replicative, Corrective, and Improvised Scripts

  • Replicated Scripts
    • Parents with their own individual scenarios from childhood that are repeated
  • Corrective Scripts
    • Choice of the opposite style of parenting
  • Improvised scripts
    • Corrective scripts repeated over time

The Therapist's Family Script

  • The therapist's background also plays a part in shaping scenarios in therapy.

Positive Framing of Replicative, Corrective, and Improvised Scripts

  • Positive Labeling
    • Can be given to replicative scrips by labeling them as being loyal to the family of origin.
  • Corrective scripts can be given a positive frame by pointing out to parents that they are struggling to provide a better experience for their children than their parents were able to provide for them.
    • Improvised scripts (unlike the historical replicative and corrective scripts) are written in response to current situations. They are improvised either out of necessity or driven by curiosity and a wish to explore new ways of relating.
      • A stimulus for improvisation is a degree of uncertainty about what to do next so that interaction must be played off the cuff.
    • Positive comments can be made about how much the family is exploring unknown territory.

Attachments and Security

  • Attachment behavior
    • The behavior of a person seeking proximity to his or her attachment figure in a potentially dangerous situation. (This is a mechanism that protects children - or adults - when vulnerable)
    • The child uses the parent as a safe base from which to explore, and parent and child stay in contact to ensure that they can get together in the presence of any potential danger.
  • In terms of script theory a potentially threatening situation evokes tightly scripted behavior in which parent and child predictably got together. Exploration allows for more improvisation.
  • The therapist can become a temporary attachment figure to the family. The therapist must be aware of the factors that threaten the family's relationship.
  • Any new behavior of uncertain origin emerging in a relationship (e.g., unexplained impatience) might be seen as either strange and treated as potentially dangerous, or novel and will arouse curiosity.
    • If it is perceived as strange, attempts may be made to avoid reprovoking the impatience, returning to old familiar ways instead.
    • If the impatience arouses curiosity, then it will probably lead to trying to discover what it is all about, and to improvise ways of dealing with it.
    • If it becomes clear that the impatience poses a problem and something has to be done about it, new solutions may be improvised out of the necessity to sort things out.
  • There are two main forms of improvisation in family relationships:
    • Improvisation that comes out of necessity (Something has to be done when old solutions are not working)
    • Improvisation out of curiosity when it feels safe to try something new, even if it is uncertain where it will lead.
  • A therapist who is trusted by the family can make the risks involved in improvisation feel less frightening, even when family relationships are insecure.
