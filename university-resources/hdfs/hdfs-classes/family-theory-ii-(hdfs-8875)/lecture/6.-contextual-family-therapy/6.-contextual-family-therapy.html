1. Contextual Family Therapy

Ivan Boszormenyi-Nagy

How Families Operate/Develop

Four Dimensions

Facts

  • Biological and historical context variables (i.e., sex at birth, national origin, race/ethnicity, etc.)
  • Also, events that you had no control over (parents divorce, illnesses)
  • Anything that would impact how families function.

Individual Psychology

  • Cognitions, affect, perceptions, experience, individual coping mechanisms.
    • Could include attachment styles here or other psychodynamic constructs

Transactions (Systemic Processes)

  • Patterns of interaction between family members.
    • Concepts of family systems theory (Boundaries, Family Rules, Triangles, Differentiation of Self, etc.) could be included here.

Relational Ethics

  • This is the cornerstone construct for Nagy's work.
  • Trust
  • Loyalty (split, invisible)
  • Entitlement (destructive)
  • All of these influence justice and fairness in relationships and are transmitted through generations.

Added a 5th Dimension

  • Ontic: 'I-Thou' relationship, or dialogue

Symptoms

  • Revolving Slate, justice and fairness
    • All humans have worth and should be provided with 'due care.'
    • When we don't get the care we deserve we develop symptoms
    • We develop destructive entitlement which continues the cycle.

Intervention

  • Multidirected partiality
  • Acknowledgement/accountability/crediting
  • Exoneration
  • Therapist use of self: you need to believe/live the theory and examine your own entitlements.

Contextual Therapy Dimensions: Integrative and Simultaneous

  • Family Transaction & Power
  • Relational Fairness/Ethical Dimension
  • Ontic
  • Facts
  • Psychology

Indicators of RE Violations

  • Dimension II:
    • Strong emotional reactions (anger, shame)
    • Behaviors (control-seeking or chaotic/risky)
      • Nay point to a destructive entitlement. As humans, we are born with value and are entitled to do care. When we do not get that, we develop a destructive entitlement. We seek others to meet our needs. We treat our partner as an object rather than a person in a relationship.
  • Dimension III (Transactions):
    • Rigid or chaotic structures
    • Power struggles
    • High conflicts
    • Conflicts over 'nothing'

What to watch out for?

  • Violation of trust
  • Destructive entitlement
  • Parentification
    • Parent using the child to get the parents do care needs met.
  • Overgiving
    • Over functioning, one of the partners decides what is wrong and how to fix it. They decide what is wrong with the other person and how they need to fix it. Getting into someone else's lane.
  • Not making 'claims'
    • People not claiming self. People not holding themselves accountable.
  • Loyalty conflicts or its 'invisibility'

Goals of Therapy

  • Restoring trust and balance of fairness in relationships
  • Opening relational resources
  • Opening a dialogue about fairness
  • Direct address
  • Presenting options
  • Enhancing an individual's capacity to consider the needs of others and acknowledgement of their acts
  • Enhancing an individual's capacity to stand up for his/her own needs and to do self-validation through acts
  • Earned merit and due consideration = balance

Role and Stance of the Therapy

  • Not neutral
    • Multidirected Partiality
      • Not being neutral, being multidirectedly partial that you acknowledge each person's perspective and saying 'I get it. I understand why you may think that way.' You want the couples to learn how to validate someone else's perspective. Not agreeing with it, but just expanding the ability for a person to have compassion for others. Getting couples to ask for what
      • You have been with me long enough you should know. Opening up of relational issues.
      • People cannot be in a relationship and give nothing. Keep looking for their contribution. Want couples to validate each other and themselves.
      • As people acknowledge that other people make contributions (due consideration), we feel that we are getting the due consideration.
    • For couples therapy - considering
  • Transitional
  • Nonjudgmental
  • Facilitating - 'sowing seeds and opening doors' and giving moratorium
    • But - therapy still can be 'short-term'
  • Avoids becoming parentified
  • Therapists' own relational histories may resonate with those of their clients
    • 'Self-of-the-therapist' issues may prevent clinicians from keeping a balanced stance towards members of the family
    • Therapist does not need to be liked by their clients. Why is it so important that your clients like you? Should your clients get better for you to be a good therapist?

Clinical Areas of Application

  • Child maltreatment
  • Blended families
  • Adoption and foster care
  • Couple relationships
  • Anger issues
  • Depression
  • Self-esteem
  • Self-destrucitve behaviors and substance abuse

Techniques

  • Multirectional Partiality
  • Acknowledgement
  • Crediting
  • Anticipatory Listening
  • Direct Address of Fairness Issues
  • Suggestions
  • Summary/Interpretations
  • Making Accountable
  • Exoneration

Treatment Plan Phases

  • Beginning
    • Safety and trust
  • Middle
    • Dialogue and relational ethics
  • End
    • Integration/solidifying constructive entitlement

Early-Phase Treatment Goals

  • Establish safety and trust
    • Alliance building
    • Assess on 4 dimensions
      • Facts, individual psychology, family transactions, relational ethics
      • Acknowledge past - identify transgenerational revolving slates
      • Screen for risk factors and ethical/legal issues
    • Emphasize strengths rather than pathology

Mid-Phase Treatment Goals

  • Relational Ethics and Dialogue
    • Behavioral changes
      • Mutual listening and validation skills
      • Develop effective dialogue skills
    • Make explicit relational ethics, i.e., power imbalances, broken trust
    • Make explicit issues involving family-of-origin
      • Destructive entitlement
      • Split loyalties
    • Deparentification
    • Exoneration

End-Phase Treatment Goals

  • Integration:
    • Solidify constructive entitlement
      • Reinforce in calm environent
        • Balance give and take
        • Enhanced trust
      • Continue work with exoneration
      • Highlight new strengths and resources
      • plan for the future

Working with Couples

  • De-escalation and soothing
  • Acknowledgement of the other:
    • Of reality, points of view
    • Deeds
  • Good starting point: "Do you see any truth in her/his words"
    • Acknowledgement of the self (instead of 'why')
    • Claiming considerations
    • Repairing past unfairness
    • Exploring family of origin model of fairness
    • Creating a couple's new fairness:
      • Repair and negotiation

Some Questions from Dr. Hibbs' Try to See it my Way: Being Fair in Love and Marriage

  • How did your childhood experiences shape your model of what you owe and deserve and your beliefs about love and fairness?
  • Does love in your family permit you to question what's unfair? (p. 108)
  • How does your unfinished business play out in your expectations of yourself and your partner? (pg. 112)
  • What did your parents appreciate about you? (pg. 127)
  • What happened when you protested unfairness (pg. 133)

Parenting

  • Parental accountability and responsibility
  • Restoration of fairness between generations of adults (parents and grandparents) benefits children
  • Assessing and addressing:
    • Loyalty issues
    • Fairness
    • Parentification
  • Checking and restoring age-appropriate balance of 'give and take'
  • It is desireable to restore relationships with parents even who abused their children because it is a way to self-freedom

Family-of-Origin/Genogram Questions - 'Self-of-Therapist' checking

  • Who was there for you? When you needed someone, who did you turn to?
  • Who did your parent (s) turn to during times of need?
  • Who did your parent (s) confide in most?
  • Whom were you able to rely on in times of need?
  • Whom were your parents able to rely on in times of need?
  • Whom could you trust in your family to see your best interests?
  • Whom could your parents trust in the family/their parents to seek their best interests?
  • Have you ever felt betrayed by any of your family members?
  • Have you heard from your parents or other family members that they felt betrayed or unsupported by the family?
