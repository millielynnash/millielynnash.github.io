1. Structural Family Therapy

  • Salvador Minuchin (1921-2017)
    • Physician in the Israeli army
    • Trained in psychoanalysis in the US
    • Studied interpersonal psychiatry with Harry Stack Sullivan
    • Worked with delinquent boys at the Wiltwyck School - where foundation for family therapy was laid.
    • Director of the Philadelphia Child Guidance clinic
  • Why did Minuchin go into family therapy?
    • He sees families in terms of particular shapes, particular stages
    • He sees himself as an instrument, a challenger of patterns

Structural Family Therapy

  • Family is depicted as a system that is comprised of Subsystems
  • A family is functional to the degree that it nurtures its members to negotiate well the demands of the outside world.
  • Boundaries set limits distinguishing subsystems from one another in varying permeability (from diffuse to rigid).
  • Subsystems separated by a diffuse boundary are enmeshed, whereas subsystems separated by a rigid boundary are disengaged.

Structural Concepts

  • Family
  • Structure
    • Structure is expressed through transactional patterns such as:
      • Hierarchy
      • Power
      • Alliances
  • Subsystem (couple, parent, sibling)
  • Boundaries
  • Coalition
  • Patterns
  • Triangles
  • Loyalties

Structural Family Therapy Assumptions

  • The family could have developed a structure different from the dysfunctional one now being displayed.
  • The family processes adaptive resources in its relational repertoire that currently lie dormant.
  • Therapy attempts to activate what is already there, but latent.

Structural Family Therapy

  • Offers a framework that brings order and meaning to family transactions.
  • The consistent patterns of family behavior reveal family structure.
  • The emotional boundaries and coalitions that make up a family's structure are abstractions.
  • The concept of family structure enables therapists to intervene in a systematic and organized way.
  • Families seek help for a specific problem.
  • Family therapists look beyond the specifics of those problems to the family's attempts to solve them.
  • Exploring dynamics of interaction.
  • Recognizing the overall organization of the family regulates those interactions.
  • The model attends to subsystems and boundaries that regular contact between members.
  • The model emphasizes enactments wherein family members are encouraged to deal directly with one another in session, permitting the therapist to observe and modify their interactions.
  • Structural family therapy provides therapists with a blueprint for how to view patterns of family dynamics.
    • The three major concepts of structural family therapy are Structure, Boundaries, and Subsystems.
      • Subsystem (Couple; Parent; Sibling)

Theoretical Formulations

  • Problems are symptomatic of the family's inability to be flexible and accommodating of changing needs.
  • All family behavior, even destructive behavior, has the goal of balancing the system to ensure its survival.
  • Family problems do not result from stresses in the system but from the effects of reactions and responses to the stresses.

Family Structure

  • Structure is:
    • The way a family is organized into subsystems whose interactions are regulated by interpersonal boundaries.
    • The organized pattern in which families interact
  • Patterns are formed based on rules that are implicit or explicit.
  • Over time, as patterns develop, roles for family members are established.
  • Patterns are isomorphic (i.e., interaction patterns are repeated in different relationships in a system).
  • The focus of treatment is on changing the family structure rather than changing individual family members.
  • The process of family interaction is like the patterns of conversation at the dinner table. The structure of the family is where family members sit in relation to one another.
  • As family transactions are repeated, they foster expectations that establish enduring patterns.
  • Family structure is reinforced by the expectations that establish rules in a family.

Subsystem

  • Families are differentiated into subsystems based on generation, gender, or function.
  • Adults are the 'organizational leaders' of the family system so whatever is present in the adult subsystem relationship will be felt throughout the whole system.
  • Children are common 'symptom bearers'
  • Organization is most healthy when there are 3 clearly defined systems:
    • Marital/Couple
    • Parental
    • Child/Sibling

Boundaries

  • Each subsystem is demarcated by interpersonal boundaries.
  • Boundaries range from rigid to diffuse.
  • Defined as rules for managing physical and psychological distance between family members and for defining regulation of closeness, hierarchy, and family roles.
Rigid Boundaries (Disengaged Subsystem)
  • Rigid boundaries are restrictive and permit little contact with outside subsystems, resulting in disengagement.
  • Disengaged systems are independent, but isolated.
  • On the plus side, this fosters autonomy.
  • But limits affection and support.
Diffuse Boundaries (Enmeshed Subsystem)
  • Diffuse boundaries result in enmeshment.
  • Enmeshed systems offer closeness, but at the expense of independence.
  • Too much closeness can cripple initiative.

Hierarchy

  • Determines who (or which subsystem) has power in the system.
  • Any structure in a family is not static, it is developed over time.
  • Hierarchies can be rigid and unfair or weak and ineffective.
  • Be aware of cross-generational coalitions.

Family Development

  • Accommodation requires the family system to be flexible and alert to changing needs of individuals in the family.
  • Boundary making refers to the demarcation of subsystems as they are formed.

Family Dynamics

  • In accommodating to each other, a couple must establish a boundary between them, as well as a boundary separating them from the outside.
  • The addition of children transform the structure of a family into a parental subsystem and child subsystem.
  • Even in healthy families, children often bring stress and conflict. Children require different styles of parenting at different ages.
  • Minuchin warned therapists not to mistake growing pains for pathology.
  • What distinguishes healthy families isn't the absence of problems, but a functional structure for dealing with them.

Development of Problems

"The problem behavior is a symptom of the dysfunctional structure of the family system."

  1. Disengaged or enmeshed subsystems
  2. Hierarchies are weak and ineffective; or rigid & arbitrary
  3. Conflict avoidance
    • Detouring which results in a scapegoat
    • Cross-generational coalition (where hierarchy may be inverted)
  • Modifications in structure are required when a family or one of its members encounters external pressure (a parent is laid off) or a developmental transition occurs (e.g., parents retire).
  • Healthy families accommodate to their changed circumstances, dysfunctional families increase the rigidity of structures that no longer work (e.g., strict parenting tightening up further when a child acts out and needs nurturance).
  • Families are susceptible to develop a dysfunctional structure during periods of:
    • Normative life cycle transition
      • The arrival of a first child
      • An adult child leaving home
    • An acute stressor
      • Natural Disaster
      • Unemployment

Family Life Cycle

  • Single Adult
  • Coupling
  • Having Children
  • Families with Young Children
  • Families with School-Age Children
  • Families with Adolescents
  • Divorce
  • Blended Families
  • Launching Children
  • Later Life

Mechanisms of Change

  • Early on the model shifted from why family members interact the way they do, and the past, to how they learned to act that way.
  • Now, the model considers not only how family members interact, but also how and why they learned to do so.
  • All behavior makes sense in context.
  • The mechanism of change is the production of new relational experiences for families.
  • Families change each other by behaving differently toward each other.
  • Enactment refers to those moments in therapy when family members interact directly with each other.
  • Enactment is not necessarily a technique. It typically occurs naturally in session. It is the 'here-and-now' of the therapy session.

Goals of Therapy

  • Alter the organization of a family so that its members can solve their own problems.
  • The goal is structural change; problem-solving is a by-product.
  • Problems are embedded in dysfunctional structures.
  • Activate adaptive patterns that are already in families repertoires.
  • Create effective hierarchy:
    • Parents should be in charge, not relate to their children as equals.
    • Differentiate individuals and subsystems by strengthening the boundaries around them.
    • Increase contact by making boundaries more permeable.

Facilitating Change

  • The therapist produces change by joining the family, probing for areas of flexibility, and then activating formant structural alternatives.
  • Joining conveys acceptance of family members and respect for their ways of doing things.
  • Only after joining can the therapist attempt restructuring.
  • Understand the family's view of their problems by tracking their perspectives (words they use to explain problems and the behavior with which they demonstrate them).
  • Enactment s reveal structural patterns and later change them.
  • Take-Away:
    • Observe and modify the structure of family transactions in the immediate context of the session.

Structure of Therapy

  • The therapist manages the nuts-and-bolts details of how the therapy process is structured including:
    • Who should attend
    • How often
    • Referrals for psychiatric evaluation
  • Therapy aims to change how family members experience each other.
  • Family members are expected to be present together for sessions.
  • Relational experiences can 'wash out' between sessions, therefore, therapy usually starts out weekly.

3 Phases of Treatment

  1. Join the family accommodate to its members' style (building alliance)
  2. Map the family structure, boundaries, and hierarchy (evaluating and assessing structure)
  3. Intervene to transform the structure to diminish symptoms (addressing the problems identified in the assessment)

Assessment

  • Observe and assess how family members respond to their dilemmas.
  • Because problems are a function of the entire family structure, it's important to include the whole group for assessment.
  • Some problems may be treated individually.
  • Problems can persist in individuals, in the social context, or the feedback between them.
  • Individual problems need to be identified and appropriate.
  • Structural therapists prefer to begin therapy with the entire family, but they do not insist on it.
  • Once the family has been assessed, the therapist often meets with specific subsystems and individuals to achieve structured goals.
Structural Assessment

  • Subsystems

4 Types of Assessment
  1. Ask questions about the presenting problem until family members begin to see that the problem goes beyond the symptom bearer to include the entire family.
  2. Assist family members in seeing how their interactions may be perpetuating the presenting problem.
  3. Briefly explore the past, focusing on how the adults in the family came to develop the perspectives that influence their interactions.
  4. Explore options that family members might take to interact in more productive ways to create a shift in the family structure and help resolve the presenting problem.

Techniques

Joining and Accommodating
  • Greet the family
  • Ask for each person's view of the problem
  • Listen carefully
  • Acknowledge what you hear
  • Model empathy that family members are temporarily not able to provide
  • Acknowledgement (feeling heard and understood) releases family members from resentment
  • Joining opens the way for family members to begin listening to one another and establishes a bond with the therapist that enables them to accept the challenges to come.
  • Intervention Language:
    • Joined with the Family
    • Engaged in Reflective Listening
    • Built Rapport
    • Acknowledged
    • Reflected
    • Summarized
    • Validated
    • Elicited
    • Emphasized
    • Challenged
    • Reinforced
  • Maintain an outsider perspective to remain objective, while becoming familiar with family's experiential reality.
  • Unless therapist is allowed to become part of the new system, no change will take place.
  • Respect the family, and its individual members, despite presenting problem (i.e., abuse)
  • The therapist joins the system in the sense that they accommodate to its style; how people talk, what words they use, how they walk, and so forth.
  • Make contact with each member of the family
    • Greeting, shaking hands (pre-COVID)
    • Using names
    • Noticing something unique about each of the members
    • Find something similar about each person and then allow the family members to recognize the similarity
Techniques in Joining and Accommodating
  • Skills involved in joining:
    1. Accommodating:
      • Matching the family's style of language with gestures or subject matter (being 'familiar' to the family)
      • Being interested in the family's interests (i.e., baseball).
    2. Validating:
      • Validate the worth of each family member.
    3. Searching for Competencies:
      • Therapist enhances self-esteem by discovering and validating competencies.
Forming Hypotheses
  • During joining, assess the Family Structure:
    • Hierarchy
      • Who has the most power?
    • The position members take in relation to each other (emotional closeness between members).
    • Boundaries between Subsystem
Joining and Accommodating
  • Assume authority for the structure of therapy.
  • Maintain control so things do not go awry.
  • Lead by following.
    • Be able to interrupt.
    • Make demands, rather than polite, requests.
  • Inviting the family to schedule the next session.
  • Validating ambivalence about coming again.
Process
  • Need to expand family's view of problem to include all family members.
  • Therapist is less concerned with the content of the message than with how it is transmitted, to whom, and with what effect.
  • Process messages usually describe the power structure of the family, Hierarchy, Boundaries, emotional tone, and Family Rules.
  • Attending to process = accommodation when the therapist responds to the underlying affect in the manner in which the statement is made.
  • Process comments are better when kept general.
Enactment:
  1. The therapist notices a problematic sequence (a hunch).
  2. The therapist initiates an enactment (talk this over with your partner).
  3. The therapist guides the family to modify the enactment (offering encouragement as needed).
  • Enmeshment
    • Watch for interruptions, speaking for one another, doing things for one another that they can do for themselves, arguing.
  • Disengagement
    • Watch for passivity, absence of emotion, absence of conflict, ignoring, lack of concern for other's interests.
  • When the enactment breaks down:
    1. Comment on what went wrong.
    2. Push them to keep going.
Structural Mapping
  • Assess interactions in session.
  • Families tend to quickly induct therapists in their culture.
  • Take into account both the problem the family presents and the structural dynamics it displays.
Highlighting and Modifying Interactions
  • Once families begin to interact, problematic transactions emerge.
  • Recognizing structure requires focusing on process, not content.
  • Achieve intensity by selective regulation of affect, repetition, and duration. Tone, volume, pacing, and choice of words can be used to raise the affective intensity of interventions.
  • Pay attention to Coalitions
  • Temper tantrums are maintained by parents who give in. They try not to give in; they just don't try long enough.
  • Empathy:
    • Use empathy to go beneath defensiveness.
  • Interrupt squabbling and using empathy talk with each family member at a time about what they're feeling.
  • Shaping Competence:
    • A particular method of modifying interaction, reinforcing positives, reinforcing use of functional alternatives already in the family's repertoire.
Boundary Making
  • Invite family members to speak for themselves.
  • Block interruptions.
  • Assist dyads in finishing conversations without intrusion.
  • Although structural family therapy begins with the whole family, subsequent sessions may be held with individuals or subgroups to strengthen boundaries.
  • Asking family members to change seats.
  • Asking family members to move seats further apart or closer together or turn toward one another.
  • Having separate sessions with individuals or subsystems to strengthen boundaries.
  • Asking one or more members to remain silent during an interaction.
  • Asking questions that highlight a problem boundary area (e.g., do you always answer for your son when he is asked a question?)
  • Blocking interruptions or encouraging pauses to enable less dominant persons to speak.
Unbalancing
  • The goal in unbalancing is to change the relationship within the subsystem.
  • The therapist joins and supports one individual or subsystem.
  • While taking sides seems like a violation of neutrality, this is done to unbalance and realign the system (note that fairness is achieved because the therapist sides with family members in turn).
  • Tools/Techniques:
    • Reframing
    • Complementarity
    • Intensity
    • Enactment
Challenging Unproductive Assumptions
  • The therapist sometimes challenges the way clients see things.
  • Act as a teacher, offering information and advice, often about structural matters.
  • Stroke and a Kick:
    • To mother, "You are very helpful." (Stroke)
    • To daughter, "Mommy sometimes takes away your voice. Can you speak for yourself?" (Kick)
Reframing
  • After the family's view of the problem has been enlarged by process comments and family is familiar with therapeutic process.
  • Therapist can present the family with a different perspective of the problem that allows them to activate new behaviors (in a new situation)
  • To formulate a reframe the therapist brings together the verbal and nonverbal messages.
  • Should take into account every member.
  • Should like the family problem with the rules of the structure.
  • Should not be explained to the family.
  • As family members describe their problems, therapists reflect on their understanding systemic reframing, which takes into account all behavior as reciprocal.
  • Use family's language.
  • Use family's content.
  • Include everyone.
  • Ensure the reframe has a nonjudgemental, non-blaming focus, and better still, has positive connotations.
  • Be prepared to restate the reframe in several different ways.
  • Avoid intrapsychic interpretations and why-because aspects in forming the reframe.
  • Realize that there is no perfect reframe.
Family Mapping
  • Structural mapping is used as an assessment tool for understanding interaction patterns.

Intervening Other Techniques

  • Blocking:
    • Stopping usual transactions.
  • Exaggerating:
    • Encouraging or sustaining interactions.
  • Silence:
    • When a response is expected, doing the unusual by not giving one - thus demanding the family to act.
  • All unbalancing, if successful, will heighten tension and intensity to fuel family to move toward change.
  • Actively shift the focus of the family from solving the presenting problem to changing the family structure. The therapist must unbalance the family's old patterns.

Summary

  • The model directs therapists to look beyond the content of problems and even beyond the dynamics of interaction to the underlying family organization that supports and contains those interactions.
  • The most important tenet in that every family has a structure, and this structure is revealed only when the family is in action.

Evaluating Therapy Theory and Results

  • Strongest empirical support comes from a series of studies on children with psychosomatic symptoms and young adults addicted to drugs.
  • Early structural family therapy studies demonstrated effectiveness in treating disruptive behavior and adolescent substance use.
  • More recent, studies have shown that structural family therapy can be effective when addressing problem behavior spanning the externalizing spectrum (e.g., disruptive behavior, adolescent substance use, conduct disorder, ADHD)

Course of Treatment

  • Treatment is concerned only with the relief of the family's suffering.
  • Interventions are limited to relief of suffering, thus treatment may be brief (<6 sessions)

Role of the Therapist

  • Therapist must be more flexible than the family.
  • Focused on change not insight (how not why of dysfunction).
  • Therapist should be in charge of all communications and coordination with collaborators.

Self of the Therapist

  • The self of the therapist is one of the primary tools for facilitating change.
  • Therapists must use themselves to relate to the family, varying from being highly involved to professionally detached (depending on the family's needs).
  • Salvador Minuchin encouraged new therapists to reflect on aspects of their personal and relational lives that shape their therapeutic process and client behavior in session.

Self of the Therapist Activity

  • Describe 'who you are' as a therapist.
  • How will 'who you are' as a therapist impact therapy with families?
  • What are your personal strengths? What are your growth areas?

Evaluating Therapy Theory and Results

  • Strongest empirical support comes from a series of studies on children with psychosomatic symptoms and young adults addicted to drugs.
  • Early structural family therapy studies demonstrated effectiveness in treating disruptive behavior and adolescent substance use.
  • More recently, studies have shown that structural family therapy can be effective when addressing problem behavior spanning the externalizing spectrum (e.g., disruptive behavior, adolescent substance use, conduct disorder, ADHD).

Empirical Support

  • Similar to all family therapy approaches, structural family therapy is not effective in all cases.
  • No research identifies readily observable characteristics shared by failed cases of structural family therapy.
  • Developed to work with poor urban families, at all socioeconomic levels, ethnic groups, sexual orientations, and across countries.
  • Indirect empirical support, as research on efficacy has focused on applicability to family treatment.
  • Studies that directly test structural family therapy's efficacy in the treatment of various presenting problems and targeted populations are needed.

The Gap between Research and Practice

  • Efficacy 'refers to treatment effects observed in randomized controlled trials.'
  • Effectiveness 'refers to treatment effects in routine practice.'
  • Currently, the evidence does not show that any of the demonstrably efficacious approaches to family therapy are more effective than other approaches, largely because there is a dearth of evidence on the efficacy of many widely used family therapy approaches.

Research and Practice: Can We Talk?

  • Family therapy research has had little impact on the day-to-day practice of family therapists.
  • The disconnection between research and practice is alarming.
  • This may be attributed to, 'An overemphasis by researchers.. On treatment packages and therapeutic models/techniques.'
  • Insufficient attention has been paid to sources of influence on treatment outcomes (the skills of the the therapist themselves)
  • Effective family therapy requires a good 'fit' between the person of the therapist and their primary theoretical orientation.
  • Family therapy is shifting to research that addresses the process aspects of the therapeutic models.

Empirical Support

  • It is important to remember there are multiple ways of knowing what works:
    • Randomized control trials
    • Case studies data
    • Observations of therapists and clients
      • Collecting qualitative or quantitative
  • Remember:
    1. Research lags behind practice in family therapy.
    2. Just because a model has been tested doesn't inherently mean it is superior.

Critiques

  • Heteronormative
  • Pathologizing (seeing problems are embedded in dysfunctional family structures)
  • Family mapping implies pathology resides in family structure, but this is not true. Problems are viewed as a failure to adjust to changing circumstances.
