2. Strategic Family Therapy

Gregory Bateson

  • Strategic grew out of Gregory Bateson's project on Schizophrenia at the Mental Research Institute (MRI).
  • The initial emphasis was on observation and research on families, but due to the influence of Milton Erikson's work, intervention strategies were also formulated.

Milton Erikson

  • Believed in using the resources of his clients and designing a 'strategy for each specific problem'
  • He did not care about perspective (insight) as long as their actions produced beneficial results.
  • Conducted therapy by paying extreme attention to details of the symptoms his clients presented.
  • In therapy:
    • Accepted and emphasized positive
    • Used indirect and ambiguously worded directives
    • Directed routine behaviors so that the resistance is shown through change and not through normal continuous actions.

Mental Research Institute

  • The MRI Pioneers:
    • Don Jackson
    • Gregory Bateson (As a consultant)
    • Virginia Satir

Strategic

  • Strategic Therapy is any type of therapy where the therapist initiates what happens during therapy and designs a particular approach for each problem.
  • Strategic therapy isn't a particular approach or theory, but a name for the types of therapy where the therapist takes responsibility for directly influencing people.

Introduction

  • Families often perpetuate problems by their own actions.
  • Directives tailored to the needs of a particular family can sometimes bring about sudden and decisive change.
  • The exchange of multilayered messages between family members define their relationships.
  • Interrupting vicious cycles that occur when attempts to solve problems only make them worse.
  • People are always communicating.
  • All behavior is communicative.
  • One cannot not communicate.
  • All messages have report and command functions
    • Report = Content = information = (What is said)
    • Command = Process = underlying relationship = How it is said
  • If no one registers a complaint, there is no problem.
  • Patterns (e.g., quiet detachment, volatile engagement) may be dysfunctional for some families and adaptive for others.
  • The approach is non-normative and complaint-based.

Strategic Family Therapy

  • Focuses on identifying and interrupting 'ironic processes' which occur when repeated attempts to solve a problem keep the problem going or make it worse.
  • The aim of therapy is simply to resolve the presenting complaint as quickly and efficiently as possible, so clients can get on with life
  • BSCT makes no attempt to specify what constitutes a functional or dysfunctional family relationship.
  • The therapist attends to what clients do, as well as how, they view the problem, themselves, and each other.
  • Often see family members individually in the context of family therapy, even when the focus of the intervention is a relational complaint.
  • The model is called 'Strategic' because the therapist intervenes to interrupt ironic processes deliberately, on the basis of a case-specific plan that sometimes includes counter-intuitive suggestions
    • Go slow
    • Engage in behavior they want to eliminate
  • What matters is the extent to which interaction patterns based on attempted solutions keep a complaint going or make it worse.
  • The problems clients bring to therapy persist only if they are maintained by ongoing current behavior of the family.
  • If problem-maintaining behavior is appropriately changed or eliminated, the problem will be resolved or vanish, regardless of its nature, origin, or duration.
  • How a problem persists is much more relevant to therapy than how the problem originated.
  • The persistence of the problem depends mainly on social interaction.
  • The behavior of one member of the family is stimulated and shaped by the response of the others.
  • How a family goes about trying to solve a problem usually plays a crucial role in perpetuating it.
  • Well-intentioned yet ironic solutions break the vicious cycle (Positive Feedback loops) that maintain an impasse.

Major Assumptions and Treatment

  • Families are stuck in a spiraling Positive Feedback loop.
  • They make common-sense but ineffective attempts to change responses.
  • For example:
    • When a younger sibling arrives, the older sibling, feeling unloved, starts screaming for attention. The parent punishes him which reinforces his feelings of being unloved, and he continues to throw tantrums.

Concepts

  • Family Rules:
    • The rules or regularities of family interaction that operate to preserve family stability.
  • Homeostasis:
    • The conservative aspect of family systems (similar to Cybernetics concept of Negative Feedback)
  • Circular Causality
  • Feedback Loops
  • First-Order Change
  • Second-Order Change
  • Hierarchical Structure
  • Prescribing Ordeals

Rules

  • Rules are typically unspoken, and govern most behavior
    • When tules promote rigid attempted solutions, it isn't just the behavior but the rule needs to change.
      • When a specific behavior within a system changes (First-Order Change)
      • When the rules of the system change (Second-Order Change)
        • Rules are changed through reframes.

Positive Feedback Loop

  • Centerpiece of the model
  • Families encounter many difficulties over the course of their lives, whether a difficulty becomes a problem depends on how fmaily members respond to it.
  • Families often make misguided attempts to solve their difficulties and, on finding that the problem persists, apply more of the same attempted solutions. This produces an escalation of the problem, which provokes more of the same, in a vicious cycle.
  • What often needs to happen? Reverse the solution!

Techniques

  • Paradoxical Injunction:
    • A technique used in Strategic Family Therapy whereby the therapist directs family members to continue their symptomatic behavior. If they conform, they admit control and expose secondary gain; if they rebel, they give up their symptoms. (Research Citations/Books/@nicholsFamilyTherapyConcepts2019)
    • Not to point out that an individual is fighting going under, but instead tell the individual to keep their eyes open until they become unbearably heavy.
  • Reframing:
    • Changing the interpretation.

The MRI Approach

  1. Identify Feedback Loop s that maintain problems.
  2. Determine the rules that support those interactions.
  3. Find a way to change the rules in order to interrupt problem-maintaining behavior.

Family Functioning and Disorders

  • Do not regard any particular way of functioning, relating, or living as a problem if the client is not expressing content with it.
  • How problems develop:
    1. Cybernetic:
      • Difficulties turn into chronic problems by misguided solutions, forming Positive Feedback escalations.
    2. Structural:
      • Problems are the result of incongruous hierarchies.
    3. Functional:
      • Problems result when people try to protect or control one another covertly, so that their symptoms serve a function for the system.

Goals of Therapy

  • Once the presenting problem is resolved, therapy is concluded.
  • If a family didn't ask for help with other issues, they aren't targeted.
  • The job is simply to get the family moving again.
  • Help families define clear and reasonable goals so that everyone knows when treatment has been successful.
  • In pushing family members to set concrete goals, they are forced to clarify vague dissatisfactions.
  • The primary goal is behavior change.

Conditions for Change

  • Alter specific patterns of communication that maintain problems.
  • Mental Research Institute (MRI):
    • The way to resolve problems is to reverse the misguided solutions that have been maintaining them.
  • Jay Haley:
    • Changes in behavior alter perceptions (rather than the other way around)
  • Milan:
    • Get families to see things differently (through reframing or positive connotation), rather than in getting family members to behave differently.

Assessment

  1. Define a resolvable complaint.
    • Obtain a very specific behavioral picture of the complaint.
      • Who sees it as a problem?
      • Why is it a problem now?
    • "What is the problem that brings you here today?" is too vague.
    • Inquire about exactly what these complaints mean.
  2. Identify attempted solutions that maintain the complaint.
    • Once the problem had been defined, the therapist tries to determine who has tried to solve it and how.
    • The solution is part of the problem.
    • Prescribe some alternative behavior!
    • The key is to stop the performance of the problem-maintaining solution.
    • MRI Approach:
      1. Introduction to the treatment setup
      2. Inquiry and definition of the problem
      3. Estimation of the behavior maintaining the problem
      4. Setting goals for treatment
      5. Selecting and making behavioral interventions
      6. Termination
    • Attempted solutions:
      • The solution is deny that a problem exists; action is necessary but not taken. For instance, parents do nothing necessary despite the growing evidence that their teenage son is using drugs.
      • The solution is an effort to solve something that isn't really a problem; action is taken when it shouldn't be. For example, parents punish a child for masturbating.
      • The solution is an effort to solve a problem within a framework that makes a solution impossible; action is taken but at the wrong level. For instance, a husband buys gifts for his wife when what she wants is affection.
    • Solutions that perpetuate problems fall into three categories:
      1. When clients deny that a problem exists (example: anorexia) ACT
      2. When clients try to solve something that really isn't a problem (example: punishing a child for masturbating) STOP ACTING
      3. When clients try to solve a problem within a framework that make a solution impossible (example: husband buys gifts for his wife, rather than giving her the affection she desires) DIFFERENT ACTIONS
  3. Understand the clients' unique language for describing the problem.

Therapy

  • To interrupt problem-maintaining sequences, therapists may try to do something that runs counter to common sense.
  • Paradoxical interventions:
    • The therapist directs the family members to continue their symptomatic behavior. If they conform, they admit control and expose secondary gain. If they rebel, they give up their symptom(s).
  • Most common symptom prescriptions where the family is told to continue or embellish the behavior they complain about.
  • Hope is that the family will comply and then be forced to reverse their attempted solution.
  • May also hope the client rebels against the therapist's advice.
  • Restraining technique:
    • Warning clients against changing to fast and relapse; reinforced the therapist's one-down position.

Prescribing the Symptom

  • The family is told to continue or embellish the behavior they complain about.
  • Sometimes the therapist prescribes the symptom while secretly hoping the clients will rebel against the directive.
  • As soon as a family member is overwhelmed, they are instructed to make a list of all the things that they might do to sabotage their life. Doing so can take the rebellion out of self-destructive behavior, thus making it less appealing and less impulsive.

Paradoxical Directive

  • Asking a couple to deliberately have an argument during the week in order to help the therapist understand how they get involved in such no-win encounters and how they are able to get each other to act unreasonably.
  • Discouraging families from trying to change too fast, warning them to go slow and worrying out loud about the possibility of relapse when improvements do occur.
  • Perhaps you should continue to wake Ricky up for school, after all, you don't want to push him to be too responsible too soon.
  • The main thing is to discover what families are doing to perpetuate the problem and then get them to do something different.

Hayley & Madanes

  • Co-founded the Family Therapy Institute of Washington D.C. (1974)
  • Hayley learned from:
    • Milton Erikson
    • Gregory Bateson
    • Salvador Minuchin
  • He adopted and modified Milton Erikson 's individual emphasis to work with families.
  • Concerned with the function a symptom served.
  • Examined rules and structure of a family (influenced by Salvador Minuchin).
  • Hayley was particularly concerned with power struggles, and often prescribed the symptom such that the cost of keeping it up outweighed the gains.
  • Madanes was concerned with incongruence hierarchies, where a child uses symptoms to change the behavior of the parent.
Dimensions of family life:
  • Family Life:
    • Over and covert rules families use to govern themselves
  • Family Homeostasis:
    • Tendency of the family to remain in its same pattern of functioning unless challenged to do otherwise.
  • Quid Pro Quo:
    • The responsiveness of family members to treating others in the way they are treated.
  • Redundancy Principle:
    • Fact that a family interacts within a limited range of repetitive behavioral sequences.
  • Punctuation:
    • Idea that people in a transaction believe what they say is caused by what others say.
  • Symmetrical Relationship s:
    • Relationships within a family are both among equals (symmetrical) and unequals (complementary).
  • Circular Causality:
    • Idea that one event does not 'cause' another but that events are interconnected and there are multiple factors behind a behavior.
Goals of Therapy
  • To resolve, remove, or ameliorate the problem the family agreed to work on.
  • Behavioral, concerned with the immediate problem, and anti-insight.
  • Structural re-organization of the family, like the structural therapies, but with sub-goals to deal with the presenting problem.
  • Process, not content-focused.
  • Emphasize short term treatment (10 sessions)
Ensuring Successful Outcomes
  1. Define a problem clearly
  2. Investigating all solutions that have previously been tried
  3. Defining a clear and concrete change to be achieved
  4. Formulating and implementing a strategy for change

Hayley & Madanes Continued

  • Therapists must be innovative
  • Therapy must be customizable to families (technique driven)
  • If families are going to change, alterations in ways their members act must precede new perceptions or feelings
  • Focus on interpersonal payoff of psychiatric symptoms
Therapy Process
  1. Social Stage:
    • Introduction and setup
    • Help everyone relax, greet each family member, make sure everyone is comfortable.
  2. Problem Stage:
    • Definition of the problem in clear behavioral terms
    • Ask each individual for their perspective, speak to the less involved parent/partner first to get their involvement, listen carefully to the way each family member describes the problem, make sure no one interrupts until each has a turn, make observations.
    • Emphasize the use of directives.
    • If therapy is to end well, it must begin proper.
  3. Interaction Stage:
    • Estimation of the behaviors maintaining the problem
    • Encourage family members to discuss their points of view among themselves, observe rather than just hearing about the interchanges that surround the problem, look for coalitions between family members against others, how functional is the hierarchy and do family members work together as a team or undercut one another?
  4. Goal Setting Stage
    • Providing directives
    • Give the family a task, directives
Reframing
  • Offering a different perspective/framework to understand problem behavior.
  • "The use of language to give a new meaning to a situation."
    • For instance, a child's misbehavior can be reframed as his way of asking for attention, not an indication of disrespect.
  • Reframes can be offered by asking circular questions:
    • "Who makes you depressed?"
    • "What does dad do when you and mom are fighting?"
  • Changing the frame of reference for understanding behavior leads to change in the rules governing interactions which in turn leads to sustainable change.
Metaphor
  • Where a symptom is often seen as a metaphor for an underlying problem.
    • Example:
      • A school problem in a child may mirror a work problem in a parent.
  • Mandanes talked about how one relationship may metaphorically replicate another and power struggles in couples (money, education, control of children, etc.)
Other Techniques
  • Negotiation (pg. 90)
  • Quid Pro Quo (pg. 91)
  • Pretend techniques (pg. 91)
  • Ordeals (pg. 91)

Hayley and Madanes vs. Milan

  • Hayley believed that telling people what they're doing wrong only mobilizes resistance.
    • Changes in behavior alter perceptions.
  • Milan group was interested in getting families to see things differently through (reframe) positive connotation.
    • Which then gets family members to behave differently.
    • Shift from behavior to cognition set stage for constructivist/narrative movements.

The Milan Group

  • Families should have clear generational boundaries.
  • Therapists should be 'neutral' and not have expectations for families.
  • Therapy helps the family by simply examining the family, asking questions to help them examine their own family structure, and exposing games after which they can redesign their family into a better structure on their own.
  • Mara Selvini Palazzoli
  • Gianfranco Cecchin
The Milan Model
  • Presession:
    • Generate initial hypothesis of presenting problem.
  • Session:
    • Validate, modify, and change the hypothesis.
  • Intersession:
    • Team discussion of hypothesis and arrival at an intervention.
  • Intervention:
    • Delivery of the intervention to the family.
  • Postsession:
    • Team discussion to analyze the family's reactions and to plan for next session.
  • Highly scripted. Families treated by co-therapists and observed by other members of the team.
Primary Interventions
  • Positive Connotations
    • Must include the entire family system and confirm the behavior of all family members as maintaining the stability and cohesion of the group.
  • Rituals:
    • Engage families in a series of actions that ran counter to or exaggerated rigid family rules.

Structure of Strategic

  1. Define the complaint in specific behavioral terms.
  2. Set minimum goals for change.
  3. Investigate solutions to the complaints.
  4. Concentrate on the 'less of the same.'
  5. Understand clients' preferred views.
  6. Use these views to suggest 'less of the same'
  7. Nurture and solidify initial change.

Structure

  • Frequency of sessions intended to maximize the likelihood that change will be durable.
  • Typically patter of brief (e.g., weekly, tapering off)
  • Therapy ends when the treatment goals have been attained (and change seems reasonably stably?)
  • Co-therapists work as a team (e.g., observing behind the mirror, leaving the room to consult)
  • Late in session, the therapist may leave so the team can help the therapist plan a homework assignment or framing intervention.

The Role of the Therapist

  • The essential role of the therapist is to persuade the family to 'do less of the same' solution that keeps the complaint going.
  • Treatment is likely to go awry if the therapist is not in control of it.
  • Good strategic therapists rarely exert control directly in the sense of offering authoritative prescriptions or assuming the role of an expert.
  • Taking a 'one down position' and a stance of empathetic curiosity.
    • I'm a little slow on the uptake here, so could you help me understand again what it is you do when he raises his voice that way?
    • I don't know if doing this when he walks through the door will make much of a difference, but if you could try it once or twice this week, at least we'll have an idea what we're up against.
  • Emapthetic restraint (e.g., go slow) is used to neutralize apprehension and resistance to change.
  • When clients fail to follow a suggestion, a common response is for the therapist to take the blame (e.g., I think I suggested that prematurely) and seek alternative routes to the same strategic objective.
  • While the model does not emphasize the importance of the therapeutic relationship, this does not mean that strategic therapists come across as cold, manipulative, or uncaring. On the contrary, many receive high ratings on rapport and alliance.

Assessment

  1. Define a resolvable complaint.
  2. Identify solution patterns (problem-solution loops) that maintain the complaint.
  3. Understand clients' unique language and preferred views of the problem, themselves and each other.
  • The first two goals provide a template for where to intervene and the third goal is relevant to how to intervene.

Assessment and Treatment Planning

  • Gather a specific behavioral picture of the complaint
    • Who is doing what?
    • If I had a video of this, what would I see?
  • Assess who sees the complaint as a problem and why it is a problem now?
  • Determine a minimum change goal
    • What would you be doing, or the two/three/four of you be doing differently that will let you know this problem is taking a turn for the better.
  • Inquire into behaviors that are closely related to the problem:
    • What has the family is done to handle, prevent, or resolve the complaint?
    • What happens after these attempted solutions?
  • Develop a picture of what 'less of the same' will look like:
    • What behavior, by whom, in what situation, will suffice to reverse the problem-maintaining solution?

Assessment

  • The key element to stopping the performance of the attempted solution, so as not to perpetuate 'more of the same' problem-maintaining solution.
  • The final assessment goal is grasping the clients' unique views. This is crucial to the later task of framing suggestions:
    • How do they see themselves and want to be seen by others?
    • What do they hold near and dear?
    • When are they at their best, and what do others notice about those times?
    • What is their best guess about why a particular problem is happening - and why they handle it the way they do?
    • Whose idea was it to come? Why now?
    • Who is most optimistic that therapy will help?

Goal Setting

  • How will you know the situation is improving?
  • What kinds of change will you settle for?
  • What will need to happen (or not to happen) to let you know that, even if you are not out of the woods entirely, you're at least on the right path?
  • What will each of you settle for?

Techniques

  • Therapeutic restraint:
    • Telling clients to 'go slow'
    • Cautioning them about the dangers of improvement
    • Giving insturctions about how to make the problem worse
  • Go slow is used to prepare clients for change, to convey acceptance of reluctance to change, and to solidify change once it begins to occur.
  • Asking the family to collaborate in performing the problem patterns (e.g., an argument) deliberately.
  • Identify problem-solution feedback loops:
    • Demand-Refuse
    • Discuss-Acoid
    • Criticize-Defend
    • Accuse-Deny
  • Encourage the family to do less of the same:
    • When members felt the need to talk about their relationship, they would first go bowling.
  • Reframing:
    • For a small set of family complaints, the goal is to help families reevaluate their problem as 'no problem,' or as a problem they can live with.
  • Acknowledging (Reinforcing):
    • When a small change occurs, the therapist acknowledges and emphasizes the clients' part in making it happen, but avoids encouraging further change directly.
  • Predicting or Prescribing:
    • Exploring the dangers of movement.
  • Therapeutic restraint / going slow
  • Prescribing the symptom / performing the problem / predicting
  • Jamming / experiment
  • Reframing
  • Less of the same
  • Acknowledge / reinforce

Critiques

  • Critics sometimes regard strategic therapy as 'manipulative' because the therapist does not usually make the rationale for particular interventions explicit to clients.
  • The therapist may say things that they do not truly believe to achieve an effective framing.
  • Proponents counter that responsible therapy is inherently manipulative and that therapeutic candor can be disrespectful. Good therapy shows profound respect for clients' subjective experiences.
  • Critics suggest strategic therapists may ignore problems such as intimate-partner violence or substance abuse, if clients do not perceive or present them as overt complaints in session.
  • The model does not attempt at helping families with personal growth or solving underlying emotional issues.

Mechanism of Change

  • The central curative factor is interruption of 'ironic processes' which depends on:
    • Accurate identification of the particular solution efforts that maintain or exacerbate the problem.
    • Specifying what less of those same solution behaviors might look like.
    • Designing one intervention that will persuade at least one of the people involved to do less or the opposite of what they have been doing.

Empirical Support

  • Applicable to any family that presents with a clear complaint.
  • Particularly relevant for families that seem resistance to change.
  • Most qualitative research (case reports) and follow-up quantitative data.
Evaluating the Theory and Results
  • Historically, most of the research on the effectiveness of Strategic Therapy wasn't very rigorous.
  • Mostly case report format.
  • Anecdotal support for its efficacy, although people tend not to write about their failed cases.
  • Case reports with Anorexia Nervosa, Schizophrenia, and delinquency (pg. 96)
  • Brief Strategic Family Therapy for adolescent substance abuse and behavior problems
  • Treatment engagement, family functioning

Summary

  • Focuses on the process of communication rather than its content.
  • Clever, prescriptive, expedient
  • Critiqued as manipulative

Major Contributions

  • Therapist is responsible for the outcome of therapy.
  • Families are stuck in well-intentioned but ineffective attempts to solve problems.
  • Paradoxical Injunction:
    • To shake the status quo of the system
  • Circular questioning and team supervision from the Milan group.
  • Team approaches to therapy

Compare and Contrast

  • As a treatment for families, strategic differs from most other approaches in that the therapist is willing, and sometimes prefers, to see family members (particularly partners) individually.
