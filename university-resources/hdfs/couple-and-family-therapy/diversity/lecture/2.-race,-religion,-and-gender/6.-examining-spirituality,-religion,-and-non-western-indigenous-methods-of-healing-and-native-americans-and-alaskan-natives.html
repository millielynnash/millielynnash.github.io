1. Examining Spirituality, Religion, and Non-Western Indigenous Methods of Healing and Native Americans and Alaskan Natives

Location of Self

  • Requires us to acknowledge positions of privilege and marginalization in the room.

Religion and Spirituality

Talking about Religion and Spirituality in Therapy

  • Potential Barriers:
    • In general, therapists are less religious than their clients and the population at large.
    • There is a historic divide between science (psychology) and religion
    • In the United States there is a desire to maintain separation between church and state
    • Some therapists avoid the topic to avoid imposing their own beliefs
    • Some therapists lack confidence in how to address religion and spirituality ethically and effectively.

What is Religion?

  • An organized system of beliefs, rituals, practices, and community, oriented toward the sacred.
  • Tends to focus on formal organizations with specifically defined and widely accepted beliefs, practices, and traditions.

What is Spirituality?

  • Transcendent beliefs and practices lived out in daily life and relationships.
  • It can be experienced outside religious structure.
  • "A process through which people seek to discover, hold on to, and when necessary, transform whatever they hold sacred in their lives."

Spiritually Integrated Therapy

  • Spiritually integrated psychotherapy is based on findings in psychotherapy research and the psychology of religion.
  • It is the practice of purposefully assessing clients' spiritual/religious belief systems and worldviews in the context of psychotherapy, while integrating spiritually oriented interventions.

Three Domains of Language and Meaning

  • Clinical
  • Spiritual
  • Moral

Clinical Interview

Implicit Inquiry

  • Any inquiry about topics commonly related to spiritual themes (i.e., meaning making, coping, social support, and morality)
    1. Explore client's worldview and/or personal philosophies
    2. Listen for client's use of 'spiritually laden' words
    3. Explore client's current experience of congruence

Spiritual Consultation in Therapy

  1. Acknowledge client's spontaneous statements of spiritual beliefs'
  2. Inquire about their spiritual beliefs and practices
  3. Inquire about how they connect spiritual, clinical, and moral dimensions of life.
  4. Express agreement with the client's spiritual beliefs or sensibilities when such self-disclosure could be therapeutic.
  5. Articulate client's dilemma without giving your own position.
  6. Point out contradictions between the client's beliefs and clinical realities or moral issues.
  7. Challenge the client's way of handling spiritual beliefs based on your own spiritual, moral, or clinical beliefs.

Explicit Interview

  • Any method of direct inquiry about a client's spirituality or religion.

Spiritual Reflection

  • The purpose of spiritual reflection is twofold:
    • Understand ourselves in our work with clients
    • Understand clients from their own point-of-view
  • Increasing our understanding helps us fulfill the role of spiritually integrated therapists by not placing our beliefs and/or bias onto the client.
  • Questions to consider:
    1. How are these clients encountering a search for meaning?
    2. How do these clients and their presenting problem represent a theme from religious tradition or spiritual worldview (e.g., hope, reconciliation, pillars of faith)
    3. Regarding this case, what is an experience of the sacred/divine/spirit?

A Justice Informed Framework for Spiritual and Religious Interventions

Indigenous Healing

Understanding Indigeneity

  • 'Indigenous' describes any group of people native to a specific region. In other words, it refers to people who lived there before colonists or settlers arrived, defined new borders, and began to occupy the land.
  • The term 'indigeneity' has emerged to describe the state of being indigenous or related to Indigenous-ness.
  • There are over 476 million indigenous people living in 90 countries across the world, accounting for 6.2% of the global population: Of those, there are more than 5,000 distinct groups.

Healing Informed Care

  • Healing-informed care is care based on Indigenous wisdom about wellness and what is necessary to heal.
    1. Balance
    2. Relational
    3. Strength-based
  • Healing has been defined as 'transition toward meaning, wholeness, connectedness, and balance.'

Indigenous Ways of Knowing

  • Holistic:
    • Interconnectedness underlies all of human experience
  • Balance:
    • Because of the interdependencies of all life, achieving and maintaining balance is important both to the individual and the larger group.
  • Moral Code:
    • The physical and metaphysical worlds are linked to a moral code.
  • Respect:
    • Respect of all living things and each other is essential for balance.
