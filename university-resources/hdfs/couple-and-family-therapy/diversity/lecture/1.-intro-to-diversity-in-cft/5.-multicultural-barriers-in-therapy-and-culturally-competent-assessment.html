Multicultural Barriers in Therapy

  • Culture consists of all those things that people have learned to do, believe, value, and enjoy. It is the totality of the ideals, beliefs, skills, tools, customs, and institutions into which members of society are born (Ratts & Pedersen, 2014).

Cultural-Bound Values of Therapy

  • Focus on the individual
  • Verbal/emotional/behavioral expressiveness
  • Insight
  • Self-Disclosure (openness and intimacy)
  • Scientific empiricism
  • Distinction between mental and physical functioning
  • Patterns of communication

Culture in Family Therapy

  • ' Family Systems Theory may be equally as culture - bound as any other form of therapy, and this limitation may be manifested in marital or couple counseling, parent-child counseling, or work with more than one member of a family.'
  • Family Systems Theory suggests that healthy families:
    1. Allow and encourage the free and open expression of emotion.
    2. View each member as having a right to be their own unique self (individuate from the emotional field of the family).
    3. Strive for an equal division of labor among family members.
    4. Consider egalitarian role relationships between partners desirable.
    5. Hold the nuclear family as the standard.

High and Low Context Communication

  • High-Context (HC) Communication
    • Anchored in the physical context (situation) or internalized in the person.
    • HC communication relies heavily on non-verbals, and the group identification/understanding shared by those communicating.
  • Low-Context (LC) Communication
    • Place a greater reliance on the verbal part of the message.

Communication Styles

  • Characteristics of communication associated with race, gender, and other group identities often manifested in verbal and nonverbal communication language.
  • 30-40% of what is communicated conversationally is verbal.

Non-Verbal Communication

  • Proxemics
    • Refers to perception and the use of personal and interpersonal space.
      • Example:
        • Physical closeness or distance when communicating
  • Kinesics
    • The term used to refer to bodily movements
      • Example:
        • Facial expressions
  • Paralanguage
    • Used to refer to other vocal cues that individuals use to communicate
      • Example:
        • Volume of voice

Non-Verbal Communication and Bias

  • Non-verbal communication tends not to be under conscious control.
  • Non-verbal behavior provides clues to conscious deceptions or unconscious biases.
  • 'Therapists who have not adequately dealt with their own biases and racist attitudes may unwittingly communicate them to culturally diverse clients.'

Microaggressions

  • Microaggressions are the 'everyday slights, indignities, and insults committed against marginalized groups because of their membership in those groups.'
Handling Microaggression in Therapy
  • The Kim Constructive Conversations Model (KCCM) is a guide to having difficult conversations about important topics with family, friends, and co-workers.
  • The aim is to deepen engagement and mutual understanding in hopes of working and healing together.
  • Informed by evidenced-based theories and tools, the KCCM emphasizes the following:
    • Understanding barriers to constructive conversations
    • Self-awareness and regulation
    • Values as anchors
    • Personal skills development
    • Healing in relationship connection
  • The KCCM
    1. Goal
    2. Barriers
    3. Values
    4. Openers
    5. Take Action
    6. Listen
    7. Respond
    8. Repeat

Culturally Competent Assessment

Collaborative Conceptualization Process

  • Use both clinician skill and client perspective to understand the problem
  • Collaborate and jointly define the problem.
  • Jointly formulate a hypothesis regarding the cause of the problem.
  • Jointly develop ways to confirm or disconfirm the hypothesis on the problem, continuing to consider alternative hypotheses.
  • Test out the hypothesis using both the client and the therapist as evaluators.
  • If the conceptualization appears to be valid, develop a treatment plan.
  • If the hypothesis is not borne out, jointly collect additional data and formulate new, testable hypotheses.

Deliberate Practice

  • Deliberate practice is individualized training activities specially designed by a coach or teacher to improve specific aspects of an individual's performance through repetition and successive refinement.
  • Deliberate practice is an evidenced-based method of skill development.
  • Deliberate practice works best when training targets each student's personal skill thresholds or current ability. If a deliberate practice exercise is either too easy or too hard, the student is unlikely to benefit from it.
  • To maximize training productivity, performers follow a 'challenging but not overwhelming' training principle.
  • This means that deliberate practice requires ongoing assessment of the student's current skill and tailoring the difficulty of exercises to consistently target a 'good enough' challenge.
  • Skill Criteria for Attention to Diversity
    1. Convey a brief empathic response to a client/family member that indicates you hear their problem or the session content.
    2. Reflect on the problem/session content, looking for opportunities to deepen the conversation as it relates to their identity and unique experiences.
    3. Ask a question that conveys continued empathy and curiosity about their identity and unique experiences with the problem.
