I. Conceptual and Ethical Issues in the Relational Context

  • This paper is intended to help therapists recognize family secrets, to clarify the theoretical and relational contexts in which they are embedded, and to suggest guidelines and approaches for their therapeutic management. A related aim is to raise and address some of the ethical issues that are inseparable from a consideration of family secrets.

Terminology:

  • Secrets involve information that is either withheld or differentially shared between or among people.
  • Secrets tend, for the most part, not to be about feelings or thoughts, but about facts, real happenings or incidents.
    • Importance of what Boszormenyi-Nagy and his coworkers have described as the ethical-existential dimension of human life in close relationships.
      • This dimension concerns the facts of a person's life and their impact on him/her and those in relationship with him/her, as well as the actions of each person toward one another, especially as they affect mutual debts, entitlements, and trustworthiness.
  • The network of persons involved in the secret as the awareness context.
    • Refers to the network as a whole and includes those who are unaware of the secret as well as those who know it.
  • A fairly simple typology of secrets can be drawn from the kinds of boundaries created in the relational system. From this perspective, three major types of family secrets emerge: (a) individual secrets; (b) internal family secrets; and (c) shared family secrets.
    • Individual secrets involve those cases in which one person keeps a secret from the other person or persons in the family.
      • In both cases, one person's secret is shared with no one else in the family. The boundary formed by the secret is located between the person holding the secret and the other(s).
    • Internal family secrets involve those cases in which at least two people keep a secret from at least one other.
      • Internal family secrets create sub-groupings within the family.
    • Shared family secrets involve those cases in which all members of the family know the secret but are pledged to keep it from persons outside the family.
      • Shared family secrets strengthen the boundaries separating the family as a whole from the outside world.
  • The person or persons who know and keep the secret can be referred to as the secret-holder(s) and the person who does not know the secret as the unaware.
  • The subject, meaning the person that the secret is about. Subject and secret-holder may be one and the same person, or they may be different.
  • The confusion that surrounds secrets may make it difficult in some cases to make absolute distinctions between knowing and not knowing a secret.
  • For some secret-holders, there may be absolute clarity about the fact that there is a secret, but others may be quite surprised to recognize the secret as such.
  • There is a need for a way to identify secrets, even when the secret-holder is unaware of them as such.
  • The therapist should immediately consider the possible existence of secrets any time the word "know" needs to be used more than once in a sentence either by the patient or the therapist.

Secrets and Loyalty

  • It is nearly impossible to overemphasize the significance of loyalty dynamics in the creation, maintenance, and eventual facing of secrets in families. Often, the secret-holder feels, or may actually be, "sworn to silence." S/he owes it to the other person(s) to keep the secret. Disclosing the secret would be experienced as an act of betrayal and would arouse guilt over disloyalty. These cases concern dynamics of loyalty to the secret-holder(s). But the awareness context is further complicated by the fact that a secret-holder may also feel loyalty to the unaware.
