[!Abstract]
In couple’s therapy, secrets between the couple often arise, and couple’s therapists must navigate a balance between their ethical and therapeutic responsibilities. This paper provides a scoping review of the literature on how couple’s therapists handle secrets in the context of couple’s therapy and discusses the ethical and clinical considerations in the context of the ethical standards of the AAMFT. Online databases were used to systematically identify peer-reviewed academic, English-language articles that focused on recommendations for therapists in handling secrets between couples in therapy. Seven articles met the search criteria. The empirical evidence supports a model of professional judgment, whereby the therapist can use their discretion about whether or not to disclose a secret on behalf of a client. To operate within the confines of ethical practice, we recommend therapists pair professional judgment with a clearly informed and signed written client policy.

Annotations

  • Self-Concealment:

    • Defined secrets as 'life experiences, personal facts, thoughts, or feelings intentionally not disclosed.'
    • Examples include concealing personal negative or distressing information.

  • Disclosure:

    • Influenced by trust in the therapist and believing that self-disclosure would be beneficial.
    • Promotes healing and attachment, but final decision about disclosure should be left to offending spouse. Voluntary consent should be obtained at the outset of therapy to avoid jeopardizing trust and to remain an equal advocate for both partners.
    • Possible exceptions to disclosure:
      • Serious or terminal illness of injured spouse; imminent divorce; potential for violence; significant psychopathology in either partner; many years have passed, and issue is unrelated facilitated disclosure; it is ethical for therapists to collude in keeping secrets. Honesty is vital to recovery.
    • After disclosure most couples choose the challenges of healing over divorce. Disclosure of infidelity is best course of action to resolve potential conflicts and improve working alliance toward couple healing and renewal.
    • Through voluntary facilitated disclosure (based on the desires of aggrieved spouse) or referral, therapists should avoid double-bind of doing therapy in cases of non-disclosed secrets.
    • The choice to disclose is subjective - one person may readily disclose what another might choose to conceal.

  • Withholding Information:

    • Though there is some literature on withholding information by the client within the context of individual therapy, the literature on the disclosure of secrets to the therapist by one member of a couple is quite sparse.

  • Secrets in CFT:

    • Responsible handling of secrets between partners in couple's therapy is necessary to avoid ethical, legal, and therapeutic problems arising from the revelation, particularly due to the sensitive nature of secrets.
    • Therapeutically, a secret has the potential to undermine the development of the therapeutic alliance between the therapist and the secret-disclosing partner if the secret is shared with the other member of the couple. Non-disclosure may also undermine the maintenance of the therapeutic alliance between the therapist and the other partner, who may perceive the therapist as triangulating with the secret holder, if the secret is not shared but arises later in the course of therapy.
      • Additionally, there are potential ethical and legal problems that may emerge due to breaking the confidentiality of an individual through sharing a secret.

  • Family Systems Theory:

    • Based on the premise that the family or couple functions as a singular emotional unit.
    • Couple system in FST considered a discrete subsystem within a family. Secrets are viewed as indirectly influencing the couple system because of the consequences and symptoms of secrets are explicitly experienced, even if the underlying reasons for their occurrence are unknown to the naïve spouse.
    • Triangulation:
      Triangulation

      • Detouring conflict between two people by involving a third person, stabilizing the relationship between the original pair. (Research Citations/Books/@nicholsFamilyTherapyConcepts2019)

      • University Resources/Research Citations/M/@markScopingReviewPractice2020:

        • Occurs when third member is drawn into the dyad in an effort to either stabilize a conflicted system or manage the stress associated with the conflict.
        • May be helpful if therapist stabilizes the dyad, but can result in hard if the therapist, wittingly or unwittingly, colludes with one member of the couple.
        • But can result in harm if the therapist, wittingly or unwittingly, colludes with one member of the couple.

  • The conjoint process provides an opening for a couple to grow and learn from one another that is often lost in traditional individual approaches.

    • The therapist may not reveal any individual’s confidences to others in the client unit without the prior written permission of that individual.

  • Many therapists work within this framework by having a policy around secrets explicitly stated in their consent form signed by clients at the beginning of therapy.
