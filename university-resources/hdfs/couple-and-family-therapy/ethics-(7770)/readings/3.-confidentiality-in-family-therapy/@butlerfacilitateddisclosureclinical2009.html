Abstract

A critical and potentially polarizing decision in treating infidelity is whether facilitating partner disclosure or accommodating nondisclosure is most beneficial following private disclosure of infidelity to the therapist. Given couple distress and volatility following disclosure, understandably some therapists judge accommodating an infidelity secret both efficient and compassionate. Employing Western ethics and an attachment ⁄ intimacy lens, we consider ethical, pragmatic, and attachment intimacy implications of accommodating infidelity secrets. Issues bearing on the decision to facilitate disclosure or accommodate nondisclosure include (a) relationship ethics and pragmatics; (b) attachment and intimacy consequences; and (c) prospects for healing. We conclude that facilitating voluntary disclosure of infidelity, although difficult and demanding, represents the most ethical action with the best prospects for renewed and vital attachment intimacy.

    • Individual Rights in Pair-Bond Relationships:
      • Individuals Possess Inalienable Rights To:
        • Relationship Choice:
          • To choose the relationships in which they are willing to participate and to be fully informed about information that bears on that choice.
        • Relationship Definition:
          • To determine the ideals and goals for their relationships.
        • Relationship Circumscription:
          • To choose the relationship conditions that are acceptable to them, defining appropriate and inappropriate behavior within and outside the relationship, and establishing relational boundaries and stipulations for what each partner expects from the other.
    • "Using the theoretical perspective of attachment security and intimacy, we argue that accommodating a secret has strong potential to harm the relationship and that the potential for benefits is doubtful." (pg. 127)
    • The final approach, professional judgment, involves the therapist reserving the right to use their professional judgment about whether or not to disclose a secret on behalf of a client, and is perhaps the most commonly documented approach to navigating secrets in couple’s therapy.
  • The therapist’s response to one partner’s confidential revelation of an infidelity secret is critically important, as the subsequent unfolding of couple therapy is unavoidably and fundamentally shaped by this early decision. Within the couple relationship, choices related to an infidelity secret include:
    • Full disclosure unilaterally ventured by the offending spouse
    • Partial disclosure unilaterally determined by the offending spouse
    • Nondisclosure unilaterally determined by the offending spouse
    • Measured disclosure determined by the expressed desires of the non-offending spouse.
  • The response to an infidelity secret is therefore likely to be a critical factor in therapy, determining the type of relationship outcomes that can potentially be achieved.
  • Therapists’ preparation for and response to a confidentially disclosed infidelity secret represent a critical early turning point in the direction and course of couple treatment.
