Strategic Family Therapy

  • Problem-centered pragmatic strategies for altering problem-maintaining sequences of interaction. (Research Citations/Books/@nicholsFamilyTherapyConcepts2019)
  • Jay Haley
Lecture on Strategic Family Therapy

Background:

  • Strategic grew out of Gregory Bateson's project on schizophrenia at the Mental Research Institute.
  • The initial emphasis was on observation and research on families, but due to the influence of University Resources/HDFS Classes/Couple and Family Therapy/Therapy Theories and Modalities/Solution-Focused Therapy/Key Figures/Milton Erickson's work, intervention strategies were also formulated.

Milton Erikson:

  • Believed in using the resources of his clients and designing a 'strategy for each specific problem.'
  • He did not care about perspective (insight) as long as their actions produced beneficial results.
  • Conducted therapy by paying extreme attention to details of symptoms his clients presented.
  • IN therapy:
    • Accepted and emphasized positive
    • Used indirect and ambiguously worded directives.
    • Directed routine behaviors so that the resistance is shown through change and not through normal and continuous actions.
  • The MRI Model, Milan Model, and Haley and Madanes all overlap.

Overview:

  • The hallmarks of strategic therapy are:
    • Using a set of novel strategies to solve family problems.
    • Generating change rather than generating insight.
    • Circumventing resistance rather than dealing directly with it.

Communication Theory:

  • People are always communicating.
  • All behavior is communicative
  • One cannot not communicate.
  • All messages have report and command functions.
    • Report = CONTENT = Information = WHAT IS SAID
    • Command = PROCESS = Underlying Relationship = HOW IT IS SAID

Strategic Family Therapy:

  • Families often perpetuate problems by their own actions.
  • Directives tailored to the needs of a particular family can sometimes bring about sudden and decisive change.
  • The exchange of multilayered messages between family members define their relationships.
  • Interrupting vicious cycles that occur when attempts to solve problems only make them worse.
  • Focuses on identifying and interrupting 'ironic processes' which occur when repeated attempts to solve a problem keep the problem going or make it worse.
  • The aim of therapy is simply to resolve the presenting complaint as quickly and efficiently as possible, so clients can get on with life.
  • BSCT makes no attempt to specify what constitutes a functional or dysfunctional family relationship.
  • The therapist attends to what clients do, as well as, how they view the problem, themselves, and each other.
  • Often see family members individually in the context of family therapy, even when the focus of the intervention is a relational complaint.
  • If no one registers a complaint, there is no problem.
  • Patterns (e.g., quiet detachment, volatile engagement) may be dysfunctional for some families and adaptive for others.

Assumptions about Problems:

  1. What matters is the extent to which interaction patterns based on attempted solutions keep a complaint going or make it worse.
  2. The problems clients being to therapy persist only if they are maintained by ongoing current behavior of the family.
  3. How a problem persists is much more relevant to therapy than how the problem originated.
  4. The behavior of one member of the family is stimulated and shaped by the response of the others.
  5. Well-intentioned yet ironic solutions break the vicious cycle (positive feedback loops) that maintain an impasse.

Concepts:

Family Rules:

  • The rules or regularities of family interaction that operate to preserve family stability.

Homeostasis:

  • The conservation aspect of family systems (similar to cybernetic concept of negative feedback).

Circular Causality

Feedback Loops

First-Order Change:

  • When a specific behavior within a system changes.

Second-Order Change:

  • When the rules of a system change.

Hierarchical Structure

Prescribing Ordeals

Rules:

  • Rules are typically unspoken, and govern most behavior.
  • When rules promote rigid attempted solutions, it isn't just the behavior, but the rule needs to change.
    • When a specific behavior within a system changes = First-Order Change
    • When the rules of the system change = Second-Order Change

Goals for Treatment:

  • Families are stuck in a spiraling positive feedback loop.
  • They make common sense but ineffective attempts to change responses.
    • For example: When a younger sibling arrives, the older sibling, feeling unloved, starts screaming for attention. The parent punishes him which reinforces his feelings of being unloved, and he continues to throw tantrums.
  • The job is simply to get the family moving again.
  • Help families define clear and reasonable goals so that everyone knows when treatment has been successful.

Conditions for Change:

  • Alter specific patterns of communication that maintain problems.
  • MRI:
    • The way to resolve problems is to reverse the misguided solutions that have been maintaining them.
  • Haley:
    • Changes in behavior alter perceptions (rather than the other way around).
  • Milan:
    • Get families to see things differently (through reframing or positive connotation), rather than in getting family members to behave differently.

Assessment:

  • MRI Group:
    • Carefully defining the presenting problem and figuring out why it is perpetuated.
    • Non-historic and symptom focused.
  • Haley and Madanes:
    • Examine behavioral sequences around the problem.
    • Examine structural anomalies (weak parental hierarchies, cross-generational coalitions)
  • Milan Group:
    • Preliminary hypothesis tested using circular questioning (systemic explanations).

MRI Brief Therapy Center:

  • Find the most motivated person in the system.
  • Gain clear understanding of problem (symptom).
  • "What will be the first signs that things are getting better?"
  • Inquire about attempted solutions.
  • Observe and interrupt problem-maintaining sequences.
  • Use therapeutic paradox.
  • Family encourages depressed member to be sad so he feels less guilt for not being happy.

Attempted Solutions:

  • The solution is to deny that a problem exists; action is necessary but not taken.
  • The solution is an effort to solve something that isn't really a problem; action is taken when it should not be.
  • The solution is an effort to solve a problem within a framework that makes solution impossible; action is taken but at the wrong level.

Haley and Madanes:

  • Haley joins and interviews the entire family.
    • Social Stage -> Problem Stage -> Interaction Stage -> Goal-Setting Stage -> Task-Oriented Stage

The Social Stage:

  • Each family member should speak and introduce themselves.
  • Therapist should ensure problems are not discussed until everyone can speak.
  • Therapist should examine family seating arrangements to assess organization.
  • Keep conclusions tentative.

The Problem Stage:

  • Shift to the therapy stage.
  • Inquire why the family is there and what the problem is.
  • Be general and ambiguous so the family can expand.
  • Respect the family hierarchy to gain cooperation.
  • Treat those who have power and influence in the family with respect.
  • Therapist should not share observations with the family.
  • Therapist should think of the problems in terms of more than one person.

The Interaction Stage:

  • It is critical family members interact with one another rather than with the therapist.
  • Therapist should redirect family members toward each other.
  • Assess family organization through interactions.

Goal-Setting Stage:

  • Families need to define desired changes.
  • Therapist can act as wise advisor and assist the family in setting goals.

Task-Setting Stages (Directives):

  • Use of directives (interventions aimed at targeting and changing specific behaviors).

Milan Systemic:

  • Pre-Session, Session, Intersession, Intervention, Post-Session Discussion
  • Positive connotation (reframing without blaming)
  • Circular questioning
  • Team approach (Reflecting team, greek chorus)
  • Neutrality

Role of Therapist:

  • Responsible for guidance of treatment.
    • We are going to focus on what the problem is.
  • Be aware of timing and pacing.
    • Meet client where they are.
  • Take the time (to form treatment plans)
    • Respond to subtle pressure from clients.
    • Avoid attempting interventions prematurely.
      • Gather enough data about where the client is at before making attempts at intervention.
  • Use of Qualifying Language:
    • "I have a suggestion to make, but I am not sure how much it will accomplish. It will depend on your ability to use your imagination..."
  • Getting client to be specific.
  • Taking one-downmanship.
    • The inherent power difference in therapy
  • Determining the format of meeting.
    • Individual vs. Conjoint
    • In what situation will individual session be beneficial?

Managing Challenging Clients:

  • 'Window Shopper' Client:
    • Re-negotiate contract
    • Working with the complainant
    • Get them down to business
  • Restrictive Client:
    • Ask permission to redirect
    • Setting specific boundary

Interventions

Paradoxical Directive:

  • Asking a couple to deliberately have an argument during the week in order to help the therapist to understand how they get involved in such no-win encounters and how they are able to get each other to act unreasonably.
  • Discouraging families from trying to change too fast, warning them to go slow and worrying out loud about the possibility of relapse when improvements do occur.
    • Never tell client why we do this.

Prescribing the Symptom:

  • The family is told to continue or embellish the behavior they complain about.
  • Sometimes the therapist prescribes the symptom while secretly hoping the clients will rebel against the directive.
  • As soon as a family member is overwhelmed they are instructed to make a list of all of the things that they might do to sabotage their life. Doing so can take the rebellion out of self-destructive behavior, thus making it less appealing and less impulsive.

Reframing :

  • Offering a different perspective/framework to understand problem behavior.
  • "The use of language to give a new meaning to a situation." (Piercy et al., 1996)
  • Reframes can be offered by asking circular questions:
    • "Who makes you depressed?"
    • "What does dad do when you and mom are fighting?"
  • Changing the frame of reference for understanding behavior leads to change in the rules governing interactions which in turn leads to sustainable change.
