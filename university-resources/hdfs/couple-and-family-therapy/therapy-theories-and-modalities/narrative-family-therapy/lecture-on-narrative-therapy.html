Lecture on Narrative Therapy

Foundation of Narrative Therapy:

Modernism vs. Postmodernism:

  • Modernism vs. Postmodernism:
    • Postmodernism was a response in many ways to modernism and, hence, they can be considered to be two aspects of the same movement. But there are major differences between modernism and postmodernism.
    • Two central tenets of the Enlightenment (beginning of the 'modern' period) were as follows:
      • The perfectibility of human nature
      • The belief in human progress through the development of human knowledge, especially the sciences.
    • In the 20
    • th century, scientific findings, like quantum physics, seriously undermined the possibility of a deterministic model of the world. As a guiding principle, reason was still affirmed as was the belief in human progress.
      • The project of modernism was to examine the impediments still holding society back.
    • Individuals ceased to believe that art and literary works bore one unique meaning; rather, postmodernists believed in deriving their own meanings from works of art and literature.
    • Modernism:
      • It was an emancipatory project whose main idea was that traditional values and institutions were oppressive, divisive and inefficient and should, therefore, be replaced by rational values and institutions.
      • Modernism led to progress in many spheres of life by changing the approach of mankind towards culture.
        • Ultimately, modernism attempted to free humanity from its historical baggage through the use of philosophy and science.
    • Postmodernism:
      • Postmodernism was a reaction to modernism and was influenced by disenchantment brought on by the second world war.
      • Postmodernism refers to the state that lacks a central hierarchy and one that is complex, ambiguous, and diverse.
      • Postmodernists are the nihilists.
      • Postmodernists like Heidegger, Derrida, and Lyotard attacked the modernist belief in rationality, objectivity and universalism in favor of relativism (the view that there is no objective truth) as well as identity.
      • Grand narratives like freedom, societal progress, scientific progress were criticized by postmodernists, who instead emphasized that difference, rather than forced unity, should be celebrated.
      • Postmodernism is a backlash against modernity.
        • The collapse of tradition created a frightening situation whereby meaning became difficult to discern amid the horror of societal breakdown.
      • Postmodernism, hence, represented a loss in faith in human reason. Postmodernism provides a bleak prognosis of the human condition and offers no real solutions.
  • Conclusion:
    - Modernism and postmodernism were both movements that emerged from an analysis of events within the modern period from the perspective of the values of the Enlightenment.
    Postmodernism:

Social Constructionism:

  • Theory that knowledge of things are not real unless we give them meaning.
  • The sense of self is a social construct. Our identity is created by interactions with other people and our reactions to society.
    • Two threads:
      • Weak (Social Constructionism):
        • Proposes that social constructs are dependent on brute facts (most basic and fundamental facts and do not rely on other facts).
        • Institutional facts are created by social conventions and rely on other facts.
      • Strong (Social):
        • The whole of reality is dependent on social habits. All knowledge is a social construct and there are no brute facts.
        • It would say we created the idea of something and how to explain it.
  • Main criticism:
    • Does not take into consideration the effect of natural phenomenon that goes on in society.
  • Social constructionism only explains reality through the thoughts of humans, not by using fundamental brute facts.

Assumptions:

  • Knowledge is constructed.
  • Therapy is concerned with the ways people interpret their behavior rather than how they behave.
  • Experience is understood in interpretation and meaning making.
  • Experiences can have multiple interpretations.
  • The Narrative Metaphor:
    • Focuses on self-defeating cognitions - the stories people tell themselves about their problems.
  • \Problems affect people.
  • Problems operate on people rather than problems being things people are doing.
  • For clients to maintain their new narratives, they need supportive communities.
    • Hence letter writing, outsider witness, reflecting teams.
  • The truth of experience is not DISCOVERED, it is CREATED.
  • Narrative accounts affect people's perceptions, stories don't just mirror life, they shape it.
  • Instead of trying to solve problems, the focus is on enriching the narratives of people's lives - bringing forth 'thick descriptions' of those aspects of people's narratives that life outside the influence of problems.
  • Alternative stories allow people to live out new identities, new possibilities for relationships, and new future.
  • People find meaning in their lives by experiencing, telling, and circulating stories of un-stories aspects of their lives and relationships.
  • Dominant discourses are socially constructed within our culture.

Major Concepts:

Dominant Discourses:
  • We 'story' and create meaning of life events using available dominant discourses - broad social stories, practices, assumptions, and expectations about how we should live.
    'Problems':
  • Experienced when a person's personal life does not fit with these societal discourses and expectations.
University Resources/Couple and Family Therapy/Keywords/Externalization:
  • Process involves separating the person from the problem.

Contribution to MFT:

  • Understanding Oppression: Dominant vs. Local Discourses
    • Dominant discourses are culturally generated stories about how life should go.
    • Local discourses occur in our heads, our closer relationships, and marginalized communities.
  • Narrative therapists help clients become aware of how these different discourses are impacting their lives.

Normal Family Development:

  • Avoid judgements about what is 'normal.'
  • Avoid categorizing.
  • Avoid the idea of general principles of what causes problems or what resolves them.

Development of Behavior Problems:

  • The stories people tell themselves.
    • Interpret their experience in unhelpful ways.
    • Patterns of tunnel vision.
  • Problem-saturated stories take hold and encourage people to respond to each other in ways that perpetuate the problem story.

Goals of Therapy:

  • Separate the problem-saturated stories to open space for:
    • New and more constructive views of themselves.
    • Look for unique outcomes
      • 'Sparking events'

Conditions of Behavior Change:

  • Deconstruction:
    • View the problem as an external entity to start challenging its influence.
  • Unique Outcomes:
    • Resistance to the problem's influence
    • Opens room for counter stories

Overview of Therapy Process:

  • Involves finding new ways to view, interact with, and respond to problems by defining the role of the problems in their lives.
  • Broadly involves the following phases:
    • Meeting
    • Listening
    • Separating persons from problems
    • Enacting preferred narratives
    • Solidifying
  • Process thickens and enriches a person's identity and life accounts.
  • Therapist negotiates the time of each next meeting as therapy progresses, one meeting at a time.
  • At the end of each meeting, the therapists asks whether the conversation has been useful, if it has, they ask how?
  • Then, the therapist asks whether the family would like to meet again, and if so, when?
  • Meetings are typically 60 minutes
  • Length of therapy varies with each family (from a single consultation to years, most somewhere in between)

Role of the Therapist:

  • Collaborative
  • Carefully observe, inquire into, and document the family's particular situation in relation to their problem
  • With permission, share knowledge from previous work with families, and ask the family to evaluate the 'fit' and usefulness of that knowledge in relation to their problem
  • Takes notes of what works and what doesn't
  • Decentered, but influential - they have skills in facilitating a conversation

The Therapeutic Relationship:

  • Meeting the person apart from the problem
  • Separating people from problems:
    • The problem is the problem
  • Optimism and hope
  • Co-author/co-editor
  • Investigate reporter

Case Conceptualization/Assessment:

  • Problem-saturated stories
  • Unique outcomes and sparkling events
  • Dominant cultural and gender discourses
  • Local and alternative discourses:
    • Attending to client language and meaning

The Therapist:

  • Holds a position of curiosity.
  • Cherishes each person/family's unique stories.
  • Invite clients to develop and live out narratives that they prefer for their lives
  • Is decentered, not an expert, but an interested collaborator.
  • They are skilled at asking questions to bring forth the knowledge and experience carried in particular stories of the family.
    • Narrative Therapy Principles:
      • Therapist influential, but decentered.
      • Person separate from problem.
      • All problems have exceptions.
      • Opposite of problem is preferred.
      • Preferred story woven into identity.
      • Impact of relationships explored.
      • Deconstruct societal norms.
      • Personal decision making supported.

University Resources/Couple and Family Therapy/Keywords/Externalization:

  • Instead of having a problem or being a problem, clients are struggling against their problems.
  • The problem is the problem.
    • Aren't interested in problem-maintaining sequences (strategic), structural issues (structural), or solutions (solution-focused), they are interested in the problem's impact on the family.
    • Social Constructionism:
      • There is no absolute truth, instead shift toward deconstructing (reexamining) established truths that oppress people's lives.

Assumptions:

  • People have good intentions (they do not need or want problems).
  • People are profoundly influenced by the discourses around them.
  • People are not their problems.
  • People can develop alternative empowering stories once separated from their problems and the cultural myths they have internalized.

Healthy vs. Pathological:

  • Therapist is interested in families' own evaluations of what is problematic and what is preferred.
  • Clients name how the problem is affecting their lives and relationships.
  • Therapist invites people to bring their 'best selves' into consideration of the problems that diminish their relationship and an exploration of how they might choose ways of living that diminish the effect of the problems on their relationship.

Problem Development:

  • Reject categorizing people.
  • In the spirit of collaboration, the therapist situates themselves WITH clients.
  • They disclose the beliefs that inform their therapy so that clients can know what they're getting into.
  • Clients are encouraged to educate the therapist on their cultural predicaments and to correct the therapist when they make assumptions that don't fit for the client's experience.
  • Problems are likely to persist as long as unhelpful stories remain fixed and obscure other more helpful versions of events.

Health:

  • Cautious about applying terminology (diagnosis).
  • Want to hear the family's stories of how the problems they name affect their lives and relationships.
  • When one of these problems appears to have invaded a relationship, it is the therapist's responsibility to ask questions that invite family members to consider the effects of that problem on their relationship, and to consider the stand they want to take in relation to it.
  • Problems are things that are separate from the families they afflict.
  • Therapists look for problematic discourses and offer families the opportunity to describe and evaluate the effects of those discourses on their relationships and lives.

Problem-Saturated Story vs. Counterstory:

  • Problem-saturated stories encourage people to respond to each other in ways that perpetuate the problem story
  • Problems may be regarded as alien invaders

Goals of Therapy:

  • Separate from problem-saturated stories (and destructive cultural assumptions)
  • Open space for new and more constructive views of themselves.
  • Separate individuals from problems and unite the family to fight a common enemy
  • Search for unique outcomes (sparkling events, exceptions) - times when they resisted the problem or behaved in ways that contradicted the problem story.

Change:

  • Help clients deconstruct unproductive stories in order to reconstruct new and more productive ones.
  • Deconstruction involves questioning assumptions
  • Reconstruction involves creating new and more optimistic accounts of experience
  • Effects Questions:
    • Elicit effects of the problem
      • "How does the problem affect you? Your ideas about yourself? Your relationship?"
      • "Clients can begin to notice areas of their lives where the problem has been less powerful."
  • Identifying unique outcomes creates room for counterplots

Mechanisms of Change:

  • Telling and retelling, witnessing, and living out of multistoried lives.
  • Once people notice how their stories are problem saturated and try to change their narratives, what they do and perceive will change as well.
  • Facilitate this process by asking questions to highlight un-storied events and to encourage meaning making around those events, then tie the meaning to memorable actions and contexts.

Length, Frequency, and Number of Meetings:

  • Therapist negotiates the time of each next meeting as therapy progresses, one meeting at a time
  • At the end of each meeting, the therapist asks whether the conversation has been useful, if it has, they ask how?
  • Then, the therapist asks whether the family would like to meet again, and if so, when?
  • Meetings are typically 60 minutes
  • Length of therapy varies with each family (from a single consultation to years, most somewhere in between)

Focusing on New Directions:

  • CAUTION:
    • Often therapy invites people to re-immerse themselves in their most problematic stories
  • INSTEAD:
    • narrative therapy invite families to explore, describe, and experience new directions in life that are already unfolding - new positions, new stories
      • Counterstory
  • Asking permission is central to the model
  • At the start of the first meeting, the therapist ask if it would be okay to get to know the family in ways that have nothing to do with the problem before they describe what brought them to therapy.
  • Later meetings often start by the therapist reading their notes from the previous session aloud and asking question, such as:
    • Can you tell me about new developments that relate to what we were talking about last time
  • Other times the therapist begins by wondering whether there have been important thoughts or events that connect to possibilities identified in the previous conversations
  • Therapists use letters, documents, and videos to document and circulate alternative stories.
    • For example, after a therapy session a therapist may write a letter posing questions that invite the family to develop an alternative story even further than they did in the session
    • Or sending a document noting the stands the family has taken in regard to a problem
    • Reading a document between sessions, and the conversations that can follow a reading, keeping a story alive and growing

The Role of the Therapist:

  • Collaborative
  • Carefully observe, inquire into, and document the family's particular situation in relation to their problem.
  • With permission, share knowledge from previous work with families, and ask the family to evaluate the 'fit' and usefulness of that knowledge in relation to their problem.
  • Takes note of what works and what doesn't
  • Decentered, but influential - they have skills in facilitating a conversation
    • Therapy is a relationship - a conversation
  • Ask questions to expose gaps in problematic stories
  • Open space for describing alternatives
  • Invite families to add, subtract, or describe differently
  • Encourage families to ask questions about the therapist's questions and comments
  • The therapist wants the family to make meaning of their own experience, but the therapist's questions inevitably shape the inquiry.
  • Families are in a better position to interpret, making meaning of, and evaluate their own experiences than outsiders are, even trained outsiders
  • Therapists have more questions than answers

Assessment Questions:

  • It sounds as though problem is part of your life now.
  • How long have you been noticing this problem?
  • What effect does the problem have on your life?
  • How does the problem impact your relationships?
  • Does problem have an impact on your relationship with other family members?
  • What do you think about the effects the problem is having on your life?
  • Are you accepting what problem is doing?
  • Are these effects acceptable to you or not?
  • Why is this? Why are you taking this position on what problem is doing?
  • How would you prefer things to be?

Assessment and Treatment Planning:

  • People's lives are multi-storied, each new telling of a story generates new possibilities for making meaning and taking action.
  • The therapist invites family members to:
    • Evaluate their current situation
    • Name the problems involved
    • Evaluate their relationship to those problems
    • Take a stand in regard to them
    • Tell more satisfying stories of their lives
    • Evaluate the usefulness of the alternative stories
  • In telling and living out the newly developed strands of alternative stories, clients evaluate aspects of their lives.
    • Private hopes and fears
    • Family interactions
    • Contributions of culture of origin
    • Traditions, legacies
  • Questioning:
    • Is that the problem speaking?
    • Whose values are guiding you in saying that?
  • By exposing discouses that support problems, families can separate from the ways of being that are supported by those discourses, and identify and recognize preferred perceptions, attitudes, and actions.

Narrative Questions:

  • What name would you give the problem?
  • What is it like to experience the problem?
  • What effect does the problem have on your life?
  • What effect does the problem have on your relationship with each other?
  • What talks have you have about the problem in your family? What impacts has that had?
  • What effects does the problem have on your other relationships?
  • How does the problem alter your relationship with yourself?
  • Is this what you want for your life? Why or why not?
  • Are we talking about what you want to be talking about?
  • Is this conversation useful? How is it useful?

Techniques:

  • Externalizing conversations
  • Relatice influence questions
    • Who's in charge, the person or the problem?
  • Identifying unique outcomes
    • Reading between the lines of the problem story
    • Can you remember a time...
  • Reauthoring
    • What does it say about you...
  • Illuminating or creating the counter-story
  • Reinforcing the new story
    • Finding audiences, supportive witnesses, allies
    • Letter writing
  • Deconstructing destructive cultural assumptions

Interventions:

Listening
  • Listen to stories as stories.
  • Be attentive and responsive to their intended meaning in telling the story.
  • Ask about non-problematic aspects of the life of family members and of their relationship.
  • Make sure the problem does not trick us into mistaking 'them' for 'the problem.'
  • Unless families insist on moving quickly into talking about problems, spend a while listening to stories about their preferences.
  • At some point, families spontaneously start to tell problem saturated stories.
  • Try to put yourself in their shoes.
  • Do not assume you understand the meaning their experience holds for them
  • Listen and ask
  • Listen with focused attention, patience, and curiosity while building a relationship of mutual respect and trust.
Deconstructive Listening:
  • Listen 'deconstructively' to family stories - the listening is guided by the belief that those stories have many possible meanings.
  • Ask families to tell their stories, look for opportunities to reflect our sense of what they said, they are saying, and to ask whether the meaning we are making fits with their intended meaning.
  • We wonder aloud whether they are useful or desirable?
  • And, what difference it makes to think of it 'that way'?
Deconstructive Questioning:
  • Cultivate an awareness of the dominant (and dominating) stories in our society/
  • Develop ways of collaboratively examining the effects of those stories when we sense them at work win the lives and relationships of the families with whom we consult.
  • There are 'taken-for-granted' realities at play.
  • Deconstructive questions aim to examine problems in detail and expose discourses that suppose them, as well as those that might undermine those discourses.
Externalizing Conversations:
  • The person/family is not the problem; the problem is the problem.
  • Externalizing - problems are seen as separate from people.
  • As we listen to people's stories, we listen for the ways they refer to their problems in their lives. If we hear names like 'fear' or 'blowing up,' we orient to those things as entities or processes that are external to families.
    • If we are not hearing the name, we might ask the family to name them.
      • What would you call that something?
    • What 'feeds' the problem?
    • What 'starves' the problem?
    • In what settings might the problem be useful?
    • Who advocates for the problem?
    • What groups of people would be opposed to the problem and its intentions?
    • What are the effects of the problem on members of the family? On their lives and relationships?
Naming the Problem and the Project:
  • Naming a problem can open a way for examining it and thinking differently about it.
  • As we ask people to evaluate their relationship to problems, they often tell us what they would prefer to have in their lives in place of the problem.
  • What are the family's preferred directions?
  • 'Standing against' the problem.
Unique Outcomes (Exceptions):
  • Have you ever been able to escape the problem even for a few minutes?
    • Is the problem always with you?
The Absent but Implicit:
  • Listen for purposes, values, hopes, and commitments that are absent but implicit in family's narratives.
  • Double listening.
  • If a person experiences despair, they must have hopes, dreams, or visions of the future that are not being fulfilled.
  • If a person experiences injustive, they must have a conception of a just world.
  • When we hear problematic stories, we listen for these sorts of implications.
Telling and Witnessing:
  • Stories need listeners as well as tellers.
  • Attentive witnesses.
  • The therapist serves as a witness, but including other witnesses, especially those involved in people's lives, can be very helpful.
  • What was it like to hear what so-and-so was saying?
  • What thoughts were you having as so-and-so was talking?
    • It is useful to ask questions that, rather than leaving the field for response wide open, more specifically invite people to respond to unique outcomes and preferred directions.
      • Were you surprised when so-and-so described you as taking the time to let others know you care?
      • What did it mean to you to hear so-and-so say how important the relationship is to them?
Outside Witness:
  • Creating a setting where people can tell their preferred stories to a group of outsiders, who then amplify and accredit the stories that they witness.
    • What stood out for them?
    • What images it brought forward?
    • How it resonated with their own experiences?
    • Where they were transported by the interview?
    • Situate their reflections in their own experience.
    • Comment on the difference that being part of the conversation makes on their own lives.
Documenting and Circulating New Stories:
  • We are interested in documenting and circulating the new stories that emerge in therapy to thicken them and keep them alive.
  • We take notes in therapy to document new stories as they develop.
    • We often refer back to these notes and read them aloud.
  • We may create videos or documents about what they have learned, with their permission, that can be viewed or read by others facing similar problems.
  • We sometimes write letters between therapy sessions.
    • In these letters, we ask questions that we did not ask in the therapy session.
      • Our intention in letter writing is to thicken and extend the knowledge that has begun to emerge in session.
  • We might invite the family to share these documents with other people in their lives.
Questions:
  • Opening space questions (unique outcomes)
    • Has there been a time when arguing could have taken control of your relationship but didn't?
  • Preference questions (preferred experiences)
    • Was this way of handling things better or worse? And how?
  • Story development questions
    • How is this different from when arguing is in control?
  • Meaning questions
    • What does it say about you that you were able to...?
  • Questions that extend the story into the future
    • What do you predict...?

Narrative Therapy: What's Your Story?

  • Aims to separate the individual from the issue and externalize problems instead of internalizing them.

Principles of Narrative Family Therapy:

  • Reality is socially constructed.
  • Reality is influenced by and communicated through language.
  • Having a narrative can help us organize and maintain our reality.
  • There is no 'objective reality' or absolute truth.

Techniques in Narrative Family Therapy:

  • Telling One's Story:
    • A technique that involves re-telling your own story to find new meaning in old experiences.
  • Externalization Technique:
    • Externalizing helps you separate you from your problems, making it easier to change something you don't like about yourself.
  • Deconstruction Technique:
    • This technique involves breaking problems down into smaller, more specific issues that are easier to understand and address.
  • Unique Outcomes Technique:
    • To help you see from a new, more positive perspective, broaden your view to see more of your own storylines, and focus on one that doesn't include your most pervasive problems.

Empirical Support:

  • Some families prefer a different kind of therapy than narrative, some are seeking instruction and expert advice.
  • Regularly ask families how the therapy is going for them, and be open and willing to refer families if they are seeking a different kind of relationship.
  • Most families coming to therapy are concerned less with how the therapy is structured than with whether their family relationships improve
  • Emphasis on 'practice-based evidence' over evidence-based practice
  • More qualitative research rather than quantitative studies
  • Little empirical support currently exists
  • Randomized controlled trials of narrative therapy do not yet exist.
