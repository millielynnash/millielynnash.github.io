Lecture on Structural Family Therapy

Structural Family Therapy:

  • Family is depicted as a system that comprised of Subsystem.
    • A family is functional to the degree that it nurtures its members to negotiate well the demands of the outside world.
    • Boundaries set limits distinguishing subsystems from one another in varying permeability (from diffuse to rigid).
    • Subsystems separated by a diffuse boundary are enmeshed, whereas subsystems separated by a rigid boundary are disengaged.

Major Concepts:

  • Major Concepts:
    • Family
    • Family Structure Structure
      • Predictable sequences or organized patterns in which families interact
      • A set of rules governing behavior patterns
      • Structures typically have some form of hierarchical structure.
        • Reciprocal and complementary functions.
        • Parental involvement with children, over versus under involvement.
        • Structure is expressed through transactional patterns such as:
          • Hierarchy
            • Determines who (or which subsystem) has power in the system.
            • Any structure in a family is not static, it is developed over time.
            • Hierarchies can be rigid and unfair or weak and ineffective.
          • Power
          • Alliances
            • Structure:
              • The way a family is organized into subsystems whose interactions are regulated by interpersonal boundaries.
              • The organized pattern in which families interact.
    • Subsystem:
      • Subsystems are often formed based on the patterns of interactions in a family.
      • Patterns of interaction are largely based on roles and rules.
      • Covert coalitions are often more significant than obvious groupings.
      • Families are differentiated into subsystems based on generation, gender, or function.
      • Adults are the 'organizational leaders' of the family system so whatever is present in the adult subsystem relationship will be felt throughout the whole system.
      • Children are common 'symptom bearers.'
      • Organization is most healthy when there are 3 clearly defined systems:
        • Marital/Couple
        • Parental
        • Child/Sibling
    • Boundaries
      • Each subsystems is demarcated by interpersonal boundaries.
      • Boundaries range from rigid to diffuse.
      • Rigid Boundaries (Disengaged Subsystem)
        • Rigid boundaries are restrictive and permit little contact with outside subsystems, resulting in Disengagement.
        • Disengaged systems are independent, but isolated.
        • On the plus side, this fosters autonomy. But limits affection and support.
      • Diffuse Boundaries (Enmeshed Subsystem):
        • Diffuse boundaries are result in Enmeshment.
        • Enmeshed systems offer closeness, but at the expense of independence.
        • Too much closeness can cripple initiative.
    • Coalition
    • Patterns
      • Patterns are formed based on rules that are implicit or explicit.
      • Over time, as patterns develop, roles for family members are established.
      • Patterns are isomorphic (i.e., interaction patterns are repeated in different relationships in a system)
    • Triangles
    • Loyalties

Assumptions:

  • Assumptions:
    • The family could have developed a structure different from the dysfunctional one now being displayed.
    • The family processes adaptive resources in its relational repertoire that currently lie dormant.
    • Therapy attempts to activate what is already there, but latent.
    • Offers a framework that brings order and meaning to family transactions.
    • The consistent patterns of family behavior reveal family structure.
    • The emotional boundaries and coalitions that make up a family's structure are abstractions.
    • The concept of family structure enables therapists to intervene in a systematic and organized way.
    • Families seek help for a specific problem.
    • Family therapists look beyond the specifics of those problems to the family's attempts to solve them.
    • Exploring dynamics of interaction.
    • Recognizing the overall organizations of the family regulates those interactions.
    • The model attends to subsystems and boundaries that regulate contact between members.
    • The model emphasizes enactments wherein family members are encouraged to deal directly with one another in session, permitting the therapist to observe and modify their interactions.
  • Structural family therapy provides therapists with a blueprint for how to view patterns of family dynamics.

Theoretical Formulations:

  • Theoretical Formulations:
    • Basic assumption is that families are organized structurally in certain ways to cope with stress.
      • Organized patterns ensure the survival of the system, all patterns have this purpose.
      • Systems both responds and reacts to internal and external stressors.
    • Problems are symptomatic of the family's inability to be flexible and accommodating of changing needs.
    • All family behavior, even destructive behavior, has the goal of balancing the system to ensure its survival.
      • Family problems do not result from stresses in the system but from the effects of reactions and responses to the stresses.

Focus of Treatment

  • The focus of treatment is on changing the family structure rather than changing individual family members.
    • The process of family interaction is like the patterns of conversation at the dinner table. The structure of the family is where family members sit in relation to each other.
    • As family transactions are repeated, they foster expectations that establish enduring patterns.
    • Family structure is reinforced by the expectations that establish rules in a family.

Family Development:

  • Healthy families not defined by the abundance of problems.
    • But rather by a functioning structure
  • Healthy families can modify their structure to accommodate changing circumstances.
    • Unhealthy families increase rigidity of structures that are no longer functional.
    • Less adaptive families increase rigidity of structures and are no longer functional.

Development of Behavior Disorders:

  • Problems arise when there are:
    1. Weak hierarchies; conflict avoidance; cross-generational coalitions
    2. Inflexible structures
    3. Failure to adjust to changing circumstances
      • Failure to reorganize themselves in order to deal with stresses in the home environment.

Goal of therapy:

  • The ultimate goal of therapy is structural changes to the family
    • Alter the family structure so that the family can solve its own problems.
    • General goal: create or strengthen executive subsystem

Assessment:

  • Therapists have two types of assessment tools:
    • Enactment
    • Spontaneous behavior sequences
    • Enactments occur when a therapist stimulates a family to demonstrate how they handle problems
    • Therapists guide the family how to modify the enactment, creating new behavior sequences.

Opening Phase:

  • Joining and accommodating (effective therapy requires challenge and confrontation)
    • "The structural therapist needs to relate to family members in a way that validates their experience but does not preclude a challenge to the status quo."
  • Working with interaction (Use of enactments)
  • Mapping structural patterns (Observing enmeshment and disengagement)

Working Phase:

  • Focus on the underlying family structure:
    1. Highlight and modify interactions
    2. Intensity (interrupt rigid and difficult patters)
    3. "Challenging to be effective must be predicated on the client's strengths. Contrary to a common misconception, it is not the 'opposite' of joining. Joining and challenging need to be seen as simultaneous qualities in any therapeutic intervention."
    4. Help people do things themselves
    5. Therapists can invite family members to participate and interact in ways that will
      • Help them function more efficiently
Transformation Phase:
  • Transformation of family structure
    • Boundary Making
      • Loosen boundaries in disengaged families
      • Strengthen boundaries in enmeshed families
      • Unbalancing
      • Reframing (adding cognitive constructions)
Interventions
  • Unbalancing
    • Blocking
      • Stopping usual transactions
    • Exaggerating
      • Encouraging or sustaining interactions
    • Keeping silent
      • When a response is expected, doing the unusual by not giving one, thereby demanding the family to react.
  • Manipulating the intensity
    • Therapist's comfort level with intense emotions
  • Enactment
  • Complementarity
    • Summarize the cycle of interaction and clarify the systemic nature of the symptoms, relieve blame from any one person, and direct the way to change.
