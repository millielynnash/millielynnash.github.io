Background:

  • Behavior therapists first began working with families in the 1970s.
    • Was limited in its approach to families because it often did not consider the complicated nature of family systems.
      • Based on learning theories; was aimed at parent training and couple communication.
      • The approach is now making changes to be more adaptive to the:
        • Complex systems imbedded in families

Leading Figures:

Ivan Pavlov:

  • Classical Conditioning:
    • Unconditioned Stimulus (UCS)
      • Food
    • Unconditioned Response (UCR)
      • Salivation
    • Conditioned Stimulus (CS)
      • Ringing Bell

Joseph Wolphe:

  • Systematic Desensitization
    • De-conditions anxiety through reciprocal inhibition.

B. F. Skinner:

  • Operant Conditioning:
    • Responses that are positively reinforced will be repeated more frequently
    • Behaviors that are punished or ignored will be reduced or extinguished

Gerald Patterson:

  • Developed behavioral parent training

Robert Liebermann:

  • Behavioral approaches to couple and family therapy

Albert Ellis (1962) and Aaron Beck (1976):

  • Introduced cognitive elements into behavioral therapy.
    • Cognitive triangle:
      • Thoughts -> feelings -> behaviors -> thoughts

Theoretical Formulations:

  • Behavior is maintained by its consequences.
  • Behavior problems are caused by:
    • Dysfunctional patterns of reinforcement between parents and children.
  • Behavior will remain resistant to change until more rewarding consequences are introduced.
  • Social Exchange Theory:
    • People strive to maximize rewards and minimize costs in relationships.
  • Cognitive theorists emphasized the need for attitude changes to:
    • Promote and maintain behavior change.
  • Behavioral approaches are typically limited to two party relationships:
    • Parent/child, spouses.
    • Fail to take into account the triadic nature of relationships.
  • Cognitive techniques became more prominent as behavior therapists realized:
    • An exclusive focus on behavior often failed to address the complicated dynamics of couple and family interaction.

Cognitive-Behavioral Family Therapy:

  • CBT/CBFT is a highly efficacious intervention to assist families in a variety of contexts (this model has empirical support)
  • CBT has focused on family's cognitive processing and behavioral interactions, while minimizing the influences of individual differences between family members.
  • CBT also addresses characteristics that each family member brings to their relationships, explaining why family members behave and interpret events in maladaptive ways.
Normal Family Development:
  • Healthy families are those in which positive behaviors are maintained by:
    • Appropriate consequences
    • Negative behaviors are extinguished through the use of punishment
  • Healthy families have high rations of benefits to costs
    (Philippines, there is no such thing as divorce)
    Healthy couples will be able to engage in:
    Effective communication skills, problem-solving skills, and conflict resolution.
    Healthy families and couples could adapt, be flexible, and change.
Development of Behavior Disorders:
  • Behavioral Family Therapy:
    • Symptoms are viewed as learned responses.
    • For behavioral therapists, no underlying meanings of symptoms are sought.
    • Attention is focused on the symptoms themselves and the environmental responses that reinforce behavior.
  • Cognitive behavioral therapists will include ideas about dysfunctional schemas:
    • Regarding family roles and relationships that are learned in one's family-of-origin,
    • Cognitive Distortions:
      • Arbitrary Inference:
        • Conclusions are drawn in the absence of supporting evidence.
      • Selective Abstraction:
        • Certain details are highlighted while other important information is ignored.
      • Overgeneralization:
        • Isolated incidents are taken as general patterns.
      • Magnification and Minimization:
        • The significance of events is unrealistically magnified or diminished.
      • Personalization:
        • Events are arbitrarily interpreted in reference to oneself.
      • Dichotomous Thinking:
        • Experiences are interpreted as all good or all bad.
      • Labeling and Mislabeling:
        • Behavior is attributed to undesirable personality traits.
      • Mind Reading:
        • This is the magical gift of knowing what other people are thinking about without the aid of verbal communication.
Goals of Therapy:
  • Behavior and cognitive changes in the way one behaves toward/thinks about family.
  • Increase the rate of rewarding interactions in family relationships.
  • Decrease the use of negative family interactions; increase positive interaction.
  • Teach effective communication and problem-solving skills.
  • Alter negative thought patterns; promoted or maintain ineffective family interactions.
Change:
  • Behavior will change when the contingencies of reinforcement are altered.
  • Identify behavioral goals.
  • Learn techniques to achieve these goals.
  • Facilitate social reinforcement of change.

Treatment Process:

Assessment (2-4 sessions):
  • A hallmark of behavioral therapy.
  • Families and couples - questionnaires and home observations are conducted.
    • For sex therapy, clinical interviews are the main assessment tool.
  • Treatment goals are set based on results of assessment.
  • 2-4 sessions (About 1.5-2 hours each)
    • Main Point:
      • To gather information as much as possible at the beginning to form treatment plan.
      • Each person takes turn to respond.
      • Gather information, perspective-taking and avoid escalation.
  • Information Gathering:
    • Self-report questionnaires, clinical interviews, observation of interactional patterns.
  • Initial Conjoint Interviews (Couple):
    • CBCT:
      • How the couple met, what attracted them to each other, how they developed a deeper involvement, positive and negative life events, prior experiences in therapy.
    • Initial Conjoint Interviews (Families):
      • Inquired about the family members' current concerns and strengths of the relationship.
      • Orients the family to the process of therapy by describing the structure and course of CBFT and the roles of therapist and family.
Goal Setting:
  • Individual family members vary in the extent to which they have a clear understanding of the factors affecting their relationships and clear goals for treatment, and how their own contributions affect the achievement of their goals.
  • Therapist determines the appropriate sequence of addressing goals in therapy.
  • Extinguish undesired behavior and reinforce positive alternatives.
    • Ignoring the tantrum; rewarding the child for putting feelings into words.
  • Redefine the family's goals of decreasing negative behavior into increasing positive responses.
  • Families have difficulty describing the behavior they want to see more of.
  • The therapist helps by asking families to make a list of things the other person did that they liked throughout the week.
    • Reviewing the list in session the next week provides an opportunity to emphasize the importance of positive feedback
  • The agenda of therapy is educational.
    • Teach communication, problem-solving, and negotiation.
Role of the Therapist:
  • Didactic role early on to strike a balance between directiveness and collaboration.
  • Reviews homework assignments, models skills, coaches family members in practicing them, and fosters motivation.
  • Balances alliances and interventions with all the family members or subsystems, so that they feel supported and remain equally invested in improving relationships.
  • Directive Role of the Therapist:
    • Sets the pace of sessions so that the goals of treatment are addressed in a timely and sequential fashion.
    • Sets the agenda each session.
    • Monitors the use of time.
    • Responsible to stay on task and address the goals of session.
    • Teaches the clients to self-monitor as well.
    • Maintains control of the session with confidence and credibility.
    • Assumes multiple roles:
      • Director, educator, facilitator, collaborator, advocate
    • Balances the alliance and interventions with all members, so they feel supported and invested.
Homework:
  • Collaborate with families in designing homework to complete between sessions.
  • Homework is based on the learning principle that to replace existing ingrained dysfunctional interaction patterns with new ones, families need to rehearse the new patterns repeatedly, particularly under 'real life conditions.'
For Couples:
  • Couples are taught to express themselves in clear, behavioral descriptions, rather than in vague complaints.
  • Couples are taught new behavior exchange procedures, emphasizing positive reinforcement.
  • Couples are helped to improve their communication.
  • Couples are encouraged to establish clear and effective decision-making.
  • Couples are taught strategies to problem-solve future complaints to extend the benefits of therapy.
    • Ask each partner to list three things they would like the other to do more often.
Interventions:
  • Imagery
  • Roleplay
  • Coaching (to switch places, to increase their empathy for one another's feelings)
  • Communication training:
    • For example, engaging in an argument without attacking, 'I-statements', and Speaker/Listener exercises
  • Problem-solving training
  • Homework Assignments
  • Mindfulness and Meditation
For Families:
  • Parents have control over the rewards and punishment of children.
    • If parents change the contingencies of reinforcement, the child's behavior will change.
  • Skills are taught in sequence (shaping).
  • Help parents define the child's positive and problematic behaviors in specific observable actions.
  • Tracking target behaviors.
  • Reinforcing desired behavior through contingent encouragement with incentive charts.
  • Disciplinary techniques include time out, ignoring problem behavior.
Interventions:
  • Observe/Assess the frequency of problem behavior, what precedes it, and what reinforcement follows (Behavior Chaining)
  • Shaping (reinforcing small steps toward change)
  • Token economies (reward good behavior)
  • Contingency contracting (agreements by parents to make certain changes following changes made by their child)
  • Time out
