1. Treatment Planning

What is a Treatment Plan?

  • A treatment plan refers to a written document that outlines the proposed goals, plan, and methods of therapy.

Components of a Treatment Plan:

  • Diagnosis
  • Presenting Problem
  • Treatment Goals
  • Treatment Objectives
  • Treatment Strategy/Interventions
  • Frequency and Duration of Treatment
  • Evaluation: Measuring Progress and Outcomes

Treatment Goals

  • Goals are the building blocks of the treatment plan.
  • They are designed to be specific, realistic, and tailored to the needs of the person in therapy.
  • The language should also meet the person on their level.
  • Treatment Goals must be SMART
  • A SMART goal provides structure and a sense of direction that supports members in increasing their chances of achieving their treatment goal (s).
    • Specific
    • Measurable
    • Achievable
    • Relevant
    • Time-Bound

Treatment Objectives

  • Goals are often broken down into objectives to support the person in therapy through the process of taking small, achievable steps toward the completion of the larger goal.

Interventions

  • Goals usually also include the various techniques and interventions the mental health professional will implement to support achievement of the larger goal.

Frequency and Duration of Treatment

  • Frequency refers to how often will they attend - weekly, every two weeks, once a month, once, as needed, etc.
  • Duration refers to how long someone will attend 1 week, 1 month, 3 months, etc.

Evaluation: Measuring Progress and Outcomes

  • Is the client achieving progress?
    • Measured in several ways:
      • Client self-report
      • Therapist observation
      • Informal and Formal Assessment

Case Conceptualization

What is Case Conceptualization?

  • Case conceptualization is a report based on information gathered, organized, and assessed to provide an explanation of clientâ€™s behavior or presenting problem.
    • It is the therapist perspective on the case.
  • Theory guides our case conceptualization or our view of the problem.
    • Family systems theory
    • Specific theoretical approach or model
  • Case Conceptualization is not a diagnosis in itself
    • There is a diagnostic component

Components of a Case Conceptualization

  1. Introduction of the client
  2. Presenting concern
  3. Background information
  4. Systemic assessment
  5. Genogram

Process of Case Conceptualization

  1. Listen to client's story and presenting problem.
  2. Gather information about how they understand their world
  3. Obtain demographic information
  4. Explore social, cultural, and historical context
  5. Assess client's strengths and coping skills

Process of Case Conceptualization

  • Assess for risk, create a problem list
  • Diagnose
  • Apply theoretical orientation and hypothesize about the nature of the problem
  • Develop goals
  • Plan interventions

Theoretical Hypothesis

  • Apply theory to information gathers in initial interview
  • What is your theoretical understanding of the client?
  • What patterns of thinking, feeling, and action need to be addressed?
  • How does theory inform the treatment goals?
