Building Blocks of Assessment:

  • Types of Clinical Assessment:
    • Two broad approaches to assessment:
      • Systemic Approach
      • Postmodern Approach
    • Formal Assessments:
      • Formal assessment involves the use of tools such as tests, questionnaires, checklists and rating scales.
      • Formal assessments often have data to determine their reliability and validity.
      • Examples:
        • Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7 Questionnaire (GAD7) - Individual
          • GAD-7 (1).pdf
        • Patient Health Questionnaire 9 (PHQ9) - Individual
          • PHQ9.pdf
        • Experiences of Close Relationships Scale - Couple
    • Informal Assessments:
      • Unstructured:
        • Writing or drawing activities
        • Journals
        • Games
        • Observation
      • Structured:
        • Checklists
        • Rating Scales
        • Interviews
        • Questionnaires

  • Mental Status Exam (MSE):

    • The mental status examination is a structured assessment of the patient's behavioral and cognitive functioning.
    • In general, the components of a mental status exam include descriptions of the following:
      • Appearance:
        • Casual dress, normal grooming and hygiene.
      • Attitude:
        • Calm and cooperative
      • General Behavior:
        • Unusual movements or psychomotor changes
      • Speech and Language:
        • Normal rate/tone/volume and without pressure
      • Emotions:
        • Reactive and mood congruent, labile, tearful, blunted, normal range, depressed, constricted, flat
      • Mood:
        • Euthymic, irritable, elevated, anxious, depressed
      • Thought and Perception:
        • Goal-directed and logical or disorganized
        • Suicidal ideation: passive, active, plan?
        • Homicidal ideation: passive, active plan?
        • Hallucinations or delusions during interview
      • Cognition
      • Orientation:
        • oriented to time, place, person, self

  • Biopsychosocial Assessment:

    • Biopsychosocial Model: “Screenshot 2023-09-27 at 10.25.48 AM.png” could not be found.
    • A Biopsychosocial Assessment is an assessment typically conducted by therapists at the beginning of therapy, which assesses for biological, psychological, and social factors that can be contributing to a problem or problems with a client.
    • Biopsychosocial Assessments enable therapist to diagnose and effectively treat clients.
    • Tasks and Process of Assessment:
      1. Make Initial Contact
      2. Develop a collaborative therapeutic relationship
      3. Use a bio-psycho-sociocultural-spiritual assessment: Person-in-Environment
      4. Defining problems
      5. Assessing safety
      6. Accessing and appreciating patient/clients' strengths
      7. Assess systems collaboration issues and strengths
      8. Expand awareness and perspectives
      9. Assess goals
    • Biopsychosocial Assessment Areas:
      • Demographic Information (Age, Ethnicity, Religion, Relational Status, Referral Status, etc.)
      • History of the Presenting Problem
      • Psychiatric History
      • Trauma History
      • Family Psychiatric History
      • Medical History
      • Medication Use
      • Substance Use
      • Family History (Family of Origin, Relationship with Parents, Siblings, etc.)
      • Social History
      • Spiritual/Cultural Factors
      • Developmental History
      • Educational/Vocational History
      • Legal History
      • SNAP (Strength, Needs, Abilities, Preferences)
      • Other Important Information
    • Guiding Principles for Assessment:
      • A systemic view:
        • Everything is connected
      • Foundational importance of bio-physiological, psychological, sociocultural, and spiritual domains in relation to multiple service systems
      • A strengths-based perspective
      • Assessment as an ongoing process

  • Bio-Physiological Assessment:

    • Regular screening and tracking during sessions of the following:
      • Health status
      • Mobility
      • Wellness
        • Exercise
        • Nutrition
        • Sleep
    • Ensure that client has primary care physician
    • Communicate with primate care physician or other health providers as needed.

  • Psychological Assessment:

    • General Psychological Issues:
      • Cognitive skills and ability (e.g.. thought process, problem-solving/decision-making skills)
      • Behavioral skills
      • Emotional competencies:
        • Affect regulation
        • Capacity for relating
      • Motivational factors
    • Overall mental status
    • Risk Assessment
    • Specific Mental Health Concerns:
      • Depression
      • Anxiety
      • Substance Use
    • Appropriate referral if needed
    • Ongoing communication between external providers (collaboration of care)

  • Risk Assessment:

    • Risk Assessment includes consideration of psychological and social factors as part of a comprehensive review to capture client needs, and to assess their risk of harm to themselves or others.
    • Assessments often aim to categorize patients as at high, medium, or low risk
    • Examples of risk areas include:
      • Suicidal ideation
      • Homicidal ideation
      • Child abuse/neglect
      • Intimate partner violence
      • Physical health issues or crises
        • Inability to care for self or others
      • Animal abuse
      • Substance abuse
    • Important factors:
      • Level of risk
      • Intent to act
      • Plan to act
      • Means to act
      • Risk and protective factors

  • Strengths Based Assessment:

    • Assessing Strengths:
      • Personal and Relational Strengths:
        • Bio-physiological
        • Psychological
        • Sociocultural
        • Spiritual
      • 'Strengths' of the problem/symptoms
      • Strengths in systems collaboration
    • Exploring the 'Strengths' and Potential 'Contributions' of the Problem
      • When was the last time that you realized that the problem was helpful to you?
      • If your problem has a voice, what do you think it is trying to share or communicate with you?
      • How might the problem be helpful to you?
      • If we could get rid of the problem, what might be some aspect of it that you might want to retain?
    • Useful Questions for Assessing Strengths:
      • What has been helpful so far?
      • What are some of your successes?
      • What are the achievements that you are most proud of having accomplished?
      • What little things are you doing that make life a little better for you?
      • What kinds of things do people compliment you on?
      • When have you stopped doing something that was difficult for you to stop?
      • When have you made an important change in your life that was difficult to make?
      • When have you broken a habit that was hard to break?
    • Outcomes of Assessment:
      • Problems:
        • Bio-psycho-sociocultural-spiritual perspective
        • Problem and attempted solutions specifically and behaviorally described (SOLVABLE).
      • Goals:
        • Desired future outcomes
        • Specifically and behaviorally described (ATTAINABLE)
        • Professional and the patient/client mutually agree to the described problems and goals.
      • Treatment plan and interventions based on mutual consensus of problem and goal

  • Collaborative Assessment and Management of Suicidality (CAMS)
    • It is a therapeutic framework for suicide-specific assessment and treatment of a patient's suicidal risk. It is a flexible approach that can be used across theoretical orientations and disciplines for a wide range of suicidal patients across treatment settings and different treatment modalities.
    • Every session of CAMS intentionally utilizes the patient's input about what is and is not working. All assessment work in CAMS is collaborative.
      • The clinician's honesty and forthrightness are key elements.
        • The CAMS clinician endeavors to understand their patient's suffering from an empathic, nonjudgemental, and intra-subjective perspective. The clinician never shames or blames a suicidal person for being suicidal.
  • Evidence-Based Psychological Treatments for Suicide:
    • Three evidence-based (randomized controlled trials) interventions and treatments that are designed to directly target suicide at risk.
      • Dialectical Behavioral Therapy (DBT)
      • Cognitive Behavior Therapy (CBT):
        • Suicide Prevention (CT-SP) and Brief CBT.
      • Collaborative Assessment and Management of Suicidality (CAMS)
  • Suicide-Specific Brief Interventions:
    • Evidence-Based (Random Controlled Trial):
      • Teachable Moment Brief Intervention (TMBI):
        • A one-time intervention 2 dats after an attempt.
      • Motivational Interviewing:
        • In-patient settings
      • Crisis Response Planning & Safety Planning (6 Steps)
      • Attempted Suicide Short Intervention Program (ASSIP):
        • 3 Sessions in-patient settings
