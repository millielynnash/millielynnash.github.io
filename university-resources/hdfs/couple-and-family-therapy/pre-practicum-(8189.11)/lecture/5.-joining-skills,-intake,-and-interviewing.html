  • Informed Consent:
    • Informed consent is the consent by a client to a proposed medical or psychotherapeutic procedure, or for participation in a research project or clinical study.
    • For consent to be informed the client must first achieve a clear understanding of the relevant facts, risks and benefits, and available alternatives involved.
    • Informed consent requires legal documentation in all 50 states.
  • Informed Consent - AAMFT Code of Ethics:
    • Informed consent generally necessitates that the client:
      • has the capacity to consent
      • Has been adequately informed of significant information concerning treatment processes and procedures
      • Has been adequately informed of potential risks and benefits of treatments for which generally recognized standards do not yet exist
      • Has freely and without undue influence expressed consent
      • Has provided consent that is appropriately documented
    • Things to include in Informed Consent:
      • Contact information
      • Your qualifications including training, experience, licensure and certifications
      • Any limitations on your practice such as being under supervision
      • Services offered
      • Your theoretical foundation and approach to therapy
      • Fee structure including broken appointments, collecting debt, and cancellation policy
      • Whether or not you will file insurance/provide documentation for your clients to file insurance
      • How you handle emergencies and client needs after business hours
      • Confidentiality and exceptions to confidentiality
      • Client rights and responsibilities
      • The professional code(s) of ethics that you adhere to
      • How to file a complaint against you
    • Steps to obtaining Informed Consent:
      • **Provide:
        • Provide client with information
      • **Evaluate:
        • Evaluate the client's capacity to understand the information.
      • Obtain:
        • Obtain consent from the client.
      • Document:
        • Document obtaining informed consent in the initial progress note.
  • Initial Session/Intake:
    1. Welcome Stage:
      • Make sure participant(s) are comfortable.
      • Review clinic policies and procedures.
      • Address any questions.
      • Overall Goal: Orient clients to therapy.
    2. Social Stage:
      • Get to know the clients outside of the problem.
      • Chat with each client briefly, asking about their occupations and interests.
      • Goal: Begin to join and build rapport with clients
    3. Assessment State - complete Mental Status Exam, biopsychosocial assessment, and risk assessment:
      • Begin initial assessment with the client system.
        • This includes individual level and relational level assessment.
        • Complete the following:
          • Mental Status Exam:
            • The mental status examination is a structured assessment of the patient's behavioral and cognitive functioning.
            • In general, the components of a mental status exam include descriptions of the following:
              • Appearance
              • General Behavior
              • Speech and Language
              • Emotions
              • Thought and Perception
              • Cognition
          • Risk Assessment:
            • Risk Assessment includes consideration of psychological and social factors as part of a comprehensive review to capture client needs, and to assess their risk of harm to themselves or others.
            • Assessments often aim to categorize patients as at high, medium or low risk.
            • Examples of risk areas include:
              • Suicidal Ideation
              • Homicidal Ideation
              • Child Abuse/Neglect
              • Intimate Partner Violence
              • Physical Health Issues or Crises
                • Inability to care for self or others
              • Animal Abuse
              • Substance Use
          • Intake Checklist:
            • A Biopsychosocial Assessment is an assessment typically conducted by therapists at the beginning of therapy, which assesses for biological, psychological, and social factors that can be contributing to a problem or problems with a client.
            • Biopsychosocial Assessments enable therapist to diagnose and effectively treat clients.
      • Primary Goal: Gain context to help facilitate change.
        • Diagnose Disorders
        • Plan Treatments
        • Evaluate the effectiveness of interventions
    4. Establishing the Problem:
      • Have each client state, without discussion, their view of the issue needed to be addressed in therapy.
      • Goal: To identify the presenting problem bringing the client(s) to therapy.

      • Have the client(s) set short and long-term goals for the therapy.
      • These are initial goals and can be considered tentative.
      • Try to work with clients to identify SMART goals.

    • Skilled Use of Questions
      • Circular Questioning:
        • Questions that draw distinctions (broaden understanding)
          • Across Time
          • Between People
          • Between Situations
          • Between Parts of a Person
        • Questions that draw connections (narrow focus)
          • Behavior
          • Feelings
          • Beliefs
          • Meaning
          • Relationships
        • Common Circular Questions:
          • Temporal Questions:
            • Encourages reflection on what has happened in the past and the changes that have taken place over time.
              • eg., Was anything different in how you were treated before, compared with now?
            • Triadic Questions:
              • Invite reflection regarding how two people's actions impact the behavior or mood of another.
                • eg., How does your father react when your mother cries?
            • Mind-Reading Questions:
              • Invite individuals to consider what someone else might think in order to explore how their relationship works.
                • eg., If your father didn't arrive home as expected, how do you think your mother would react?
