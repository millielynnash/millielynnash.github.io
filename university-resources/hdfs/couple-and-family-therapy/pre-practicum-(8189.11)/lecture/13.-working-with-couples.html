Telehealth

Telepresence and Virtual Professionalism

  • Telebehavioral health (TBH) professionals can demonstrate how to apply appropriate techniques to maximize therapeutic atmosphere in both physical and virtual environments as well as minimize distraction and interruptions.
  • Professionals can show how to approximate an in-person relationship and foster spontaneity through TBH.

Creating a Virtual Office

  • When setting up your virtual office we must consider the following:
    • Physical location
    • Privacy
    • Lighting
    • Comfort
  • Where is your home based or online office located physically?
    • Is it near a power source?
    • Is there a way to 'close the door' to your office? (i.e., is there at least a door between your office space and other common areas in your location?)
    • Is it in a high traffic area?
  • How can you create a distraction free environment?
    • Turn off anything that makes noise
    • A sign outside the door can help remind others not to make noise during the session
    • Avoid a space with lots of clutter on the desk
    • Be mindful of what is behind you in the camera's view
  • How can you create a quality video image?
    • Make sure you are well lit.
    • Are there shadows being cast on my face?
    • Am I lit well enough to see me clearly?
    • Find the best source of lighting.
      • Overhead light (may not be the best)
      • Desk lamp
      • Single floor lamp (often good; 4-5 ft.; bulbs point up to the ceiling)
    • Make sure your camera is well placed.
      • Eye level with the camera straight at you (ideal)
    • Practice good posture (use the picture in picture)
  • How can you create a therapeutic environment?
    • Find an office space with minimal distractions or a solid color background.
    • Be mindful of clothing to limit distractions
      • Solid colors are best
      • Avoid busy patterns
      • Avoid large jewelry and hats

Couples Therapy: Things to Remember

  • Couples therapy is dyadic.
  • The relationship is the client.
  • It is important to have 1 (no more than 2) shared goals for therapy.
  • Both partners must participate.
  • We need to establish clear treatment objectives.

Couples Therapy Process

  • Session 1: Initial Intake Session (80 minutes)
  • Session 2: Individual Subsystem Session (Partner 1) (50 minutes)
  • Session 3: Individual Subsystem Session (Partner 2) (50 minutes)
  • Session 4: Conjoint Couples' Session (50 minutes)
  • Session 5 and Beyond: Conjoint Couples' Session
First Session Tasks
  • Welcome and Introductions
  • Orientation to Therapy and the First Session
  • Review Informed Consent and Clinic Policies
  • Question and Answer
  • Risk Assessment
  • Initial Understanding Presenting Problem
  • Begin Development of Treatment Goals
  • Reflection and Wrap-Up
Individual Subsystem Session Tasks
  • Welcome and Quick Overview of the Session
    • Remind clients of no secrets policy.
  • Risk Assessment (Past and Present)
  • Assess for Infidelity
  • Review Individual Functioning
  • Understanding Presenting Problem from the Individual Perspective
  • Continue Development of Treatment Goals
  • Reflection and Wrap-Up
Session 4: Conjoint Session
  • Before this session, therapist should have an initial systemic hypothesis and theory based conceptualization of the case.
  • During the session:
    • Provide clients with a summary of information gathered in sessions 1-3.
      • If necessary, discuss new information or secrets shared in sessions 2 and 3
    • Review and confirm treatment goals and objectives
      • Goals and objectives must be SMART
    • Continue assessment as needed for individuals and/or relationship
    • Begin intervention or working phase
Session 5 and Beyond
  • WORK the treatment plan
  • Continue to evaluate your hypothesis and conceptualization between sessions
  • Interventions should be based on the client's presenting problem as well as your theoretical conceptualization
