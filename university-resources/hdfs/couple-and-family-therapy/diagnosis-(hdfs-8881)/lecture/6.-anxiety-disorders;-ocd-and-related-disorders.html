1. Anxiety Disorders; OCD and Related Disorders

Anxiety Disorders

Major Players for Adults

  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder → Panic Disorder → Social Anxiety Disorder

Major Players for Children

  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder
  • Separation Anxiety Disorder

Anxiety Disorder Diagnosis

  • It is generalized anxiety disorder? (May also include Panic Attacks [specifier])
    • If not
  • Common feature is excessive fear and and anxiety and related behavioral disturbances that significantly affect the individual's capacity to function.
  • What differentiates the diagnosis?
    • The types of objects/situations that induce fear, anxiety, and avoidance, and the associated cognitive ideation.
  • Fear:
    • The emotional response to real and perceived imminent threat.
    • Fear is associated with surges of automatic arousal, fight or flight, thoughts of immediate danger, escape behaviors.
  • Anxiety:
    • The anticipation of future threat.
    • Anxiety is associated with muscle tension, vigilance in preparation for future danger, caution, and avoidance behaviors.

Normal or Normative?

  • Anxiety disorders differ from developmentally normative fear or anxiety.
  • The anxiety is excessive or persisting beyond developmentally appropriate periods.
  • They differ from transient or stress-induced anxiety by persistence (lasting 6+ months)

Onset

  • Develop in childhood
  • Persist if treated

Risk Factors

  • Temperament
  • Environment
  • Genetic/Physiological

General Anxiety Disorder

  • Excessive anxiety and worry about a number of events or activities.
  • The intensity, duration, or frequency of the anxiety and worry is out of proportion to the anticipated event.
  • Difficult to control worry
  • Interferes with psychosocial functioning
  • Symptoms are pervasive, pronounced, and distressing
  • Longer duration
  • Occurs without precipitants
  • CAUTION: Generalized Anxiety Disorder can be over-diagnosed in children

Panic Disorder (Panic Attacks)

  • The abrupt surge can occur from a calm state or an anxious state.
    • Palpitations, pounding heart, or accelerated heart rate
    • Sweating
    • Trembling or shaking
    • Sensations of shortness of breath or smothering
    • Feelings of choking
    • Chest pain or discomfort
    • Nausea or abdominal distress
    • Feeling dizzy, unsteady, light-headed, or faint
    • Chills or heat sensations
    • Paresthesias (numbness or tingling sensations, likely in the hands and legs)
    • Derealization (feelings of unreality) or depersonalization (being detached from oneself)
    • Fear of losing control or 'going crazy'
    • Fear of dying

Assessment Tools

  • Depression Anxiety Stress Scales - DAAS-21
  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder Questionnaire IV - GADQ-IV
  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7 - GAD 7
  • Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale - HARS
  • Patient Health Questionnaire 4 - PHQ-4

DSM-5-TR and Family Systems

Impact of Anxiety Disorders

  • Anxiety disorders have considerable impact on children, adolescents, adults, and families.
  • Anxiety disorders significantly impair physical, psychological, social, and relational functioning of family members.
  • Anxiety disorders are associated with higher rates of relational distress.
  • Anxiety can have a detrimental impact on the family.
  • Individuals with first-degree relatives (e.g., parents, siblings) with an anxiety disorder are at increased risk to develop an anxiety disorder.
  • 30%-40% of the variance in anxiety disorders is due to genetics.
  • Anxiety is contagious. Anxiety runs in families.

Assessment

  • Measure the anxiety symptoms, the severity of symptoms, and factors maintaining the disorder.

Assessment Process

  • Build alliance
  • Assess symptoms
  • Assess factors maintaining the disorder.
  • Alliance is the most important common factor. Alliance is strongly related to positive treatment outcomes.
Relational Assessments
  • Multidimensional Anxiety Scale for Children (MASC)
  • Screen for Child Anxiety Related Emotional Disorders (SCARED)
  • Revised Dyadic Adjustment Scale
  • Revised Conflict Tactics Scale
  • Family Accommodation Scale-Anxiety

Family Systems Interventions

  • Research on family therapy for anxiety disorders is limited.
  • Family Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (FCBT) is the most well researched model (with mixed findings)
  • Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT)
  • Family Psychoeducation
  • Coping Skills Training
    • Deep breathing exercises (e.g., paced breathing)
    • Progressive Muscle Relaxation (i.e., technique of tensing and releasing different muscle groups)
    • Cognitive Restructuring (i.e., identifying anxious thoughts and replacing them)

Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorders

  • Major Diagnosis:
    • Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder
  • Less Common:
    • Body Dysmorphic Disorder
    • Hoarding Disorder
    • Trichotillomania
    • Excoriation
  • Characterized by obsessions and/or compulsions.
  • Obsessions:
    • Recurrent and persistent thoughts, urges, or images that are experienced as intrusive and unwanted.
  • Compulsions:
    • Repetitive behaviors that an individual is driven to perform in response to obsession or rigid rules that must be applied.

Is it Normative?

  • OC-Related disorders differ from developmentally normative preoccupations and rituals.
  • Symptoms persist beyond developmentally normative preoccupations and rituals.
  • The symptoms are distressing and cause impairment in functioning.

Specifics

  • The specific content of obsessions and compulsions varies.
  • Common symptom dimensions include:
    • Cleaning
    • Symmetry
    • Repeating
    • Ordering
    • Counting

Obsessions (Thoughts)

  • Fear of getting contaminated by people or the environment.
  • Disturbing sexual thoughts or images.
  • Fear of blurting out obscenities or insults.
  • Extreme concern with order, symmetry, or precision
  • Recurrent intrusive thoughts of sounds, images, words, or numbers.
  • Fear of losing or discarding something important

Compulsions (Behaviors)

  • Excessive or ritualized hand washing, showering, brushing teeth, or toileting.
  • Repeated cleaning of household objects
  • Ordering or arranging things in a particular way
  • Repeatedly checking locks, switches, or appliances
  • Constantly seeking approval or reassurance
  • Repeated counting to a certain number

Treatment

Psychotherapy

  • Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT)
    • Exposure therapy is effective for reducing compulsive behaviors, even for people who did not respond well to medication. With ERP, people spend time in a situation that triggers their compulsions (such as touching dirty objects) and they are prevented from engaging in their typical compulsion (such as handwashing). Although this approach may cause feelings of anxiety at first, compulsions decrease for most people as they continue treatment.
  • Medication
  • Psychotherapy and Medication

DSM-5-TR and Family Systems

  • Individual Assessments
    • There are many evidence-based assessments that explore OCD and related disorders from an individual perspective
  • Family Systems Assessments
    • Level 2 Repetitive Thoughts and Behaviors (Child and Adult versions)
    • Family Accomodation Scale of Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder Parental Attitudes and Beliefs Scale
