Trauma- and Stressor- Related Disorders

  • Common feature is psychological distress following exposure to a traumatic pr stressful event that significantly affects the individual's capacity to function.

Acute Stress Disorder

  • Distinguished from PTSD because the symptom pattern is restricted to a duration of 3 days to 1 month following exposure to the traumatic event.

Posttraumatic Stress Disorder

  • Subcategories of symptoms for PTSD
    • Trauma
    • Intrusion
    • Avoidance
    • Negative alterations in cognitions and mood
    • Marked alterations in arousal and reactivity

Trauma

  • Exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence in one (or more) of the following ways:
    1. Directly experiencing the traumatic event (s)
    2. Witnessing, in person, the event (s) as it occurred to others
    3. Learning that the traumatic event (s) occurred to a close family member or close friend. In cases of actual or threatened death of a family member or friend, the event (s) must have been violent or accidental.
    4. Experiencing repeated or extreme exposure to aversive details of the traumatic event (s) (e.g., first responders collecting human remains; police officers repeatedly exposed to details of child abuse).

Traumatic Events

  • Exposure to war as a combatant or civilian
  • Threatened or actual physical assault
    • Robbery, mugging, childhood physical abuse
  • Threatened or actual sexual violence
    • Forced penetration, alcohol/drug-facilitated sexual assault, abusive sexual contact, non-contact offenses, trafficking
  • Being kidnapped, taken hostage
  • Terrorist attack, torture, incarceration as prisoner of war
  • Natural and man-made disasters
  • Severe motor accidents

Intrusion

  • Presence of one (or more) of the following intrusion symptoms associated with the traumatic event (s), beginning after the traumatic event (s) occurred:
    1. Recurrent, involuntary, and intrusive distressing memories of the traumatic event (s). (Note: In children older than 6 years, repetitive play may occur in which themes or aspects of the traumatic event (s) are expressed.
    2. Recurrent distressing dreams in which the content and/or affect of the dream or related to the trauma event (s). (Note: In children, there may be frightening dreams without recognizable content)
    3. Dissociative reactions (e.g., flashbacks) in which the individual feels or acts as if the traumatic event (s) were recurring. (Such reactions may occur on a continuum, with the most extreme expression being a complete loss of awareness of present surroundings.) (Note: In children, trauma-specific reenactment may occur in play)
    4. Intense or prolonged psychological distress at exposure to internal or external cues that symbolize or resemble an aspect of the traumatic event (s).
    5. Marked physiological reactions to internal or external cues that symbolize or resemble an aspect of the traumatic event (s).

Avoidance

  • Persistant avoidance of stimuli associated with the traumatic event (s), beginning after the traumatic event (s) occurred, as evidenced by one or both of the following:
    1. Avoidance of or efforts to avoid distressing memories, thoughts, or feelings about or closely associated with the traumatic event (s).
    2. Avoidance of or efforts to avoid external reminders (people, places, conversations, activities, objects, situations) that arouse distressing memories, thoughts, or feelings about or closely associated with the traumatic event (s).

Negative Alterations

  • Negative alterations in cognitions and mood associated with the traumatic event (s), beginning or worsening after the traumatic event (s) occurred, as evidenced by two (or more) of the following:
    1. Inability to remmeber an important aspect of the traumatic event (s) (typically due to dissociative amnesia, and not to other factors such as head injury, alcohol, or drugs).
    2. Persistent and exaggerated negative beliefs or expectations about oneself, others, or the world (e.g., 'I am bad', 'No one can be trusted,' 'The world is completely dangerous,' 'My whole nervous system is permanently ruined').
    3. Persistent, distorted cognitions about the cause or consequences of the traumatic event (s) that lead the individual to blame himself/herself or others.
    4. Persistent negative emotional state (e.g., fear, horror, anger, guilt, or shame).
    5. Markedly diminished interest or participation in significant activities.
    6. Feelings of detachment or estrangement from others.
    7. Persistent inability to experience positive emotions (e.g., inability to experience happiness, satisfaction, or loving feelings).

Arousal Reactivity

  • Marked alterations in arousal and reactivity associated with the traumatic event (s), beginning or worsening after the traumatic event (s) occurred, as evidenced by two (or more) of the following:
    1. Irritable behavior and angry outbursts (with little or no provocation), typically expressed as verbal or physical aggression toward people or objects.
    2. Reckless or self-destructive behavior.
    3. Hypervigilance
    4. Exaggerated startle response
    5. Problems with concentration
    6. Sleep disturbances (e.g., difficulty falling or staying asleep or restless sleep)

Specify

  • With dissociative symptoms:
    • The individual's symptoms meet the criteria for posttraumatic stress disorder, and in addition, in response to the stressor, the individual experiences persistent or recurrent symptoms of either of the following:
      1. Depersonalization:
        • Persistent or recurrent experiences of feeling detached from, and as if one were an outside observer of one's mental processes or body (e.g., feeling as though one were in a dream; feeling a sense of unreality of self or body or of time moving slowly).
      2. Derealization:
        • Persistent or recurrent experiences of unreality of surroundings (e.g., the world around the individual is experienced as unreal, dreamlike, distant, or distorted). Note: To use this subtype, the dissociative symptoms must not be attributable to the physiological effects of a substance (e.g., blackouts, behavior during alcohol intoxication) or another medication condition (e.g., complex partial seizures).

    • (co-occurence in veterans 48%)

Adjustment Disorder

  • Emotional or behavioral symptoms in response to an identifiable stressor
  • Stressor may be a single event or there may be multiple stressors
  • Stressors may be recurrent or continuous
  • The disturbance begins within 3 months of a stressor and lasts no longer than 6 months after the stressor or its consequences
  • Stress, not necessarily a traumatic event

DSM-5-TR and Family Systems

Why is Trauma a Family Issue?

  • Trauma takes root in and impacts an individual's limbic and endocrine systems
  • For individuals to develop a stress reaction, some sort of traumatic event must occur.
  • Traumatic events originate in an individual's system or environment
  • Trauma originates outside the individual
  • Trauma disuptes the affected individual's larger systems
  • Traumatized individual's reactions to stress are best understood in the context of their experiences as a member of a family system
  • (intergenerational trauma; historical trauma; blood memory)
  • The stress that results from trauma can negatively affect relationships
  • Caregivers who are struggling with trauma may have compromised caregiving
  • Compromised caregiving can exacerbate children's difficulties
  • Trauma impairs a family's ability to support and nurture each other
  • Traumatic events drain family's time, energy, effort, finances, etc.

Evidence Based Individual Treatment

  • Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT)
    • Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT)
      • CPT attempts to provide client with skills to handle distressing thoughts
      • Understand how trauma changes one's view of the world, self, and others
      • Focus on examining and challenging thoughts about trauma
    • Prolonged Exposure Therapy (PE)
      • Education
      • Breathing retraining
      • Real world practice (in vivo exposure)
      • Talking through the trauma (imaginal exposure)
  • Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR)
    • Identification of a target memory, image, about the trauma
    • Desensitization and reprocessing
    • Installing positive thoughts and images, negative images are no longer distressing
    • Body Scan:
      • Focusing on tension in the body, to identify additional issues need to address in later sessions
  • Pharmacotherapy (SSRIs)
    • Sertraline (Zoloft)
    • Paroxetine (Paxil)
  • Combination of Psychotherapy and Pharmacotherapy

Major Diagnosis

  • Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID)

Dissociative Disorders

  • Common feature is disruption and/or discontinuity in consciousness, memory, identity, emotion, perception, body representation, motor control, and behavior that significantly affects the individual's capacity to function.

DSM-5-TR and Family Systems

  • Dissociation is often experienced as an internal, isolating experience
  • The impact of current partners and families is profound
  • Treatment can be enhanced when considering the impact on families and family's impact on dissociation
  • Family reactions to dissociation range:
    • Dismissal, disbelief, relief, shame, surprise, confusion

Overview of Dissociation

  • Dissociation is a disintegration of psychological functions such as attention, consciousness, and memory
    • Ranging from zoning out to the clinical diagnosis ofDID
  • Amnesia: an inability to recall id DID
  • Amnesia: an inability to recall identity or events, losing time
  • Identity Fragmentation: Lack of integration between memory and sense of self
  • Depersonalization: Disconnecting with the outside world
  • Identity Alteration: Distinct personality states develop

Treatment

  • The primary focus of treatment is integration
  • Associating those elements of functioning that have been disconnected
  • The client's current family environment is vital to the treatment prognosis
  • The inclusion of family in therapy requires careful consideration, especially with regarding to childhood trauma
  • Internal Family Systems
  • Contextual Treatment
  • Couples Therapy
    • Developmental Couples Therapy for Complex Trauma
    • Internal Family Systems Couples Therapy
