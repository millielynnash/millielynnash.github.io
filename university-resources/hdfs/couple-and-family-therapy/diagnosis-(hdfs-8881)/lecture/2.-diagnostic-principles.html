1. Diagnostic Principles

The Relationship between Diagnosis and Treatment

  • DSM-TR is a manual for assessment and diagnosis of mental disorders and does not include information or guidelines for treatment of any disorder.
  • Determining an accurate diagnosis is the first step toward being able to appropriately treat any medical condition, and mental disorders are no exception.
  • DSM-5-TR is helpful in measuring the effectiveness of treatment, as dimensional assessments will assist clinicians in assessing changes in severity levels as a response to treatment.

History of DSM:

  • 1840:
    • Category 'insanity' first attempt to quantify and name psychiatric diagnoses.
  • 1844:
    • APA published a predecessor of the DSM: A classification of institutionalized mental patients
  • Early 1900s:
    • First description of schizophrenia by Emil Kraeplin.
  • 1917:
    • Statistical Manual for the Use of Institutions for the Insane, included 22 diagnoses.
  • 1952:
    • First DSM, 130 pages long, 106 disorders
  • 1994:
    • DSM IV, 886 pages long, 297 disorders
  • 2000:
    • DSM IV-TR, 943 pages long, 365 disorders
  • 2013:
    • DSM 5, 991 pages long, 157 disorders
  • 2022:
    • DSM-5-TR, 1050 pages long

Definition of Mental Disorder

  • A mental disorder is a syndrome characterized by clinically significant disturbance in an individual's cognition, emotion regulation, or behavior that reflects a dysfunction in the psychological, biological, or developmental processes underlying mental functioning.
  • Mental disorders are usually associated with significant distress or disability in social, occupational, or other important activities. An expectable or culturally approved response to a common stressor or loss, such as the death of a loved one, is not a mental disorder.
  • Socially deviant behavior (e.g., political, religious, or sexual) and conflicts that are primarily between the individual and society are not mental disorders unless the deviance or conflict results from dysfunction in the individual, as described above.

DSM

Preface:

  • The DSM is a classification of mental disorders with associated criteria which facilitate diagnosis.
  • It is the standard reference for clinical practice in mental health.
  • Please also be familiar with the World Health Organization's International Classification of Diseases (ICD) (ICD-10 Codes)

Introduction:

  • The science of diagnosis and mental disorders continues to evolve.
  • Too rigid a categorical system does not capture clinical experience or important scientific observations.
  • The validity of diagnosis is important (that is... the degree to which diagnostic criteria reflect the comprehensive manifestation of an underlying psychopathological disorder).
  • Clinical training and experience is needed to use the DSM for determining a diagnosis.
  • The diagnostic criteria identify symptoms, behaviors, cognitive functions, personality traits, physical signs, and durations that require clinical expertise to differentiate from normal life variation and responses to stress.
  • Reliable diagnoses are essential for:
    • Guiding treatment
    • Identifying prevalence rates
    • Identifying clients for clinical research
    • Documenting public health information

Advantages and Disadvantages to the Diagnosis

Advantages

  • Standardizes billing and coding
  • Standardizes diagnoses and treatment
  • Guides research
  • Guides treatment

Disadvantages

  • Oversimplifies human behavior
  • Increases risk of misdiagnosis or over-diagnosis
  • Provides labels, which can be stigmatizing

Assessment Measures

  • Growing scientific evidence favors dimensional diagnosis of mental disorders.
  • Takes into account individual subjective report of symptom experience and clinician interpretation.
  • Integrates both objective and subjective data to enhance the accuracy of the diagnostic process.
  • Integrates measures administered at initial interview and over time to track symptom status and response to treatment.

Dimensional vs. Categorical

  • A categorical approach to assessment relies on diagnostic criteria to determine the presence or absence of disruptive or other abnormal behaviors, whereas a dimensional approach places such behaviors on a continuum of frequency and/or severity.

Steps

  1. Ask Categorical (Yes/No)
  2. Ask Frequency (How often?)
  3. Ask Intensity/Severity (Rating: mild, moderate, severe)
  4. Ask Duration (How long does it last?)

Severity Measures

  • Disorder-specific and correspond closely to the diagnostic criteria.
  • Some are self-completed by the individual, couple, or family.
    • Interpreted by clinician
    • Shared back with the individual, couple, or family
  • Others require a clinician to complete

WHODAS

  • Categories:
    • Cognition (understanding and communicating)
    • Mobility (moving and getting around)
    • Self-Care (hygiene, dressing, eating, and staying alone)
    • Getting Along (interacting with other people)
    • Life Activities (domestic responsibilities, leisure, work and school)
    • Participation (joining in community activities)
  • A generic assessment instrument for health and disability
  • Used across all diseases, including mental, neurological, and addictive disorders
  • Short, simple and easy to administer (5 to 20 minutes)
  • Applicable in both clinical and general population settings
  • A tool to produce standardized disability levels and profiles
  • Applicable across cultures, in all adult populations
  • Directly linked at the level of the concepts to the International Classification of Functioning, Disability, and Health (ICF)

Diagnostic Assessment

  • An assessment of the member's needs based on:
    • Baseline measurements
    • Symptoms
    • Behavior
    • Skills
    • Abilities
    • Resources
    • Vulnerabilities
    • Safety
  • Assessment methods and use of standardized assessment tools.
  • Clinical summary, including recommendations and prioritization of needed mental health or other services.
  • Involvement of the member and member's family in the assessment and his or her preferences or referrals to services.
  • Sufficient data to support findings on all axes of the current Diagnostic and Statistic Manual (DSM), and any differential diagnosis.

Cultural Formation

  • Understanding the cultural context of illness is essential for effective diagnostic assessment and treatment.
  • Culture:
    • Systems of knowledge, concepts, rules, and practices that are learned and transmitted across generations, including language, religion, spirituality, family structure, lifecycle stages, ceremonial rituals, customs, legal systems.
  • Race:
    • Culturally constructed category of identity that divides humanity into groups based on superficial physical traits attributed to biological characteristics
  • University Resources/Couple and Family Therapy/Keywords/Ethnicity:
    • Culturally constructed group identity used to define people and communities. Rooted in a common history, geography, language, religion, or shared characteristics.

Cultural Identity:

  • Describe the client's racial, ethnic, and cultural group (s) that might influence relationships with others, access to resources, and developmental and current challenges. Involvement with host culture and major culture. Language abilities, access to care, social integration, need for interpreter. Religious affiliation, socioeconomic background, family of origin, migrant status, gender identity, sexual orientation, etc.

Cultural Conceptualizations of Distress

  • Describe the cultural constructs that influence how the individual experiences, understands, and communicates symptoms. Cultural syndromes, idioms of distress, and cultural explanations for symptoms. Use of professional, tradition, alternative, and complementary sources of care.

Psychosocial Stressors

  • Identify key stressors and supports in the social environment. Local and distant. Role of religion, family, and social networks (e.g., friends, neighbors, coworkers) in providing emotional, instrumental, and informational support. Levels of functioning, disability and resilience.

Cultural Features of the Relationship between Client and Clinician

  • Identify differences in culture, language, social status, gender identity, sexual orientation, education, etc that may cause difficulties in communication, diagnosis, and treatment. Experiences of racism and discrimination. Problems in eliciting symptoms or misunderstanding the cultural and clinical significance of symptoms.

Why Are Cultural Concepts Important to Diagnosis?

  • To avoid misdiagnosis
  • To obtain useful/relevant clinical information
  • To build rapport and engagement
    • Speak the language of the client
  • To improve treatment efficacy
  • To guide clinical research
  • To clarify cultural epidemiology
