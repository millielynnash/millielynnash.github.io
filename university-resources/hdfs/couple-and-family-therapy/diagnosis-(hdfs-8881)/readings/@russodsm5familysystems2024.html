Chapter 2: Systemic Levels in Neurodevelopmental Disorders

  • Neurodevelopmental disorders can be defined as a series of complex traits that emerge as a result of genetic determinants interacting with various poorly understood environmental components resulting in a diverse clinical presentation.
  • The onset and diagnosis of neurodevelopmental disorders occurs in childhood.
    • These disorders rarely experience a remission or relapse and are considered to be a stable set of symptoms over time, and thus neurodevelopmental disorders appear throughout the individual's life span.

Chapter 4: Relational Functioning: Understanding Bipolar and Related Disorders

DSM-5 and Family Systems

  • Bipolar disorder affects and is affected by the family system and successful treatment is enhanced when family embers understand the course and treatment of the illness, respond in supportive ways, and stay patient and empathetic within the context of constructive communication and problem-solving.

Overview of Bipolar Disorder

  • Bipolar Disorder takes a significant toll on personal efficacy and self-worth and is often bewildering to family members as they try to manage the symptoms of the illness and support their affected loved ones in the midst of frustrating and competing interpersonal, social, and logistical factors. The emergence of Bipolar Disorder as manifested by intense periods of mania, often followed by periods of deep depression, disrupts peace, tests relationships, clouds future dreams and aspirations, and often destroys marriage and family relationships.

Primary Features of Bipolar Disorder

  • The presence of mania (As manifest in Bipolar I Disorder) or hypomania (As manifested in Bipolar II Disorder), which is a state of intense, energized mood, cognition, and behavior, resulting in an increased capacity for creativity and clarity of thought (Rational and irrational), rapid ideas and racing thoughts, sometimes irritability and impatience with others, reduced need for sleep, distractibility, and objective-focused activity, and/or energy present most of the time during the episode.
    • With mania and hypomania, there is a decreased need for sleep, increased sexual desire, and a need for stimulation, which can involve risk-taking behaviors resulting in painful and/or harmful consequences. The subjective experience of mania is also characterized as paradoxical where there is a lightning-fast epiphany beyond the vision of all others, which produces a feeling of grandiosity and power. Many who experience mania describe feeling frustrated at their limited capacity to experience and carry forward these creative insights, like drinking water from a fire hose.
    • Mania, but not hypomania, is also sometimes accompanied by delusional thinking and psychosis, which mimic symptoms of Schizophrenia. Hypomania contains similar symptoms manifest in mania, but of a lesser duration and lesser intensity, and is a characteristic of Bipolar II Disorder.
  • The most frightening time for those experiencing mania is when it begins to dissipate because for many it signals the descent into depression. Another complex manifestation of depression is Bipolar With Mixed Episodes. These depressive episodes are characterized by deep sadness, hopelessness, feelings of emptiness, fatigue, lack of energy, loss of appetite, listlessness, dysphoria, and apathy for a minimum of 2 weeks.
    • Depressive episodes are often attended by shame and embarrassment for the consequence of manic actions, a lack of pleasure in activities once enjoyable, the need to isolate from others, a sense of worthlessness, guilt, stomach aches and pains, troubled sleep, and thoughts of death. Whereas in mania, the risk of death is typically from thrill seeking, in depressed individuals, the primary risk of death is suicide.
    • Many who suffer from bipolar depression describe the subjective feeling of depression as intense as is the feeling of mania. Mania is characterized as the 'highest of highs' whereas depression is characterized by the 'lowest of lows.'
    • Suicide is a very real concern for the bipolar client, family, and friends, and those treating bipolar persons. In the course of antidepressant of bipolar disorder, or depression, the first symptoms that respond to treatment are a lack of energy (i.e., people become more energized) and sleep (i.e., people tend to sleep between when on antidepressants), and one of the last symptoms to clear up is mood. This makes people with Bipolar Disorder especially susceptible to suicide because they actually have the energy to kill themselves. States of high energy along with depression increase suicidal risk.
  • The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM) categorizes Bipolar and Related Disorders as:
    • Bipolar I Disorder:
      • Characterized by a manic episode lasting 7 days or longer
    • Bipolar II Disorder:
      • Characterized by a hypomanic episode lasting no more than 4 days.
    • Cyclothymic Disorder:
      • Characterized by hypomanic symptoms that would not meet the full diagnostic criteria for a hypomanic 'episode' and depressive symptoms that would not meet the full symptom criteria for a depressive 'episode.'
        • This includes a lower level of intensity of both extremes, a lower level of frequency with symptoms occurring for 4 days compared to 7 days for a manic episode, and a subthreshold of symptoms necessary to meet the disorder.
    • Substance/Medication-Induced Bipolar Disorder:
      • Characterized by symptoms of mania precipitated by a particular medication or substance (e.g., illicit or prescribed) and Bipolar Disorder Related to a Medical Condition, in which symptoms of mania can be precipitated by a health-related condition.
  • Mania becomes the primary symptom distinguishing Bipolar Disorder from other depressive disorders and causes significant 'impairment in social and occupational functioning,' as well as a disruption to family relationships and functioning, which is the general standard that distinguishes problematic behavior from a mental disorder.

Associated Features of Bipolar Disorder

  • Prevalence rates for all Bipolar Disorders for adults are 3.9% over the lifetime with 2.6% of the adult population meeting diagnostic criteria in a given year and 2.5% of adolescents meeting them over the lifetime with 2.2% of the population meeting criteria in a given year. Approximately 1.7% of adolescents report having mania along during the lifetime, and 1.3% report having manic symptoms in a given year.
  • 50% to 67% of adults reported the onset of Bipolar Disorder before the age of 18 and 15% to 28% reported experiencing symptoms before 13 years.
  • There is some evidence suggesting that women experience Bipolar II Disorder at a higher rate. Women also tend to experience higher 'rapid cycling' (i.e., four or more mood episodes within a single year) and more 'mixed states' (i.e., mixed symptoms or 'features' of mania and depression within a single episode) than men.
  • Bipolar Disorder with Mixed Features is especially difficult to treat with psychotropic medication because of the nature of simultaneous manic and depressive symptoms.
  • There is a hereditary component in Bipolar Disorder, 'A family history of Bipolar Disorder is one of the strongest and most consistent risk factors for Bipolar Disorders.'
  • Over 90% of those experiencing a manic episode eventually have recurring episodes and roughly 60% of individuals having a manic episode also have a depressive episode, so the likelihood is high that mania persists within individuals with multiple episodes, including episodes of depression. The persistence of symptoms within the interepisode phases of the illness indicates that, for many, there is never a break within this chronic condition.
  • Depressive episodes in the bipolar cycle are much more difficult to treat and that individuals spend much more time in depressive states than manic ones; research suggests as much as three times as many weeks during the year with depressive symptoms versus symptoms of mania. Bipolar Disorder is a chronic, often debilitating condition that requires a multifaceted treatment approach to manage effectivity.
  • For both adults and adolescents, symptoms of Bipolar Disorder can be comorbid with a wide variety of other disorders including anxiety disorders (including specific phobias), attention deficit hyperactivity disorder, personality disorders, substance use disorders, serious and often untreated medical conditions, and, for adolescents, disruptive and/or conduct disorder, and 'disorders with prominent irritability.'

Treating Bipolar Disorder Symptoms

  • The primary treatment of Bipolar Disorder is psychopharmacologic, and typically starts with lithium bicarbonate and other mood stabilizers, antidepressants, and atypical antipsychotics and anxiolytics that are often prescribed in the acute phase of the illness.
    • Because individuals with Bipolar Disorder often have sleep disturbance, sleep medications are often prescribed to regulate circadian rhythms. Good sleep hygiene is one of the easiest and best ways to help prevent triggering mania. The use of electroconvulsive therapy is an option for entrenched, severe, and persistent depressive symptoms.
  • A common and frustrating feature of medication treatment for bipolar individuals is medication compliance. Individuals affected with Bipolar Disorder often find the effects of drug therapy as blunting the euphoric effects of the experience of mania, and develop abhorrence to the side effects of the medication. They are also often resistant to the idea that they will be taking medications for the rest of their lives in order to maintain a balances mood, to help them make reasoned choices, maintain their interpersonal relationships, and support systems, and lower the incidence of additional and severe episodes.
  • Re-hospitalizations for bipolar patients was lowest in the multifamily group, and the researchers pointed to the potential that patients may have learned to see themselves as less of a burden on family members, to experience more support from others (especially their own families), to feel less isolated, and to feel enhanced hope in learning how to cope more successfully with their condition.
    • Family and social relationships are central to the affected person's ability to live successfully with this condition.

Family Systems and Bipolar Disorder

  • Individuals experiencing manic and depressive symptoms reflect the bewilderment, anger, hopelessness, and existential despair often felt in family members who struggle to support their impacted loved one while at the same time trying to make sense of what is difficult to accept: that their loved one has a chronic condition that will persist throughout their life and, as family members, they too travel a parallel path.

Practical and Emotional Consequences

  • The tangible consequences include being introduced into the legal system with adolescent or adult family members who may act out during a manic episode and violate the law.
  • Other potential, practical consequences include unemployment resulting from inconsistent or poor job performance; impulsive and poor financial decisions resulting in loss of income, savings, or retirement resources; loss of social status within the community; interfacing with mental health agencies and social services and a wide variety of mental health personnel; and often being marginalized by a mental health system that does not recognize the legitimate burden family members carry and the inability to access important treatment information on behalf of their affected loved one because of right to privacy and confidentiality.
  • Sometimes partners of spouses, sons and daughters of parents, and parents of children may cut off and/or leave or divorce the impacted relative because of the stress attendant to this condition. These life disruptions cast a pall on other family and social activities and center the collective energy and family resources on addressing the problem. It is difficult to maintain hope in the future under these conditions.

Intangible Consequences

  • The intangible or emotional fallout for the family is a real and felt experience and centers on working to accept the disorder and attendant consequences.
    • When an affected individual is in a manic state, family members may initially find the observed behavior as interesting or exciting, but soon turn to distress and anxiety as their loved one becomes more impulsive, unpredictable, and erratic; the family member may leave and not return home for days. If the manic episode is new to family members, there is often confusion about how to respond and about the immediate need for perspective. Often, family members seek to discern differences between the illness and the characteristics of the affected loved one in an attempt to make sense of their behaviors. If the affected individual is a parent, children and other family members need reassurance; if a child, parents may seek to discipline or reason with their loved one with little success.
    • Some become violent, destructive, and/or manifest delusional and psychotic symptoms that are frightening, and family members are faced with having to protect and be protected from the affected family member.
    • If the manic episode is repeated as part of the course of the illness, family members become retraumatized to the episode and may seek equilibrium through a variety of behaviors, ranging from acting out themselves (mostly in children) to calm responding to the situation and following a predetermined plan. Often family members try to reason with the affected member with little success. During episodes of depression, family members seek to motivate and instill hope within their family member and often face their own sense of hopelessness and despair when attempts to support, encourage, and offer solutions are rejected or treated with indifference.
  • "One's attention is focused on the wellbeing of the person with Bipolar Disorder in such a way that one's own needs take second place. Being closely related to someone with Bipolar Disorder also means simultaneously foreseeing and following the often sudden and unexpected changes of the person with Bipolar Disorder."

Relational and Cultural Features

  • The DSM system is based on a medical model and is inherently limited in validating treatment approaches other than medical.
    • The Cultural Formulation Interview provides a framework to assist clinicians in assessing the cultural identity of the affected individual, perceptions of the distress (including how the cultural group of the client would view the problem), psychosocial stressors and 'cultural features of vulnerability and resilience,' and cultural aspects of both the client and the clinician that might impact treatment. Viewing culture therapeutically includes a perceptual expansion of cultural groups to include age, gender, sexual orientation, religion, language, occupation, military service, social networks, substance use, migration status, geographical region, relationship status (i.e., married or divorced), and not simply race and ethnicity.
  • Though Bipolar Disorder presents with similar incidence in men and women across races and cultures, there is some observable difference in the course and progression of the disorder. This is due primarily to differences in initial diagnosis, access to treatment, and perceptions of stigma associated with mental health.
    • Comparing individuals with Bipolar Disorder from African ancestry with those with European ancestry, a trend was observed in the misdiagnosis of Bipolar Disorder, most often as Schizophrenia.
      • It is possible that behaviors observed in minority patients during a manic phase are more commonly misconstrued as psychotic rather than manic. Another possibility for the misdiagnosis of schizophrenia in minority patients with Bipolar Disorder may be related to fears associated with seeking mental health services and an inherent mistrust of a system that has historically oppressed and marginalized them. Presenting as extremely guarded, paranoid, or having a blunt affect - all common symptoms of schizophrenia - may be rational expressions resulting from their previous experience of racism.
  • Minority groups are disproportionately affected by challenges to healthcare access and experience greater stigmatization for seeking mental health services.
    • Some minority groups may be disproportionately affected by environmental factors and social inequities, which may directly and indirectly contribute to both the development and progression of Bipolar Disorder.
  • It is advocated that clinicians use empathy and authenticity when working with Bipolar Disorder clients, and to not be afraid of conflict within the process of making therapeutic contact.
    • "The meeting and initial work with manic persons is often complicated. When we meet with them, we collide with them. We have to confirm the collision, not deny it."
  • Demonstrating empathy for paranoid and grandiose thinking is not only critical for a successful therapeutic alliance, but gives the clinician a more accurate assessment as to the extent of the client's delusions, grandiosity, and paranoia.
  • Counselors modeling for clients a sense of humor and often a playful attitude in their interactions together help clients learn to more realistically view themselves and reality-test relationships. Humor, appropriately and skillfully used, also helps reinforce for clients that their counselors do not view them as a culmination of their manic and depressive episodes, but as people who can be related to, which is empowering to them.
    • Bipolar-affected clients often know that they negatively impact loved ones and others, and, as a result, feel deep shame and guilt for what is often irritating and offensive behavior, having a place that is safe for them to explore different ways of seeing themselves is important.
  • The job of the clinician:
    • First, to seek to existentially be with manic patients and then, to counterprojectively give perspective to those patients about their manic worldview, without completely denying it.
      • This can lead to a healthy therapeutic alliance, which itself has a mood-stabilizing effect. Along with mood-stabilizing medications, this alliance can lead patients towards full recovery.
    • The therapeutic relationship provides a place where the bipolar client can experience acceptance and the family can receive real and targeted support.
    • It is important therapists show patience and adaptibility in working with patients with Bipolar Disorder and their families.
      • Self-esteem, belief in one's own efficacy, and engendering hope for the present and future functioning is vital for those suffering from Bipolar Disorder. This awareness on the part of the therapist guides treatment and ameliorates the dysfunctional beliefs, the black-and-white thinking, and blunts the corrosive effects of mania and depression.
    • Counselors should directly involve the family in the treatment of the identified family member with Bipolar Disorder. Family members can often provide much more accurate and rich perspectives regarding what happens when the family member with Bipolar Disorder is manic and/or severely depressed.
    • Counselors can help clients with Bipolar Disorder to develop 'advanced directives' that may include signed consent from clients about how the illness is to be addressed or treated, including being treated against their will if they are in a full-blown manic or depressed episode.
      • We want clients with Bipolar Disorder to be thinking about the course of their illness and making decisions about treatment when they are balanced, lucid, and making rational judgements rather than when they are manic or depressed. This can best be accomplished within the context of supportive family members who can express understanding and empathy, while at the same time express clearly the consequences of clients' manic and depressive behaviors and advocate strongly for responsive and responsible actions to be take.

Vulnerability-Stress Model

  • Genetic, biological, and environmental factors do not appear independent of each other but all 'interact in bringing about episodes of mood disorder or in protecting against their occurrence.' Understanding how these factors mutually impact and are impacted by each other over the course of the disorder can help manage episode occurrence and develop resilience in affected individuals and their families.
  • Genetic and biological vulnerability includes the manifestation of the disorder in members of an affected person's family (i.e., who in the family has or has had the bipolar or related disorders), brain functioning (i.e., to what level may there be structural or brain abnormalities and quality of functioning and dysfunction in neurotransmitters), and other biological factors (e.g., chemical imbalances, changes in hormonal and immune system functioninig, sleep regulation). When vulnerability is high in these areas, the amount of environmental stress needed to trigger a manic or deppressive episode tends to be low.
    • Those with high vulnerability have a low tolerance for and little protection form events that others without the disorder might manage quite well. Those with low vulnerability to genetic, neurologic, and biological factors would likely not be vulnerable to mood episodes unless there were high social stressors at play.
    • Low environmental stress acts as a protective factor from the occurrence and reoccurrence of mood episodes. Because the nature of genetic or biological vulnerability may be hard to detect and determine, managing environmental stress becomes a high priority in treating and managing bipolar episodes and their severity.
  • Psychosocial Provoking Agents:
    • Largely impact vulnerability and the manifestation of manic and depressive states. Because Bipolar Disorder impacts individuals and families in often unpredictable and disorientating ways, and because of the consequences within the family, family members often assume patterns of responsing that range form being long-suffering, helpful, and supportive to being critical, blaming, and something uncooperative. These reactions contribute significantly to the emotional climate within the home and impact the perpetuation of continued episodes.
    • 'Cause and Effect relationships between mood disorder symptoms and family environmental factors are bidirectional: Families are affected by Bipolar Disorder as much as they affect it.'
  • The Vulnerability-Stress Model was developed from research on family interactions with patients with schizophrenia.
  • Emotional Expression (EE) is a measure of emotional attitudes among relatives of a psychiatric patient. It is defined as critical, hostile, or emotional overinvolvement statements and attitudes family members communicate overtly or indirectly to the member (s) affected by the disorder.
    • High EE Level within Family (Z 63.8) captures this dynamic.
    • Individuals with Bipolar Disorder are two or three times more likely to deteriorate into a manic or deppressive episode in families where high EE exists and experience prolonged and more severe manic or depressive states.
    • High EE causes stress that contributes significantly to the perpetuation of mood episodes.
    • EE develops and is perpetuated between the affected child and their parents. The child reinforces high EE reactions within parents, which impacts all relationship functioning. Children who exhibit behaviors and attitudes consistent with a low tolerance for stress, unstable mood, elevated anxiety, irritability, and problematic cognitive functionining largely meet the criteria for high biological and generic bulnerability.
    • Generational influences within high EE families can significantly impact the vulnerabilities of family members who may be predisposed to Bipolar Disorder and bulnerable to criticism toward their condition.
  • The Vulnerability-Stress Model helps clinicians assess and further understand not only the relationships between genetic, biological, and socioenvironmental factors, but helps focus attention on the EE and the quality of interpersonal reactions in the home that could be either provoking manic or depressive mood states or protecting affected individuals from their recurrence.

Family Systems Assessments

  • Assessments for Bipolar Disorder are characterized as the individual assessment and diagnosis of the disorder while family assessment addresses essential aspects of the family environment including the quality of family interactions.

Individual Assessment

  • A thorough biopsychosocial assessment should be conducted on the client upon the initial intake (or shortly thereafter) to capture a comprehensive context in which the presenting problems occur.
  • All biopsychosocial assessments should include:
    • Mental health history
    • Medical family history
    • Personal history that includes a developmental timeline
    • Conducting a mental status exam (i.e., appearance, behaviors/psychomotor activity, attitudes affect and mood, intelligence, and reliability, judgement, and insight)
    • Legal problems
    • Substance use
    • Spirituality/religion
    • Cultural/ethnic background
    • Suicidal ideation (including ideation, plan, attempts)
    • Financial concerns and socioeconomic status
    • Resources and Resiliency factors
    • Utilizing the Level I and Level II Cross-Cutting Symptom Measures associated with the DSM-5 can also provide a targeted assessment of symptoms, including the Altman Self-Rating Mania Scale.
      • Another helpful measure that can detect both threshold and subthreshold levels of mania is the Composite International Diagnostic Interview Bipolar Disorder Screening Scale.
  • The goal of individual assessment is to obtain a differential diagnosis; it is important to:
    • (a) administer formal assessments ethically and according to the protocols specified in specific instruments including being trained in the administration and interpretation of data
    • (b) gather and make sense of the data and share the results with clients in a manner that is comprehensible, clearly explaining the eliminations of assessment and assessment results.
    • (c) use the data to confirm existing clinical impressions and/or to generate new clinical directions.
  • Consultation with other professionals, including those of a treatment team is essential.
  • The family can provide important context for the counselor in diagnosis and treatment, which leads to the process of family assessment.

Family Assessment

  • As part of the protocol of a comprehensive family assessment with all willing family members present, the counselor have the family (or a family member and the affected bipolar member) engage in conversation regarding their relationships or how they have attempted to solve problems they experience for 5 to 10 minutes and to observe and note the number of EE statements that are generated within the interaction and what the statements are.
    • This gives important data about the level of EE, but sensitizes counselors to what family members value, what they may be struggling with, and the process and context of their conversations.
    • Developing sensitivity to the quality and types of interactions family members have can give counselors useful information in designing treatment, including the Genogram, which can be used to develop an understanding of where Bipolar Disorder (and other mental health problems) has occurred within the family.
      • A genogram is a multigenerational family tree that symbolically represents different dynamics related to family and individual functioning that is psychologic, cultural, and interpersonal in nature.
  • The Mood Disorder Burden Index was developed to assess caregiver perceptions and experiences in three core domains: patient mood symptoms, caregiver's worries about the future, and caregivers' interpersonal difficulties with the patient.
  • The Quality of Life in Bipolar Disorder Scale:
    • A self-administered instrument designed as an alternative to psychiatric measures that fail to assess for treatment outcome and recovery and well-being for those afflicted with Bipolar Disorder. It can give counselors insight into how the individual with Bipolar Disorder has been impacted by the disorder and, indirectly, how the family has been impacted.
  • Counselors should always be mindful of being client centered, empathically inquisitive, and validating of the subjective experience of clients and their families as information is gathered during the assessment process. Assessment is a continuous process of measuring symptom reduction, improvement in family communication and problem-solving, acceptance, and self-efficacy living with Bipolar Disorder.

Family Systems Interventions

  • Pharmacological intervention with an integration of individual psychotherapy is more effective than either protocol alone.
  • Goals for treatment have focused on symptom reduction and prevention of future episodes.
  • Psychosocial stressors can directly contribute to, or even catalyze, the occurrence of a bipolar episode. The occurrence of an episode can aggravate psychosocial stressors.
  • Broader systemic influences including systemic racism, poverty, a global health crisis can all put a strain on an individual's ability to regulate emotion, something that is particularly difficult for individuals with Bipolar Disorder.
    • A cycle can develop of constant challenge and crisis, which may self-perpetuate without the disruption of systemic treatment.
      • Treatment of Bipolar Disorder should take an integrative approach to best serve the client's needs which invariably includes family systems work. Bipolar Disorder actually comprises a group of heterogeneous disorders, with significant variations in etiology, manifestation, and severity.
  • The questions becomes not whether family systems treatment is or is not effective for Bipolar Disorder, but how and when it can be applied and adapted according to the needs of the client and family.

Family-Focused Treatment for Bipolar Disorder

  • FFT is an integrative approach to treatment of Bipolar Disorder, which combines psychoeducation and family therapy. It has been shown to enhance family cohesion and reduce family conflict.
  • FFT begins with a thorough assessment of individual symptomology and family systems dynamics, and proceeds through a structured and preset treatment, with specific goals throughout the duration.
    • Important elements of assessment:
      • Gather as much information during intake as possible.
      • Identify family conflicts that may be contributing to the stress across the system, which in turn can exacerbate Bipolar Disorder symptomology in the identified client.
      • Determine the presence and severity of other environmental stressors - such as low socioeconomic status, experiences of oppression and marginalization, or experiences of extreme stress or trauma - which can impact family members individually as well as the relationships across the system.
      • Understand the strengths and protective factors across the system that can support the emotional regulation of the individual with Bipolar Disorder, as well as the overall health of the family system.
        • This information informs both the psychoeducation and therapy components of treatment, as it guides both specific goals and interventions.
      • Understand the clients' cultural beliefs, practices, and influences on the family system. This is important to not only to provide a context to the experiences and dynamics of the system, but also to provide the counselor with a perspective on how FFT may need to be adapted somewhat to maximize efficacy.
      • Determine appropriateness of the type of intervention.
        • The extremes of bipolar episodes cause significant conflict in family systems, and may make some or all members unwilling to participate. Not all individuals are appropriate for the FFT protocol. Persons who are in acute phases of Bipolar Disorder, have developmental disabilities or other biological conditions that impact cognition, refuse to adhere to medication protocols or have a comorbid active substance use disorder are among those for whom FFT is not recommended.
  • FFT is an effective treatment for Bipolar Disorder and its use can significantly impact relapse of the disorder.
  • Higher rates of anxiety, depression, and general distress were found in caregivers of those diagnosed with Bipolar Disorder.
    • Family-based interventions improve illness outcome and caregiver well-being. FFT has been found to have a positive impact on the mental health of caregivers, even when the diagnosed family member is not participating in the treatment.
  • The course of treatment of FFT for Bipolar Disorder is fairly structured and is based on three treatment stages: (a) psychoeducation, (b) communication enhancement training (CET), and (c) problem solving.
    • Treatment using FFT usually lasts approximately 9 months, and is structured around 21 sessions that generally generally begin following an acute phase of Bipolar Disorder. Clinical sessions are conducted weekly for the first 3 months, followed by a step-down to bi-weekly, then once per month. The philosophy of FFT is that Bipolar Disorder is experienced by the family, not just by the diagnosed client.
      • As Bipolar Disorder episodes are viewed as 'disaster' experiences shared by the family system, which in turn cause disequilibrium.
    • The treatment is reestablishing balance and harmony to the system, which in turn supports the health and wellness of the diagnosed client.

General Goals

  • Counseling goals are identified early in treatment and are informed from intake and assessment sessions.
  • FFT for Bipolar Disorder begins with 6 general goals that outline and guide the overall process:
    1. Integrate the experiences associated with episodes of Bipolar Disorder.
      • The focus of this goal is to understanding the diagnostic features of Bipolar Disorder as well as the experience of it from the perspective of the diagnosed individual. It also helps family members accept both the severity and significance of the diagnosis.
    2. Accept the notion of a vulnerability to future episodes.
      • The focus of this goal is in the recognition that Bipolar Disorder is a chronic, persistent, lifelong diagnosis that requires continual support. The diagnosed individual - as well as the family - may believe that the condition can be 'cured' and thus have an unrealistic expectation of treatment. The periods of stability that occur between episodes can often contribute to this incorrect assumption.
    3. Accept a dependency on mood-stabilizing medication for symptom control.
      • Consistent use of mood-stabilizing medication can minimize the intensity of Bipolar Disorder episodes and prevent future occurences.
      • The undesirable side effects of many medications can make the client less willing to maintain this portion of the treatment protocol.
    4. Distinguish between the client's personality and the Bipolar Disorder.
      • Over time, the diagnosed individual may be viewed by family members solely through the lens of the disorder. This can lead to inaccurate assumptions about the meaning and importance of behaviors.
    5. Recognize and learn to cope with stressful life events that trigger recurrences of Bipolar Disorder.
      • FFT is based on the Vulnerability-Stress Model. This model includes both an understanding of preexisting biological conditions that create vulnerability and the environmental stimuli that trigger or catalyze reactions based on this vulnerability.
        • While biological components cannot be altered, clients and families should understand how psychosocial stressors such as conflictual relationships, major life changes, and severe emotional stressors can impact the vulnerability of an individual with Bipolar Disorder.
    6. Reestablish functional relationships after the episode.
      • The key features of this goal are support and connection. The before, during, and after phases of Bipolar Disorder episodes can be aggravated by the conflicts that arise across family systems in response to the situation.
      • A goal for treatment is enhancing communication patterns within the family system and providing families with coping skills.

Psychoeducation

  • In the initial stage of treatment using the FFT model, the primary goal is psychoeducation. This stage is broken down into phases with focus on different informational needs.
    • In the beginning, the clinician imparts a great deal of vital information to the family system. It is important for this to be done in a warm, interactive manner. Clinicians using FFT must be aware of the potential for this stage to devolve into a solely instructional experience and take intentional, conscious steps to avoid this.
      • Continually checking in with family members and encouraging discussion about the material can minimize this risk and enhance rapport with family members.
  • In the beginning of the psychoeducation stage, the clinician should provide an overview of the FFT protocol, reasons for family participation, and the role of the clinician in the treatment process. Treatment goals should also be addressed, both the general FFT-based goals and the specific goals for the family system. This is also a good time to provide an overview of Bipolar symptomology, as well as prodromal symptoms and environmental triggers.
    • The clinician should take the time to explore the unique experiences and perspectives of each family member. Often it is the associated meaning for the system members that impacts the manner in which they cope with and react to a Bipolar Disorder episode.
  • Discussions around environmental triggers are an important way for families to better understand the blind spots they may have regarding the diagnosed family member's vulnerabilities. Events that may be considered minor, benign, or otherwise simply normal life events to some family members may prove to be substantially more stressful for the family member with Bipolar Disorder.
    • A lack of empathy for the different experiences of the family member with Bipolar Disorder may also create conflict, as the affected family member may feel unsupported and misunderstood.

Chapter 6: Applying Systems to Anxiety Disorders

DSM-5-TR and Family Systems

  • The DSM-5-TR anxiety disorders share the key features of excessive or paralyzing fear and/or intense anxiety with associated disturbances in behavior.
  • More adults were reporting anxiety-related symptoms at the beginning of the pandemic while more youths reported anxiety-related symptoms by the end of 2020.
    • Various factors such as fear for the health and welfare of themselves and their families, inconsistent daily routines including school closures, and mounting tension in their homes have led to increased reports of anxiety in children.
    • Despite experiencing anxiety-related symptoms to be diagnosed the individual must exhibit symptoms of significant distress or impairment across multiple domains in functioning (e.g., familial, social, academic, or occupational).

Diagnostic Features of Anxiety Disorders

  • Anxiety disorders share the key features of intense fear or anxiety, avoidance of feared stimulus, and can cause life interferences, emotional distress, and impaired functioning.
  • The distinguishing features differentiating the DSM-5-TR anxiety disorders are the focus of each anxiety disorder or the core feared stimuli, and the focus of the fear informing the other individual domains of focus in the DSM. These domains include:
    • Cognitive Response
      • The cognitive response for the anxiety disorders is worry. Individuals with anxiety tend to exhibit two cognitive errors:
        • Overestimating the probability of a negative event (i.e., thinking a negative event or outcome is much more likely to happen than objective reality)
        • Catastrophizing (i.e., thinking the worst-case scenario will happen)
    • Physical Response
      • The physical component is the physical response to fear such as muscle tension, difficulty in breathing, dizziness, and other symptoms associated with activation of the sympathetic nervous system.
    • Behavioral Response
      • The behavioral response to anxiety is the active avoidance of the feared stimuli.
  • In SAD (social phobia), the core feature is the fear of negative evaluation by others. It follows that across the other individual domains the core feature of fear, anxiety, or both would be consistent. An individual meeting criterion for SAD would fear being evaluated by others (core focus of their anxiety; cognitive component). The individual may sweat, tremble, or experience heart palpitations (physical component) as well as avoid giving speeches or talking in class (behavioral component).

Associated Features of Anxiety Disorders

  • Anxiety disorders represent one of the highest prevalent disorders within children and adults. It is estimated that 15%-20% of children and adolescents are affected by an anxiety disorder. An estimated 40 million adults in the US have been diagnosed with an anxiety disorder.
  • Lifetime Prevalence is the percentage of individuals who meet criteria for a disorder during their lifetime.
  • Point Prevalence is the percentage of individuals who meet criteria for a disorder during a specific period of time.

Impact of Anxiety Disorders

  • Anxiety disorders severely impair physical functioning, psychological functioning, social functioning, academic functioning, and the overall quality of life of an individual.
  • Individuals suffering from anxiety disorders are sick more days from work and have lower work productivity.
  • Individuals with anxiety disorders are common patrons of primary care settings, specialty clinics, and emergency room services.
  • Individuals with anxiety disorders have higher rates of relationship distress and divorce.

Risk Factors for Anxiety Disorders

  • A Risk Factor is defined as any attribute, characteristic, or experience that increases the likelihood of developing an anxiety disorder.
    • A risk factor and the root cause of an anxiety disorder may differ greatly; therefore, having a risk factor is not the same as causing an anxiety disorder.
  • Genetics or hereditability increases risk for all of the anxiety disorders. Individuals with first-degree relatives (e.g., parent or sibling) with an anxiety disorder are at an increased risk to develop an anxiety disorder.
    • 30% to 40% of variance in the anxiety disorders is due to genetics. Other risk factors contribute more than half of the variance to the development of anxiety disorders.
  • A strong link between the Temperament style that is of Behavioral Inhibition (BI) and anxiety disorders. Characterized by withdrawal, fearfulness, and avoidance of novel situations or unfamiliar people.
    • Another Temperament style related to risk for developing anxiety disorders is Emotionality.
    • Most temperaments are inherited, and family members are also likely to have similar temperaments. Parents are likely to model and reinforce their child's tendency toward BI or emotionality.
  • Stressful life events and trauma relate to an increased risk of developing an anxiety disorder, such as early parental loss, childhood abuse, previous trauma, and peer victimization.

Individual and System Factors Maintaining Anxiety Disorders

  • Anxiety disorders are often maintained by several individual factors. An individual factor is identified as something that is internal or based on the individual as opposed to a family or societal factor that maintains anxiety disorders.
    • The individual factors and systemic factors influence one another in a reciprocal manner. An individual's behavioral response and cognitions develop within the context of their family.
  • Several behavioral factors have been linked to the maintenance of anxiety disorders. Behavioral Avoidance maintains all of the anxiety disorders.
    • In the short term, behavioral avoidance is a coping strategy that temporarily relieves anxiety; however, long-term behavioral avoidance is a key factor in maintaining all anxiety disorders.
    • Many times the larger system, either the family or school, reinforces behavioral avoidance of feared stimuli.
  • Cognitive factors that maintain anxiety are the thoughts and thinking styles contributing to maintaining an anxiety disorder.
    • Attentional Bias for threat maintains anxiety disorders. Individuals with an anxiety disorder tend to pay more attention to stimuli that appear threatening to them, and they have difficulty noticing anything else other than the perceived threatening stimuli. Individuals with anxiety disorders perceive neutral stimuli as dangerous compared to individuals without an anxiety disorder.
    • A second cognitive factor related to the maintenance of anxiety disorders is Cognitive Avoidance.
    • Attentional bias and cognitive avoidance are internal processes; however, the family system or larger systems influence the internal process of attentional bias and cognitive avoidance. Many parents model attention bias for children.
      • Parent may model cognitive avoidance, teaching their children to respond in the same manner. In adult romantic relationships, a partner may influence the process of cognitive avoidance as well.

Relational and Cultural Factors

  • The majority of research on the etiology and treatment of anxiety disorders focuses on the individual variables or individual therapy.

Relational Factors Associated with Anxiety Disorders

  • Relational factors related to the etiology or maintenance of anxiety disorders:
    • Relationship Quality
      • Lower relationship quality within the couple or family system relates to anxiety disorders.
      • Couple conflict, lower relationship satisfaction, and couple violence all correlate with adult anxiety disorders.
      • Couple distress has been linked to an increased risk in developing an adult anxiety disorder.
      • Conflict between parents consistently relates to childhood anxiety disorders.
      • Individuals within a relationship who experience high attachment anxiety will often question their partner and behave in hypersensitive ways in an effort to avoid rejection which increases relationship distress.
    • Communication
    • Accommodation

Chapter 14: An Intersectional Systems Approach to Counseling Sexual Issues

  • Sexual dysfunction (SD) is typically viewed as biologically based, yet a person's lived sense of sexual wellness is most often situated within relational, interpersonal, and cultural contexts.
  • Sexual health is shaped by the interaction of a person's unique biological, psychologic, and cultural experiences and has significant impacts on mental, emotional, and relational well-being.
  • Earlier editions of the DSM were also critiqued for omitting gendered differences in sexual functioning.
  • Clinical judgement based on biopsychosocial context is recommended in diagnosis, particularly when the client possesses identities that are marginalized socially and within clinical research.

DSM-5-TR and Family Systems

  • Sexual Dysfunctions (SD) in the DSM-5-TR include Delayed Ejaculation, Erectile Disorder, Female Orgasmic Disorder, Female Sexual Interest/Arousal Disorder, Genito-Pelvic Pain/Penetration Disorder (GPPPD), Induc ed Sexual Dysfunction, Male Hypoactive Sexual Desire Disorder, Premature Ejaculation, Substance/Medication-Induced Sexual Dysfunction, Other Specified Sexual Dysfunction, Unspecified Sexual Dysfunction.
  • Clinical judgement is urged in ruling out the following causal factors:
    • Inadequate sexual stimulation
    • Cultural factors that may impact the experience of pleasure
    • Problems better explained by a nonsexual mental disorder, substance use, a medical condition, or by several relational distress, partner violence or other stressors.
  • The DSM-5-TR uses the subtypes of lifelong, acquired, generalized, and situational to designate the onset of the sexual problem. A variety of factors that should be considered during diagnostic assessment include:
    • Partner factors (e.g., partner's sexual problems; partner's health status)
    • Relationship factors (e.g., poor communication; discrepancies in desire for sexual activity)
    • Individual vulnerability factors (e.g., poor body image; history of sexual or emotional abuse), psychiatric comorbidity (e.g., depression, anxiety), or stressors (e.g., job loss, bereavement)
    • Cultural or religious factors (e.g., inhibitions related to prohibitions against sexual activity or pleasure, and attitudes toward sexuality)
    • Medical factors relevant to prognosis, course, or treatment
  • If severe relationship distress, partner violence, or significant stressors better explain the sexual difficulties, then a sexual dysfunction diagnosis is not made, but an appropriate V or Z code for the relationship problem or stressor may be listed.
    • The usefulness of reframing the term dysfunction as a 'sexual concern,' 'issue,' or 'challenge' in the effort to depathologize sexual difficulties and adopt a more normalizing perspective when discussing sexual issues with clients.

New and Emerging Trends

  • Modalities of family/relationship systems therapy (e.g., internal family systems, Imago therapy, and emotionally focused therapy) to support the treatment of sexual issues for individuals and relationships.
  • Sexual issues are most often first encountered and then subsequently treated and resolved within the context of intimate partnerships.

Relational and Cultural Features

  • Sexuality encompasses sex, m gender identities and roles, sexual orientation, eroticism, pleasure, intimacy, and reproduction and is experienced and expressed in thoughts, fantasies, desires, beliefs, attitudes, values, behaviors, practices, roles, and relationships.
  • In the United States, societal sexual norms are often extensions of cisgender, heterosexual, White, patriarchal, Judeo-Christian, and able-bodied assumptions of sexuality.
    • For this reason, many individuals - especially those with historically marginalized identities - develop an internalized sexual worldview that lies in contrast with their own sexual realities. This incongruence can lead to misinformation about one's sexuality, internalized shame, insecurity, and a myriad of other barriers that may negatively impact sexual functioning, sexual pleasure, and sexual communication and negotiation with partners.
  • Culturally or religiously imposed values can shape a person's emotional experience related to sexuality in meaningful ways that can hinder and/or support one's sexual well-being.
  • Intersectionality - which posits that individuals hold multiple, intersecting social and cultural identities, each carrying certain privileges and oppressions given the individual's broader sociocultural and political landscape.
    • Counselors seek to understand the ways in which a client's complex experience of power and oppression shapes their sexual functioning, as well as to explore the client's unique protective and risk factors given their multifaceted identities.

Family Systems Assessments

  • Even in instances when sexual concerns are not the primary reason for seeking counseling, sexual assessment provides the clinician with an important opportunity to assess for possible comorbidity with presenting issues, better understand the client's cultural and relational values, and normalize the presence of sexual discussion amidst the counseling process.

General Sexual Assessment

  • General sexual wellness assessment can be included in initial intake paperwork, as well as in follow-up sessions once therapeutic rapport has been established. Examples include:
    1. How would you currently describe your overall sexual wellness?
    2. What concerns, if any, do you currently have about your sex life or current level of sexual satisfaction?
    3. On a scale of 1 (low) to 10 (high), how would you currently rate your sexual wellness? How did you arrive at that number? What changes would you like to see in that number?
    4. Are there any issues related to your sexual health and wellness that you would like to discuss in counseling?
  • For couples, general sexual wellness assessment can include other items on couple intimacy and sexual wellness. Examples include:
    1. How would you currently rate your sexual intimacy today? How does that compare to other times in your relationship?
    2. If you could have any changes to your current sexual relationship, what would you hope to see?
    3. How do you define physical intimacy in your relationship? How important is physical intimacy to your overall relationship health?
    4. How might your sexual relationships be related to your primary reasons for seeking couples counseling at this time?
  • Counselors can help clients with initial discomfort by emphasizing:
    1. Sexuality is a normal and natural element of human existence
    2. Sexual health often has important implications for clients' mental health and may assist the counselor in providing holistic and informed mental healthcare
    3. Clients are free to answer only those questions that they feel comfortable answering, and they are also welcome to respond at a later time.

Specialized Sexual Assessment

  • Following an intersystemic framework, counselors can approach sexual assessment systematically and contextually.
    • An intersectional systemic approach, 'balances attention to the individual, couple, and family system, as well as the larger systems in which we live.'
    • This framework outlines the five domains impacting client sexual wellness:
      1. Individual-biological/medical
      2. Individual-psychologic
      3. Dyadic relationship or couple dynamics
      4. Intergenerational influences
      5. Contextual factors such as society, culture, history, religion, and physical/geographic environment.
Individual-Biological Assessment
  • The first level of assessment, individual-biological/medical, is aimed at understanding what if any, underlying biological and/or medical issues might be causing the sexual issue, including causes related to natural aging and developmental hormonal changes.
    • People who have recently given birth may experience hormonal shifts that limit sexual desire, which may also be connected to culturally deprived beliefs about sexuality and motherhood related to reduced sexual drive. Examples include:
      1. Do you currently have any physical issues and/or medical conditions? How has this impacted your sexual wellness and functioning?
      2. Are you currently taking any medications? What affect, if any, does this medication ahve on your sexual wellness and functioning?
      3. Has disability impacted your sexual wellness? If so, how?
      4. How has your sexual wellness and functioning changed as you have aged?
  • Even if no medical/biological issue is reported, referral to a medical doctor or sexual health specialist (if the client has not done so already) is advised if sexual issues are present that appear to be biologically/physiologically based.
Individual-Psychologic Assessment
  • The second level of assessment is individual-psychologic, which assesses potential psychological factors for sexual dysfunction, including comorbidity with existing and prior mental health issues.
  • Psychotropic medications used to treat mental health issues can also interrupt sexual functioning.
    • Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (e.g., SSRIs) have been shown to further induce sexual side effects, such as lowered libido, and arousal/orgasm difficulties.
  • Assessment questions of the psychologic dynamics of sexual dysfunction include:
    1. Have you previously experienced mental health issues? If so, what was the impact of those issues on your sexual wellness?
    2. How does your mood typically impact your libido and sexual functioning?
    3. When you feel (client's mental health symptoms), how does that tend to impact your sexual well-being? How, if at all, does your sexual desire and ability to experience pleasure shift when your symptoms are at their worst?
  • Over the course of treatment, it is recommended to regularly monitor any changes in the client's sexual function as an additional point of insight into the client's overall mental health, as well as to establish a timeline of the sexual problem.
Relational Assessment
  • The counselor will assess the relational dynamics of the sexual issue, which includes relational attachment, conflict history, communication, intimacy, and the presence of intimate partner violence (IPV).
  • The goals of relational assessment are for the client(s) and counselor to explore how the dynamics of the relationship may be contributing to the sexual issue, as well as how each individual partner is coping with the presence of sexual difficulties.
    • Common areas to explore include the client's sexual history with their current partner, communication strategies used by the client(s) when discussing sexual issues with their partners, and patterns of initiating, negotiating, and engaging in sexual activities.
  • A sexual history assessment may also be a helpful tool in discovering the couple's shared and individual levels of sexual knowledge, communication patterns, and sexual desires/interests.
  • Example items for sexual history assessment include:
    1. How did you first become sexually intimate with your partner? How would you describe your initial sexual connection? How has this changed over time?
    2. What types of sexual activities do you and your partner engage in?
    3. What types of partners and solo activities give you the most pleasure?
    4. How would you like to see your sexual relationships change or grow?
    5. How is sex initiated in your relationship? How do each of you respond when you're interested in sex, but your partner isn't?
    6. How do you communicate your sexual needs and desires to your partner?
    7. What challenges do you face, if any, in talking to your partner about sex?
  • The sexual worldview of individuals in a relationship may also have significant impacts on the quality of sex in the relationship, as well as the quality of the total relationship. Sexual problems can cause physical and emotional distress, shame, and embarrassment during sexual encounters, which can inhibit the level of satisfaction and pleasure derived by all partners.
    1. How do you feel when you're experiencing the sexual issue?
    2. How does your partner respond to you when you're experiencing the sexual issue?
    3. What are your major fears or insecurities about this issue?
    4. How, if at all, are these fears related messages you received about gender/race/sexuality growing up?
  • An imbalance of power (e.g., physical, emotional, financial, and/or childcare responsibilities) in the relationship can create challenges to the development of trust and intimacy, which can affect the sexual relationship and can further affect the individual's sexual functioning.
  • Before addressing the sexual wellbeing of the relationship, existing violence and/or severe conflict would first need to be addressed.
    • Example items to assess the presence of relational distress and violence include:
      1. How do you typically address, navigate, and resolve conflict in your relationship?
      2. How does your relationship typically handle jealousy, anger, and resentment?
      3. When, if ever, have you experienced physical or emotional violence in your relationship?
      4. How does conflict tend to impact your sexual relationship with your partner?
Intergenerational Assessment
  • The Genogram, which allows for the clinician to interview the client, sketch out family patterns of relating through generations, and assess the client's present difficulties from an intergenerational point of view. By creating a genogram with the client or couple, the counselor can locate patterns of Enmeshment, Emotional Cutoff, and Triangulation that may be connected to the sexual issues experienced by the client or couple.
    • The genogram allows the couple to understand the sexual issue from a more contextual and systemic perspective, which can initiate new, more functional patterns of relating. In this way, the genogram is both an assessment tool and an intervention strategy (McGoldrick, 2008)
  • Questions on the sexual genogram focus on six core areas:
    1. Messages received about sexuality/intimacy and gender
    2. Communication and behaviors related to sexuality
    3. Sexual secrets
    4. Lacking information
    5. Each partner's response to the other's genogram
    6. Goals for growth in the client's/couple's genogram.
    • Sample items to include on the sexual genogram include:
      1. How did you first learn about sexuality?
      2. How do members of your family, community, and cultural background(s) tend to view sexuality?
      3. How did your caregivers and family members respond to your sexual development (e.g., secondary sex characteristics, masturbation, first dating experiences, and access to safer sex/family planning resources)?
      4. Are there sexual events that are silenced or not talked about in your family-of-origin?
      5. Have you previously experienced difficulties with your arousal or orgasm?
      6. How have these difficulties impacted you and your partner(s)?
      7. How do you feel about the look, smell, and taste of your genitals? How does this impact your sexual connection with your partner(s)?
      8. What is the meaning/purpose of sex for you?
Contextual Assessment
  • The goals of this final level of assessment are for the client to gain awareness of impact of cultural values on their sexual wellness, as well as to affirm the unique resiliencies the client has developed in response to these overarching systems.
  • Example items to include in the contextual assessment:
    1. How, if at all, did religion impact your understanding of sexuality growing up? How about your cultural backgrounds?
    2. Which of your identities are most salient to you? What assumptions/expectations exist for each of your identities with respect to sex (e.g., race, gender, sexual orientation, age, etc.)?
    3. How do members of your cultural background tend to view sex and gender? Masturbation? Sexual pleasure?
    4. In what ways, if at all, have you felt like your sexual well-being was limited because of your identities?
    5. What sexuality supports do you wish you had received as a young person?
    6. What changes have you made to your personal understanding of sexuality throughout your life? What is most empowering about your current sexual worldview?
  • Often when clients simply articulate the unspoken limitations of sex-negative value systems on their lives, they are empowered to update these perspectives in ways that are more congruent with their current ethical framework.

Family Systems Interventions

  • A primary aim of family systems counseling is for the client to glean new insights into the relational dynamics in their life and to subsequently feel empowered to shift the boundaries, attachments, and communication patterns of those relationships that are not actively serving the client's mental and sexual wellness.
  • The PLISSIT model describes four levels of care ranging from introductory to specialized - Permission, Limited Information, Specific Suggestions, and Intensive Therapy - which can be applied to client.
    • The first level of care, permission, describes the counselor's role in giving the client express permission to discuss sexual issues in counseling, while emphasizing that sexuality is a normal, natural, and worthwhile topic of examination throughout a person's life.
      • Permission-giving is intended to affirm clients' sexual well-being, destigmatize sexual discussion, and invite continued and ongoing sexual exploration throughout treatment.
    • Often limited information is needed in order to provide the client with additional knowledge related to their specific sexual issue (i.e., psychoeducation).
    • Specific Suggestions may be required to improve the issue, such as giving a client a homework assignment to view their genitals in the mirror, practice sensate focus with their partner, or engage in a series of nonsexual touch exercises with their partner in the counseling office. (Counselors are recommended to join sexuality-specific organizations for additional learning opportunities such as the Association for Counseling Sexology and Sexual Wellness (ACSSW) and the American Association for Sexuality Educators, Counselors, and Therapists (AASECT))
    • The final level of care, intensive therapy, ins indicated in situations when the client is not finding relief from previous levels of treatment, which often signals comorbidity with underlying trauma, mental illness, and/or addiction issues. The need for intensive therapy may also prompt referral to a certified sex therapist, sexologist, or some other sexual healthcare.
    • PLISSIT is not used in lieu of an evidence-based treatment approach, but rather as a supplemental directive informing the progression of treatment.

Ethical and Legal Implications

  • Sexual assessment should be invitational, responsive to the client's cultural background and comfort level, and expressly relevant to the client's presenting issues. Questions based on the counselor's personal, nontherapeutic curiosity should be avoided.
  • University Resources/Couple and Family Therapy/Keywords/Countertransference can occur when a client brings up a topic that has personal resonance with the counselor and in situations where the client or client's situation reminds the counselor of someone/something in their own life.
    • Countertransference is a normative experience for counselors; however, the counselor's initial awareness of countertransference must be followed up with consultation, supervision, personal therapy, and/or continued education in order to prevent personal reactions from further impacting the therapeutic process.
    • Arousal can be a naturally occurring response to sexual discussion; however, it is important for the client and counselor to adhere to clear boundaries if arousal/attraction occurs.
      • "Sexual discussion can often stir up a wide range of confusing and exciting emotions. Maintaining a professional counselor-client relationship is critical in keeping this space safe and sacred for you. "
      • "I am getting the sense that this conversation is exciting you right now. While that can be a common response to sharing intimate sexual details, I want to remind you of our professional boundaries. If you need a break from this conversation, you are welcome to step outside, grab some water, or reschedule for a later time."
  • The importance of normalizing the existence of client attraction and focusing on creating opportunities aimed at supporting counselors as they establish and uphold boundaries, process 'forbidden' feelings, and practice enhanced bracketing skills with a peer or supervisor.
  • Counselors must clearly communicate goals, priorities, and rationale for the decision to apply a sexual dysfunction diagnosis, as well as facilitate an informed discussion of the risks associated with sharing a diagnosis with an outside source, such as a health insurance company or in the case of a judicial subpoena.
    • Before the client consents to formal diagnosis for healthcare coverage reasons, the counselor and client must collaboratively weight out the benefits and limitations of this formal documentation.
