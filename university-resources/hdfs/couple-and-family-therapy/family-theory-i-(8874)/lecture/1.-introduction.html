Terms

  • In many contexts, the term 'couple' has replaced 'marriage' because of its emphasis on the bond between family members/individuals.
  • This shift in language is important - taking a non-judgmental approach.
  • There is a social value implied by the more traditional terms (which privileges marriage).

Language/Terms

  • Use the most inclusive term possible (e.g., family)
  • With respect to all types of relationships, use the term 'couple' as it broadly encompasses those within a committed relationship (regardless of marital status).
  • With respect to persons of various gender identities and sexual orientations, use the term 'partner' rather than gendered language (whenever possible).
  • While the terms such as spouse, wife, husband, boyfriend, girlfriend may be preferred by clients - intentionally using the term 'partner' may be more inclusive.
  • With respect to those we serve, we will refer to them as clients as opposed to patients (whenever possible).

How is CFT Different than other Mental Health Disciplines

  • Psychiatrists are physicians whoo specializes in the diagnosis, treatment, and prevention of mental health and emotional problems. (M.D. needed)
  • Psychologist study the human mind and behavior. (Masters needed)
  • Social Workers help people function the best way they can in their environment, deal with their relationships, and solve problems. (Bachelors degree needed, unless clinical social work which needs a masters level degree)
  • Counselors assist people with personal, family, educational, mental health, and career decisions and problems. (Masters needed)

What is C/MFT?

  • A family's patterns of behaviors influences the individual and therefore may need to be a part of the treatment plan.
  • In CFT, the unit of treatment isn't just the person - even if only one person is 'the client'; it is the set of relationships in which the person is embedded.
  • Brief
  • Solution-focused
  • Specific with attained therapeutic goals
  • Designed with attainable therapeutic goals
  • Designed with the 'end in mind'
  • MFTs treat a wide range of serious clinical problems including:
    • Depression
    • Relationship issues
    • Anxiety
    • Biopsychosocial-rooted problems
  • Research has shown that CFT is as effective or more effective than standard individual treatments for many mental health problems including:
    • Schizophrenia
    • Affective Mood Disorder
    • Substance (Ab)use
    • Child Conduct Disorder
    • Anorexia
    • Autism
    • Chronic Illness
    • Couple/Partner Distress

Politics of C/MFT Licensure in the US

  • Psychiatry raised concern about scope of practice
    • Wanted to keep MFTs from diagnosis
  • Psychology sought amendments to the scope to place restrictions on psychological testing, and some oppose licensure in general.
  • Social Work concerned with title restriction
    • Wanted to call its members MFTs
  • Counselors (ACA) historically most active opponents
    • Opposed MFT licensure all together
    • Labeled the discipline as a subspecialty of counseling

  • Professionals from many different disciplines may at times utilize family-based interventions, or work from theories or models that are core to the CFT discipline.
  • Does that mean they are CFTs?

Family Therapy Theories

  • Classic Schools
    • Bowen
    • Strategic Family Therapy
      • Jay Haley
      • Depathologize problems by viewing them as normative life-cycle complications.
      • Consider possible adaptive functions for the family.
      • Acknowledge the helpful, alberit misguided, intentions of individuals within a family to help one another.
      • In such reformulations, new solutions can more readily become apparent.
    • Structural Family Therapy
      • Salvador Minuchin
      • No family style is inherently normal or abnormal.
      • Whether organizational patterns are functional or dysfunctional depends largely on their fit with the family's developmental and social demands.
      • The strength of the system requires clear yet flexible boundaries and subsystems for the ability to shift organizational patterns to accommodate needed change.
    • Experiential Family Therapy
      • Virginia Satir
      • Carl Whitaker
    • Psychoanalytic Family Therapy
    • Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT)
  • Developments
    • Contextual Therapy
    • Solution-Focused Therapy
      • Focus on solutions (i.e., what works), future-oriented.
      • Focusing on exceptions (i.e., those times when the problem was not present).
      • Direct toward building solutions rather than increasing insight into punitive maladaptive psychological mechanisms.
    • Narrative Therapy
      • Michael White
      • David Epston
      • Families have good intentions and neither want nor need problems.
      • Families can develop empowering stories when separated form their problems and constraining cultural beliefs.
      • Redirect focus from causal assumptions of dysfunction to appreciate the toxic effects of many dominant discourses in the social world.
  • Others
    • Internal Family Systems (IFS) Therapy
    • Evidenced-based treatments
    • Couples
      • Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT)
      • Gottman Therapy
        • Relationship Education there
      • Marital Preparation
      • Multi-group Couples Therapy

The Evolution of Family Therapy

The Major Themes in the Evolution of CFT

  • Reaction to psychoanalytic traditions
  • Research on schizophrenia
    • Acknowledge of family and contextual factors in mental illness
  • Adoption of systems theories

Major Markers of CFT

  • 1920s
    • Group Therapy Movement
  • 1940-1950
    • The Child Guidance Movement (1940s-1950s)
  • 1950s
    • Communication and role theories
  • 1970s
    • Brief therapy theories

Context Matters!

  • Therapists first encountered families as adversaries.
  • Therapists begin to notice that often when a client got better, someone else in the family got worse, almost as though the family needed a symptomatic member.
  • Clients improved in the hospital only to get worse when they went home.

Immediacy / In the Room

  • Parents sometimes use their children as a buffer to protect themselves from intimacy/
    • I.e., You're scratching your eyes for your mother so she'll have something to worry about. You're a good boy. Good children sacrifice themselves for their parents.
    • I.e., Minuchin: As a father of a child in danger, what you're doing isn't enough. Father: What should I do? Minuchin: I don't know. Ask your son.

Group Dynamics

  • Groups dynamics are applicable to family therapy because group life is a complex blend of individual personalities and properties of the group.
  • The process/context distinction in group dynamics had a major impact on family therapy.
  • Attend to how family members talk as much as to what they say.
  • Family members have a long history and a future together.
  • Continuity, commitment, and shared distortions all make family therapy different from group therapy.

Child Guidance Movement

  • Concluded that the real problem wasn't a child's symptoms, but rather the tensions in the family that were the source of those symptoms.
  • Tendency to blame the parents, especially mothers (this was an evolutionary misdirection that haunts the field).
  • We need to understand the family in order to diagnose problems.

Couples Therapy

  • Many do not distinguish between couple and family therapy.
  • Couples therapy is just family therapy applied to a particular subsystem.
  • However, there is a case to be made that couples therapy is a distinct enterprise (see Gurman texts)

Conclusion

  • For many years, therapists resisted the idea of seeing clients' families in order to safeguard the privacy of the therapeutic relationship.
  • Converging developments in the 1950s led to a new view that the family was an organic whole.
  • What they observed was that the behavior of schizophrenia fit with their families; what they concluded was that the family was the cause of schizophrenia.

Family Dynamics and Schizophrenia

  • Champions of family therapy challenged the assumption that schizophrenia was a biological disease. Psychological healers everywhere cheered. Unfortunately, we were wrong.
  • The observation that schizophrenic behavior seems to fit in some families doesn't mean that families caused schizophrenia.
  • Families of schizophrenic members suffered for years from the assumption that they were to blame for their child's psychoses.

The Fundamental Concepts of Family Therapy

What does it mean to say 'The Family is a System'?

  • To understand this better, consider the example of a mobile.
    • When you move any one piece of a mobile, all the other pieces move too. They do not exist in isolation from one another, and movement in any one part of the system will affect all the rest of the parts of the system.

Cybernetics

  • The study of feedback mechanisms in self-regulating systems.
  • Feedback Loop:
    • The process by which a system gets the information necessary to maintain a steady course.
    • If you see behavior persist over time, there is likely something maintaining that behavior.
    • Feedback loops can be negative or positive.
    • Negative Feedback:
      • Indicates that a system is straying off the mark and that corrections are needed to get back on track.
    • Positive Feedback
      • Reinforces the direction a system is taking.

Systems Theory

  • What shows up as one individual's behavior may be a product of the relationship.
  • Family therapists use concepts to describe how people in a relationship contribute to what goes on between them (e.g., pursuer/distancer; overfunctioning/underfunctioning)
  • At the core, the theory is about:
    • Structure
    • Functioning
  • The whole is greater than the sum of its parts.
  • Mapping sequences of interaction (which reveal how a system works)
  • Homeostasis:
    • The self-regulation that keeps the system stable
  • Families tend to resist change, which explains why so many clients remain stuck despite heroic efforts to improve.

General Systems Theory

  • A system is, 'Any entity maintained by the mutual interaction of its parts.'
  • A system can be composed of smaller subsystems.
  • Every system is a subsystem of larger systems.
  • Open vs. Closed systems
  • Unlike machines, families change when necessary to adapt to new circumstances.

Social Constructionism

  • Social Constructionism:
    • Seeing the world through a pair of glasses.
  • Reframing:
    • Relabeling behavior to shift how family members respond to it.
  • The goal of therapy shifted from interrupting problematic patterns of interaction to helping clients find new perspective on their lives.
  • When they said reality was constructed, they meant socially constructed.

Social Construction

  • Interpretations are shaped by our context.
  • Interpretation of our experience mediates behavior.
  • Social constructionists place more emphasis on the intersubjective influence of language and culture.
  • Therapy becomes a process of deconstruction, freeing clients from the tyranny of entrenched beliefs.

Solution-Focused

  • Inherent in most forms of therapy is the idea that before you can solve a problem, you must figure out what is wrong.
  • But solution-focused turns this assumption on its head.
  • The best way to solve problems is to discover what people do when they are not having the problem.
  • Exceptions to a complaint.

Narrative

  • Therapists create a shift in their clients' experience by helping them reexamine how they look at things.
  • Externalization:
    • Redefining problems not as properties of the individuals who suffer them, but as alien oppressors.

Take Aways

  • The focus of therapy has expanded toward the wider context.
    • This process started when therapists looked beyond individuals to their families.
    • Suddenly unexplained behavior made sense.
  • Early on, family therapists focused on behavioral interactions surrounding problems.
    • Next, they recognize that those interactions were manifestations of a family's underlying structure.
    • Structure became the target of change.
  • Later, family structure was seen as the product of multigenerational processes that were governed by belief systems.
    • Then, therapists aimed their interventions at those underlying beliefs.
    • More recently, it dawned on therapists that those belief systems do not arise in a vacuum
  • Behavior is shaped by transactions we don't always see.
  • Systems concepts help make complex interactions predictable.

Concepts

  • Family Structure:
    • Defines the organization within which interactions take place.
    • Interactions shape structure, but once established structure shapes interactions.
    • Subsystems
    • Boundaries
  • Family Life Cycle
  • Context:
    • People are products of their context.
    • Behavior is powerfully influenced by interactions with other family members.
  • Circular Causality:
    • Problems are sustained by an ongoing series of actions and reactions.
  • Triangles
  • Process/Content
    • Focus on the process of communication (how people talk), rather than the context (what they talk about)

Basic Techniques of Family Therapy (from Symptom to System)

Initial Contact / Intake

  • The goal is to get an overview of the presenting problem and arrange to meet.
  • Listen to the description of the problem and identify all members of the family, household, and others who might be involved.
  • Establish connection and engagement.
  • Ask how the problem is affecting other members of the family.
  • You'd like to hear from everyone, at least initially, in order to get as much information as possible.
  • Reminder calls help increase likelihood of attendance.

First Session

  • Build alliance with the family.
  • Develop hypothesis about what is maintaining the presenting problem.
  • Establish rapport.
  • Gather information.
  • Introduce yourself.
  • Orient the family to the therapy room (physical or virtual) and the format of sessions.
  • Invite family members to speak one at a time.
  • Give everyone a chance to be heard.
  • Establish a bond of understanding with each member of the family.
  • Process:
    • How did you feel about coming in?
    • What were your reactions to coming in?
  • Take a family history.
  • Conduct the genogram to diagram the family network.
  • Listen for family life cycle transitions.
  • Search for strengths and resilience.
  • Remain curious and respectful.
  • Explore the process of family interaction by asking questions about how family members relate to one another and inviting them to discuss their problems with one another in session.
  • What is keeping the family stuck?
  • Share recommendations for treatment.

Early Sessions

  • What is maintaining the problem?
  • Maintain therapeutic alliance.
  • Listen to and acknowledge family members' points of view.
  • Through empathic understanding, family members feel respected and become open to being challenged.
  • When one person is presented as the problem, challenge linearity by asking how others are involved or affected? What role do others play in creating or maintaining the problem? How have they responded to it?
  • Challenge unhelpful interactions by pointing out patterns that seem to be keeping the family stuck.
  • The more you do X, the more they do Y - and the more you do Y, the more they do X.
  • Listen to people's feelings.
    • Listening is a silent activity, rare in our time, even among therapists.
  • Family members are not likely to reconsider their assumptions until they've been heard and understood.

Middle Sessions

  • Help family members deal with one another more effectively in session.
  • Take a less active role and encourage family members to interact more with one another.
  • Step back and observe.
  • When family members address their conflicts directly, they tend to become reactive; anxiety is the enemy of listening - help them listen and manage anxiety.
  • Stay calm:
    1. You are not responsible for solving the family's problems.
    2. Just look for the constraints that are keeping them stuck.
    3. Be curious.
    4. Display empathy and respect.

Termination

  • Review the therapeutic relationship.
  • Say goodbye.
  • Reinforce progress.
  • Review what the family has accomplished.
  • Anticipate and plan for upcoming challenges.
    • How will you know when things are heading backwards? And what will you do?
    • How will you know if it is time to come back to therapy?
  • Check-in with the family following treatment, if possible.

Assessment

  • The presenting problem
  • Understanding the referral route
  • Identifying the systemic context
  • Stage of family life cycle
  • Family structure
  • Communication
  • Substance use/abuse
  • Domestic violence and sexual abuse
  • Affairs/infidelity
  • Cultural context (race, ethnicity, culture, gender identity, sexual orientation, class, disability, etc.)

Presenting Problem

  • Explore the presenting problem.
  • Explore attempts to deal with the problem.
    • What have they tried?
    • What has been helpful?
    • What hasn't been helpful?
    • Has anyone who is not present been trying to help or hinder?

Referral

  • Who referred the clients and why?
  • What are their expectations?
  • What expectations have they communicated to the family?
  • Is participation voluntary or mandated?
  • Who recognized the need for treatment?
  • What other agencies are or will be involved with the case?

Identifying the Systemic Context

  • Obtain a clear understanding of the interpersonal context of the problem.
  • Who is in the family?
  • Are there other important figures in the life of the problem who aren't present?
  • Family of origin, immediate family, extended family, family of choice, etc.
  • Are there other agencies involved? What is their input? Does the family see them as helpful?
    • Remember that family therapy is an approach to people in context.

Family Structure

  • Family problems become entrenched because they are embedded in powerful but unseen structure.
  • What is the family's structure?
  • What are the subsystems?
  • What is the nature of Boundaries between the subsystems?
  • What Triangles are present?

Substance Use, IPV, Affairs

  • Assess for substance use, IPV, and affairs.
  • Consider assessment tools if needed.
  • Be aware of mandated reporting.

The Age Old Debate

  • General Practitioner
    • What is your scope of practice?
  • Specialist
    • Do you specialize in particular populations? Or problems?
  • Most family therapists assume their approach can be applied to any problem. Of course their may be exceptions.

Philosophical Foundations of Family Therapy Theories

General Systems Theory

  • The whole is greater than the sum of its parts.
  • Systems can be viewed in terms of hierarchy, organization, and subsystems.
  • Systems strive toward self-preservation and therefore its members work in service of the system, as well as themselves.

The Family as a System

  • All behavior makes sense in context.
  • No one family member is responsible for the family's distress.
  • Family therapists work to see the big picture.
  • Even when a client presents with an individual issue (e.g., depression, anxiety), family therapists view it within the larger relational context.

Systemic and Social Constructionist Theories

  • Share the following assumptions:
    1. A person's lived reality is relationally constructed.
    2. Personal identity and individual symptomology are related to the social systems in which they are embedded.
    3. Changing one's language and description of the problem alters how it is experienced.
    4. Truth can be determined only within relational contexts.

Systemic vs. Social Construction

  • Systems theory
    • Systems metaphor
    • A family is a system, a group of individuals who coordinate meaning and their understanding of the world.
  • Social constructionist
    • Textual metaphor
    • People narrate their lives to create meaning using the social discourses available to them.

Postmodernism

  • Skeptical of objective reality.
  • Reality is constructed.
  • Reality is constructed through language.
  • Reality is negotiated through relationships.
  • Shared meanings coordinate social action.

Modernism Assumes

  • Founded upon the logical-positivist assumptions of an external, knowable truth.
  • Therapist assumes expert role.
  • Therapist is an expert who assumes the primary responsibility for identifying problems and goals, often assumes the role of a teacher or mentor.
  • Theory and research are the primary sources of information for identifying problems and diagnosis.
  • Therapist uses theory and research to select treatment approaches.

Humanistic

  • Humans are essentially good.
  • All people naturally tend toward growth and strive for self-actualization.
  • Focus on treatment is the subjective, internal world of clients.
  • Therapeutic interventions target emotions with the goal of promoting catharsis (release of repressed emotions).
  • A supportive, nurturing environment promoted therapeutic change.
  • Examples such as Carl Whitaker and Virginia Satir

Systemic Therapy

  • One cannot communicate; all behavior is a form of communication.
  • An individual's behavior and symptoms always make sense in context.
  • All behaviors and symptoms serve a purpose within the system, allowing the system to maintain Homeostasis.
  • No one individual controls behavior in a system and therefore no one individual can be blamed for problems in the family.
  • Problematic behavior emerges from the interactions between members of the system.
  • Therapeutic change involves altering the interaction patterns within the system.

Postmodern

  • Objective truth can never be fully known because it must always pass through subjective and intersubjective filters.
  • The human mind does not have access to an outside reality independent of human interpretation.
  • Knowledge and truth are culturally, historically, and relationally bound.
  • Personal reality and truth are shaped by language and relationships.
  • Language affects how identity is shaped and experienced.
  • The identification of the problem is a social process that occurs through language.
  • Therapy is a process of co-constructing new realities.

Integrative

  • As therapists become more aware of theories, they learn to skillfully integrate ideas from other approaches in a way that is philosophically congruent with their own approach.
  • However, therapists who are 'eclectic' or 'integrative' in a way that is not grounded in a single philosophical set of assumptions will confuse their clients.
  • Clients will be confused if each week the client is required to relate differently to the therapist and to assume a different level of participation (i.e., sending contradictory messages).
  • If the therapist is able to keep the philosophical assumptions consistent throughout therapy, then the therapist can adapt concepts and techniques from other approaches without sending conflicting messages.

    • Forensics, testimony, competence, informed consent, avoiding conflicts, child custody, professional opinions
  • Standard VIII: Financial Arrangements
    • Disclosure of financial policies, recovery procedures, bartering
  • Standard IX: Advertising
    • Professional representation, affiliations, educational credentials, qualifications
