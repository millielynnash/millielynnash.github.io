5. Solution-Focused Therapy

  • Steve de Shazer
  • Insoo Kim Berg

Features of Solution-Focused Family Therapy

  • Miracle question
  • Rating/Scaling (1 to 10)
  • Break
  • After intermission, the therapist will give the client/family compliments, sometimes followed by a suggestion or homework.

Basic Rules

  • If it ain't broken, don't fix it.
  • Once you know what works, do more of it.
  • If it doesn't work, don't do it again, do something different.

Solution-Focused Family Therapy

  • Solution-focused family therapy begins with clients' descriptions of how they want their lives to be different.
  • It can be understood as beginning with the end of the story, rather than the beginning of the problem.
  • The basic idea is to avoid escalating problem talk by instead focus on eliciting movement in a helpful direction (i.e., focusing on solutions).
  • Focus is on solutions, on what works for clients.
  • Respect clients own resources.
  • Directed toward building solutions rather than increasing insight into punitive maladaptive psychological mechanisms.
  • Future-oriented
  • Focusing on those times when the problem was not present (exceptions).
  • The task of therapy is to do something different.
  • What is happening in your lives that you want to continue to have happen?

Introduction

  • The most fundamental difference between the emphasis between problem-focused therapy and solution-focused therapy is the emphasis each give to the concept of 'solution.'
  • Solution-focused family therapy seeks to identify exceptions to the problem and develop new solutions that work.
    • What brings you in?
    • What are your best hopes for this meeting?
  • Therapists avoid diagnostic categories and preconceived notions of what may be healthy or unhealthy.
  • Accept the client's version of what is, and what is not, the problem.
  • It isn't necessary to know what causes problems in order to make things better.
  • Families that come to therapy are capable of behaving effectively, but their effectiveness has been blunted by a negative mind-set.
  • The model draws attention to forgotten capabilities, which release families from preoccupation with their failures and restores them to their capable selves.
  • Problems are seen as overwhelming because families see them as always happening, times when problems are not happening, aren't noticed, or are dismissed.

Theoretical Formulations

  • People are constrained by narrow views of their problems that perpetuate rigid patterns of false solutions.
  • The meanings people attribute to behavior limit the range of alternatives they will use to deal with a situation.
  • Changing the way people talk about their problems.
  • The language of problems tends to be different from the language of solutions.
    • Problem talk:
      • Negative
      • Focuses on the past
      • Implies permanence of problems.

Family Development

  • Clients are experts in their own lives.
    • Clients know what they need
    • Is there anything else I should have asked you or that you need to tell me?
    • The therapist needs to know how to set aside their own frame of reference as much as possible and explore those of their clients.
  • People are resourceful.
  • Problems are normal life-cycle complications.
  • Families have the abillity to construct solutions that can enhance their lives.

Problem Development

  • Solution-focused therapists steer families away from speculating about problem formation.
  • Problems persist in the way people define situations and in the misdirected actions they persist in taking.

Mechanism of Change

  • Set clear goals and build on exceptions to problems.
  • Good therapy is more than implementing specific techniques, it also involves therapeutic alliance.
  • The goal of solution-focused therapy is to resolve the presenting problem as expeditiously as possible.
  • The goal is not about how families should be structured, but only what they want different in their lives.
  • The model emphasizes the importance of assisting clients in creating well-defined goals, framed in positive terms and modest enough to be achievable.
    • What will you be doing instead?
  • The model works by helping clients amplify exceptions to their problems – effective solutions already in their repertoire.
  • Changing the way people talk about their problems is all that needs to be accomplished.
  • As the family and therapist talk more and more about the solution they want to construct together, they come to believe in what they are talking about.
  • It is easier to get people to talk differently about their problems than it is to get them to change their behavior.
  • Getting people to talk positively will help them think positively and ultimately act positively to solve their problems.
  • Change perception helps with the change of behavior. As a picture of positive future develops, the family begins to see themselves differently and they respond to what they see.
  • They begin living in the solution, not the problem.
  • The 'virtuous cycle' gets going, the family gets unstuck, and they move forward.

Structure

  • Time limited
  • Sessions are made one at a time
  • One may be enough
  • Typical course includes 3-5 sessions
  • Clients can return as-needed
  • Often a week to a few weeks apart
  • Once you know more of what works, do more of it

Assessment

  • Gather a brief description of the presenting problem.
  • Move to directly asking clients about how things will be different in their lives then their probelms are solved.
  • Ask about times in the clients' lives when their problems do not happen or are less severe.

Assessment Questions

  • What do you think the problem is now?
  • How will you know when the problem is solved?
  • How will you know when you don't have to come here anymore? What will the signs be?
  • What will have to be different about others involved in the situation?
  • What is your wildest fantasy about what you want to happen?

Resistance

  • What would it take to get your family member (or the court) off of your back?

Role of the Therapist

  • Takes little intake information because they want to hear clients' constructions of their problems firsthand (in session).
  • Asks more about perceptions than feelings.
  • Affirms clients' positions by conveying understanding of their point of view.
  • Doesn't function as an expert; clients are experts on what they want to change.
  • Takes an active position in moving clients away from worrying about their predicament and toward steps to solution.
  • Anyone who is concerned about the problem should attend sessions and are part of the solution.
  • Consultant
  • Guardian of the conversation
  • Don't get bogged down in 'problem talk'
  • Focus on what is right
  • Direct clients away from the problem-saturated narrative.
  • Therapist attempts to create a context for the family to develop more useful ways of responding.
  • Focus on the 'here and now'
  • The future provides a blank canvas on which the family can paint a picture of their wishes and hopes.
  • Assumes a position of 'not knowing.'

Goal Development

  • What needs to happen as a result of coming here so that afterward you will look back and be able to say it was a good idea?
  • What needs to happen as a result of coming here so that this will not have been a waste of your time?
  • Often in our experience, we have found that in between scheduling an appointment and coming in, something happens that contributes to making a problem better. Has anything happened to improve the problems that brought you here today?
  • Constructing a more detailed description of the therapeutic goal.

Assessing / Scaling

  • Once a description of the goal has been developed, the therapist asks the client to assess their current level of progress toward that goal by imagining a scale of 1-10, where 1 represented the problem at its worst, and 10 is the point at which the problem is gone.

Combating Resistance

  • So, what has to happen to get your mother off of your back?
  • What is the minimum we need to accomplish so that you won't have to keep coming for more sessions?
  • What would it take to get your family (or court) off of your back?

Techniques

  1. Developing well-focused goals within the clients' frame of reference
  2. Generating solutions based on exceptions
  • Goal setting
  • Miracle Question
  • Exception Question
  • Coping Questions
  • Scaling Questions
  • Compliments

Questions

  • Before Session
    • Between now and next time we meet, I would like you to observe what happens that you want to continue to have happen?
  • In-Session
    • What brings you here today? How can I be helpful to you?
    • What changes have you noticed since you first made the call to set up this appointment?
  • Exceptions
    • When in the past might the problem have happened but it didn't?
  • Solution-focused therapists intervene and interrupt 'problem talk' before it escalates into demoralizing bickering, blaming and defending, accusations, etc.
  • Agency
    • How did you do that? How did you get that to happen?
  • Coping
    • How have you managed to cope, endure, keep going?
  • Scaling (i.e., making numbers talk)
    • What will it take to move from a 3 to a 4?
    • On a scale of 1 to 10, 1 being absolutely no hope, and 10 being complete hope, what number would you give your current level of hope?

Assessment Problem Description

  • Therapy begins with a description of the clients' problem.
  • How were you hoping I could help you?
  • Asking for clients' perceptions
  • Acknowledge clients' perceptions, using clients own language as much as possible.
  • As clients what they've already tried to resolve their difficulties.

Goal Setting

  • How do we know when to stop meeting like this?
  • Goals should be:
    • Small
    • Salient to clients
    • Concrete, behavioral, measurable
    • Achieveable
    • Involve hard work
    • Start of something
    • Involving new behaviors
  • What will be present? What will be happening?
  • After hearing and acknowledging the clients' description of the problem and what they've tried to do about them, next step is to establish clear and concrete goals.
    • Specifically, how will you be doing this?
    • How will the two of you know when you solved your problems? How will things be different?
    • What will be the first sign (or smallest step) that will tell you that you're moving in the right direction? What else?
  • The clearer the goal, the easier to measure progress.
    • Can you tell me more specifically what will be happening when you two are getting along better?
    • What will you be doing differently?
  • Modest enough to be achievable.

Miracle Question

  • Now, I want to ask you a strange question. Suppose that while you are sleeping tonight and the entire house is quiet, a miracle happens. The miracle is that the problem which brought you here is solved. However, because you are sleeping, you don't know that the miracle has happened. So, when you wake up tomorrow morning, what will be different that will tell you that a miracle has happened?
  • Invite clients to envision positive outcomes and to activate problem-solving by giving them a mental picture of their goals.
  • Helps clients to look beyond the problem to see that what they really want might not be the elimination of the problem per se but to be able to do things that the problem has been obstructing.
  • Suppose one night there is a miracle and while you are sleeping the problem that brought you into therapy is solved.
    • How would you know?
    • What would be different?
    • What will you notice the next morning that will tell you there has been a miracle?
    • What will your [family member] notice?

Exploring Exceptions

  • Helping families see that their problems have exceptions - times when they don't occur.
  • Exceptions are solutions that families already have in their repertoire.
  • Probing for exceptions (e.g., times when the family didn't have the problem) invites them to recognize that some potential solutions may already be in their grasp.
  • Finding exceptions in the recent past is most useful because clients can remember them in greater detail; also since these exceptions just happened, it is more plausible they could happen again.
  • When in the recent past might the problem have happened but didn't (or was less intense or more manageable)?
  • What's different about those times when the problem doesn't happen?
  • How have you let your partner know when they do something that makes a positive difference to you?

Coping Questions

  • How did you manage that?
  • What keeps you going under such difficult circumstances?
  • How come things aren't worse?
  • Punctuate with enthusiasm and support
  • Work to keep solution talk in the forefront.
  • Listen carefully for an exception to the problem, then punctuate that exception by getting more details about it and congratualating the family on it.
  • Connect the exception to the family's goals by asking how the goal would be reached if this exception were to occur more often.

Scaling Questions

  • The therapist asks:
    • On a scale of 1-10, with 1 being how depressed you felt when you called me and 10 being how you feel the day after the miracle, how do you feel right now?
      • "It is a 2."
      • "So, you feel a little better than when you called. How did you achieve this improvement?"
      • "What do you think you need to do to achieve a 3?"
  • Used to get clients to quantify their confidence that they can maintain their resolve.
    • On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you that you will be able to avoid losing your temper this week?
    • What do you have to do to stick to your guns this time?

Compliments

  • Wow! How did you do that?
  • To be effective, compliments should point toward what to do more, not what to eliminate.
  • Compliments can be used to highlight successful strategies and keep clients focused on those that work

Break and Feedback

  • Team approach with therapist in session and colleagues behind the mirror.
  • Therapist usually takes a 10 minute break near the end of the session to compose a summary message to the clients.
  • The summary messages begins with a recap of what the therapist heard the clients say during the interview, including the problem, its background, the client's goals, and presession progress and strengths.
    • What I heard you tell me today is that... Did I hear all of you correctly?
Recap is Followed by
  • A statement reflecting the therapist's reaction, including an expression of empathy.
    • "I'm not surprised you are so depressed."
  • A reflection of the emotional impact on the client.
    • "My sense is that you must really be hurting."
  • Compliments on pre-session changes or strengths
    • "I was impressed by how many things you've tried to make things better."
  • Some content on the client's goals
  • Suggestions for building on positives

Therapist Suggestions

  • Formula first-session task
  • Do more of what works
  • Do something different
  • Go slow
  • Do the opposite
  • The prediction task

Later Sessions

  • Devoted to finding, amplifying, and measuring progress
  • Inquire about progress, seeking detailed descriptions of any movement toward the family's goal and the clients' role in attaining it.
    • What's better?
    • What happened that you liked?
    • Tell me more. Walk me through how the two of you did that.
    • Wow. That sounds great. What part did you especially enjoy?
    • And what else is better? What do you think the next step might be?
    • How did you keep things from getting worse?
    • What's your idea about what might be helpful?

Common Errors

  • Sometimes the model is portrayed as an approach that does not permit discussion of the problem, but this is not accurate. When done skillfully, families do not feel rushed. Emotion is not avoided, but it is also not sought or elicited.
  • Therapist is present as a real, genuinely concerned person, but does not engage in unneeded personal self-disclosure.

Termination

  • How can we know when to stop meeting like this?
  • What needs to be different in your life as a result of coming here for you to say that meeting with me was worthwhile?
  • If you wait till all of the family's problems are solved, you will never stop seeing them.
