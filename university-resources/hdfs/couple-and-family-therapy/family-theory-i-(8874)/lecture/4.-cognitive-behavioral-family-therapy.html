1. Cognitive-Behavioral Family Therapy

Techniques of All Therapy

  • Engaging and making comfortable
  • Joining
  • Creating alliance
  • Building rapport
  • Active listening
  • Open-ended questions
  • Identifying strengths and why now
  • Reinforcing positives
  • Mapping family system/ Genogram
  • Exploring family dynamics (assessment)
  • Reflecting
  • Summarizing
  • Reframing
  • Circular questioning
  • Acknowledge/reinforce

Techniques of CBT

  • Thoughts and perceptions
  • Acting or behaving
  • Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) is a form of psychological treatment that has been demonstrated to be effective for a range of problems including depression, anxiety disorders, alcohol and drug use problems, marital problems, eating disorders, and severe mental illness.
  • Problems are based in unhelpful thinking and behavior.
  • Families can learn better ways of coping, which improves symptoms.

Arose Out of Behaviorism

  • Behavior is maintained by its consequences.
  • Consequences that increase behavior are reinforcers.
  • Consequences that decrease behavior are punishers.

CBFT Foundation

  • Behavior is regulated by its consequences.
  • Responses that are positively reinforced will increase.
  • Responses that are punished or ignored will be extinguished.
  • CBT/CBFT is a highly efficacious intervention to assist families in a variety of contexts (this model has empirical support)
  • CBT has focused on family's cognitive processing and behavioral interactions, while minimizing the influences of individual differences between family members.
  • CBT also addresses characteristics that each family member brings to their relationships, explaining why family members behave and interpret events in maladaptive ways.

Predictors of Relationship Stress

  • Scarcity of positive outcomes available for each family member.
  • Deficits in communication and problem solving.
  • High frequency of negative or punishing exchanges reciprocated by partners, parents, family members.
  • Family members are more likely to track negative behavior selectively and to make negative attributions for such behavior.
  • They reciprocate negative behavior with negative behavior.
  • Primary distress results from unmet fundamental needs create secondary distress (withdrawal or verbally abuse among family members)

Family Dynamics

  • Behavior is maintained by its consequences.
  • Ratio of costs and benefits to behavior (something is maintianing it)
  • Good relationships are under positive reinforcement ocntrol

What is the Foundation of CBT

  • CBT recognizes the influence of both the family's behavioral interactions (processes) and the major themes (content) that serve as the basis for relationship discord.

CBFT

  • Thoughts change to promote behavioral change (thoughts -> behavior)
  • Emotions and actions are mediated by cognitions (thoughts -> emotions)
  • Understand cognition (beliefs, attributions, expectancies) makes it possible to identify triggers to behavioral patterns.
  • Uncovering hidden/automatic assumptions that keep people stuck.

Unhelpful Thinking

  • Overgeneralizations
  • Magnification and minimization
  • Personalization
  • Dichotomous thinking
  • Labeling and mislabeling
  • Also called black and white thinking, catastrophizing, defense mechanisms

Major Tasks of CBFT

  • Help families become more active observers and evaluators of their own automatic thoughts, long-standing assumptions, and of their relationship standards.

Goal Setting

  • Individual family members vary in the extent to which they have a clear understanding of the factors affecting their relationships and clear goals for treatment and how their own contributions affect the achievement of their goals.
  • Therapist determines the appropriate sequence of addressing goals in therapy.
  • Extinguish undesired behavior and reinforce positive alternatives.
    • Ignoring the tantrum; rewarding the child for putting feelings into words
  • Redefine the family's goals of decreasing negative behavior into increasing positive responses.
  • Families have difficulty describing the behavior they want to see more of
  • The therapist helps by asking families to make a list of things the other person did that they liked throughout the week.
  • Reviewing the list in session the next week provides an opportunity to emphasize the importance of positive feedback.
  • This agenda of therapy is educational:
    • Teach communication, problem-solving, and negotiation

Change

  • Behavior will change when the contingencies of reinforcement are altered.
  • Identify behavioral goals
  • Learn techniques to achieve these goals
  • Facilitate social reinforcement of change

Structure of the Therapy Process

  • Brief (about 20 weekly sessions)
  • Use of homework assignments
  • Phase out therapy gradually.
    • Once the family shows evidence of substituting positive interactions for negative ones and goals are achieved.
  • Booster sessions
Homework
  • Collaborate with families in designing homework to complete between sessions.
  • Homework is based on the learning principle that to replace existing ingrained dysfunctional interactional patterns with new ones, families rehearse the new patterns repeatedly, particularly under 'real life conditions.'

CBFT Popularity

  • CBFT has been popular in two primary sub-systems:

Couples

  • All couples run into conflict.
  • Conflict resolution is a critical skill.
  • Healthy families are not problem free, but they can resolve conflicts when they arise.
  • They describe their own feelings and request changes in the behavior of others rather than criticizing or complaining.
CBCT
  • Good relationships are not made in heaven, they are a product of learning effective coping behavior.
  • Families need problem solving skills
  • Therapy includes:
    • Clinical interview
    • Rating specific behaviors
    • Relationship satisfaction questionnaires
CBCT Techniques
  • Couples are taught to express themselves in clear, behavioral descriptions, rather than in vague complaints.
  • Couples are taught new behavior exchange procedures, emphasizing positive reinforcement.
  • Couples are helped to improve their communication.
  • Couples are encouraged to establish clear and effective decision-making.
  • Couples are taught strategies to problem-solve future complaints to extend the benefits of therapy.
    • Ask each partner to list three things they would like the other to do more often.
Other Techniques
  • Imagery
  • Role-play
  • Coaching (to switch places, to increase their empathy for one another's feelings)
  • Communication training
    • For example:
      • Engaging in an argument without attacking
      • 'I' statements (Using 'I' Statements.pdf)
      • Speaker listener exercises (Practice Exercise (Speaker Listener Activity))
  • Problem-solving training
  • Homework assignments
  • Mindfulness and meditation
  • Sensate focus (p. 164)
  • In vivo desensitization (p. 164)

Parenting

  • Parents ahve control over the rewards and punishment of children.
  • If parents change the contingencies of reinforcement, the child's behavior will change.
  • Skills are taught in sequence (shaping)
  • Help parents define the child's positive and problematic behaviors in specific observable actions.
  • Tracking target behaviors
  • Reinforcing desired behavior through contigent encouragement with incentive charts.
  • Disciplinary tehcniques include Time-Out and ignoring the problem behavior.
Techniques
  • Observe/Assess the frequency of problem behavior, what precedes it, and what reinforcement follows (Behavior Chaining)
  • Shaping (reinforcing small steps toward change)
  • Token Economy (reward good behavior)
  • Contingency contracting (agreements by parents to make certain changes following changes made by their child)
  • Time-Out
  • Once assessment is complete, the therapist decides which behaviors should be increased or decreased.
  • Once effective rewards are chosen, parents are taught to shape desired behavior by reinforcing approximate behaviors.
  • Ignoring
  • Rewards

Broad Techniques

  • Identifying Automatic Thoughts
    • Assist family members in identifying automatic thoughts
    • To improve the skills of identifying automatic thoughts, clients are encouraged to keep a diary to jot down situations that provoke the thoughts and the resulting emotional reactions.

Barriers to Joining and Alliance

  • Joining with all members of the family simultaneously can be challenging.
  • Families in conflict often desire to form an alliance with the therapist, each family member trying to convince the therapist that the other members are responsible for relationship problems.
  • Do not be triangulated!

Joining and Alliance

  • CBFT therapists hear the seriousness of the concerns while not siding with individual family members.
  • CBFT therapists use empathic reflective listening with one family member and then the other, followed by statements summarizing the reciprocal interwoven concerns of all members.
  • CBFT therapists are a neutral party whose agenda is to help family members achieve their personal goals for family relationships.
  • CBFT therapists often define relationship problems in 'dyadic terms.'
  • Blocking (e.g., interrupting) if necessary

The Practice of CBFT

  • Inclusion of other individuals if necessary
  • Medication, and individual therapy can be used in conjunction with CBFT
  • Co-therapy model is allowed
  • Both individual and conjoint sessions are allowed.
  • Clients may be more likely to divulge information about their past in private sessions.
  • CBFT therapists tell clients they will keep this information confidential, but if it might be influencing the family's current interactions, they encourage the person to share it in a joint session
  • Secrets
  • Be up front with clients about the limits of confidentiality
    • Assist them with sharing in a joint session (rather than outing them)
The Role of the Therapist
  • Didactic role early on to strike a balance between directiveness and collaborationl
  • Reviews homework assignments, model skills, coaches family members in practicing them, and fosters motivation.
  • Balances alliances and interventions with all the family members or subsystems so that they feel supported.
The Directive Role of the Therapist
  • Sets the pace of sessions so that the goals of treatment are addressed in a timely and sequential fashion.
  • Sets the agenda each session
  • Monitors the use of time
  • Responsible to stay on task and address the goals of session
  • Teaches the clients to self monitor as well
  • Maintains control of the session with confidence and credibility
  • Assumes multiple roles:
    • Director
    • Educator
    • Facilitator
    • Collaborator
    • Advocate
  • Balances the alliance and interventions with all members, so they feel supported and invested.
Assessment and Treatment Planning
  • Primary Goals:
    • Identify the concerns and potential areas of enrichment/growth.
    • Clarify the cognitive, behavioral, and affective factors associated with family members, dyadic subsystems, and the family environment.
    • Determine the appropriateness of family therapy
      • Including each family members' level of commitment and goals for treatment.
Assessment Methods
  • Information gathering:
    • Self-report questionnaires, clinical interviews, observation of interactional patterns
  • Initial conjoint interviews:
    • How the couple met, what attracted them to each other, how they developed a deeper involvement, positive and negative life events, prior experiences in therapy.
    • Inquires about the family members' current concerns and strengths of the relationship.
    • Orients the family to the process of therapy by describing the structure and course of CBFT and the roles of therapist and family.
Technique: Guided Behavior Change
  • Increasing the frequency and magnitude and positive behaviors
  • Love days
  • Date night
  • Departing with a kiss
  • "Work together with the family to develop a series of agreements on how they want to make changes in their relationships to meet the needs of the family members, to help their relationship function effectively, and to interact positively with their environment." (Gurman et al., 2015, p. 36)
Technique: Skill-Based Intervention
  • For sharing thoughts and emotions (p.38)
  • For listening (p. 38)
  • For decision-making conversations (p. 38)
  • "The therapist provides the family with instruction in the use of particular behavioral skills, through didactic discussions and/or other media (e.g., readings, videos). The instruction is followed by opportunities for the family to practice behaving in the new ways."
Interventions that Address Cognitions
  • Selective Attention
    • What each person notices about the partner and the relationship
  • Attributions
    • Inferences about determinants of relationship events
  • Expectancies
    • Predictions of what will occur in the relationship in the future
  • Assumptions
    • What each person believes people and relationships actually are like
  • Standards
    • What each person believes people and relationships should look like
  • Socratic Questioning
    • Understanding the underlying issues through evaluations of the logic of thinking
  • Guided Discovery
    • Clarify each person's standards.
    • Discuss advantages and disadvantages of existing standards.
    • If standards need alteration, help revise them to form new acceptable standards.
    • Problem solve how new standards will be taken into account behaviorally.
    • If partners standards continue to differ, discuss ability to accept differences.
Interventions Focused on Emotions
  • Normalize emotional experiences, positive and negative
  • Clarify thoughts, then relate these to emotions
  • Use questions, reflections, and interpretations to draw out primary emotions
  • Describe emotions through metaphors and images
  • Discourage attempts to distract the self from experiencing emotions
  • Encourage acceptance of the individual's experience by family members
    • For example, asking one family member to recount a particular incident in detail while helping the individual enrich their expression in a way that helps the individual and the family.
Interventions Focused on Negative Emotions
  • Schedule times to discuss emotions and related thoughts with your partner/family member
  • Practice 'health compartmentalization'
  • Seek alternative means to communicate feelings and elicit support
  • Tolerate distressing feelings

Sources of Difficulty for Therapeutic Change

  • Roles become familiar and predictable
  • Knowledge and skill deficit
  • Reluctant to change due to being hurt in the relationship

Treatment Applicability

  • Intimate partner aggression
  • Infidelity
  • Individual psychopathology
  • Health problems
  • Obsessive-compulsive disorder
  • Anorexia nervosa

Empirical Support

  • Widely evaluated
  • Found to be efficacious for distressed couples and families and improving relationship functioning
  • Effective in alleviating distress and improving communication
  • Effects have been found in relatively brief interventions (lasting 8-12 weeks)
  • Finding generalize across continents with experienced and student therapists
  • Most families appear to maintain gains in relational adjustment for up to 1 year.
