3. Behavioral Family Therapy

Behaviorism Basics:

  • Linear thinking:
    • Behavior is based on previous consequences.
  • Major Tenet:
    • Behavior is determined by its consequences more than its antecedents.
  • Types of conditioning:
    • Classical Conditioning
    • Operant Conditioning

Philosophy of Change Framework

  • Big Picture:
    • Behavioral Family Therapists are less likely to talk about how families develop and/or operate. They are concerned about the present, and how the problem is being reinforced.
  • Symptoms:
    • Although they are reluctant to do so, Behavioral Therapists have delineated what distinguishes a 'healthy family' and a 'dysfunctional family' (pg. 69-74 and 97-104 in FT Handbook chapters)

Well Functioning Families:

  • Mutually shared goals and expectations.
  • Support for the stresses associated with the expected transitional life stages, and other stressful life events.
  • Mutual influence, that is positive more than negative.
  • Boundaries (more implicit by separating family into dyads)
  • Tolerance for Deviance
  • Skills in conflict resolution
  • Adaptability
  • Flexibility
  • The ability to change when necessary

Interventions

  • The goal of treatment is to change the problem behavior by changing the consequences of it, or the contingencies of it.
  • Techniques
  • Use of Self

Behavioral Family Therapy

  • Parent Training:
    • Intervention begins with an assessment of the target child's behavior. The goal of the assessment is to get a baseline of the behavior so that you can quantify change.
    • Functional Analysis of Behavior:
      • Once the problem behavior is identified, then the antecedents and consequences surrounding the behavior are explored. (stimuli, organismic variables, target behavior/response, consequences, contingencies).
      • Report the assessment to the family.
    • Intervene in the pattern.
      • Step 1: Induction (teach the lingo)
      • Step 2: Demonstration (show them how it works)
      • Step 3: Implementation (contingency contracts, time-out, star charts, marble game, etc.)
    • Generalization:
      • The goal is that this training will be generalized to other behaviors, but booster sessions are also helpful.

Functional Family Therapy

  • Alexander and Parsons (1982) created for troubled adolescents and their families. Incorporates cognitive aspects and takes a more systemic approach, while the goal is behavioral change.
  • Integrates three perspectives:
    • Ecological
    • Learning Theory
    • Cognitive
    • Two Phases of Treatment

Functional Family Therapy Phases of Treatment

  • Therapy:
    • Designed to modify attitudes, assumptions, expectations, labels and emotions of the family. The major focus is to relabel (reframe) the problem behavior into something positive.
  • Education:
    • Help family behave differently in conjunction with reattributions.

Resistance:

  • Resistance to treatment is considered an impediment to change. It is a natural consequence of the inappropriate application of technology and failure of the clinician to understand the meaning of the behavior.

Meaning of Behaviors

  • Functional Family Therapy relabels behaviors to reduce resistance and to motivate change by providing reattributions for the causes of family members' behaviors.
    • Change in meaning is a necessary precursor to behavior change.
    • The relabeling (reframe, positive connotation, etc.) operates at three levels:
Relabelling:
  • Interactional Level:
    • Benign description of the interpersonal impact of the behavior (distance regulation)
  • Motivational Level:
    • Recasts the motive for the behavior in a benign light.
  • Systemic Level:
    • Emphasizes the similarities between people, a family theme.
  • If the reframe works it interrupts automatic processing, interrupts the behaviors that follow from this processing, and thus, changes the contingencies around the problem behavior.

Functions of Behaviors:

  • Functional Family Therapy emphasizes paying attention to the interpersonal functions of behaviors:
    • Functions:
      • A person's pattern of behaviors, feelings, and thoughts mediate amounts of psychological relatedness in a relationship with another person (distance regulation)
      • The expression of the function can be labeled good, bad, or neutral, the function itself is not judged.
      • These functions are properties of relationships not traits.

Critique

  • Linear Thinking:
    • Cognitive processes need to be incorporated, although some would argue that they were never eliminated in the first place (i.e. cognition is a behavior). Meanings of behaviors, attribution processes, belief sets, automatic thinking can get in the way of contingency change.
  • Unit of analysis tends to be the dyad:
    • Behavior therapists have moved toward more contextual assessments, but each move out focuses on the one to one relationship rather than the system as a whole.
  • Dependency on the therapist?
    • Therapist teaches the model.
  • Based on postivism
    • Not postmodern, it is modern.
  • Politics of behavioral couple therapy, acceptance.

Behavioral Marital Therapy

  • Same process as parent training. Assessment is always first. Social Exchange Theory viewpoint.
  • Could include couple discussing an issue in the room, with therapist observing communication patterns. Also includes evaluating the relationship itself (pg. 106-107).
    • Strengths
    • Major complaints
    • Sex life (affection)
    • Future goals
    • Alternatives to the relationship
    • Individual functioning of each spouse
  • Intervention:
    • Once assessment is complete you set goals with the clients, focusing on one problem (well defined) at a time, this comes in the form of a contract.
      • Major Goals:
        • Increase positive control while decreasing aversive control.
        • Improve communication, which should facilitate future problem solving.
      • Interventions:
        • Active listening with behavioral descriptions of the problem
        • Teach new behavior exchanges
        • Encourage couple to establish clear and effective means of sharing power and decision making.
        • Teach strategies for future problem solving
  • Techniques:
    • Caring Days
      • Make a list of what your partner could do to make you feel cared for. The partner does not need to do anything in return. Do not include sex as it can be seen as coercion.
    • Quid Pro Quo Contracts
      • Not very common because we get into the standstill of 'they have to do it first'
    • Good Faith Contracts
      • Both partners engage in those behaviors whether their partner does or does not.
  • Politics and Power

Integrated Behavioral Couple Therapy

  • Politics of Behavioral Couple Therapy (i.e. Jacobson)
    • Jacobson saw the issue with classic BMT as a problem with power, who gets to say what needs to change?
    • IBCT was developed from these concerns, the focus shifted to acceptance and forgiveness.
  • IBCT Deep Analysis:
    • D
      • Key differences between partners in personality, interests, goals, etc.
    • E
      • Emotional sensitivities or vulnerabilities that each partner brings to the relationship (create a vicious cycle)
    • E
      • External circumstances (i.e., stressful circumstances which could exacerbate the problems created by differences and vulnerabilities)
    • P
      • Patterns of interaction that couples get into as they try to navigate what comes from DEE above.
  • Intervention based on the idea that all relationship problems result from the combination of a triggering action or inaction and a sensitive reaction.
  • They suggest that relationship problems can be resolved by altering the triggering action and the vulnerable response (emotional acceptance).
  • Empirical support for the model in both clinical trials and efficacy research.
