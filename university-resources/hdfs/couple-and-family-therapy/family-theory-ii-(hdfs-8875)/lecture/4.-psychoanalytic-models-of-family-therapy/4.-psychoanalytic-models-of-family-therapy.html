4. Psychoanalytic Models of Family Therapy

Object Relations Theory: Big Picture

  • Most psychoanalytic models are based on Freud's idea around the identity, ego and super ego, or the development of self. For Freud the drives were sexual (in terms of possession and control), for newer psychoanalytic ideas the drives are more around connection and validation of the self (good or bad).
  • Object Relations Theory in some ways provides a bridge between psychodynamic theory and systems theory. We relate to people in the present based on expectations formed by early experience.
  • Normal Development (?)
    • Early development is based on attachment theory/separation-individuation constructs.
    • Infant is born ready to be in a reciprocal relationship. This is activated by relating to a parent, by being loved and loving.
    • Stages of development based on object relations: internal object (mental image of self and other, and self in relation to other).

Stages of Object Relations

  • Objectless (primary narcissism/autistic) there is no other, only self.
  • The following stages focus on the development of self through relations with internal and external objects: first the actual interactions and then the unconscious residue of the interactions.
  • As humans, we exist in the context of relationships not as single units: There is no self without an object, there is no object without a self.
  • Infants develop in a context of concentric 'holding environments.' As infants internalize the guarding, protecting, soothing and nurture of this 'holding environment' they begin to form a self (even if the environment is inadequate).
  • These early experiences then form our 'unconscious' drives for connection or separation. 'Real' others are 'used' to satisfy those drives, and we find others to validate our sense of self (good or bad).

Health/Dysfunction

  • Health: achieving and preserving psychic wholeness through good object relations, integrating our 'unconscious' with our consciousness.
  • Self and its relationships with others rest on and are expressed by the degree to which relations between the self and object are gratifying (but some theorists would suggest that since we are so narcissistic in the beginning, the relationships with others are never gratifying enough. Is it possible to be healthy?)

Symptoms

  • Fairbairn suggest that as we internalize objects we are inevitably disappointed by them, in order to defend against this pain, we do the follow:
    • We split off those aspects that are too painful to be tolerated.
    • Repress these intolerable aspects.
    • This modifies the ego.
  • Pathology occurs when some aspects of the repressed self-object system are excessively repressed, leaving the central self depleted with an impoverished relationship to the 'ideal object.'
  • Other forms of pathology occur when the central self system is taken over by one of the usual repressed systems, such as those whose relations are characterized by continual patterns of distance and rage with the apparent disappearance of reality.
  • Fear-dictated flight from early object relations is also considered the deepest root of psychological problems.

Dysfunctional Family

  • Skynner (1981)
    • Diffuse boundaries
    • Unclear identities
    • Satisfaction from fantasy as opposed to reality
    • Difficulty coping with separation and loss
    • Attempts to preserve past states of relationships
    • Attempts to manipulate others into rigidly held views of them

Intervention

  • The major goal of Object Relations Theory is to free family members of unconscious restrictions so that they will be able to interact with one another as whole, healthy persons on the basis of current realities rather than unconscious images.
    • The encounter between the therapist and client is the intervention. The relationship is the crucible for change and growth.
    • The therapeutic process is the same as 'normal development' during infancy. The therapist provides the holding environment, contains/introjects the clients' emotions, resolves them, understands them, somehow makes them acceptable, and then the client introjects the resolution of the emotions. Therapist projects back a 'corrective experience.'
    • Self of therapist is key:
      • Transference and counter-transference are the keystones of any psychoanalytic therapy, meaning the therapist must constantly be aware of who they are' for the client, and who the client is for them.
    • Change occurs through insight and working through.
    • Techniques:
      • Listening
      • Empathy
      • Interpretation and maintaining neutrality with respect to what or how things change

Byng-Hall (1995) Family Scripts

  • Big Picture (how families operate/develop):
    • He uses attachment theory as a base. Early attachment relationships create 'working models' of how relationships are supposed to be, they create 'scripts.' as people interact with each other to become a family, they create their own 'family script' based on these early 'working models.' families have attachment patterns that are full of meanings about abandonment, engulfment, intrusion, etc. (i.e., distance regulation)
    • These scripts provide routines for family life which can be followed automatically. They are shared expectations of how family roles are to be performed within various contexts.
    • These scripts may be carried out from generation to generation in one form or another. Some use replicative scripts (repeat what has happened in the past), while others use corrective scripts (trying not to repeat scripts from the family of origin). There is a constant tension between these two.
    • Healthy vs. Dysfunctional families
      • Healthy families have scripts that provide a set of roles with which each member can identify and which provides room for improvisation when facing new situations. These scripts promote interactional awareness:
        • Consequences of interaction
        • Self-awareness (why you are acting in a particular way)
        • Empathic awareness (why others are acting in particular ways)
        • Understanding the meaning of the interaction
        • Shared coherent perception of what is happening

Symptoms

  • Symptoms occur when there is difficulty updating scripts. They may occur because the script necessary remains someone or something too painful to relive. Intolerable information is blocked or edited both intraphysically and interpersonally (secrets)\
  • Symptoms may also occur when a family script is used to prevent what members may fear to be an even worse disaster. Even though the current script is painful, it is rigidly clung to (the solution has become to problem)

Interventions

  • Major goal is improvisation (flexibility). In order to create an environment safe enough to explore options however, the therapist must provide a secure base from which to explore. Paradoxically, scripts, although repetitive, provide the background against which change can occur.
  • Attachment notions
    • Secure
    • Curious
    • Improvisation
    • Uncertainty
    • Insecure
    • Avoid strangeness
    • Stick with the familiar

Difference from other Psychoanalytic Models

  • Instead of conflict in the family arising out of one person's pathology which extends to influence another's behavior, family scripts are encoded in all family members' minds, but each family member plays a particular role. Therefore, each member of the family can be taught to see how their role contributes to the problem, rather than asking the 'sick one' to reown the bad object and working through these issues.

Intervention Strategies

  • Reduce strategies that undermine problem-solving abilities (i.e., triangulating, scapegoating, etc.)
  • Reduce strategies that undermine security (threats about leaving, control, etc.)
  • Move toward collaboration rather than coercion and blame.
  • Re-edit scripts so the roles are fillable by the individuals in the family.
  • Move closer to an ideal script in which a wide scope of improvising is possible.
  • Develop a hopeful script.
  • Promote ownership of family's success, attributable to them not you as the therapist.

Transference/Countertransference

  • This is still important for the therapist to attend to. The therapist needs to contain the family's emotions in a way so as not to become another player in their script. Therapists own scripts can also be replayed, thus it is essential that the therapist understand their own family script. Byng-Hall outlines methods for staying out of the script and for containing emotional impact:
    • Staying out:
      • Use teams, get supervision, have a clear plan, use circular questioning
      • Containing:
        • Reflecting on the process, seeing emotions as valuable information, differentiating your script from the family's, share your emotional experience with the family.

Critique of Psychoanalytic Theories

  • Absolves people of responsibility for their behavior (it is my mother's fault)
  • Lack of empirical testing
  • Tautological thinking:
    • Make an interpretation, it is rejected, patient is resistant, you must be onto something.
  • Perspective is individualist or dyadic without revision.
  • Deterministic, linear thinking (it is my mother's fault)
  • Blaming the victim (you put yourself in this situation, because of what your mother did)
  • Mother blaming
  • Long process
