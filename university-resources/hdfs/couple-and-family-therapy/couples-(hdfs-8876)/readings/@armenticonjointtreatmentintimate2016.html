Abstract

Gender-speciﬁc men-only interventions for intimate partner violence (IPV) have had limited success beyond the effects of arrest alone. This review outlines current U.S. state-mandated treatments for IPV, acknowledges how conjoint treatment may better address a subtype of IPV that is not motivated by power and control, examines and reviews previously conducted couples-based treatments, and highlights policy implications and future directions. Empirical ﬁndings and theoretical support for the Duluth model, cognitive– behavioral approaches, and IPV typologies are presented, followed by a systematic review of selected quasi- and true experimental studies on couplesbased approaches for IPV. U.S. state guidelines that absolutely exclude conjoint approaches are overly restrictive, considering the poor evidence of effectiveness for current gender-speciﬁc groups. Further, the theoretical foundations of most men-only groups assume unilateral, male-to-female violence, which does not ﬁt at least one-third of court-involved cases and leaves treatment needs of couples experiencing problematic relationship dynamics unmet. For a carefully screened subset of couples that experience predominantly “situational violence,” conjoint communication and relationship skills training groups may be a viable alternative to the Duluth model and cognitive–behavioral men-only groups.

Paper Summary for Discussion

Introduction

  • "Some U.S. state guidelines are so restrictive that they appear to prohibit anything except a Duluth model intervention." (p. 109)
    • "No true experiments have found the Duluth model to be effective." (pg. 109)
  • "The intervention piece of the Duluth model has minimal impact on reducing recidivism over and above the effect of arrest and probation alone." (p. 110)
  • "Psychologists tried to improve men's group outcomes by incorporating cognitive-behavioral techniques known to be effective in treating other externalizing behaviors, including anger and substance abuse issues." (p. 110)
    • "The outcome statistics on these cognitive-behavioral men-only groups are disappointing, comparable with those of the Duluth model men's group." (p. 110)
  • "No single current battering intervention modality outperforms any other and there is room for improvement in adequately reducing IPV recidivism." (p. 110)
  • Primary Goal of Journal Article:
    • "The primary aim of this review is to introduce the potential utility of conjoint intervention as an alternative approach to both Duluth model and Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT) interventions for a certain subset of intimate partner abusers."
    • "Reviewed studies included treatment conditions in which both members of a couple experiencing IPV were involved in direct intervention and follow-up measures obtained from a combination of victims only, both victims and perpetrators or conviction reports."

The Duluth Model

  • "Beginning in the 1980s, the Duluth model men’s groups were implemented as a deferred-adjudication intervention, prompted by judges so that the influx of new arrests for IPV did not overwhelm jails and prisons." (p. 110)
  • "The Duluth model was based on ideology and field-based interviewing rather than scientific testing." (p. 110)
  • "The Duluth model creators developed the “power and control wheel,” which conceptualizes IPV as one form of abuse to maintain male privilege." (p. 110)
  • The primary objective is to abandon authoritarian relationships and achieve effective forms of behavior that fall on the “equality wheel,” which fosters an egalitarian mindset within relationships. In this model, re-educating men is the goal." (p. 111)
  • "This model focuses predominantly on maladaptive beliefs held by men regarding women, IPV, and gender stereotypes." (p. 111)
  • "The Duluth model curriculum posits that the mechanism of change is replacing maladaptive attitudes held by men. Besides using the “Time-Out” technique, it does not provide strategies for functional changes in relationship dynamics." (p. 111)

Cognitive-Behavior Approaches

  • "Like the Duluth model intervention, cognitive–behavioral therapy (CBT) interventions are also typically administered to men in a group setting." (p. 111)
  • "Treatment places the importance of examining and eliminating violent behavior at the forefront." (p. 111)
  • "Violence is considered to be learned behavior and must in turn be replaced by learned nonviolent behavior. Similarly to the Duluth model intervention, this type of intervention seeks to rehabilitate the sole perpetrator of the IPV." (p. 111)
  • "Cognitive–behavioral interventions for IPV outline the functionality of violent behavior, explore the pros and cons of using violence to solve problems, and propose alternative solutions for dealing with antecedents of violence." (p. 111)
    • "These strategies include cognitive restructuring of negative attributions, anger management techniques, and resocialization." (p. 111)
    • "Unlike the Duluth model, cognitive– behavioral interventions adopt an explicit therapeutic rather than psychoeducational stance in an effort to reduce violent behavior." (p. 111)
  • Although there is a proliferation of typologies of IPV perpetrators, rarely is intervention tailored to the needs of specific types of perpetrators. (p. 111)
  • Survey data from a national longitudinal sample suggested reciprocal or bilateral violence, with both partners perpetuating violence against each other, occurs in 50% of U.S. couples reporting IPV, while another study suggested that the rate of couples experiencing bidirectional violence is as high as 60%. (p. 112)
  • Treatment approaches that assume unilateral, male-to-female violence may not address a significant portion of the types of problems that couples present with when seeking treatment. (p. 112)

Johnson's Typologies of IPV

  • First called Patriarchal Terrorism then to Intimate Partner Terrorism and then to Coercive Controlling Violence
    • Patriarchal terrorism exemplifies men’s systematic use of control tactics against women, which can include violence but also economic subordination, isolation, and threats. (p. 112)
    • Both men and women can perpetuate. (p. 112)
    • This involves severe forms of severe forms of violence with a goal of dominating or controlling one's partner, and is often fueled by positive attitudes about violence in general. (p. 112)
    • Coercive controlling violence is also a hallmark feature of the later coined term 'characterological violence.' (p. 112)
      • A perpetrator of predominantly characterological violence is likely to be violent in all intimate relationships. (p. 112)
  • Common Couple Violence (to Situational Couple Violence)
    • Common couple violence was created to serve as a gender-neutral term, and is thought to result from relationship conflict that leads to violence, rather than patriarchic motivations related to control. (p. 112)
    • Common couple violence, a label that potentially minimized the seriousness of the problem, was changed to situational couple violence. (p. 112)
    • Situational violence includes behavior such as pushing, grabbing, and slapping, and typically occurs with lower frequency. This type of violence is not motivated by underlying desires to dominate and control one’s partner, but rather occurs as a result of situational stressors within a relationship that are repeatedly remedied by using violence as a problem-solving strategy. (p. 112)
    • The manifestation of recurring, bilateral IPV is usually sustained by interactive and dyadic factors, such as distorted emotions, thoughts, and beliefs among intimate partners. Such factors contribute to relationship dysfunction associated with bilateral IPV, and situational violence is its most common form. (p. 112)
    • For couples that experience situational violence, the violence may stop when partners enter new relationships with less volatile partners. (p. 112)
    • Characterological violence is characterized by a pathological desire for power and control, but even levels of coercive control may fall on a continuum. (p. 112)
  • Appropriateness for conjoint treatment will vary depending on levels of coercive violence, and whether characterological or situational features dominate the overall picture of violence. (p. 113)

Criticisms of Conjoint Interventions

  • Female victims of violence who had participated in conjoint therapy said that they often felt intimated or afraid in therapy and refrained from sharing these feelings with their therapists. (p. 113)
  • Victim blaming is always a possibility in the context of court-mandated interventions. Men sent to jail or to any type of mandated intervention often blame their female partners for putting them in such a situation. (p. 113)
  • Placing partner-violent men in a group setting can in actuality offer justification from other group members that beliefs about using violence in intimate partner relationships is acceptable. (p. 113)

Evidence for Conjoint Interventions

  • Failure to address dyadic conflict that occurs in relationships may contribute to further perpetration of violence, instead of protect individuals from it. (p. 113)
  • To date, eight studies have tested couples approaches for addressing IPV. (p. 113)
    • One study concluded that conjoint interventions for IPV can be used safely, at least if they are used in conjunction with probation, and especially for couples with alcohol problems. (p. 113)
    • Results from two studies showed that BCT lowered IPV in alcoholic men from 60% to 24% for men who continued to drink following treatment and to 12% for men who abstained from alcohol following treatment. (p. 114)
  • Women who were victims of intimate terrorism/characterological violence would not—and should not—attend conjoint groups.

Intervention for Situational Violence: Creating Healthy Relationships Program

  • To carefully and systematically designate which couples would be appropriate for conjoint treatment, researchers have created and validated the Situational Violence Screening Tool (SVST). (p. 118)
    • Both men and women are interviewed to rule out controlling behavior, fear, severe abuse, drug and alcohol abuse, and antisocial and borderline personality disorder features. (p. 118)
  • Validated screening tools are a critical first step for identifying situationally violent couples for whom conjoint interventions may be particularly effective. (p. 118)
  • Creating Healthy Relationships Program (CHRP)
    • The CHRP is a 22-week multi-couple group program that focuses primarily on skills including effective conflict management and creating a shared meaning within the relationship. This program is based on the sound relationship house theory, which emphasizes factors that contribute to relationship satisfaction and longevity in committed relationships.
