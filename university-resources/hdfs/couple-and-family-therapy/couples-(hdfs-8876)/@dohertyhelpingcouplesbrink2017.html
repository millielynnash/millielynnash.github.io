1. Background and Overview of Discernment Counseling

  • Mixed-Agenda Couples
    • Clinical presentation that may account for up to 30% of couples in clinical practice.
    • These are couples in which one spouse is seriously considering divorce and is reluctant to try couples therapy (but does show up for the first session) while the other spouse wants to preserve the marriage and is eager to start couples therapy.
      • We refer to the first spouse as leaning-out and the second as leaning in.
  • Discernment Counseling creates a holding environment for the couple to develop clarity and confidence in deciding on the next step for their relationship, whether it is divorce or a shared commitment to couples therapy to repair the relationship.

The Clinical Challenges

  • Proceeding with standard couple assessment and intervention risks alienating the leaning-out spouse who is resistant or ambivalent about doing therapy. Holding back from starting couples therapy risks alienating the leaning-in partner who is ready to go and may be desperate to stabilize a rocky marriage.
  • Sometimes the divergent goals do not become clear until near the end of the first session after the therapist has done an initial assessment and outlined a provisional treatment plan.
  • The therapist may fall into one of two traps:
    • Trying to talk the leaning-out spouse into trying therapy for a number of sessions.
      • This can put you into an alliance with the leaning-in spouse who may have been insisting they try couples therapy.
      • In doing so, you risk pursuing the distancer, a strategy that works poorly in marriage and therapy.
      • Sometimes the leaning-out spouse reluctantly goes along with, say, a contract for 3 months of couples therapy as an experiment to see whether things can change. But typically this spouse is not invested personally in the therapy; the burden falls on the leaning-in spouse to start making meaningful changes that will satisfy the other partner's desire to see progress. The therapy starts out unbalanced, with one customer or change agent and one observer or evaluator.
      • Although trying to persuade an ambivalent spouse to try couples therapy is better than giving up on the marriage, it carries two risks that discernment counseling aims to avoid: allying with one partner and starting half-hearted couples therapy.
    • Witholding any intervention plan until the couple is clear about their goals.
      • Although they may believe this is a neutral stance, it ends up siding with the leaning-out spouse by offering nothing to deal with the impasse presented by competing agendas - in effect, saying to the leaning-in partner, "I can't help you with your marriage unless your spouse agrees to try."
      • Another version of giving up is to try to get the leaning-in spouse to hear the reluctance of the other partner to stay in the marriage and then to accept the inevitability of divorce.
      • Either way, the therapist distances themselves from the couple's dilemma by offering no meaningful way to deal with their differences over where to take the relationship.
    • Some therapists work with mixed-agenda couples by offering several conjoint assessment sessions before making a decision on therapy or divorce.
      • This approach is not in the category of a trap (because it does not create an alliance with one spouse), but it does involve the tactical mistake of seeing both spouses together in the room for this assessment and consensus building.
      • The central problem is that this approach misses the reality that the decision to stay or leave is first an individual one, a personal choice made by each partner.
        • The decision is best pursued in individual conversations with the therapist (the discernment counseling approach) rather than in conjoint sessions in which there is likely to be pressure to conform or resist. Having both spouses in the room for open-ended exploration invites the partners to share everything the think and feel in front of each other - a risky proposition in the face of possible rejection and divorce.
      • Doing pre-therapy assessment primarily with both partners together in the room often leads to either painful disclosures with little immediate chance for healing because there is no contract for therapy, or to withholding and playing safe, which can lead to paralysis, after which someone announces that therapy is not working.

Origins of Discernment Counseling

  • The discernment counseling protocol was inspired by Betty Carter, who developed (but never published) an original way to work with mixed-agenda couples.
    • There are two key insights.
      • The first was that couples who are divided on whether to stay together bring such different needs and agendas to couples therapy that they should each be worked with separately, in individual conversations. The therapist helps the leaning-out spouse make a decision on whether to divorce or stay and work on the marriage. The therapist helps the leaning-in spouse bring their best self forward to try to save the marriage.
      • The second key insight was that both spouses can tolerate knowing that the therapist is helping the other with a different, and seemingly competing, agenda.
  • Discernment counseling became something more formal and public beginning in 2008, with Doherty and Bruce Peterson, a family court judge who was interesting in how many couples in the divorce process were open to reconciliation.
    • Judge Peterson used two analogies:
      • Filing for divorce is like getting on a superhighway with no exit ramps or rest stops, and it is also like having a trap door open and falling down a chute that leads to only one destination.
      • Findings found that 30% of spouses in the divorce process (measured at the individual level) expressed interest in reconciliation services, and in 10% of couples, both partners independently expressed that interest.
  • Discernment counseling was the term coined because the goal was for couples to work through the complexities of their situation and decide on a future direction for their marriage. (the term counseling is used deliberately to distinguish the approach from what people think of as problem-solving, relationship-improving couples therapy).

Overview of Discernment Counseling

  • Discernment counseling is a short-term process (one to five sessions) with the goal of greater clarity and confidence in the decision about a direction for the marriage, based on a deeper understanding of what has happened to the marriage and each partner's contribution to the problems.
    • It is not intended for cases in which one partner has made a final decision to divorce and simply wants a venue to convince their spouse to accept the decision.
      • The immediate decision is whether to carve out a 6-month period of all-out effort in couples therapy to restore the marriage to health, with divorce off the table during that time. At the end of 6 months, they can reopen the decision about divorce on the basis of what they have learned about the prospect of successfully rebuilding their marriage.
    • Therapy aims for improvement in the relationship (e.g., increased intimacy, decreased toxic conflict), whereas discernment counseling aims for clarity and confidence about a next step. The goal of discernment counseling is not to solve marriage problems but to see whether they can be solved.
    • There is no relationship homework for couples in discernment counseling, no teaching of skills during sessions, and no enactments of deeper ways to connect.
    • If clients ask for ways to manage their problems during discernment counseling, we use a 'restraint from change' approach by reminding them that relationship change should not be expected during discernment counseling.
  • Path 1:
    • The marraige as it has been: the status quo, neither divorce nor working in therapy to improve the relationship.
  • Path 2:
    • Separation or divorce
  • Path 3:
    • A 6-month commitment to couples therapy (and sometimes other forms of help, such as substance abuse counseling, parenting classes, or individual therapy) with divorce off the table and with the possibility of putting divorce back on the table at the end of the 6 months.
    • The idea behind path 3 is to move beyond amibalence about working on change and see whether it is possible to put the marriage into a place that would be good for both partners.
    • Path 3, as we emphasize repeatedly, involves each partner developing a personal agenda for change on the basis of a deeper understanding of what has happened to the marriage and their role in the problems.
  • An advantage of framing the immediate outcomes as paths is that it moves clients past the dichotomous choice between staying together or breaking-up.
    • The therapist too can support the idea that something has to change - the marriage cannot continue as it has been. So sometimes the discernment process begins with agreements instead of disagreement - something has to change; the old ways of being married is no longer viable.
    • Implicitly we are saying that divorce is not a failure in this process; it is simply one of the options to be explored in a serious way, even thought only one spouse is actively considering it.
    • Sometimes it has taken years to build up the courage to put divorce on the table in a chronically distressed marriage. Path 3 avoids the 'in or out' conundrum by offering a provisional commitment to work hard on the relationship for a period, with divorce not an option for this period but something that can be revisited later if things do not change enough.
  • Most of the work occurs in separate conversations with each spouse, with carefully orchestrated sharing of take-home messages from the individual conversations.
    • The flow of the sessions goes this way:
      1. Time with the couple together
      2. Meeting with one spouse alone (the other goes to the waiting room)
      3. The other spouse returns and the first spouse summarizes key learning from the individual conversation
      4. Meeting with the second spouse alone
      5. The second spouse shares learning from that conversation
      6. A check out with both spouses during which time the therapist shares reflections on the process thus far.
    • The majority of the time is with each partner alone.
    • Times when both parties are together in the room are carfully orchestrated to minimize demoralizing exchanges and to maximize the focus on what each is learning about themselves in the process, rather than what each wants the other to change.
    • The individual conversations have both similar and different areas of focus for the leaning-in and leaning-out partners. similar themes are helping each better understand the dynamics of the relationship (their relational dance), their individual contributions to the problems, and how these relational and personal issues could be addressed in couples therapy (and sometimes with other helping resources). In terms of different themes, the therapist helps the leaning-out spouse figure out which path to take on the basis of an exploration of the sources of the person’s pain and discouragement about the prospect of change. The focus is on making a decision that has integrity for one’s self and others on the basis of a deeper understanding of self and the marriage and how the problems could be addressed in couples therapy if that is the chosen direction.
    • The limit of five sessions, although not immutable, creates an expectation of intense work within a limited time frame.
      • Going on much longer also makes it difficult for the couple not to expect relationship change and for the therapist to not lapse into doing couple interventions.
      • We say one to five sessions and not five sessions because clients are asked each time about whether they want to do an additional session up to five.
  • some people are emotionally flat when their spouse is in the room and then liven up when our one-to-one time begins.

2. Theory and Research

  • There has been strikingly little clinical literature on how to work with these couples.
  • One study recommended slowing down the decision-making process about divorce and then negotiating for a number of therapy sessions.
  • No research study offered a detailed protocol for working with mixed-agenda couples or an evaluation of the approach the author used.

3. Communicating with Clients Before Starting Discernment Counseling

  • A big part of this approach is helping clients see the difference between traditional marital therapy and discernment counseling and allowing them to indicate their willingness to engage in the process.
  • We are also interested in knowing exactly what the couple knows about Discernment Counseling and what they hope to get out of a discernment counseling experience.
    • The clients must leave the conversation with an understanding that the goal for discernment counseling is to help them gain greater clarity and confidence in their decision making about the future of their marriage.
    • If your practice has a website, include a page describing discernment counseling so potential clients can educate and even screen themselves before calling to set up the first appointment.

History and Current Situation

  • We pay specific attention to whether they are currently living together and the status of any legal proceedings associated with divorce, including whether anyone has consulted with a lawyer. If so, do they have the lawyers on retainer, and how far along are they in the legal divorce process?
  • After collecting some basic information over the phone, we typically ask something such as, "Can you tell me a bit about your current situation and how do you think it might be helpful to your situation?"
    • It is recommended to ask them how they heard of discernment counseling. Knowing this source may also provide clues about how far into the formal divorce process they are.

Talking with the Leaning-Out Partner Over the Phone

  • We listen specifically for which member of the couple is leaning-out of the relationship. Leaning-out partners often play a key role in determining whether couples come in at all for a first session.
  • It is important that leaning-out partners feel heard and that they understand how this process is different from anything they have tried as a couple before. It is also important to assess whether leaning-out partners have made a decision to divorce or are open to the work of discernment. The following questions can be helpful in determining this:
    • "Would you say that you've made your decision about the future of your marriage or are you still seeking some level of clarity in this process?"
    • If they report that they have made a decision, you can ask about their level of confidence in the decision: "How confident are you that you're making the right decision?"
  • Those who call you will still be seeking some level of clarity in their decision making. They may say they are 'pretty sure' they want a divorce but feel they need to go through this process just to be 'sure,' or they may feel they 'owe it to' their partner, their marriage, or even their children. Both of these situations represent an appropriate use of the discernment counseling model.
  • For those leaning-out partners who indicate they know they want a divorce and would like to use the session to 'break the news' to their partner, or for those who say they do not know how to tell their partner, you can inform them that this is not consistent with the goals of discernment counseling. You might respond in the following manner:
    • "It may be that discernment counseling is not exactly a good fit for what you want to do. The goals of discernment counseling are to help people get a greater sense of clarity and confidence in their decision making about a direction for the future of their marriage based on a better understanding of what happened to the marriage to get it to the brink of divorce and each person's role in that. If you're hoping for a different outcome, like helping your spouse understand why you want a divorce, discernment counseling is not appropriate for your situation."
  • For those who ultimately want to help to annouce their divorce intentions, you can point them to other services such as divorce mediation or 'closure counseling.'

Talking with the Leaning-In Partner Over the Phone

  • When talking with the leaning-in partner at the intake, you should gather their perspective on the current situation in the marriage.
    • Usually, the leaning-in partner makes it clear that they want to preserve the marriage, but if not, ask that question directly. Often the leaning-in partner is desperate to do something, anything, to save the marriage.
    • In these situations, you can suggest that they purchase and begin reading Michele Weiner-Davis's (2002) book, The Divorce Remedy.
      • They learn that they must first look at themselves and their own behavior before the marriage can get to a healthier place. You might use the following language to make this recommendation:
        • "Her book is written specifically for people who have recently found out that their spouse is considering divorce. She gives some practical advice on how you can act toward your partner at this stage of the process. There are probably things you can be working on that will make the situation somewhat better without making your partner feel as though he or she is committing to working on the relationship. This approach may be your best option at the moment."
  • We cannot emphasize enough the value of respecting client autonomy during this process. Never assume that both parties are equally interested in attending discernment counseling.
    • Discernment counseling is a way of meeting both parties where they are and not privileging one person's agenda over the other.
      • "It is really important that your spouse feel as though they have a choice in whether to come to discernment counseling. If he or she feels forced into it, it won't go well and will likely contribute to more estrangement from you on their part. "
  • Never assume that because one spouse has told you about something that has been happening in the relationship, the other spouse knows about it.

One-on-One Nature of the Phone Conversation

  • The intake conversation must be conducted one on one, without the person's partner or anyone else in the room. The person on the intake phone call has to be able to speak freely about their perspective on the problems they are having and the struggles they are experiencing making a decision regarding the future of their marriage.
    • "Are you in a place that you can speak freely without interruptions?"
  • If there is noise or other distractions, you may want to suggest a different time or place for the call. You also should ascertain whether the client has told their spouse about discernment counseling.
    • "Have you discussed discernment counseling with your spouse? What do you think is his or her level of interest in participating? Does your spouse know that you and I are speaking today?"
  • Avoid the temptation to speak to one partner right after the other for the sake of convenience. It is probably worth taking the time at the phone intake to ensure that no one is feeling forced into having this conversation.
  • There is also benefit to suggesting that the first partner you speak with explain to their partner that they have spoken with a discernment counselor and that if the spouse is interested, they can e-mail or phone the counselor to set up an interview.
    • In no case should you cold call the other partner, who may not be aware of the initial contact from the spouse.
    • Consider using the following script as a guide to the initiator of the phone call:
      • "It sounds like discernment counseling is appropriate for your situation according to what you've told me. The next step is for your partner and me to chat about their take on the situation and whether they are interested in discernment counseling. What might be a good idea for you is to tell your partner that we talked today, and point her to the website that describes discernment counseling. Further, you can tell them that if they are interested in doing this work, they can contact me directly and we can have a similar conversation to the one you and I just had. What is important here is that no one feels coerced to be part of this process. By putting the ball squarely in their court, they can demonstrate their willingness to do discernment counseling by initiating the call. "
    • Respect the autonomy of each person to engage in this process at their own pace.

Share the Purpose, Goals, and Process of Discernment Counseling

  • The phone intake is to describe, in sufficient detail, the purpose, goals, and process of discernment counseling. This overview helps ensure that you and the clients are on the same page with regard to expectation.
    • It is important to point out how discernment counseling is different from marital therapy. The following is some language you can use:
      • "The goal of discernment is to help couples arrive at greater clarity and confidence in their decision making about the future of their marriage based on a deeper understanding of the problems in the marriage and each person's contributions to them. "
      • "Discernment counseling is typically a brief counseling modality and could last anywhere between one and five sessions. There is no need to commit to all five sessions because each of you would only commit to attending one session at a time. "
      • "The format of the sessions consists of meeting as a couple with the counselor as well as having some time dedicated to one-on-one work with the counselor. After each one-on-one portion of the session, each partner will summarize what he or she is 'taking with them' from the time with the counselor and share it with the partner who was waiting outside the room. The first session is 2 hours, and subsequent sessions are an hour and a half each. "
      • "In discernment counseling, we discuss the paths that are before every couple on the brink of divorce. Path 1 we call the status quo path and represents your relationship continuing the way it current is. Path 2 represents the separation or divorce path. Path 3 is the possible reconciliation path, in which the couple agrees to take divorce off the table for a 6-month period of couples therapy, and each one commits to work on the relationship to see whether they can get the relationship to a healthier place. These three paths frame the discussions we have in discernment counseling."
      • "Discernment counseling is different from typical marriage counseling. The goal is not to solve your problems but to help you decide whether to try to solve your problems or choose a different path. It's not about immediate change in your relationship but seeing what changes each of you might need to make in the future if you decide to work on yourselves and your marriage."
      • Finally, you can end this portion of the phone intake by asking how this process sounds to them and whether they are still interested on the basis of what you have discussed: "Does this sound like a process that might hold some value for you in your current situation?"

Safety Screening

  • We screen for safety and whether the couple has a history or any current experiences with intimate partner violence (IPV). What we are mostly concerned with is whether one of the partners is feeling coerced to participate and whether participating in discernment counseling will put one of the partners at risk of IPV.
    • "We want to make sure everyone who pariticpates in discernment counseling does so voluntarily and that no one is put at risk. So I have a few questions that we ask everyone who contacts us for an appointment.
      • Are you feeling pressured by your spouse to participate in this project? Yes / No.
        • If yes, does the pressure seem coercive (for example, is it accompanied by threats with personal or legal ramifications)? Yes / No
      • Have you or your spouse ever had an order of protection from a court? Yes / No
        • If yes, who?
    • My last questions are about what happens when you and your partner get into arguments or fights. Again, our goal is to make sure everyone is safe.
      • Has your partner ever done something to you out of anger that has caused you physical pain, bruises, or bodily injury? Yes / No (If yes, get specifics)
      • Do you ever feel afraid of your spouse? Yes / No
        • If yes, are you in danger now? Yes / No
      • If yes to either, I can help you talk to someone at an agency that can help you. Would you like me to give you that information? In an emergency, urge the person to call 911. Ask if you can call back to check on the person.
  • After having screened for safety and seeing no red flags, the individual on the phone is told that they seem to be an appropriate candidate for discernment counseling. Remember, it is not enough to just ask one partner these questions; both spouses have to be screened for this assessment to have validity.

Conclusion

  • Respecting autonomy and confidentiality is also a primary goal of the intake process.
  • When intakes are correctly conducted, each spouse should feel heard and respected and believe that the discernment counseling process has something to offer each of them, regardless of their leaning-in or leaning-out status.

4. First Session Protocol

  • We strongly recommend a 2-hour block of time because of the amount of ground to be covered in the session. After both partners' perspectives are heard in response to a series of questions, the discernment counselor meets one-on-one with both members of the couple, and each briefly shares what he or she learned in the individual conversations. The session ends with the couple in the room together for a summary of the work that was done that day and any impressions the discernment counselor has to share. If both spouses agree, the second discernment counseling session is then scheduled.

Opening Session with Both Spouses Together

  • With Mixed-Agenda Couples, neither is much in touch specifically with their partner’s perspective, and often their estrangement can lead them to have little interest in understanding the other’s view.
    • In this first meeting together, each person is asked to share their own and listen to the other’s views on how the relationship got to the point where divorce is a possibility, how they have tried to fix the problems they have identified, and how their children (if any) play a role in their decision making. They are also asked to provide a brief account of a time when things were going well in the relationship.
    • The important part in this session is that both people feel heard and respected.
  • The discernment counselor begins this session with the first question:
    • "What do you hope to get out of our time together today?"
    • After each responds with his or her hopes for the session, you can either move on to the next question or simply acknowledge what you have heard.
  • Sometimes people’s expectations are not directly in accord with the goals of discernment counseling.
    • You can gently nudge them in the direction of more appropriate expectations for the 2-hour block of time:
      • "I know that you’re looking for answers and that right now things are really tough in this marriage, but I’m not sure this session will fully answer whether divorce or reconciliation is the right direction. And we won’t be trying to solve your marriage problems today. My hope is that the things we discuss today will help launch a conversation that contributes to you both being more clear and confident in how you’ll choose to proceed with your marriage."

Four Standard Questions

  • Continuing with both people in the room, there is a set of questions that all clients are asked in the first session.
    • From the four key questions that launch discernment counseling, you should begin to get a sense of the couple’s overall relationship and some of the patterns that have governed their interactions. This is key to helping the couple see their current struggles in a new light. It is not just the recognition of the problems but seeing one’s role in the problems in the relationship and patterns of interaction.
    • Pay careful attention to the pain that is driving the leaning-out spouse to put divorce on the table and to whether the leaning-in spouse seems to understand that pain.
  • Sometimes there is such a disconnect between the two narratives that you have a sense that neither partner is hearing a core message of the other. You can then offer the following brief exercise:
    • "I’d like each of you to take a moment to reflect on whether there is something important you want your spouse to hear that you are not sure he or she is hearing. Take a moment to reflect silently, and then I invite you to say it."
  • Opening section of the session: Time management is crucial. Aim for no more than 40 minutes for the initial couple part of the first session, which means posing the questions, taking notes, nonverbally tracking, and just occasionally intervening to clarify and maintain the process of each speaking for him- or herself.
Divorce Narrative Question:
  • "What's happened to your marriage that's gotten you to the point where divorce is a possibility?"
  • You can ask either person to start, and you can ask either person to start, adn you can acknowledge that each partner will probably have a unique take on this question.
  • At times you may have to be a bit of a traffic cop to control the flow of the conversation and to stop one person from interrupting to 'correct' the story of the other. We regularly find ourselves saying things such as,
    • "It sounds to me you don't quite agree on how they are presenting the story of how you got to this place in your marriage. That's understandable. In a moment I am going to ask you the same question because I know that you may see things somewhat differently. I'm mostly concerned with getting the views from both of you on how things got to be the way they are."
  • Frequently one member of the couple responds for both people, as if they speak for the couple and there is high agreement on their version of the divorce narrative. When we hear this, we can stop the conversation and remind the person who is talking to speak only for themselves.
    • "I know you have a picture of how things got to be the way they are, but I'm concerned with the way you are referring to 'we' instead of 'I.' For example, you just said, "We know we'll always love each other." That may be true for purposes of our conversation, I want to be really clear that each of you speaks only for yourselves - your thoughts and experiences. So, I'm wondering whether what you really meant to say was that you'll always love them. I just want to make sure I'm not reading into anything you say and that both of you understand that the other may have a unique perspective on the situation."
  • We intervene when we hear these types of 'diagnostic' impressions. What we meant to avoid is one partner being an 'expert' on the other's psychological state. It takes away from one of the goals of the discernment process, which is for each person to see his or her own unique contributions to the decline of the marriage.
  • It is important to hear each person so you can empathize with each one of them.
  • A good discernment counselor has to convey that they understand both perspectives and are willing to help both partners. Another reason to know the divorce narrative well is to begin to hear and even map out the couple's relationship pattern.
    • Most of them have never realized that their interactional pattern is predictable, routine, and can be changed and modified through good couples therapy.
  • The process of asking for a divorce or initiating a divorce flies in the face of the decision and commitment to marry someone.
  • In discernment counseling, we get the divorce narratives of both partners so we can refer to them in future conversations.
Repair Narrative Question:
  • "Can you tell me what you've done to try and fix the problems in your marriage, whether on your own or with help?"
  • This might be because they are not sure what “counts” as a repair attempt. so, we often suggest things that might have happened:
    • "When you say you haven’t really done anything, I’m wondering whether there are things you actually have done, but you’re just not giving yourself credit for them. For example, you may have read a self-help book or connected with a therapist, even if it was just a temporary thing. Sometimes people seek out a wise family member to bounce ideas off but don’t consider this as “actively doing something” about their marital problems. When you consider all the ways you may have tried to fix things, what comes to mind?"
  • They are on the brink of divorce in part because they stopped turning toward each other at the developmental milestones of life. Fortunately, for many of these couples, just realizing that this has happened provides hope that if they expend effort in trying to get the marriage to a healthier place, they could see important changes.
  • After hearing the couple’s responses to both the divorce and the repair narrative questions, you may be getting a sense of their unique interactional pattern. How do they deal with conflict or difficult situations? How do they respond to one another when life is stressful and one or both are experiencing anxiety? Later on, in individual conversations, you can begin to help them map out and describe how they respond to one another and see how they each “do” relationships, this one and probably any other intimate relationship in the future. You can begin to hypothesize possible ways to influence that pattern if the couple were to choose couples therapy in Path 3.
  • The divorce narrative and repair questions, you have not gotten a good sense of how the couple interacts when they differ or have conflict, we recommend asking the following explicit question before proceeding with the rest of the four basic questions:
    • “Can you tell me a bit more about what happens between the two of you when you get into arguments or disagreements or when you experience conflict?”
  • Once again, what you are trying to uncover is how the couple responds to relationship interactions when times are difficult or stressful.
  • Each one of these patterns can be changed, but to do so, each member of the couple has to see his or her unique contribution to the pattern.
Children Question (When the Couple has Children)
  • "What role, if any, do your children play in your decision-making about the future of your marriage?"
    • The purpose of this question is to open a space for the couple to talk about their concerns for the children, who are major stakeholders in any decision they make.
  • Getting a sense of how the children figure into the decision-making process helps the discernment counselor understand what may be motivating each partner to work on the marriage or leave it. We do not comment on what they say in response to the children question but may refer to their answer in the individual conversations.
Best-of-Times Question
  • "Can you tell me about a time in your relationship, since the time you met, when you felt the most connection and joy?"
    • It comes last to end the couple time on a positive note, if possible.
    • But it is difficult to rewrite the relationship narrative to the point where they could escape any memories of a time when the couple found connection and joy with one another.
  • The responses we get from this question typically center on good early years, with fun and sharing activities before the advent of children or significant work responsibilities.
  • We encourage you not to settle for the minimalist response but rather probe for positive detail:
    • Client:
      • "Well, the early days, when we were dating - those were the best times we had."
    • Counselor:
      • "What was it about those early days that made it so good? What kinds of things were going on, or what felt good about being with her?"
    • We encourage you to get this description as thick as possible (while being brief) so you can draw on any strengths in future sessions or conversations. The response to this question can also be diagnostic.

Transitioning to Individual Conversations

  • Before starting the individual conversations, it can be helpful to review the three paths that will be the focus of the individual conversations. You could say the following:
    • "As we turn next to individual conversations, I’d like to remind you that in discernment counseling we focus on three potential paths that are in front of you as a couple. Path 1 is staying together as you have been. It’s the status quo path. Path 2 is the separation or divorce path. Path 3 is the reconciliation path and includes a commitment to 6 months of couples therapy to see whether you can put your marriage into a healthier place for both of you. It is not the path of staying married or avoiding divorce forever. It involves taking divorce off the table for 6 months, during which time you both commit to working on yourselves and the relationship. At the end of the 6 months, you can evaluate any progress you’ve made and make a determination whether your overall relationship health is on a positive trajectory. At that point, you may have a better sense as to whether you want to pursue divorce or continue in your marriage. These three paths will frame our individual conversations today."
  • At this point in the session you are not looking for a response about the paths from either of them. In some cases, a leaning-in partner can be all too eager to let you know that he or she is “all in” on Path 3 at this stage of the session. If that occurs, gently remind him or her that you will talk more about the paths in the individual sessions.
  • End the couple time by letting them know that the rest of the session will consist of individual conversations followed by a brief summary of what each has learned in the individual time, followed by a brief couple time at the end for reflections and wrap-up.

Individual Conversations (About 35 Minutes Each)

Leaning-Out Partner

  • We typically begin first with leaning-out partners. our rationale is that they are usually the ones who are less interested in the discernment process and less open to reconciliation and who may be feeling misunderstood and judged by the spouse and others who worry about a possible divorce.
    • A partner who does not feel understood by the discernment counselor will have little incentive to engage in the process fully. Therefore, begin by inviting the leaning-out partner to tell you more about his or her pain in the marriage and the reasons for considering divorce. Be empathic, and listen for additional relationship patterns or significant events that have contributed to the decline of the marital relationship.
  • Within no more than 10 minutes, switch to a conversation about the paths. Almost everyone, when asked about the three paths, rules out Path 1.
    • The status quo is what brought them to the brink of divorce and to your office. Neither one is likely, at this stage, to suggest that things are just fine the way they are.
  • For the leaning-out partner Path 2 has been on his or her mind quite a bit.
    • You should find out more about exactly what steps he or she has taken to formalize the divorce.
    • Each answer to questions asked to understand what attempts towards divorce the leaning-out partner has done Point to a different level of intentionality and may even address how serious the leaning-out partner is about the divorce, with more formal actions speaking the loudest.
