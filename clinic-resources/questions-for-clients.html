Questions for Clients

Discernment

Session 1

  • Questions for both:
    • What happened to your marriage that's gotten you to the point where divorce is a possibility?
    • Can you tell me what you have done to try to fix the problems in the marriage, whether on your own or with help?
    • What role, if any, do your children play in your decision making about the future of your relationship?
    • Can you tell me about a time in your relationship, since the time you met, when you felt the most connection and joy?
  • Questions for Leaning-Out partner (see this person first):
    • Tell me more about your pain and reasons for considering divorce.
    • (Within no more than 10 minutes) switch to a conversation about the paths.
    • Find out how far they have gone in formalizing divorce plans (contacted attorney, papers drawn up, etc.)
    • Ask them their perspective on their contributions to the problems in the marriage.
    • Prepare a summary statement for the other partner about something they have taken away from the individual time, then invite partner 2 in the room to hear the summary statement.
  • Now work with the leaning-in partner:
    • Help them articulate that they understand their partner's concerns and can understand why their partner might be interested in divorce.
    • Ask about their perspective on their own contributions to the problems in the relationship.
    • Help them figure out how to respond without being too anxious or withdrawing completely.
    • Summary statement:
      • Must convey the sense that the leaning-in partner understands why their partner is contemplating divorce, and some empathy for this position.
    • Once both people are in the room again, and the leaning-in partner has shared their summary statement, then the therapist shares their observations.
      • What themes have you noticed, and your perception of how they got to this place.
      • Couple does not have to agree on a path at the end of this session, but they may, they recommend that the couple return for at least a 2 nd discernment session before deciding, especially Path 3.

Solution-Focused

  • What is happening in your lives that you want to continue to have happen?
  • What do you think the problem is now?
  • How will you know when the problem is solved?
  • How will you know when you don't have to come here anymore? What will the signs be?
  • What will have to be different about others involved in the situation?
  • What is your wildest fantasy about what you want to happen?

Resistance

  • What would it take to get your family member (or the court) off of your back?

Goal Development

  • What needs to happen as a result of coming here so that afterward you will look back and be able to say it was a good idea?
  • What needs to happen as a result of coming here so that this will not have been a waste of your time?
  • Often in our experience, we have found that in between scheduling an appointment and coming in, something happens that contributes to making a problem better. Has anything happened to improve the problems that brought you here today?

Combating Resistance

  • So, what has to happen to get your mother off of your back?
  • What is the minimum we need to accomplish so that you won't have to keep coming for more sessions?
  • What would it take to get your family (or court) off of your back?

Before Session

  • Between now and next time we meet, I would like you to observe what happens that you want to continue to have happen?

In-Session

  • What brings you here today? How can I be helpful to you?
  • What changes have you noticed since you first made the call to set up this appointment?

Exceptions

  • When in the past might the problem have happened but it didn't?

Agency

  • How did you do that? How did you get that to happen?

Coping

  • How have you managed to cope, ensure, keep going?

Scaling

  • What will it take to move from a 3 to a 4?
  • On a scale of 1 to 10, 1 being absolutely no hope, and 10 being complete hope, what number would you give your current level of hope?

Assessment Problem Description

  • Therapy begins with a description of the clients' problem.
  • How were you hoping I could help you?
  • Asking for clients' perceptions
  • Acknowledge clients' perceptions, using clients own language as much as possible.
  • As clients what they've already tried to resolve their difficulties.

Narrative

Assessment Questions

  • It sounds as though [problem] is part of your life now.
  • How long have you been noticing this [problem]?
  • What effect does the [problem] have on your life?
  • How does the [problem] impact your relationship?
  • Does [problem] have an impact on your relationship with other family members?
  • What do you think about the effects [problem] is having on your life?
  • Are you accepting what [problem] is doing?
  • Are these effects acceptable to you or not?
  • Why is this? Why are you taking this position on what [problem] is doing?
  • How would you prefer things to be?

Narrative Questions

  • What name would you give the problem?
  • What is it like to experience the problem?
  • What effect does the problem have on your life?
  • What effect does the problem have on your relationship with each other?
  • What has it talked you into about your family? What impacts has that had?
  • What effects does the problem have on your other relationships?
  • How does the problem alter your relationship with yourself?
  • Is this what you want for your life? Why or why not?
  • Are we talking about what you want to be talking about?
  • Is this conversation useful? How is it useful?

Relative Influence Questions

  • Who is in charge, the person or the problem?

Identifying Unique Outcome s

  • Reading between the lines of the problem story
  • Can you remember a time...

Reauthoring

  • What does it say about you...

Externalizing Conversations

  • If we are not hearing the name, we might ask the family to name them
    • What would you call that something?
  • What 'feeds' the problem?
  • What 'starves' the problem?
  • Who benefits from the problem?
  • In what settings might the problem be useful?
  • Who advocates for the problem?
  • What groups of people would be opposed to the problem and its intentions?
  • What are the effects of the problem on members of the family? On their lives and relationships?

Unique Outcome

  • Have you ever been able to escape the problem even for a few minutes?
  • Is the problem always with you?

Telling and Witnessing

  • What was it like to hear what so-and-so was saying?
  • What thoughts were you having as so-and-so was talking?
  • It is useful to ask questions that, rather than leaving the field for response wide open, more specifically invite people to respond to unique outcomes and preferred directions
    • Were you surprised when so-and-so described you as taking the time to let others know you care?
    • What did it mean to you to hear so-and-so say how important the relationship is to them?

Outsider Witness

  • Creating a setting where people can tell their preferred stories to a group of outsiders, who them amplify and accredit the stories that they witness
  • What stood out for them?
  • What images it brought forward?
  • How it resonated with their own experiences?
  • Where they were transported by the interview?
  • Situate their reflections in their own experience
  • Comment on the difference that being part of the conversation makes on their own lives

More Questions

  • Opening space questions (unique outcomes)
    • Has there been a time when arguing could have taken control of your relationship but did not?
  • Preference questions (preferred experiences)
    • Was this way of handling things better or worse? And how?
  • Story development questions
    • How is this different from when arguing is in control?
  • Meaning questions
    • What does it say about you that you were able to...?
  • Questions that extend the story into the future
    • What do you predict...
