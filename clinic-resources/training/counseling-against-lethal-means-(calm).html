Counseling Against Lethal Means (CALM)

Why Does Reducing Access Make A Difference?

Key Points:

  • The acute phase of a suicidal crisis is when a person is ready to actually make an attempt to end their life.
  • Suicidal crises are often brief.
  • While some attempts follow a long period of deliberation, other attempts are acted on hastily and may be difficult to predict.
  • Most people who attempt suicide don't necessarily want to be dead, but they want the pain they feel to stop.
  • Most people who attempt suicide and survive do not ultimately kill themselves - even people who make a very serious suicide attempt.

Suicidal Crisis

  • Acute Suicidal Crisis: The period when a person transitions from being at risk for suicide or considering suicide to being ready to actually make an attempt.

Common Characteristics:

  • Can come on rapidly
  • Can be difficult to predict
  • Can lead to an unplanned suicide attempt
  • People were asked when they has first started thinking about making an attempt; 48% said they starting thinking about it within 10 minutes of attempting.
  • Often brief
    • The period during which a person is actually ready to take their life is usually brief. Rarely is it a chronic state. The impulse to die often fades. It may never recur, or it may flare up episodically.
    • Duration of Suicidal Crisis:
      • Evidence by Attempters:
        • 0-10 minutes: 48%
        • 11-60 minutes: 2%
        • 1-24 hours: 9%
        • 1-7 days: 16%
        • 1-4 weeks: 15%
        • 1-12 months: 11%
    • Long-Term Survival:
      • More than 90% of people who attempt suicide and survive do not actually kill themselves.
      • 60% of people who commit suicide die on their first attempt. (Means reduction saves lives by decreasing the likelihood that an attempt will be fatal)
        • In describing the moment he jumped from the Golden Gate Bridge, Ken Baldwin said, "I instantly realized that everything in my life that I'd thought was unfixable was totally fixable - except for having just jumped."

Who Needs Lethal Means Counseling?:

Clients at Risk:

  • It is not easy to determine.
  • Examples:
    • Clients who have suicidal thoughts.
    • Clients who are in distress and have attempted suicide in the past.
    • Clients who are struggling with mental health or substance misuse issues, especially if they are also coping with painful life crises.

Talk about Suicide:

  • A clinician should ask directly about suicidal thoughts and feelings. Talking about suicide does not create or increase risk, in fact, it can reduce the risk of suicide.

Ask Direct Questions:

  • A clinician’s ability to calmly, compassionately, and matter-of-factly explore suicidal thoughts and behaviors may encourage an individual to disclose suicidality, if it exists. Often, clients who have considered suicide will be relieved that you raised the subject.

Guidelines to follow:

  • Use direct questions about current or past suicidal thoughts and behavior.
  • Let the client know that other people have similar thoughts and feelings.
  • Any of your clients who have a prior history of suicidality or current distress could benefit from lethal means counseling.

Get Specific Information:

  • If your conversation with your client results in disclosure of past suicidal thoughts or behaviors, probe more about current suicidal feelings. Find out specific details about their suicidal thoughts and behaviors.
    • Some Questions to Ask:
      • Tell me about a couple of times in the past few months when things were the worst for you—did you think about suicide?
        • If yes, then ask: Did you take any specific steps toward an attempt? What methods did you think about? What kept you alive? 
      • Tell me about the last couple of days—how much would you say suicide has been on your mind, if at all? 
        • If so, then ask: Are there specific methods you’ve thought about?
      • Have you thought about how you'd kill yourself? 
        • If yes, then ask: Have you thought about when or where you'd kill yourself?
        • If they mention a specific method of suicide, then ask: What other methods are you thinking about?
      • Is there a specific scenario where you think you would attempt suicide?
      • Have you ever attempted suicide in the past, or started to?

Don 't Rely Solely on Disclosure:

  • Don't rely solely on disclosure of suicidal thoughts, behaviors, and plans. Lethal means counseling makes sense for anyone at risk.
  • Individuals who struggle with mental health and/or substance use issues, and who are experiencing a major crisis, stress, or trauma, are also at risk of suicide and should receive lethal means training.

Key Points:

  • Individuals who currently have suicidal thoughts would benefit from lethal means counseling.
  • Individuals who have attempted suicide in the past and are currently in distress would benefit from lethal means counseling.
  • Individuals who struggle with mental health/substance abuse issues, especially when they are also experiencing painful life crises, would benefit from lethal means counseling.
  • Ask clients at risk directly about suicidal thoughts, behaviors, and plans.
  • Ask clients at-risk specifically about their suicide plans, such as how and when they would do it.
  • Don't rely solely on disclosure of suicidal thoughts, behaviors, and plans. People sometimes deny these even when they exist. Also, suicidal thoughts can emerge rapidly and many attempts are unplanned.
  • Lethal means counseling should take place before a suicidal crisis occurs.

What are the Lethal Means?

Suicide Deaths:

  • Firearms is the method used most frequently in suicidal deaths.
  • Suicidal Death Rates by Method:
    • 51% Firearms
    • 26% Suffocation/Hanging
    • 12% Poisoning/Overdose (Poisoning from intentional drug or medication overdoses)
    • 3% Gas Inhalation (Inhaling gas, usually carbon monoxide from motor exhaust)
    • 2% Jumping/Falling (Jump from high place like bridge)
    • 2% Cutting (Sharp instruments like razor)
    • 4% Other Methods (like drowning)

Lethality (Case Fatality Rate):

Means Matter:

  • The lethality of the method used in a suicide attempt is one of the biggest factors determining whether the person lives or dies. While the intent matters, means also matters.
  • If the method a person wants to use for suicide if not available when they are actually ready to go through with an attempt, one of two things is likely to occur:
    • They will delay the attempt as they try to get access to the method they want to use. The greater the delay, the greater the chance the suicidal crisis will pass.
    • They will substitute another method. If the substituted method is less lethal, this increases the chance the person will not die. And most people who survive an attempt do not later go on to kill themselves.
  • The two methods used in the majority of nonfatal suicidal attempts are overdosing with medications and cutting with sharp instruments. Many people think these two methods are far more deadly than they actually are. This misperception may save a lot of lives.
  • We recommend that you do not share specific information with your clients about the low lethality of sharp instruments and medications.

Considerations for Means Reduction:

  • Which suicide methods are amenable to a means reduction approach? It depends on the following criteria:
    • Number of Suicide Deaths:
      • The number/percent of suicide deaths caused by a specific method.
    • Lethality:
      • The proportion of attempts by a given method that end in death (case fatality rate).
    • Irreversibility:
      • The inability for someone attempting suicide to stop once it has begun or to be rescued. More people stop an attempt than carry through with it.
    • Accessibility:
      • The immediate availability of the method. Access is particularly important when attempts occur rapidly with little or no planning.
    • Acceptability:
      • Cultural and/or individual tolerance or preference for using a particular method to kill oneself.
  • Cutting:
    • Low Number of Deaths
    • Low Lethality
    • Low Irreversibility
    • High Accessibility
    • High Acceptability
  • Jumping/Falling:
    • Low Number of Deaths
    • High Lethality
    • High Irreversibility
    • Low Accessibility
    • Low Acceptability
  • Gas Inhalation:
    • Low Number of Deaths
    • Moderate Lethality
    • Low Irreversibility
    • Moderate Accessibility
    • Low Acceptability
  • Poisoning/Overdose:
    • Moderate Number of Deaths
    • Low Lethality
    • Low Irreversibility
    • High Accessibility
    • High Acceptability
  • Suffocation/Hanging:
    • Moderate Number of Deaths
    • Moderate Lethality
    • Low Irreversibility
    • High Accessibility
    • Moderate Acceptability
  • Firearms:
    • High Number of Deaths
    • High Lethality
    • High Irreversibility
    • High Accessibility
    • High Acceptability

Implications :

  • Firearms are used in more suicide deaths in the US than all other methods combined. They are also highly lethal irreversible, and easily accessible in many homes with guns (one-third of homes have guns). Since gun owners can voluntarily undertake steps to reduce access, you will want to discuss safe firearm storage with all clients at-risk of suicide.
  • Medication Overdoses are not as lethal as firearms (though some medications and poisons are very lethal). It is also usually possible for someone to stop an overdose mid-attempt or to be rescued. However, overdose is by far the most frequent method of attempt, and medications are easily accessible in most homes. Therefore, it is wise to routinely advise at-risk clients to dispose of unnecessary medications and keep accessible only quantities that do not pose a lethal threat.
  • When your client's suicidal ideation focuses on any other method, you will want to discuss with them ways to reduce access to that method.

Key Points:

  • More suicide deaths are from firearms than any other method in the US.

  • Firearms, jumping, and suffocation are most lethal than other methods.

  • Cutting and poisoning/overdose have low lethality, on average.

  • The method used in a suicide attempt can determine whether the person lives or dies.

  • Use a method reduction approach for any lethal method that is mentioned by clients at risk.

  • How amenable each method is to a means reduction approach depends on the following criteria:

    • Frequency
    • Lethality
    • Irreversibility
    • Accessibility
    • Acceptability

  • Since firearms are used in most suicide deaths and they are also highly lethal, acceptable, and accessible (in homes with guns), be sure to ask all clients at risk if they have access to firearms and discuss ways to reduce access.

    • A method is considered lethal if a high proportion of attempts by that method end in death.
    • Approximately 80%-90% of intentional, self-inflicted gunshot wounds are fatal, according to data from emergency departments and death certificates.
    • Only 1%-2% of intentional, self-inflicted wounds from sharp instruments, like a razor, are fatal. Cutting is not a highly lethal method. However, it is the second most commonly used method in attempts.
    • Only 1%-2% of intentional overdoses or poisonings are fatal, according to data from emergency departments and death certificates. This is on average, however, certain medications and poisons are far more lethal than others. In addition, medication overdoses are the most common method of suicidal attempts.

What are some ways to Reduce Access?

Feasibility of Reducing Access to Lethal Means:

The Building Blocks:

  • People who attempt suicide typically struggle with ongoing mental health or substance abuse issues.
  • While some attempts are planned, many are not. Acute suicidal crises are often brief, and escalation from thought to action can occur rapidly.
  • The lethality of the method used in an attempt can determine whether that person lives or dies. If a highly lethal means is not available, the individual's chance of survival is greater.
  • Over 90% of people who attempt suicide and survive do not take their life later on, so reducing access to lethal means can save many lives.
  • An effective way to protect clients at risk of suicide is to reduce their access to highly lethal means before they experience an acute suicidal crisis.

What's Feasible:

  • Feasibility:
    • Can the client or their family actually take the steps at home to reduce access to the method?
  • Reducing access to firearms and medications used for poisoning/overdose could feasibly be accomplished at home and doing so could potentially save thousands of lives in the U.S. if routinely implemented with people at risk for suicide.
  • Access can be feasibly be reduced for these two methods:
    • Firearms:
      • Firearms are used in roughly half of all suicide deaths, are highly lethal, and are very accessible if they are kept at home.
    • Medications:
      • Medication overdoses are by far the most common method in suicide attempts. Although the vast majority do not result in death, some medications and medication combinations are highly lethal - and, if kept unlocked at home, very accessible.
  • We recommend routinely discussing firearm and medication storage with all clients at risk of suicide. If a client's suicidal ideation focuses on a method other than firearms or medication, we also recommend discussing ways to reduce access to that method as well.

Firearms:

Reducing Access to Firearms:

  • When discussing firearm storage with clients and their family members, the goal is to maximize the client’s safety by reducing immediate access to firearms as much as possible while they are at risk for suicide. There are two main options for reducing access to firearms.
  • Safest: Store firearms away from the home:
    • Storing firearms away from home is the most effective way to ensure clients don't have easy access to a gun if they experience a spike in suicidal feelings. Off-site options are listed in the next section.
  • Second-best: Secure storage inside the home:
    • The second-best option is securely locking firearms in the home (or disassembling guns and locking a critical component). The person at risk should not have the keys, know where the keys are kept, or know the combination to the storage unit. Keep in mind that safes and locks can be broken into or picked, some more easily than others. But any measures that put time and distance between a suicidal person and a firearm may save their life.

Storing Firearms Away From The Home:

  • Storing firearms away from the home temporarily until the person improves is the best way to reduce access to them.
  • Self-Storage Rental Units:
    • Many facilities allow firearm storage. Typically, the smallest units are 5 ft. By 5 ft., but some offer storage drawers or bins, which are less expensive. Prices typically include a small one-time administrative fee as well as a monthly charge that depends on unit size. Guns should be unloaded and locked when stored in a facility.
  • Friends or Relatives:
    • Storing firearms with a trusted person is an excellent and free option, provided the person is legally allowed to hold them. Some state laws require a background check prior to transfer to ensure the individual is not prohibited from possessing firearms.
  • Shooting Ranges:
    • Shooting ranges and sportsmen’s clubs sometimes offer locker rentals for a relatively low monthly fee. Typically, only the owner holds the key and no background check is required because the ownership of the guns is not transferred. Guns should be unloaded and locked when stored.
  • Law Enforcement:
    • Some law enforcement agencies will temporarily store firearms for safety purposes; others will not. It is best to know the processes and policies in your region before you need to implement these with a client. You or your client should contact the agency first; walking in unannounced with firearms could be mistaken as a threat. Some agencies will pick up the firearms at the person’s home. A background check to retrieve the firearms may be required. Most agencies offer disposal services, if that is desired.
  • Gun Shops:
    • Some gun shops offer storage services, and a few specialize in this service (e.g., provide pick-up services, offer discounts for longer-term and multiple gun storage). Prices vary, although some shops will provide regular customers with free storage when there is a safety concern. A background check to retrieve the firearms may be required, depending on the type of storage the shop uses.
  • Pawn Shops:
    • Pawning guns for a small loan amount can be a relatively low-cost, secure storage option in most states. Interest charges must be paid monthly. To reclaim their firearms, gun owners must repay the loan and go through a background check to verify they are not prohibited from owning firearms.

When Is It Safe to Bring Guns Back Home:

  • Clients and family members who decide to store their firearms away from their house usually want to know when it’s safe enough to bring their firearms back into the home.
    • "Yes. … It’s a case-by-case decision based on how the client is doing. Acute suicidal feelings can spike episodically. While these spikes are often brief, they can recur. It is prudent to wait until there has been a relatively long period of stability before returning the guns."

Storing Firearms Inside the Home:

  • Temporary storage of firearms away from the home cannot always be arranged immediately and isn’t always acceptable to the client or others in the home.
Ways Clients or Support Person Can Reduce Access to Firearms:
  • Store in a gun safe or lock box:
    • Lock guns, unloaded, in a gun safe or lock box. Many gun owners have multiple guns and ALL of them should be locked up, including rifles and self-defense guns.
  • Remove a component:
    • Remove a key component of the guns (such as the slides or firing pins) and lock them up - or better yet, store them away from the home.
  • Use Gun Locks:
    • Trigger locks, cable locks, and clamshell locks aren't as safe as locking in a secure gun safe, but they are better than not locking it at all - and when used in conjunction with a safe, can provide an additional layer of protection.
  • Change the Combination or Key:
    • If guns are already locked, change the combination or key location in case the person at risk knows how to access them.
  • Remove Ammunition:
    • Remove ammunition from ALL firearms. Keep all ammunition out of the home for now - or, at least, locked separately.
  • Hiding guns in the home is not recommended since family members often know the hiding places.
  • We recommend triple safety when storing them in the home: Locked and unloaded, with ammunition locked up separately (Ideally away from the home while the client is at risk)
  • What About a Self-Defense Gun?
    • In some situations, clients or members of their household may want to keep their gun available for self-defense purposes. If this is the case, how you approach the situation and what you say will depend on whether the gun owner or the household member is the person at risk.
Recommendations to respond:
  • Person At Risk:
    • If the gun owner is the person at risk:
      • Discuss the specific threats that are a concern to the client and the realistic risk of home invasion crimes.
      • Find out what other means of protection they would consider, such as self-defense sprays (if legal in your state), flood lights, an alarm system, or a dog.
      • Discuss what they are willing to do to keep themselves safe from suicide. 
      • Would the client consider locking a self-defense gun and having another family member hold the key? If there’s resistance, consider creative solutions, such as putting pictures of loved ones or other reminders of reasons to live on their gun or lock box to interrupt or slow down a suicidal impulse. Work toward additional steps to reduce access over time.
  • Household Member:
    • If the gun owner is a member of the household:
      • First, discuss the probabilities of suicide attempt versus risk of home invasion in the near term (i.e., the risk of a violent home invasion is very small for most people and is dwarfed by the far greater risk of suicide if a despairing person has a gun close at hand)
      • If the gun owner is concerned about protecting family members, it may be helpful to mention that part of protecting them is keeping everyone safe from suicide.
      • If self-defense is determined to be essential, one option is for the gun owner to keep the firearm either on them or in a quick-release handgun safe to which the person at risk has no access.

Medications:

Reducing Access to Medications:

Lethal Combinations:
  • Overdose accounts for roughly two-thirds of suicide attempts treated in the hospital, but only a very small proportion (2% or less) prove fatal. However, some medications are more dangerous than others, especially when taken in combination. With over 250,000 attempts annually, at least 5,000 people die each year from an intentional overdose.
Opiods:
  • According to data from the National Violent Death Reporting System (NVDRS), the medications most frequently involved in overdose suicides are opioids (e.g., prescription pain medications such as oxycodone, methadone, and morphine). 5  In suicide deaths by drug overdose, over half tested positive for an opioid. 6 Opioids, when used alone or in combination with other types of drugs or alcohol can cause death by depressing the respiratory system.
Other Drugs:
  • Other drugs that are frequently implicated in overdose deaths include benzodiazepines (e.g., anxiolytics and muscle relaxants), antipsychotics, antidepressants, sleeping pills (both prescription and over-the-counter, especially diphenhydramine—Benadryl), and over-the-counter pain relievers like acetaminophen (Tylenol). These substances usually do not appear alone but rather in combination with an opioid, with one another, and/or with alcohol. Alcohol use can increase the lethality of an overdose and may increase the risk that the person will carry through with an attempt.
  • Unfortunately, information about lethal combinations of medications is readily available on the Internet.

Recommendations for Medications:

Unused Medications:
  • Safely dispose of all unneeded and outdated medication in the home.
Opioids:
  • Opioids should not be kept at home unless absolutely necessary to manage pain.
    • If opioids must be kept at home, they should be locked and inaccessible to the person at risk. If the at-risk client must take the opioids to manage pain, discuss the possibility of having another household member or support person dispense the pills.
    • In high-risk situations involving opioid use, also keep at least two doses of Narcan Nasal Spray, which is a highly effective rescue drug that lay people can administer. When used in time, Narcan can immediately reverse the effects of an opioid overdose.
Abuse-Prone Drugs:
  • Always lock up abuse-prone drugs such as prescription painkillers, anxiety medications, amphetamines, sedatives, and tranquilizers. Many inexpensive lock boxes can easily be broken into. A steel toolbox secured by a padlock is a safe, inexpensive option.
Other Necessary Medications:
  • For other necessary medications (both over-the-counter and prescription), only keep on hand limited quantities that would not constitute a serious danger if taken together. Lock up the rest.
Safe Quantities:
  • Advise families to ask their doctor or pharmacist for guidance on what constitutes a safe quantity of medication to have in their home. The Poison Control Hotline at 800-222-1222 (24 hours a day, 7 days a week) can also provide guidance on safe quantities. A family member or support person should seek this information, not the person at risk for suicide, since the information could be used to increase the lethality of an attempt.

Recommendations for Prescribing:

Toxicity:
  • Use lower-toxicity rather than higher-toxicity medications, if possible, such as a selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor (SSRI) or serotonin and norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor (SNRI) antidepressant rather than a tricyclic (TCA) antidepressant.
Quantities and Combinations:
  • Limit medication to quantities and combinations that, even if taken all at once, will not do serious harm.
    • Consider requiring weekly refills to reduce lethality and increase contact.
    • Take into account the client's use of alcohol and other drugs, including over-the-counter medications. Some combinations are more deadly than others.
Choose Your Words Carefully:
  • If you have a client with a prescription for psychiatric medications, choose your words carefully so that you don’t send mixed messages or inadvertently inform the person about the lethality of the medications.

Other Methods:

Reducing Access to Other Methods:

  • If a client’s suicidal thoughts focus on methods other than guns or medications, incorporate those methods into your lethal means counseling. For example, if ideation focuses on filling the garage with car exhaust or crashing the car into a telephone pole, and the client has strong suicidal feelings, discuss ways to limit access to the car, such as having a family member hold onto the car keys temporarily.
  • What if ideation focuses on a certain method?
    • There are times when a client’s ideation focuses on a very specific method that serves as their escape plan and may also have personally significant meaning. For example, your client carries around a belt that he could use to hang himself like his uncle did. If ideation focuses in this very specific way on one particular item, work with the client on agreeing to give up that item for now, while identifying strategies for staying alive.
  • What about methods that cannot easily be restricted at home?
    • Sometimes a client’s ideation focuses on suffocation or other highly lethal methods that are nearly impossible to control access to. These clients may need close supervision from family, friends, or others to ensure they don’t attempt suicide, particularly if their suicidality is of a more chronic or recurring nature. The client may opt to stay in a hospital if they feel the supervision would be helpful.
  • Just One Part of a Safety Plan:
    • In all cases, reducing access to lethal means should be part of a broader collaborative safety plan aimed at helping a client learn how to cope with suicidal feelings, as well as part of a broader therapeutic plan aimed at reducing the reasons for dying and increasing the reasons to live.

Conclusion:

Key Points:

  • To reduce access to firearms, the safest option is to store them away from the home. Possible storage options include with a relative or friend; with law enforcement; in a self-storage rental unit; or at a gun shop, shooting range, or pawn shop.
  • To reduce access to firearms that are stored in the home, use triple safety measures: Lock all firearms (or disassemble guns and lock a critical component), unload all firearms, and lock ammunition separately (or don’t keep ammunition at home for now).
  • Hiding guns in the home is not recommended since family members often know the hiding places.
  • Guns that are kept at home for self-defense must be addressed since they are usually the ones that are most accessible.
  • To reduce access to medications, remove unneeded medications from the home; keep only non-lethal quantities on hand; and lock up abuse-prone drugs, such as prescription painkillers, anxiety pills, amphetamines, sedatives, and tranquilizers.
  • Discuss ways to reduce access to any method a client mentions using.

How Should I Counsel Clients About Lethal Means?

Overview:

When, Who, and What:

When:
  • Since escalation to a suicidal crisis can happen rapidly, the goal is to make the home safer for clients at risk before they are actively suicidal.
Who:
  • Counseling on access to lethal means is for three types of clients:
  • Clients who have recently had suicidal thoughts.
  • Clients who have attempted suicide in the past and are currently in distress.
  • Clients who are struggling with a difficult mental health or substance abuse issue, particularly when they are also coping with a life crisis, such as relationship breakup, housing dislocation, arrest, or financial or job problems. These clients may not be currently suicidal but could become suicidal quickly.
What:
  • Routinely discus ways to reduce access to firearms and medications routinely with any client at risk of suicide. In addition, discuss any other method that a client with suicidal ideation considers using.

Your Approach:

General Guidelines:

  • Lethal means counseling is a client-centered intervention, not a one-size-fits-all approach. Your approach and the language you use are important to making this conversation effective, whether speaking to your client or to their friends and family members.
Collaborative Conversation:
  • Have a collaborative conversation and work together. With this approach, the client is the expert and the clinician is a resource.
Temporary Nature:
  • Focus on the (hopefully) temporary nature of both the mental health crisis as well as the need to reduce access to lethal means.
Specific Feasible Plans:
  • Make specific and feasible plans.
Cultural Differences:
  • Be sensitive to all types of cultural differences.
A Collaborative, Guiding Approach:
  • A guiding approach that is collaborative and client-centered is usually best, such as that used in motivational interviewing. Seek input from the client about their own reasons for - and against - reducing access, their own safety concerns, and what they would be willing to do. A collaborative approach accepts the client's values and works from the client's own motivations.
    • "I can see how hard this has been for you lately, and I'm concerned about you. Are you concerned too?"
    • "What ideas do you have about ways to increase your safety in case you experience suicidal thoughts?"
  • If your client is a gun owner:
    • "Lots of people have guns at home. Some will store their guns outside of their home temporarily - such as at a relative's house or a gun shop - until they are feeling better. What do you think about a strategy like that?"
A Directive Approach:
  • A directive approach has a less collaborative tone and puts the clinician in the role of the expert. With certain clients, this approach may be more appropriate, for example:
    • Clients who are too overwhelmed or stressed to make clear-headed decisions.
    • Clients from certain cultures who may feel more comfortable getting advice rather than being asked for their ideas.
  • Examples of Directive Statements:
    • "I'm going to recommend that for now you store your guns away from home. Is that something you can do?"
    • "You'd be safer if you stored your guns away from home for now."
    • "I'm going to suggest you use this gun lock and have your wife keep the key."
    • "It would be best if you kept only a week's supply of medications at home and had a family member store the rest."
    • "For now, let's have your partner store your prescriptions and over-the-counter medications in a lock box."

Common Situations:

  • No Disclosure of Suicidal Thoughts:
    • Some clients may disclose that they are suffering but say they are not suicidal. This may well be true for some clients; however, others may in fact be suicidal or likely to become so. If the client is having a very difficult time, and especially if life stressors are also present (e.g., custody battle, painful breakup, upcoming prison sentence, housing dislocation, work troubles), engage them in a conversation about reducing access to firearms and medications in case suicidal feelings develop.
    • Examples of how to respond to clients who say they are not suicidal:
      • "I'm glad you're not feeling suicidal right now. Sometimes people who are struggling may have a crisis and suddenly experience strong suicidal feelings. I'm not saying this is likely to happen, but if it does, I want to be sure you make it through safely and get the help you need. Can we talk over a plan, just in case?"
    • When clients you suspect are suicidal don't disclose:
      • "It's not unusual for people who have experienced the kind of pain you are going through recently to feel like it will last forever and to think they would rather be dead. Can we talk over a plan for how you'd stay safe if you started feeling suicidal?"

Challenging Scenarios:

  • Some clients, even if they are suicidal, may express resistance to reducing access to lethal means, like firearms. If clients or family members seem resistant, listen to what they say and work collaboratively toward solutions that address their concerns. For instance, some gun owners find the idea of living without a firearm in the home, even temporarily, very difficult. Don’t minimize that this may be a difficult decision.
    • Examples on how to respond to clients who are in denial or resistant:
      • When clients won't tell you whether they have a gun:
        • Will you tell me what you’re worried would happen if you say you have a gun? I may be able to ease those worries.
      • When clients think you are trying to take their gun away:
        • "I am not questioning your right to own guns. Since you’ve talked about feeling suicidal, I’m concerned about helping you stay safe. Have you also been concerned?”
      • When clients are concerned about what will go in their record:
        • “Before we talk about your specific situation, I can let you know what sort of information I will write down and what I can omit.”
      • When a client is concerned about chronic pain:
        • “I hear that you are concerned you won’t have access to your medications during a flare-up. Let’s come up with a plan that supports both your health and safety.”
      • When a family member is resistant to safely storing medications at home:
        • “While it is true your child could get pills from a store, most suicide attempts occur at home with little or no preparation. Let’s talk about a plan that addresses your concerns and also ensures your daughter cannot take enough pills to harm herself.”

Special Situations with Firearms:

Conversations About Guns:
  • Conversations about reducing access to lethal means are not always straightforward or easy. This can be especially true when discussing firearms. As a clinician, if you own guns you may feel more at ease raising the topic and more knowledgeable about storage options. If you aren’t a gun owner, it’s possible you might feel uneasy broaching the topic or overestimate client resistance. However, most gun owners value firearm safety and protecting their family—values that dovetail well with preventing suicide.
    • When firearms are a family tradition and bonding experience:
      • Consider alternatives
      • Discuss safety
      • Store guns elsewhere
    • When guns provide safety and protection:
      • Weigh concerns:
        • Weigh the safety concerns of suicidality versus the concern of temporarily not having a firearm at home.
    • Consider other options:
      • Consider other things that could help keep the family safe such as pepper spray, alarms, or outdoor motion-detector lighting.
    • Prioritize client's health:
      • Underscore that part of protecting the client's family is ensuring the client is alive and well.
      • Discuss behavioral history:
        • Ask about any history of impulsive or unexpected behavior, and build on that to reinforce your concern.
    • When guns provide security due to PTSD:
      • Ask how client feels:
        • Ask if the client trusts themselves with a loaded gun close at hand.
    • Consider behavioral strategies:
      • Use a behavioral intervention (e.g. behavioral modification, behavior shaping, rehearsal) or other interventions that focus on taking small steps to reduce access and gradually increasing these steps.
Working with Youth:
  • Talking to Parents/Guardians:
    • When working with a person under age 18 who is at risk for suicide, involve parents or guardians. Let them know that you are concerned about a possible suicide attempt and explain that reducing access to lethal means can reduce the likelihood of a suicide death.
    • When the youth has expressed suicidal ideation:
      • “The fact that your child has had suicidal thoughts means we need to take the risk of suicide seriously. We’ve talked about getting him into treatment. I’d also like to talk about steps you can take at home to help keep him safe.”
      • "If a child or teenager attempts suicide, it’s usually at home and it’s often a quick decision. Putting time and distance between them and a method that could do serious harm is one of the single most effective steps to protect your child."
    • When the youth is not currently suicidal but at risk:
      • “I’ve asked her about suicidal thoughts, and she hasn’t disclosed any. Suicide is still a concern when kids are struggling. These thoughts can develop rapidly, often without warning, and can prompt an unplanned suicide attempt. Therefore, we routinely suggest a few important steps you can take at home.”
      • "With everything going on in your home right now and the impact on your child, it is best to make the environment in your home safer in case a suicidal crisis occurs unexpectedly. It is safest to temporarily remove all firearms from the home and to reduce access to medications, particularly those for pain and other drugs of abuse, while your child is at risk."
Firearms and Youth:
  • Since firearms are the leading cause of suicide deaths, be sure to talk with the youth and their parents/guardians about guns in the home.
  • Gather information from youth:
    • Talk to your young clients about whether there are guns in the home and find out if they have access to them. For example, some clinicians ask youth, “How long would it take you to get access to guns in your home?”
    • Talk to the parents about gun access and storage:
    • Speak to the adult in the household who is the primary gun owner (unless there are contraindications such as spouse abuse). 
      • Find out the following:
        • How are the guns stored in the home? Since gun owners often have more than one, gather information about all of them.
        • Does their child have access to any of the guns in the home? Many parents underestimate their children’s knowledge about where guns are stored and how to access them (e.g., key or combination). 
        • Are there other homes where the youth stays that may have guns? Discuss all possibilities, such as joint custody situations, relatives, hunting buddies, or other friends.
        • Make sure the youth cannot hear your discussion (even if it is by phone), especially if there’s a chance the child is unaware of household firearms.
          • Explain the focus on firearms, if necessary:
            • If parents ask why you are focusing on firearms, explain that they are the most lethal method of suicide and that unlike nearly every other method, there’s no opportunity for the person to change their mind or be rescued mid-attempt. Since suicidal feelings are often short-lived and can come on unpredictably, putting time and distance between an at-risk youth and firearms is an effective way to protect them.

Key Points:

  • Lethal means counseling is client-oriented.
  • When discussing lethal means with clients or family members, make it a collaborative conversation.
  • Your approach and the language you use matters. A collaborative, guiding approach is usually best. A more directive approach is less collaborative but may be more appropriate with certain clients.
  • When working with a young person at risk for suicide, always involve parents or guardians.

What is the Plan?

  • Together with your client, collaborate on a plan to reduce access to lethal means. Depending on the client’s situation, this plan usually covers firearms and medications, as well as any other methods that are the focus of your client’s ideation.
  • The plan should include the following:
    • Clear roles and tasks:
      • Who will do what (this is especially important to do if others are involved)
    • A timetable:
      • When each part of the plan will be done

Make A Plan:

Involve Family Members:

  • Family members may not be aware that suicide attempts usually occur with little or no planning and that the method a person chooses often depends on its availability. It can be helpful to inform family members or significant others and to enlist their support in reducing access to lethal means, for example, if there are medications which the client must access daily or occasionally.
Benefits and Considerations:

  • Benefits:

    • Involving family can be very effective. In fact, families counseled to remove or secure lethal means are far more likely to do so than those who are not counseled. Additionally, results of a pilot study indicate the majority of families reported that the counseling they received was respectful of their needs.

  • Considerations:

    • Some clients will not want you to enlist the help of their family members. In these situations, close friends or peer mentors may be more acceptable and less risky to involve than family members. Collaborate with your client on who would be best to engage.
      - Permission:
      - Ask your client for permission to talk with others before doing so (e.g., spouse, romantic partner, parents, friends, or peer mentors). For minors (under age 18), you may talk to the parents or legal guardian without the client’s permission. To help maintain your relationship with a minor, however, be sure to inform your client before speaking with a parent or guardian.
      - Permission Denied:
      - If a client does not give you permission to talk with anyone, yet you believe that removal of a lethal means is necessary for the safety of that individual, this could be considered permissible communication. Consult the current HIPAA guidelines for situations like this.
      - Remember, in order to collaborate effectively with a client (or their parents), the clinician should:
      - Use language that indicates acceptance of gun ownership without judgement.
      - Focus on the (hopefully) temporary nature of both the mental health crisis and the need to reduce access to lethal means.
      - Be sensitive to all types of cultural differences, recognizing that gun owners tend to prefer language that emphasizes options and autonomy.
      • Consider the Client's Reaction:
        • Before finalizing a plan to reduce access to firearms, or any suicide method, consider your client's reactions to it and the likelihood of follow-through with the plan. If you are working with the parents or guardians of youth, take their reactions into consideration. The client’s (or parents’) response may reveal whether the plan is likely to be carried out.
          • Examples:
            • Initial reluctance from client:
              • Is your client initially reluctant to implement the plan and then relieved when they agree? This may indicate strong commitment to that particular method and a positive indication that, for now at least, they are electing to live.
            • Readily agreeable client:
              • If your client readily agrees to give up access to means and puts up no resistance, you may need to be a little suspicious. Sometimes lack of resistance means that the person is already considering a substitute method or holding something back.
                • For example, the client may be thinking, “Okay, now that I don’t have guns, I’ll just use pills.” If you have doubts, say something like, “Thank you for your help on this. Now I’m wondering what other means you’ve considered and if those means are available to you.”
            • Mixed messages from family members:
              • If you are working with family members and one member is agreeable but the gun-owning family member seems reluctant, this may indicate a lack of commitment by the gun owner to follow through with the plan.
    • Monitor Progress:
      • Document and Monitor:
        • Once you've arrived at a mutually acceptable plan for reducing access to lethal means, document it in your notes.
          • Access to lethal means:
            • For all persons identified at risk for suicide, indicate that you have assessed for specific access to lethal means.
          • Recommendations and Steps:
            • List any recommendations and steps agreed upon with the client to reduce access to lethal means and make the environment safer.
          • Contact with Others:
            • Indicate if you contacted family members or a support person to confirm the removal or securing of lethal means. If the client or family has engaged law enforcement, a pharmacy, or others to hold or dispose of lethal means, this should be documented in the medical records.
        • One way to ensure that lethal means counseling becomes a standard component of working with people at risk of suicide is, where possible, to build prompts into electronic templates and paper forms that serve as a reminder. For example, systems that use electronic medical records can embed specific lethal means documentation into templates for intake assessments, crisis appointments, therapy or medication appointments, and many other appointment types.
      • Recommendations for Follow-Up:
        • Never assume that the agreed-on plan will go as intended. Follow-up is essential.
        • Suggestions for monitoring progress:
          • Make follow-up calls
            • Call to check on progress and assess how the client is doing. Or ask the client or family member to contact you when they've carried out the tasks in the plan.
          • Continue to assess suicide risk:
            • Assess and monitor your client's risk of suicide on an ongoing basis. Things can improve or deteriorate rapidly. Return to discussions of means reduction as needed.
          • Adapt plan
            • Make any necessary changes to the plan if the client's situation changes. For instance, if a client moves out of their current home and in with a friend, the plan would need to be updated for the new living situation, and the friend would need to be collaboratively engaged in the plan.
          • Encourage close supervision
            • Encourage family, relatives, and friends to provide closer supervision since clients may struggle with their moods or have episodes of suicidal thoughts interspersed with stretches of feeling okay.
    • Key Points:
      • Collaborate on a plan to reduce access to lethal means and enlist the support of family and friends.
      • Agree on roles and timetable.
      • Document the plan in your notes and monitor progress.
      • Continue to monitor the client's suicide risk as treatment continues.
      • Update the plan to reduce means, as needed.
