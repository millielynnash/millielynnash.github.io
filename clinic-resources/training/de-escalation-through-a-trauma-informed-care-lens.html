De-Escalation through a Trauma-Informed Care Lens

Objectives:

  • Increase understanding of the prevalence, characteristics and impact of trauma on individuals.
  • Understand the neurobiology of trauma and how the stress response system impacts functioning.
  • Identity and apply trauma-informed strategies for deescalating crisis situations and working with people who are emotionally distressed.

Overview of Trauma and Impact on Functioning

Definition of Trauma:

The Three 'E's' of Trauma:

  • An event, series of events, or set of circumstances that is experienced by an individual as physically or emotionally harmful or life threatening and that has lasting adverse effects.

Types of Trauma:

Family Trauma:

  • Abuse, neglect, experiencing or witnessing violence, incarceration of family members, family substance abuse, sudden or expected loss of a loved one

Refugee and Immigrant Trauma:

  • Exposure to war, political violence, torture, forced displacement, migration and acculturation stressors, fears of deportation

Medical Trauma:

  • Pain, injury and serious illness; invasive medical procedures or treatments

Poverty:

  • Lack of resources, support networks, or mobility; financial stressors; homelessness

Natural Disasters:

  • Hurricanes, fires, floods

Human-Caused Disasters:

  • Accidents, wars, environmental disasters, acts of terrorism

Community Violence:

  • Robberies, shootings, assault, gang-related violence, hate crimes, group trauma affecting a particular community

School Violence:

  • Threats, fights, school shootings, bullying, loss of a student or staff member

Complex Trauma:

  • Exposure to multiple traumatic events from an early age.
  • The immediate and long-term effects of these experiences over development.

Historical Trauma:

  • "The cumulative emotional and psychological wounding across generations, including the lifespan, which emanates from massive group trauma." (Braveheart)
  • Examples:
    • American Indian and Alaskan Native communities
    • Communities of color
    • Holocaust survivors
    • Japanese-American survivors of internment camps
    • LGBTQ communities

Racial Trauma:

  • Potentially traumatic experiences resulting from:
    • Direct experiences of racial harassment
    • Witnessing racial violence toward others
    • Experiencing discrimination and institutional racism

Degrees of Trauma:

Acute Trauma:

  • A single traumatic event that is limited in time

Chronic Trauma:

  • Experiencing multiple traumatic events

Complex Trauma:

  • Exposure to chronic trauma which impacts the child's development
    • Usually caused by adults entrusted with the child's care

Acute Stress vs. Post-Traumatic Stress

Acute Stress:
  • NORMAL response/reaction to stress and even trauma (4-6 weeks)
Posttraumatic Stress:
  • Exaggerated and prolonged stress response beyond 4-6 weeks and even years later
    • Dysregulation of stress chemistry - stress hormone levels don't decrease
    • Increased activation of Sympathetic Nervous System (heart rate, startle response, fight response)

Facts:

  • 70% of adults in the US have experienced some types of traumatic event at least once in their lives.
    • 223.4 million people
  • In public behavioral health, over 90% of clients have experienced trauma.
  • Trauma is a risk factor in nearly all behavioral health and substance use disorders.

Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACE):

  • Collaboration between the CDC and Kaiser Permanente study of over 17,000 adult patients in health maintenance organizations (1995-1997); 2 waves of data collection
  • Gathers information about health history and exposure to the following events prior to age 18:
    • Recurrent physical abuse
    • Recurrent emotional abuse
    • Sexual abuse
    • An alcohol or drug abuser
    • An incarcerated household member
    • Someone who is chronically depressed, suicidal, institutionalized or mentally ill
    • Mother being treated violently
    • One or no parents
    • Emotional or physical neglect
  • “How ACEs influence health and wellbeing through lifespan.png” could not be found.

Exposure to ACE's is a Pervasive Problem

  • Nearly two-thirds Ohioans have been exposed to ACEs
  • Ohioans more likely to experience multiple ACEs:
    • People of color
    • People with low-income
    • People with a disability
    • Residents of urban and Appalachian counties

Is All Stress Bad?

  • Stress:
    • Unpredictable, Severe, Prolonged -> Vulnerability
    • Predictable, Moderate, Controlled -> Resilience

Pandemic Fatigue

  • Natural response to the prolonged uncertainty and disruption caused by a pandemic
    • Lack of motivation
    • Stress over tasks that would generally be managed well
    • Changes in eating or sleeping habits
    • Irritability
    • Feelings of hopelessness
    • Difficulty concentrating

Stress:

  • At any given point in time, we have a limited supply of 'adaptive energy'
  • In the best of conditions, this reservoir is quite high and will allow us to meet unusual challenges
  • Chronic and prolonged stress related to COVID-19 and other stressors has depleted the reservoir of adaptive energy, making us all more vulnerable to stress.

Neurobiology of Trauma-Stress Response System

The Stress Response:

  1. The amygdala senses threat and sets off the alarm.
  2. Thinking brain assess the situation.
  3. Thinking brain goes offline. Emotional brain activates fight or flight response.
  4. Thinking brain helps shut off the alarm and helps us to calm down.

The Stress Response and Trauma:

  • An experience becomes traumatic when it overwhelms our system for responding to stress.
  • The emotional brain continues to sound the alarm and send messages to fight or flee, even after the threat has passed.

Nervous System:

  • Autonomic Nervous System:
    • Direct role in physical response to stress.
      • Sympathetic Nervous System:
        • 'Fight or flight' response
        • Body shifts energy resources toward fighting off or fleeing a threat
        • Signals release of adrenaline and cortisol
        • Heart beats faster, respiration rate increases, blood vessels in arms and legs dilate, digestive process changes, glucose levels in blood (sugar energy) increase
      • Parasympathetic Nervous System:
        • Facilitates recovery once acute stressor is over
        • Over-activity can contribute to stress reactions
  • Chronic stress can result in a long-term drain on the body
    • Wear and tear
    • Continuous activation of the nervous system can be problematic in all systems.

Chronic Activation of the Stress Response System

  • Musculoskeletal System:
    • Muscles in body are in constant state of guardedness
      • Muscle tension, headaches, migraines, back pain
  • Respiratory System
    • Shortness of breath
    • Rapid breathing
  • Cardiovascular System
    • Elevated heart rate, elevated blood pressure, elevated stress hormones, inflammation
  • Endocrine System
    • Increased production of cortisol
  • Gastrointestinal System
    • Changes in gut bacteria, pain, bloating, discomfort
  • Esophagus
    • Eat more or less than usual, increased use of substances, heartburn/acid reflux

Risk and Protective Factors

Individual Factors:

  • History of previous exposure to trauma
  • Age of exposure
  • Gender
  • Cognitive ability
  • Self-efficacy
  • Biological factors (e.g. flexibility in thinking, emotional regulation)

Environmental Factors:

  • Nature of the traumatic event
  • Proximity to the traumatic event
  • Culture and ethnicity
  • Level of social support
  • Quality of parent-child relationships, parent mental health, and parental history of trauma
  • Health of the broader community

Post-Trauma Pathways:

  • Positive:
    • Resilience
      • A positive, adaptive response to significant adversity.
        • Adaptable, caring, and supportive relationship with an adult.
        • A sense of mastery over life circumstances.
        • Strong executive function and self-regulation skills.
        • Affirming faith or cultural traditions
      • Individuals may demonstrate resilience in one type of situation but not another.
      • Coping skills that support resilience can be developed at any age.
      • Children do not develop the capacity to positive adapt to adversity in isolation.
      • Regardless of resources, individuals who face extreme adversity are likely to be significantly impacted.
      • We all play an important role in fostering resilience.
    • Recovery
    • Post-traumatic growth
  • Negative:
    • Severe persisting distress
    • Decline
    • Stable maladaptive functioning
  • We need to presume the clients we serve have a history of traumatic stress and exercise “universal precautions” by creating systems of care that are trauma-informed.

Principles of Trauma-Informed Care

  • Understanding trauma and its impact
  • Promoting safety
  • Ensuring cultural competence
  • Supporting control, choice, autonomy
  • Collaboration and mutuality
  • Integrating care
  • Healing happens in relationships
  • Recovery is possible

Managing an Escalating Situation

What is De-Escalation?

  • Bringing a volatile and potentially explosive situation to a peaceful, calm resolution by verbal means... and no one getting hurt.
  • Key Components:
    • Body language:
      • Facial expression
      • Posture
      • Position
    • Tone of voice
    • Active listening

Prevention: Awareness of Yourself

  • Judgement/Bias/Fatigue/Distractions
  • Body language:
    • Facial expression
    • Posture
    • Position
  • Tone of voice
  • Being in tune with your stress signals
    • Do you have a good sense of how your body communicates when it is overwhelmed?
  • Your PERCEPTION of your ability to effectively manage crisis situations.

Prevention: Awareness of the Individual

  • Body language
  • History of aggression/violence/belligerence
  • Self image
  • Life circumstances
    • Legal issues, work stress, social support, financial situation
  • Drug and alcohol use/abuse
  • Physical health
  • Mental functioning
  • Emotional health
  • Trauma exposure

Impact of Traumatic Stress

  • Affect person's perception of self, the world, and the future
  • Affect person's:
    • Ability to trust others
    • Sense of personal safety
    • Effectiveness in navigating life's challenges
      • Difficulty processing information
      • Short-term memory impairment
      • Perceptual field narrows
    • Ability to regulate emotions effectively

Trauma Exposure Response

  • Feeling helpless and hopeless
  • A sense that one can never do enough
  • Hypervigilance
  • Diminished creativity
  • Inability to embrace complexity
  • Minimizing
  • Chronic exhaustion/physical ailments
  • Dissociative moments
  • Sense of persecution
  • Guilt
  • Fear
  • Anger and cynicism
  • Inability to emphasize/numbing
  • Addictions
  • Grandiosity:
    • Inflated sense of importance

Prevention: Awareness of the Environment

  • Overcrowded?
  • Loud?
  • Overstimulating?
  • Other clients present? Kids present?
  • Other staff visible? Able to support/get help if needed?
  • Person kept waiting? Ignored?
  • Objects around that could be used for violence?
  • Open path to exit?

Prevention: Bringing it All Together

  • Think and plan ahead
    • Be familiar with agency policies/procedures
    • Develop plans for 'high risk' clients in advance
  • Develop plans for common scenarios based on your role, department, program, etc.
  • Monitor and manage your own stress level

De-Escalation Strategies

Pro-Active vs. Reactive

  • Clients in crisis require structure and boundaries to help re-establish stability
  • Engaging client in discussion (repeated concerns can inadvertently contribute to chaos and triangulation; following the identified plan is the best way to support and care for these clients)
  • Parallel Process:
    • Don't get pulled into the chaos
    • Setting firm, clear boundaries creates safety and minimizes potential for triangulation and miscommunication
  • Identifying single point of contact for clients help ensure we have clear, consistent communication and avoids potential for triangulation

Remember: Strengths Based Approach

  • People are doing the best they can to get their needs met
  • Escalation is likely a learned behavior
  • Be PROACTIVE, not REACTIVE
    • Attend to person before things escalate

Assessing the Situation

  • Stage 1: Anxiety/Agitation
  • Stage 2: Argumentative/Defensive
  • Stage 3: Verbally, Physically Threatening
  • Stage 4: Assaultive

Concrete vs. Abstract Thinking

  • The more threatened the individual, the more 'primitive' (or regressed) becomes the style of thinking and behaving.
  • When a traumatized person is in a state of alarm:
    • They will be less capable of concentrating
    • They will be more anxious
    • They will pay more attention to non-verbal cues
      • Tone of voice, body posture, and facial expressions

Understanding Triggers:

  • Trigger:
    • Any stimulus that acts as a reminder of past overwhelming experience
      • Internal:
        • Body sensations, emotions, etc.
      • External:
        • Sounds, sights, smells, facial expressions, etc.
  • Common triggers:
    • Unpredictability, sudden change, transitions
    • Loss of control
    • Feeling vulnerable
    • Feeling rejected
    • Confrontation
    • Loneliness
    • Sensory overload
    • Intimacy (safety, love, security, family)
    • Peace, calm, quiet
  • 'Re-Experiencing' can lead to out-of-control behavior
    • When someone with a trauma history begins to escalate, it may be because they are remembering or re-experiencing a traumatic event
    • They may be reminded of the trauma and be flooded or overwhelmed with memories of that earlier traumatic event.

Managing an Escalating Situation

  • Remember your superpowers:
    • Body language
      • Facial expression
      • Posture
      • Position
    • Tone of voice
    • Active listening
  • Calm and caring attitude
  • Ask for help

Remember:

  • Someone who is escalated is under stress and not likely to think clearly
  • Your perception of your ability to cope with the situation is going to impact your own stress level when managing these interactions
  • De-escalation skills start with self-awareness.
    • Understanding how stress affects how you act, think, and communicate

De-Escalation Tips:

  • Speak in a calm, slow, warm, clear voice
  • Show that you care
  • Be aware of your non-verbal communication
  • Acknowledge feelings
  • Maintain eye contact (but don't stare)
  • You may need to repeat yourself; the client may be distracted
  • Be patient; give the situation time; time is on your side
  • Remain a friendly tone and join with the client
    • Helps the client see that you and the client are a team
    • "We are going to figure this out"
  • Allow the client to vent ("Tell me more about that")
  • Use 'Please' and 'thank you' often
  • Accept the client's feelings, thoughts and behaviors without judgement
    • Remember that most of us have been irrational and said inappropriate things when we are under extreme stress
  • Effective communication often depends as much on how you say the words you choose as much as the words themselves
  • Be vigilant and aware of your surroundings
  • Alert others, if needed
  • Evaluate each situation for violence
  • Keep an open path
  • Remove yourself/others if situation is getting out of control

Goals of Verbal De-Escalation

  • Open up clear lines of communication
  • Build trust and validate the client's situation (even if you don't agree)
  • Get the client talking about concerns as a route to calming
  • Gather the necessary information to make a good resolution

Managing Yourself

  • If a client is escalated, they feel out of control which tells us that their normal coping measures are not working
  • Keep your focus. You are trying to give the client a sense that they are in control.
    • When someone feels out of control, they are more likely to escalate
  • Your ability to engage a client in conversation successfully can resolve the conflict
  • If it seems you may not get there, ask for help

Open-Ended Questions

  • Shows interest
  • Close-ended questions may be necessary but client is more likely to tell you what you want to hear
  • "What can I help you with today?" vs. "Do you want to schedule an appointment?"
  • "What time of day works best for you?" vs. "Can you come at 2 pm?"
  • "Can you tell me..."

Affirmations

  • Being expressively/explicitly kind
  • Builds rapport and nurtures competence
    • "I appreciate you telling me what's wrong."
    • "It took a lot of courage for you to reach out today."
    • "You've done a great job of keeping your appointments in the past. We really appreciate that."

Reflective Listening

  • Validates and shows you’ve heard the client
  • Mirror back what you’re hearing is going on • Allow further explanation of the concern by not inserting your opinion
  • Listen for feelings, themes, motivations, unmet need
    • “It sounds like you’ve had a really tough time.”
    • “You’re really angry about what’s happened.”
    • “You’re ____.”
      • Insert any emotion—“mad,” “upset,” “frustrated,” “worried,” etc. You can’t do it wrong!
    • “Sounds like…”
    • “Seems like…”
    • “It feels like…”
    • “To you, it looks like…”
    • “I am hearing…”
    • “I am wondering if…”

Summarize

  • Help transition the client to resolution:
    • “So you’ve been through a lot and you’re really upset about everything that’s happened before. It sounds like we need to get you in to talk to someone. Would it be okay to get you scheduled for an appointment?”
    • “The last time you were here, you thought you got the run around and didn’t have time to see the provider. You were really frustrated by all that. How about if we get you scheduled again and see if we can get things resolved?”

Managing Yourself

  • Remember the overall goal - client care
  • If you take a LESS authoritative, LESS controlling, LESS confrontational approach, you actually will have MORE control in the situation.

Barriers to Effective Communication

  • Responding angrily
  • Not listening
  • Criticizing
  • Being defensive
  • Arguing with the client
  • Engaging in power struggles
  • Ordering the client what to do
  • Threatening (to cancel an appointment, etc.)
  • Minimizing the importance of the client's concern
  • Moving quickly (can be perceived as aggressive)

Avoid

  • Saying "You need to calm down"
  • Trying to be right
  • Giving commands
    • "lower your voice"
  • Arguing with the client
  • Taking the words or actions of the client personally (even if the client tries to make it personal)
  • Minimizing the situation ("It is not that bad")
  • Asking a lot of questions
  • Asking 'Why' questions
  • Forcing discussion

Managing the Interaction

  • Do not express anger, impatience, or irritation
    • Breathe
    • Take a moment away, if possible
  • Never use inflammatory language ("You are acting crazy")
  • Notice if you are feeling as though you have to rush or feeling like you are stuck if it takes time to get to work through the client interaction
    • Breathe through it, be patient
    • Request help, if needed
  • Don't intervene too quickly or try too hard to control the interaction by interrupting or talking over the client

After an Event

  • Follow agency policy
    • Document
  • Debrief
    • Strategies to manage/avoid similar situations in future
      • What went well? What didn't go well?
      • Did anything contribute to the escalation?
      • Did anything help diffuse the situation?
    • Support for staff, clients, and individuals involved

Trauma Informed Care (No Blame, No Shame Mindset)

  • People are coping the best way they can - our job is to help them find a better way
  • Requires building trust, creating safety, and creating a trauma informed culture that provides a safe and healing environment
  • Healing takes more than therapy
    • Support services can create therapeutic, healing experiences - a place of safety

Trauma Informed Care (Approach to De-Escalation)

  • Screen for trauma history
  • Deepen attunement skills - learn to interpret behavior effectively as communication
  • Employ person-centered, strengths-based thinking and language
  • Provide stability and empowerment
  • Consider the physiological, psychological, and social risks of physical interventions - choose the least restrictive option as a last resort if somebody is a danger to themselves or others
  • Prioritize debriefing after a crisis to help foster resilience and develop successful coping skills

Top 10 De-Escalation Tips

  1. Be empathetic and non-judgemental
  2. Respect personal space
  3. Use non-threatening non-verbals
  4. Avoid overreacting
  5. Focus on feelings
  6. Ignore challenging questions
  7. Set limits
  8. Choose wisely what you insist upon
  9. Allow silence for reflection
  10. Allow time for decisions
