Strategies for Working with High Conflict Couples

  • Why are couples behaving so badly?
    • "The average couple waits 6 years before seeking out therapy." (Gottman)
      • In those 6 years there is:
        • Build up of resentments
        • Reinforced bad habits
        • No boundary enforcement
        • Erosion of positive perspective
        • Unconscious bias kicks - assumptions about purposeful poor behavior choices
        • Missed bids for attention
        • Depletion of emotional bank account
        • Never had any positive role models to begin with
    • From a survey conducted in September 2020:
      • Increases in substance misuse:
        • Alcohol (88%)
        • Marijuana (37%)
        • Prescription Opioids (15%)
      • Domestic violence cases increased by 25%-33% globally.
  • How are these influences showing up in the clients in the therapy room?
    • Passive/aggressive behaviors
    • Shut down/Withdrawn/Stonewalling
    • Withholding Sex or Avoiding Intimacy
    • Narcissistic Behavior Responses
    • Gaslighting
    • Anger
    • High Conflict
    • Boundary Crossing
    • Persistent and Ongoing Flooding
    • Unwilling to Repair/Apologize/Take Responsibility

Preparation:

  • How to prepare yourself for the unexpected:
    • When you open your office door, look for body language and non-verbals.
    • Have confidence and KNOW that you are the expert in the room. Own it and be brave.

Intention:

  • Set the tone at the beginning of the session when you feel something is off.
  • Let the clients know what you feel when you feel it, and be willing to talk about what is happening. (Do not wait for clients to bring it up or ignore it)
  • Model for your couples that you are going to talk about it, not let things be passive-aggressive or avoid dealing with hot topics.
  • Remind clients who escalate that they are not in therapy to repeat old patterns.
  • Pause and interrupt when you see unacknowledged emotion happening in the room. That is your job.

Boundaries when Couples Escalate

  • Don't let your couples argue or verbally explode on each other. They can do that at home for free.
  • Take Control.
    • Develop your phrase to create stop-action with clients. Say things like, 'pause, stop,' or call out your client's name, etc.
  • Be direct.
    • What is acceptable and what is not acceptable about words or behavior in the room. What you would like your clients to do instead (as for their positive need).
    • Why it's not okay for them to say or do the thing that you witnessed in the room.
    • Let the clients know how painful it is for you to witness, so you can't imagine what it's like for their partner.

Understanding Each Partner

  • Keep your eyes on each client in the room. Are you letting one partner high jack the session? Stop the speaking partner and ask, 'What's coming up for you?' Notice if the other partner checks in.
  • Be mindful of the need to stop and tend to the party not speaking. This is how you keep explosions from getting out of control because you have been checking in on what is happening on the other side of the couch.
  • Step in for clients when they don't have words and find a way to speak their pain to their partner.
  • Keep it fair, so both partners feel you are connected with their pain, needs, and wanted outcomes.

Using Theory to Psychoeducate Couples

  • Normalize high emotion and disappointment of partner not being able to acknowledge perspectives not because they don't love you. Differences on family of origin, difference in expectations, etc.
  • Prior traumas and life experiences make it hard for partners to always regulate their emotions in ways they would hope.
  • Untreated mental health issues impact ability to manage emotions.
  • Not finished working on the 'Me' stuff versus the 'We' stuff and bleeding over into our relationship.
  • Discuss the impact of substances and the impact it has on being able to manage emotions.
  • Gottman Method:
    • Flooding is expected because partners are experiencing physiological arousal. Utilize pulse oximeters to help validate their elevated heart rate.

Diffuse

  • Be okay with breaking it up.
    • Sometimes you need to have one couple leave the room while you do some one-on-one with the other partner.
    • Let an explosive partner leave if they want. Don't chase them or try to convince them to stay or come back.
    • Talk about the flooding in the room and share ways to help self-regulate, like taking deep breaths or pausing to find ways to reframe.

Evaluate if the Couple is Ready for Therapy

  • You need to be prepared when working with couples that you might need to a break from couples therapy or stop all together based on how the clients present.
  • This is your call as a therapist to evaluate if working with the couple is contraindicated because of co-occurring mental health, substance, unresolved trauma, suicidal ideation, personality disorders, characterological DV, or anger management issues.
  • Often necessary to request that clients seek out individual therapy to strengthen their individual coping skills before attempting additional couples' therapy.
  • Expect your clients to disagree and give you pushback. Attitude is everything in this conversation. Remember, your clients are not your peers, and we often have to make unpopular decisions about the course of care.

Self-Check-In

  • When you feel anxious and flooded as the therapist:
    • Notice where you are breathing.
    • Plant both feet on the floor. Take your shoulders back against your chair, take a deep breath into your belly, and repeat to yourself:
      • "This is not my pain to feel."

Be Curious vs. Furious

  • Get in touch with your client's inner 5-year-old old in the room. What is hurting them, and what is the catastrophe scenario that is playing out for them?
  • When I de-escalate, so do my clients. When my voice gets softer and quieter, so does my clients, and they can often match my tone and calm demeanor.
  • Don't shy away from acknowledging they have the right to feel upset, and it makes sense they would be even if their partner does not agree.
  • Ask their partner to find something they said that they can validate for their partner. Remind them this is not the same as agreeing.
  • Focus on the behavior or the words you heard.
    • We can help facilitate meaningful conversation about the emotions each partner is feeling.
    • Ask the partner who is feeling a big emotion related to their partner to name their emotion. Then talk the behavior they say their partner do or the words they heard them say.
    • Remind and monitor clients to avoid telling their partner their evaluation of why their partner did it or the mind set they were in when it happened. This often happens and only creates defensiveness in their partner. No one likes to be told how they feel.
    • Ask for your positive need. Even if the answer is no - you need to hear yourself asking for your need.

HELPFUL Interventions in Session and Beyond

  • Gottman Method Stress Reducing Conversation
    • 10-minutes each partner. Listening for understanding and share emotion. (Not problem-solving)
    • How do clients know it's working? Does your partner feel hear and understood? Then yes.
    • Use of open-ended questions to help partners attune, connect, and provide empathy and support.
  • Questions that allow for each partner to draw out their partner's experience, such as:
    • "What is this like for you? What do you need? What don't you like about this situation?"
  • Gottman Method Dreams within Conflict
    • Asks clients to suspend problem-solving so they can have a better understanding of the childhood stories, values, or core ethics and beliefs that keep their partner rooted in their perspective.
    • Clients take turns asking each partner a set of questions so they can better experience what it's like to walk in their partner's shoes.
    • Preparation for compromise like partners who love each other.
  • Gottman Method Injuries Exercise
    • Helps partners find a structured and safe format to talk about old injuries in a way that helps facilitate compassion, acceptance, and understand about prior and shared traumas.
    • Creates awareness and insight about the impact and ways these traumas have shaped the way each partner views their world.
    • Helps evaluate how clients have learned to cope and survive. Can help some clients gain insight on how they behave in relationships in ways that no longer serve them.
    • Helps couples therapist normalize and make recommendations of ways those old injuries get triggered in the relationship and behavior changes that might be beneficial.
  • Gottman Method Appreciations Exercise
    • Changes tone and energy in session.
    • Allows each partner to find something positive to notice about their partner to demonstrate holding each other from a positive perspective.
    • Makes deposits into each partner’s emotional bank account.
    • Starts conversations about how to do small things often in the relationship for highly disconnected couples.
    • Way to end session, so couples can ground before leaving in turmoil.

Gaslighting (Impacts and Behaviors)

  • If apologies are made, they are often expressed as implying you misunderstood something but without remorse for poor behaviors
  • Outside behaviors or the other person is ALWAYS the source of conflict or turn it around on the other person implying they are really the culprit for the issue
  • Self-doubt creeps in and the partner being gaslit internalizes their lack of hard work in the relationship as the cause of all the problems.
  • Guilt develops about bringing up issues in the relationship.
  • It feels very much like the result of narcissistic behavior responses.
  • The partner who is doing the gaslighting may do so unconsciously to gain control or power in the relationship when they feel out of resources or support.
  • Manipulation that involves making someone question their own reality, feelings, and experiences of events, in order to maintain CONTROL over that person.

Behaviors

  • Deflecting the other person by use of lack of acknowledgement, negative body language, and minimizing statements about the other person’s perspective or feelings.
  • Unwillingness to accept any part of the responsibility for a misunderstanding or conflict.
  • Intentional implication that the other person is over-reacting or has fabricated a situation in order to create doubt or challenge the other person’s reality
  • Being cut off from making points, asking questions, or providing input during a disagreement.
  • No acknowledgment of your hurt feelings when expressed. Lacks Empathy.
  • Clients are often overly invested in using narcissist label with each other.
    • Only 5% of population are diagnosable with narcissistic personality disorder
    • You should be aware that most clients who have diagnosable narcissistic personality disorder are not going to be served in couples therapy and may be contra-indicated.

Interventions to Work with Narcissistic 'Behaviors'

  • Gottman Interventions to Bring into Session
    • Introduces the idea that anything our partner does for us is a gift - they are not required to do so because we are in a relationship with each other.
    • Softened Start Up:
      • This is helpful for the narcissistic leaning partner to learn how to be clear in their needs and learn how to share why it is important to them. This helps them to slowly understand they are not entitled to anything.
    • Appreciations:
      • Help the narcissistic partner to think about what the other person adds to their life and to acknowledge it.
    • Accept the things you can't change.
      • Helpful for both partners to understand there are some things that are unlikely to ever completely be different in their dynamics. Helps partners make a choice about the relationship from that space of acceptance.
    • Aftermath of a Regrettable Incident:
      • Introduction to Narcissistic person about their role in feeling disrespected or unappreciated.
    • Active listening exercises like the Gottman-Rapport

Best Ways to Carry Yourself in Session with Narcissistic Personality Disorder Clients

  • You must interrupt patterns of rage, repeated interruptions, storming off, or screaming.
  • Be on the lookout for a partner who has NPD to be overly charming or makes big declarations of victimhood, or says their only goal is “to fight for the relationship.”
  • Avoid becoming a victim of triangulation or becoming caught up in buying into the hope that you can fix the narcissistic behaviors.
  • You MUST be comfortable being direct, holding a strong position of authority in the room, and be willing to call out poor behavior that is impacting the relationship.
  • Strongly suggest and sometimes require that both partners be in individual therapy and check in with this therapist to make sure they are aware of the dynamics happening in the relationship.
  • The only goal you may accomplish is validating the victim that they are correct in their reality and that they can trust their intuition. You may serve as the objective voice this person needs to get clear and end the relationship.
  • Don’t fight, argue, or try to overpower the narcissist.
  • There is a very strong likelihood that despite your support that these sessions may not go as you hope!
  • Many clients who have narcissistic behaviors can and will use the tools they learn in session to abuse, further manipulate, or weaponize against their partner.
  • True NPD will NOT be ok with the change in or loss of power and will retaliate.
  • Evaluate if the partner is becoming more agitated, aggressive, and if you suspect there is an increased likelihood for physical abuse-stop sessions.
  • Recognize you can and potentially should take a break from therapy with the couples.
  • Should you refer them to seek individual therapy for a while and then re-evaluate if they are good candidates for couples therapy?
  • Couples therapy can re-traumatize the partner who is being victimized.
  • Consider if this is in the scope of your practice? Do you need to refer out?

Take Time at End of Session to Reframe What Happened

  • Make time before letting clients go to let them know that it was a hard session and how important it was to have it.
  • Let your partner know that if they are flooded when they will come back and talk about it with you. Make a date with each other to come back and process.
  • Check in with each partner about what they will do to self-soothe.
  • Remind clients that emotions and feeling hurt or misunderstood happens in relationships. The couple is not in this situation because they don’t love each other but because the stakes are so high. This is your person and if we are not ok then nothing feels ok.
  • Let them know you are proud of them for doing the work.
